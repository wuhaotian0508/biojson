# Introduction

It is estimated that the extent of saline-alkaline soils in irrigated lands will increase by $5 0 \%$ due to the application of chemical fertilizers in the near future [1]. Notably, approximately $6 0 \%$ of salinized soils develop alkaline stress, characterized by elevated sodium carbonate $\left( \mathsf { N a } _ { 2 } \mathsf { C O } _ { 3 } \right)$ and bicarbonate $\left( \mathsf { N a H C O } _ { 3 } \right)$ levels, which impose more severe physiological constraints than conventional salinity $( \mathsf { N a C l } / \mathsf { N a } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S O } _ { 4 } )$ [2]. While salt stress primarily disrupts plant homeostasis through osmotic imbalance, oxidative burst, and ion toxicity [3,4], alkaline stress presents a dual challenge, involving both pH-induced cellular alkalization and ionic disequilibrium, which exacerbates metabolic dysfunction. The high pH levels associated with alkaline stress can disrupt membrane potential and inhibit the absorption of essential micronutrients such as iron (Fe), zinc (Zn), and manganese (Mn) [5]. The accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) is a hallmark of plant responses to abiotic stresses, particularly under alkaline conditions, where redox imbalance leads to oxidative damage of proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids [6]. 

To mitigate the overaccumulation of ROS and associated oxidative damage, plants utilize an antioxidant system that detoxifies ROS, thereby maintaining cellular redox homeostasis [7]. AsA, a key component of the non-enzymatic antioxidant system, serves as a pivotal scavenger of excessive ROS, such as singlet oxygen $( ^ { 1 } 0 _ { 2 } )$ and superoxide anions [8]. Additionally, AsA serves as an essential cofactor for various enzymes, facilitating a wide range of enzymatic reactions within the cell [9]. In plants, the primary biosynthetic pathway for AsA is the Smirnoff–Wheeler (Lgalactose) pathway [10]. This pathway is initiated by phosphoglucose isomerase (PGI, EC 5.3.1.9) and culminates in the action of Lgalactono-1,4-lactone dehydrogenase (GLDH, EC 1.3.2.3), which catalyzes the terminal step of AsA synthesis [11]. 

GLDH, a mitochondrial inner membrane-anchored FADdependent oxidoreductase, catalyzes the terminal stereospecific oxidation of L-galactono-1,4-lactone to AsA in the Smirnoff-Wheeler pathway, constituting the rate-limiting step in maintaining cellular redox homeostasis in higher plants [10,12]. This step in the GLDH-catalyzed process represents a crucial regulatory point in the AsA biosynthetic pathway, a subject of extensive investigation in recent years [13–15]. It has been demonstrated that GLDH activity is essential for plant growth and development, playing a key role in mediating responses to environmental stresses. For instance, the GLDH gene is associated with enhanced drought resistance in wheat, primarily through the regulation of stomatal opening, and a significant reduction in TaGLDH expression in wheat 

leads to decreased GLDH activity and AsA levels [16]. Additionally, overexpression of GLDH enhanced tolerance to oxidative stress by regulating AsA synthesis in tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum) BY-2 cells [17]. In tobacco, overexpression of RrGalLDH could increase the tolerance to salt stress by increasing AsA accumulation, shoot length and fresh weight [18]. To date, however, there is limited information regarding the role of GLDH in cotton under abiotic stresses, particularly alkaline stress. 

Cotton, a major cash crop renowned for its exceptional natural fiber, demonstrates remarkable tolerance to saline-alkali stress compared to other crops. This characteristic makes it a key model species for investigating plant adaptation mechanisms in marginal soils, particularly due to its pioneer role in saline-alkali ecosystems [19–22]. However, cotton faces persistent physiological challenges throughout its life cycle, exacerbated by the increasing frequency of extreme weather events in recent years. These challenges include drought, waterlogging, cold, salt and alkaline stress, among others. In particular, the damage inflicted by alkaline stress, which is more severe in comparison to other stress factors. Under $\Nu _ { 4 } \subset \phantom { \sc } 0 _ { 3 }$ stress at the three-leaf stage, cotton seedlings exhibited severe root desiccation and blackening, with significant loss of leaf gloss and darkening of the leaf veins. Additionally, chlorophyII content and relative water content were markedly reduced [21]. Under ${ \mathsf { N a H C O } } _ { 3 }$ stress, cotton seedlings showed brownish-yellow roots, reddening at the base of the stem, wilting of cotyledons, and signs of water loss and wilting in the true leaves, with yellowing along the leaf veins [23]. $\Nu _ { 4 } \subset \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ stress also inhibits the growth of the cotton hypocotyl, significantly reducing the length of hypocotyl cells [24]. Therefore, investigating the morpho-physiological adaptation processes of cotton in alkaline stress environments, along with decoding the genomic determinants underlying alkali tolerance, holds critical scientific significance for advancing the development of stress-resilient crops. 

In this study, we performed a comprehensive evolutionary analysis of GLDH gene family, including phylogenetic reconstruction, characterization of exon–intron architectures and conserved motif compositions, identification of cis-acting elements in promoter regions associated with stress responses, and expression profiles of GhGLDH genes in Gossypium hirsutum. Moreover, the phenotypes of cotton under different salt and alkaline stress at germination and three-leaf stage were also assessed. Furthermore, a novel alkaline stress-responsive gene designated GhGLDH35A was excavated and its molecular characteristics were characterized. The function role of the GhGLDH35A gene was preliminarily verified in both cotton and Arabidopsis, revealing its active response to alkaline stress. Based on these findings, we proposed a potential mechanism 

underlying the action of GhGLDH35A under alkaline stress, providing essential insights for the development of alkali-tolerant cotton cultivars. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The G. hirsutum accession Zhong 9807 (alkali tolerance) and GK50 (alkali sensitivity) was utilized as genetic material to investigate salt and alkali resistance during germination. The germination experiment was conducted on filter paper under salt and alkaline stress conditions, following established protocols [24]. Briefly, cotton seeds were surfaced sterilized, then aseptically placed between layers of filter paper and subjected to dark-phase germination at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ for 2 d. Seeds exhibiting consistent germination vigor and synchronized growth patterns were surface sterilized again and wrapped in filter paper soaked in solutions of 100 mM NaCl, 50 mM ${ \ N a } _ { 2 } S O _ { 4 }$ , 50 mM $\Nu _ { 4 } \subset \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ and $1 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m M }$ ${ \mathsf { N a H C O } } _ { 3 }$ , with ${ \mathrm { d } } { \mathrm { d } } { \mathrm { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathrm { O } }$ treatment as the control. The wrapped seeds were placed in beakers, ensuring that the lower part of the filter paper was submerged in the respective solutions. Germinate occurred in darkness at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ for 5 d. Each treatment included 100 seeds, with 3 biological replicates. Root length and fresh weight were measured 5 d after germination $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ with dark. For root length and fresh weight measurements, 10 germinated seeds per replicate were selected. Cotton seedlings were subsequently cultured at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } C$ under a $1 6 ~ \mathrm { h }$ light $/ 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ dark cycle. For alkaline stress treatment, cotton seedlings at the three-leaf stage were exposed to $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m L } }$ of 100 mM NaCl, 50 mM ${ \Nu } _ { 4 } . { \cal S } 0 _ { 4 }$ , 50 mM Na2- ${ \bf C } { \bf O } _ { 3 }$ and 100 mM ${ \mathsf { N a H C O } } _ { 3 }$ solutions, respectively, with ${ \mathsf { d } } { \mathsf { d } } { \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ treatment as the control, for 12 h. Phenotypic observation was conducted following the treatment. 

After surface sterilization, Arabidopsis seeds were plated on 1/2 Murashige and Skoog (1/2 MS) agar media and subjected to cold stratification at $4 ^ { \circ } C$ in complete darkness for 3 d, and subsequently germinated under standard growth conditions $2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ with a 16/8h light/dark cycle). To assess stress tolerance, seeds were aseptically cultivated on 1/2 MS agar media supplemented with $5 ~ \mathrm { \ m M }$ ${ \mathsf { N a H C O } } _ { 3 }$ , with 1/2 MS agar medium without ${ \mathsf { N a H C O } } _ { 3 }$ serving as the control. For stress treatment at the rosette stage, Arabidopsis plants were treated with $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m L } }$ of 100 and $2 0 0 \mathrm { m M } \mathrm { N a H C O _ { 3 } }$ solutions, with 200 mL ${ \mathsf { d } } { \mathsf { d } } { \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ treatment as the control. Phenotypic changes were observed. During the germination stage, the root length of 6 Arabidopsis plants was measured for each treatment, and the fresh weight of the 6 plants was recorded $\left( \mathrm { n } = 6 \right)$ ). At the rosette stage, the whole plant was weighed to determine fresh weight, with 3 plants per genotype. All experiments were performed with 3 biological replicates $\left( \mathrm { n } = 3 \right)$ ). 

# Sequences alignments and phylogenetic analysis

To analyze the evolutionary relationship within the GLDH family, the corresponding family genes were first identified. Genome sequences for G. hirsutum (data from NAU), G. barbadense (data from HAU), G. arboreum (data from CRI), and G. raimondii (data from JGI) were downloaded from Cotton Functional Genomic Database (CottonFGD) (https://www.cottonfgd.org/) [25]. The Hidden Markov Models (HMM) of PF04030 and PF01565 were downloaded from the Pfam website to retrieve the protein sequences of GLDH from the 4 cotton species. HMMER 3.0 software, using default parameters, was employed to screen the sequences, selecting those with e-values less than 1e-5 that contained candidate genes for the target domains. Sequences for Arabidopsis thaliana (A. thaliana) 

(TAIR 10) and Theobroma cacao (T. cacao) (version 10) were obtained from Phytozome v12.1, using ALO and FAD_binding_4 as search keywords. CD-Search [26] was then used to further screen the obtained GLDH protein sequences, eliminating incomplete C- and N-terminal regions to identify the complete GLDH sequences. Protein sequence alignment was performed using the MUSCLE tool in MEGA7 software. Subsequently, a Neighbor-Joining (NJ) tree for the 4 cotton species was constructed with 1000 bootstrap replicates using the Poisson substitution model with default parameters in MEGA7. The model selection program in MEGA7 was utilized to determine the most appropriate model, revealing that $\mathrm { L } G + G$ was the optimal model. Additionally, a Maximum Likelihood (ML) phylogenetic tree was generated based on the $\mathrm { L } G + G$ model to infer the evolutionary history of 4 cotton species, Arabidopsis, and T. cacao, with 1000 bootstrap replications and other parameters set to default. 

# Collinearity analysis of GLDH genes

The orthologous and paralogous genes were detected using MCScanX tool in TBtools software [27]. Specifically, we applied an overlap window of 25 genes, a gap penalty of 5, and a significance threshold of 1e-10. The integrated evolutionary profiling of coding sequence divergence (Ka/Ks dynamics) and duplication architectures was conducted using TBtools with the Nei-Gojobori model. For visualizing collinear relationships, the ‘‘dual synteny plot” output format in TBtools was utilized. Duplication types were classified according to previously established criteria [28]. Genes located within the same chromosomal block (e-value $\leq 1 \mathsf { e } - 5$ ) were designated as tandem duplicates, while genes located within the same genome but outside the same chromosomal block were categorized as segmental duplicates. Genes derived from different genomes or sub-genomes across the 4 Gossypium species were assigned to whole genome duplication. The divergence time in cotton was estimated based on the calculated Ks values $( \mathbf { t } = K s / 2 \mathrm { r , ~ r = ~ }$ $2 . 6 \times 1 0 ^ { - 9 } $ ), as described in previous studies [29,30]. 

# Analysis of the conserved protein motifs, gene structure and cis-acting element in promoters of GhGLDHs

The conserved motifs of GhGLDH proteins were identified using the Multiple Em for Motif Elicitation (MEME) tool, available at https://meme-suite.org/. The analysis was performed with a maximum of 10 distinct motifs, while all other parameters were set to their default values. The evolutionary relationship, gene structure, and conserved motifs of GhGLDH proteins were visualized using the Visualize MEME/MAST Motif Pattern plugin in TBtools. Upstream DNA sequences of 2000 bp from the GhGLDH genes were retrieved from the CottonFGD database and used as promoters. Cisacting elements in the promoter regions of GhGLDH genes were predicted using the Plant CARE website (https://bioinformatics. psb.ugent.be/webtools/plantcare/html/). Finally, a schematic representation of the phylogenetic tree and cis-acting elements was generated using Simple BioSequence Viewer in TBtools software. 

# Expression patterns of GhGLDH genes under different stresses

To investigate the expression levels of GhGLDH genes, RNA-seq data (PRJNA490626) were obtained from NCBI, encompassing expression patterns across different tissues as well as under cold, heat, salt, and PEG stress conditions [29]. Additionally, transcriptome-wide analysis of GhGLDH gene expression under alkaline stress was conducted using publicly available RNA-seq datasets (GSE165472) [23]. All transcriptomic data were integrated to construct a comprehensive transcriptome, employing Perl 

scripts for data processing. Following transcriptome reconstruction, expression levels of all transcripts were estimated using StringTie and edgeR. StringTie was utilized to calculate the FPKM values for mRNA expression levels. To account for mean–variance dependency, expression values were log-transformed using the $\log _ { 2 }$ (FPKM + 1) function. A heat map was generated using TBTools software, with fragments per kilo base of exon per million fragments mapped (FPKM) representing the expression data. 

# RNA extraction and qRT-PCR

Total RNA was extracted from cotton root, stem, and leaf tissues using the FastPure Universal Plant Total RNA Isolation Kit (Vazyme Biotech Co.,Ltd). cDNA synthesis was performed using the Easy-Script® All-in-One First-Strand cDNA Synthesis SuperMix for qPCR (One-Step gDNA Removal) (TransGen Biotech). Then qRT-PCR was performed on a 7500 fast real-time PCR System (Applied Biosystems, USA) with TransStart Top Green qPCR SuperMix (TransGen Biotech). AtACTIN2 (AT3G18780) was validated as an endogenous control in Arabidopsis thaliana, while GhUBQ (Gh_D11G1120) was established as a conserved housekeeping gene in cotton. Genes that exhibited significant expression under all 4 abiotic stress conditions (cold, heat, salt, and drought) were selected for further analysis under alkaline stress conditions. For the analysis of gene expression in response to light and dark stress, genes containing a higher number of light-responsive elements in their cisregulatory regions were chosen to examine the effect of light on gene expression. Relative transcript abundance was calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C t } }$ method [31], and statistical analysis was conducted using 3 biological replicates of each experiment. The gene specific primer pairs utilized in this study are provided in Supplementary Table S3. The dissociation curves for gene expression analysis involved in this study is provided in Supplementary Table S5.

# Analysis of alkaline resistance of yeast transferred to GhGLDH35A gene

# Genetic transformation in Arabidopsis

To further explore the function of GhGLDH35A, p1300 vector was used for the overexpression experiment in Arabidopsis. The CDS of GhGLDH35A was cloned into p1300 vector under the control of the CaMV35S promoter for constitutive expression, and the connected vector was transformed into Agrobacterium GV3101. Transgenesis was achieved via Agrobacterium-driven floral dip genetic modification system [33]. Screening transgenic lines via kanamycin resistance markers followed by PCR-mediated molecular characterization, and primers used for plasmid constructs are listed in Supplementary Table S3. All phenotypic and physiological assays were performed on T3 homozygous lines to ensure genetic stability. A total of 15 independent T1 transgenic lines were initially obtained via Agrobacterium-driven floral dip genetic modification system. Among them, three lines (L3, L6, L7) exhibited strong kanamycin resistance, and their segregation ratios were consistent with the expected result for a single gene locus (3:1 in the T1 generation) in T2 lines. These lines were further advanced to the T3 generation to confirm their homozygosity. qRT-PCR analysis revealed that the expression levels of the GhGLDH35A gene were highest in lines L3, L6, and L9. Three lines (L3, L6, L7) were selected for subsequent experiments.

# Results

Phylogenic network modeling and conserved linkage assessment of GLDH gene family 

We identified a total of 86 GLDH homologs in G. hirsutum, 82 in G. barbadense, and 45 in each of the diploid species G. arboreum and G. raimondii through a systematic HMMER-based screen using PF04030 and PF01565 Pfam domains (Supplementary Table S1). Comparative analysis revealed a two-fold increase in the GLDH gene number in tetraploid cottons (G. hirsutum and G. barbadense) compared to their diploid counterparts (G. arboreum and G. raimondii). In addition, Phytozome was utilized to compare homologous genes from other species using domain-specific queries (ALO and FAD_binding_4). Eventually, 28 genes were found in Arabidopsis thaliana (A. thaliana), and 51 in Theobroma cacao (T. cacao) (Supplementary Table S1). To elucidate the evolutionary relationship of GLDH genes among 4 cotton species, A. thaliana and T. cacao, a phylogenetic tree was reconstructed by using 337 protein sequences. We classified the GLDH gene family into 3 distinct clades, with subfamily c predominating through its substantial representation of 260 members $7 7 . 1 5 \%$ of total), while subfamily b had the least genes, with only 9 GLDH genes (Fig. 1A). Interestingly, phylogenetic topology demonstrated a monophyletic clustering of 4 cotton species with T. cacao, consistent with a previous study that cotton and T. cacao share a common ancestral lineage [36]. In addition, NJ tree of 4 cotton species was constructed to illustrate the relationship between diploid and allotetraploid cotton (Fig. 1B), and comparative analysis revealed a two-fold increase in the GLDH gene number in tetraploid cottons (G. hirsutum and G. barbadense) compared to their diploid counterparts (G. arboreum and G. raimondii). 

Whole genome duplication, segmental duplication, and tandem duplication were identified as principal evolutionary mechanisms governing gene family diversification [37]. In this study, comparative collinearity mapping detected 697 duplicate gene pairs, among which 175 were orthologs (122 were segmental duplicate gene pairs, accounting for $1 7 . 5 \ \%$ , 53 were tandem duplicate gene pairs, accounting for $7 . 6 \ \%$ ), and 522 were paralogous (522 whole genome duplicate gene pairs, accounting for $7 4 . 9 \ \%$ ). # GhGLDH genes respond positively to abiotic stress

To elucidate the functional significance of GhGLDH genes in cotton’s environmental resilience, we first analyzed the expression patterns of GhGLDH genes under cold, hot, salt and drought stress using SRA/GEO datasets (PRJNA490626) retrieved from NCBI website [29]. GhGLDH genes that appear in clusters tend to have the same expression pattern and were up-regulated in response to above 4 abiotic stresses, such as GhGLDH19A and GhGLDH19D, GhGLDH22A and GhGLDH29D, GhGLDH20A and GhGLDH26D, GhGLDH30A and GhGLDH34D, GhGLDH9A and GhGLDH15D, GhGLDH12A and GhGLDH13D, GhGLDH35A and GhGLDH39 (Supplementary Fig. S2). Therefore, we speculated that closely related genes tended to have similar expression levels and exhibit consistent responses to abiotic stresses. Besides, individual genes responded differently to the 4 stresses. The expression levels of GhGLDH9A, GhGLDH15D, GhGLDH36D and GhGLDH39D were higher than those of other genes under cold stress. The expression of GhGLDH11A, GhGLDH12A, GhGLDH19D and GhGLDH39D were higher under heat stress. GhGLDH11A, GhGLDH35A and GhGLDH15D were more likely to be highly expressed under salt stress, while GhGLDH35A, GhGLDH19D and GhGLDH39D were more expressed under PEG stress. In summary, the GhGLDH35A gene seems to play a crucial role in responding to these abiotic stresses. 

To gain deeper insights into the expression patterns of GhGLDH genes under alkaline stress, RNA-Seq data (GSE165472) [23] was utilized to characterize the response of GhGLDH genes to alkaline stresses (Fig. 2B). It was found that the majority GhGLDH genes exhibited a positive response to alkaline stress, such as GhGLDH11A, GhGLDH10A, GhGLDH9A, GhGLDH12A, GhGLDH20A, GhGLDH24A, GhGLDH34A, GhGLDH13D, GhGLDH15D, GhGLDH16D, 

GhGLDH17D, GhGLDH19D, GhGLDH20D, GhGLDH24D, GhGLDH26D, GhGLDH26D, GhGLDH36D, GhGLDH39D, especially the GhGLDH35A gene, which maintained transcript abundance maxima in both root and leaf. Interestingly, the expression of the GhGLDH35A gene is also significantly induced under all 4 types of abiotic stresses (Supplementary Fig. S2), suggesting that it may be a key gene in stress response. 

Considering the expression levels of above genes in abiotic stresses, we selected the GhGLDH genes with high expression levels for further analysis. Firstly, we detected the expression levels in different tissues, and the results showed that different genes had different tissue expression specificities (Fig. 2C). Compared with other genes, GhGLDH12A and GhGLDH13D have the highest expression in fibers, GhGLDH35A, GhGLDH20D and GhGLDH39D in leaves, GhGLDH9A, GhGLDH20A, GhGLDH34A and GhGLDH26D in cotyledon, GhGLDH35A, GhGLDH16D, GhGLDH20D and GhGLDH39D in roots and stems, while the transcript abundance of GhGLDH26D, GhGLDH34D, GhGLDH36D and GhGLDH35A were higher in seeds. 

# Alkaline stress induces more severe physiological dysregulation in cotton compared to neutral salinity

Although alkaline stress is a kind of salt stress, previous studies have shown that crops react differently to salt and alkaline stress [21,40]. At the same time, we observed that GLDH family genes also had differential responses to salt and alkaline stresses. To more intuitively capture the phenotypic manifestations of salt and alkaline stress, different salt and alkaline stress treatments were carried out in germination stage and three-leaf stage. Additionally, to eliminate the toxic effect of different concentration of ${ \ifmmode \mathsf { N } } { \else \mathsf { N } } { \fi { } { \mathsf { a } } } ^ { + }$ , we set the same concentration of ${ { \ N } _ { { \bf { a } } } } ^ { + }$ $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m M } ) }$ with $1 0 0 \ \mathrm { m M \ N a C l }$ 50 mM ${ \ N a } _ { 2 } S O _ { 4 }$ , 50 mM $\Nu _ { 4 } \subset \mathrm { O } _ { 3 }$ , 100 mM ${ \mathsf { N a H C O } } _ { 3 }$ during germination. # GhGLDH35A, a protein localized to mitochondria, was induced by alkaline stress

To better understand the damage caused by alkaline stress in cotton, we analyzed the phenotype of cotton seedlings under alkaline stress. The results showed that the leaves exhibited wilted, the base of the stem turned brown red and the root appeared brown yellow. These observations suggest that the cotton seedlings may experience severe oxidative stress and accumulate more ROS (Fig. 5A). Then DCFH-DA staining was carried out and found that more ROS accumulated in cotton leaves under alkaline stress (Fig. 5B). As a type of ROS, hydrogen peroxide $\left( \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } \right)$ is widely discussed to be involved in stomatal closure [43], so we wanted to explore the relationship between stomata and ROS in cotton under alkaline stress. Compared with treatment without $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ , alkaline stress was found to promote the closure of stomata, significantly reducing stomatal area and aperture. Interestingly, exogenous $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ also had a similar effect on alkaline stress, and could also significantly reduce stomatal aperture and stomatal area, with no significant difference observed when compared to alkaline stress (Fig. 5C–E). These findings suggest that alkaline stress induces the generation of ROS, and the accumulation of ROS plays a crucial role in the induction of stomatal closure. 

GhGLDH35A, exhibiting prominent alkaline-elicited transcriptional activation, was selected as a candidate gene for subsequent functional characterization, and subsequent tissue expression profiling was conducted. The highest expression was found in leaves, followed by roots, and the lowest expression was found in stems, all of which increased significantly under alkaline stress (Fig. 5F), indicating that GhGLDH35A gene exerts critical functionality in alkaline stress adaptation. After characterizing the differential expression of GhGLDH35A in roots, stems, and leaves, its expression in yeast was also investigated, and it was found that alkaline stress could inhibit the growth of yeast strains, and the inhibition effect became stronger with the increase of alkaline stress concentration. In addition, compared with the pYES2 empty vector, the expression of GhGLDH35A gene in yeast increased the resistance of yeast strains, especially under $3 0 ~ \mathrm { \ m M } ~ \mathrm { N a H C O _ { 3 } }$ stress, the pYES2: GhGLDH35A yeast strain could grow normally, while pYES2 could not (Fig. 5G). These findings further confirm that GhGLDH35A gene can enhance tolerance to alkaline stress in yeast. Considering that the last step in the synthesis of AsA by the L-galactose pathway is synthesized in mitochondria by GLDH [10,44], we hypothesized that GhGLDH35A was localized in mitochondria. To test this, we performed a subcellular localization and confirmed that GhGLDH35A was localized in mitochondria (Fig. 5H). 

# Silencing GhGLDH35A gene increased sensitivity to alkaline stress

To identify the function of GhGLDH35A gene, VIGS of GhGLDH35A gene was performed to suppress the expression of 

GhGLDH35A in cotton (Fig. 6). After infection for 2 weeks, the pYL156: PDS plants displayed the systemic photobleaching symptoms, demonstrating successful establishment of virus-mediated transcriptional silencing (Fig. 6A). qRT-PCR analysis verified that the expression of GhGLDH35A was suppressed in the leaves of the GhGLDH35A-silenced plants, accompanied by a reduction in GLDH activity (Fig. 6B and C). The ion leakage rate results indicate that silencing the GhGLDH35A gene significantly increased the ion leakage rate (Fig. 6D). Then GhGLDH35A-silenced plants and control plants were exposed to ${ \mathsf { N a H C O } } _ { 3 }$ alkaline stress, it was found that compared with the control plants, the GhGLDH35A-silenced plants were more sensitive to alkaline stress, as evidenced by wilting and yellowing of the leaves (Fig. 6A). Since alkaline stress will accumulate excessive ROS, we assessed ROS levels in the leaves of GhGLDH35A-silenced plants, and the results showed that compared with pYL156 plants, the GhGLDH35A-silenced plants accumulated more ROS (Fig. 6E), with significantly higher fluorescence intensity observed in the silenced plants under alkaline stress (Fig. 6F). Extensive biochemical studies have established that AsA is a core redox metabolite regulating the levels of ROS under abiotic stress in plants [45], and GLDH gene is involved in the synthesis of AsA. Consequently, we measured the AsA and dehydroascorbic acid (DHA) content under alkaline stress. Compared with pYL156 plants, the AsA and DHA content of the GhGLDH35A-silenced plants exhibited a notable decrease compared to the control plants (Fig. 6R–T). Moreover, GhGLDH35A-silenced plants showed reduced activities of GLDH enzyme and ROS-scavenging enzymes (POD, APX, CAT, SOD and DHAR) (Fig. 6M–Q), paralleling the decline in transcript abundance of GhAPX (Fig. 6J), GhCAT (Fig. 6K) and GhPOD (Fig. 6L). 

Stomata are the channels for the exchange of gases $\mathbf { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $0 _ { 2 }$ ) and water between plants and the environment, and it was found that they are widely involved in abiotic stress by controlling the opening and closing [46,47]. Study was found that GLDH can modulate the stomatal aperture in common wheat under drought stress [16]. GLDH control stomatal aperture to reduce water loss in Arabidopsis [48]. In this study, when subjected to alkaline stress, the stomata area and stomatal aperture of GhGLDH35A-silenced plants were exhibited pronounced augmentation compared with pYL156 plants (Fig. 6G–I), suggesting that GhGLDH35A may control stomatal movement to resist alkaline stress. In addition, the physiological parameters between the GhGLDH35A-silenced and pYL156 plants were measured. Compared to the pYL156 plants, the leaves of silenced plants showed significantly higher MDA content (Fig. 6U), and the total chlorophyII content, chlorophyII a content and chlorophyII b content were decreased by $4 2 . 4 6 \%$ , $5 4 . 4 8 \%$ and 35.85 in GhGLDH35A-silenced plants, respectively (Fig. 6V–X), which may be related to the observed the yellowing of the leaves. 

These findings demonstrated that GhGLDH35A may positively regulate tolerance to ${ \mathsf { N a H C O } } _ { 3 }$ stress. It appears to modulate the plant’s response to alkaline stress through various pathways, including the regulation of AsA content, the expression levels of ROS scavenging genes, and the enzymatic activity of ROS scavenger-related enzymes, thereby maintaining ROS homeostasis. Additionally, and GhGLDH35A may influence stomatal aperture and chlorophyII content. Future validation of its regulatory role will require complementation assays, overexpression studies, and metabolite rescue experiments.

# Discussion

With the increasing global climate change and environmental pressure, especially the expanding area of global saline-alkaline land, unraveling alkali-specific signaling cascades now stands as a pivotal undertaking in plant molecular ecology. Cotton, as a pioneering crop in saline-alkali soils, experiences saline-alkali stress throughout its growth and developmental stages. Salinealkaline conditions bifurcate into salt stress $( \mathsf { N a C l } / \mathsf { N a } _ { 2 } \mathsf { S O } _ { 4 } .$ - dominated) and alkaline stress $\mathrm { ( N a H C O _ { 3 } / N a _ { 2 } C O _ { 3 } }$ -induced). In recent years, most of the research focuses on exploring the mechanism of salt stress [49,50]. Salt-induced stress can be categorized 

into three main pathways: osmotic stress, ionic stress, and secondary stress caused by osmotic stress and ion stress [4]. In contrast, alkaline stress can also produce high pH stress in addition to the above-mentioned stress, which is more harmful to plants. This study examined the effects of different types of salt (NaCl and $\mathrm { N a } _ { 2 } S 0 _ { 4 } ,$ ) and alkaline stress $\mathbf { \Delta N a } _ { 2 } \mathbf { C O } _ { 3 }$ and ${ \mathsf { N a H C O } } _ { 3 }$ ) on cotton. Saline-alkaline stress could inhibit the growth of root length at the germination stage, but the root length of alkaline stress was significantly lower than that of salt stress, and it also had a similar effect at the three-leaf stage (Fig. 3). The results showed that the alkaline stress poses a significantly greater threat to cotton than salt stress, suggesting that more attention should be directed toward investigating the molecular mechanism of alkaline resistance in cotton. Elucidating the molecular regulatory networks governed by saline-alkali tolerance-associated loci provides critical insights for precision breeding frameworks targeting crop improvement under saline-alkali stress conditions, but only a limited number of genes have been identified that enhance alkaline stress adaptability in cotton [51–53]. 

Alkaline stress, similar to salinity, induces hyperaccumulation of ROS, disrupting redox homeostasis and biomolecular integrity, which is a hallmark of plant stress adaptation [2,6,23,54,55]. Our results support this paradigm: GhGLDH35A-silenced cotton exhibited elevated ROS levels under stress (Fig. 6E and F), whereas overexpressing Arabidopsis lines showed reduced ROS accumulation (Fig. 7B, E and I), consistent with established ROS detection methodologies [52,56,57]. Crucially, we identify GhGLDH35A as a dual-functional regulator in cotton, bridging AsA biosynthesis and mitochondrial resilience − a role previously uncharacterized in this species. 

AsA, a master antioxidant and developmental regulator [58], is synthesized via GLDH-dependent pathways conserved across plants, such as wheat [16], tobacco [17,18], lettuce [59], and strawberries [60]. In the present study, silencing of GhGLDH35A resulted in a $3 8 . 1 4 \ \%$ reduction in AsA levels (Fig. 6R–T), while its overexpression enhanced AsA production in Arabidopsis (Fig. 7O–Q), thereby firmly establishing its role in the D-mannose/L-galactose pathway. Notably, the disproportionate decline in GLDH activity compared to AsA content (Fig. 6R–T) suggests compensatory mechanisms, potentially involving L-galactose dehydrogenase [10,61,62], reminiscent of redundancy observed in other plant systems [48]. Given that the last step in AsA synthesis occurs in the mitochondria and is catalyzed by GLDH, and considering that GLDH has been reported to be indispensable for the assembly and functional maintenance of plant respiratory complex I (RCI) [63], we hypothesize that GhGLDH35A may have a dual function in the mitochondria. On one hand, it could increase tolerance to alkaline stress by synthesizing AsA, which helps to eliminate ROS in the mitochondria. Supporting this hypothesis, VIGS experiments targeting GhGLDH35A showed a significant reduction in AsA levels and a marked increase in ROS content (Fig. 6A and E), indicating that GhGLDH35A plays an essential role in maintaining AsA homeostasis and mitigating oxidative stress in the mitochondria. On the other hand, GhGLDH35A may act as a molecular chaperone critical for the structural integrity and stoichiometric assembly of complex I, as previous studies have demonstrated that GLDH is necessary for the accumulation of complex I, with the absence of complex I in the gldh mutant serving as compelling evidence [64]. This dual functionality aligns with the roles of wheat TaGLDH and Arabidopsis GLDH, which coordinate stomatal regulation and drought tolerance through AsA-mediated ROS control [16,48], but expands the paradigm to mitochondrial adaptation under alkaline stress. Although our study highlights GhGLDH35A’s central role in cotton stress resilience, several important questions remain. Does GhGLDH35A physically interact with RCI subunits or modulate their transcription? Is its chaperone-like activity mechanistically distinct from 

its enzymatic function? Addressing these questions could lead to novel strategies for engineering crops with enhanced organellar stress tolerance, harnessing the dual roles of GLDH as both an antioxidant catalyst and a structural regulator. 

The study of gene families can reveal the diversity of gene functions, evolutionary processes and the mechanism of environmental adaptation. In our study, it was found that GLDH genes share 2 domains, FAD-binding 4 and ALO. Various enzymes use FAD as a co-factor, with the majority harboring a conserved FAD-binding motif characteristic of the vanillyl-alcohol oxidase (VAO) flavoprotein superfamily [65]. According to these domains, we identified GLDH genes in 4 cotton species and constructed an evolutionary tree. Evolutionary partitioning analysis delineated 3 clades within the GLDH gene family (Fig. 1). We also observed that monocot species formed distinct clusters, separate from dicot plants, throughout the phylogenetic tree, indicating conserved and divergent evolutionary patterns between monocots and dicots. Gene duplication drives functional diversification, serving as a pivotal evolutionary engine for environmental adaptation and speciation processes [66]. Orthologous/paralogous and gene duplication analysis revealed that the GLDH genes of the A-genome and GhAt subgenome share a common ancestor, while the GLDH genes of the Dgenome and GhDt sub-genome are derived from a distinct common ancestor. we also found that GhGLDHs, along with other cotton GLDHs, underwent strong purifying selection. Based on our comprehensive analysis, we conclude that the GhGLDH gene family in upland cotton has undergone tandem, segmental, and whole genome duplications throughout its evolution, ultimately driving the expansion of the GhGLDH gene family (Fig. 2B, Supplementary Fig. S1, S2), which was converges with the predominant evolutionary trajectories documented in plant multigene families [28,67,68]. In our analysis, $9 8 . 2 8 \ \%$ of the total duplicated genes across the 4 Gossypium species exhibit a $K a / K s$ ratio of less than 1.0, suggesting that the cotton GLDH gene family has undergone strong purifying selection, with only limited functional divergence. The divergence time was significantly different among the diploid species (Supplementary Table S4). The amplification of the tetraploid may have resulted from species-specific expansion, combined with gene duplication events and Ks dating analysis. Overall, the organization of putative conserved motifs aligned with the clustering of GLDHs into various classes, suggesting that these motifs may be responsible for the functional specificity observed among GLDH proteins across different classes. The unique motifs present in different subfamilies likely reflect both the conservation and the specific functions of the GhGLDH gene family. Moreover, certain motifs and their arrangements were similar among the various GhGLDH members within the same class, highlighting the high conservation of protein structures within specific subfamilies, aligning with studies that GLDH is deeply conserved in other plant species [69]. Compared to other plants, the limited variation in the exon/intron structure of GhGLDHs observed in upland cotton suggests partial structural divergence within the GhGLDH gene family. However, the consistent exon–intron organization observed across different subfamilies indicates that these genes have been highly conserved throughout evolution. 

Cis-acting elements regulate promoter-level adaptation mechanisms during plant adaptation to environmental stressors [39]. In this study, cis-acting elements of GhGLDH genes were implicated in plant hormones and stress-related elements (Fig. 1H). Notably, almost all GhGLDH genes contain light-responsive elements, raising the question of whether the expression levels of GLDH genes were mediated by light. In rice, AsA synthesis is regulated by light, and intense light can induce the expression of GLDH gene, but the expression is reduced in darkness [70]. GLDH gene in tobacco leaves was regulated by light, and its expression level was consistent with that of AsA content [14]. Interestingly, a study showed 

that bagging could affect the accumulation of chlorophy II and AsA, and reduce the expression of the GLDH and DHAR genes in Actinidia eriantha [42]. A study showed that CmGalLDH gene expression decreased when melon seedlings were moved to darkness [13]. The majority of GhGLDH genes exhibit higher expression levels in light compared to dark conditions. However, some genes, such as GhGLDH15D, GhGLDH39D, GhGLDH20A, and GhGLDH34A, did not exhibit significant changes. We hypothesize that this may be due to sequence disruption mediated by transposons, binding of transcriptional repressors, or epigenetic silencing through H3K27me3 marks, which will require further investigation to confirm. qRT-PCR results showed that light can induce the expression of GhGLDH35A, and the expression of GhGLDH35A gene decreases significantly under dark conditions. Compared to the pYL156 plants, the silenced plants showed a significant decrease in net photosynthetic rate under light treatment (Supplementary Fig. S3). We hypothesize that light signaling may regulate GhGLDH35A expression through photoreceptors, such as phytochromes or cryptochromes, which play key roles in light perception [71]. These photoreceptors could influence the expression of GhGLDH35A in a light-dependent manner, potentially through the modulation of circadian factors or downstream signaling pathways that involve the regulation of ROS production and AsA biosynthesis. The increased levels of AsA could, in turn, provide photoprotection by scavenging ROS generated under stress conditions, thereby supporting photosynthesis and maintaining cellular integrity. 

The functional significance of GhGLDH genes in the adaptation to abiotic stress (including salt, drought, cold, hot and alkaline stress) is highlighted by their marked induction under a range of stress conditions, including alkaline stress (Fig. 2, Fig. 4A). Among these, GhGLDH35A exhibited the most pronounced transcriptional activation, warranting further investigation of its role. Silencing of GhGLDH35A in cotton resulted in a $5 3 \%$ reduction in GLDH activity, alongside a $3 8 . 1 4 \%$ decrease in AsA levels and a $3 5 . 5 1 \ \%$ reduction in DHA levels, establishing this isoform’s involvement in the D-mannose/L-galactose pathway for AsA biosynthesis (Fig. 6). The disproportionate decline in GLDH activity relative to AsA content is consistent with previous observations of functional redundancy among enzymes involved in AsA synthesis [48], including the cooperative activities of GLDH and L-galactose dehydrogenase [10,61,62]. In addition to its role in AsA biosynthesis, GhGLDH35A plays a critical role in modulating ROS homeostasis by regulating antioxidant enzyme systems. Silencing GhGLDH35A resulted in disrupted activities of key antioxidant enzymes, including CAT, POD, SOD, APX, and DHAR (Fig. 6L–S), as well as downregulation of their corresponding gene expression (Fig. 6I–K), underscoring its broad regulatory role in oxidative stress defense. Notably, APX, a central $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ scavenger linking AsA metabolism to redox balance [72], and DHAR, which is essential for AsA recycling [73], were significantly impaired (Fig. 6J, M and R). These phenotypes were similar to those observed in CrDHAR1-deficient plants under oxidative stress [74] and were rescued in GhGLDH35A-overexpressing Arabidopsis under alkaline stress (Fig. 7S). These results parallel the salt tolerance conferred by OsDHAR1 in rice [75], highlighting conserved roles of AsA regeneration pathways in stress adaptation. Taken together, our data position GhGLDH35A as a central hub for alkaline stress resilience, orchestrating both AsA biosynthesis and antioxidant enzyme activation. While the precise mechanism by which GLDH regulates antioxidant gene expression remains unclear, potential crosstalk with ROS signaling or transcriptional networks warrants further investigation. This dual function—sustaining AsA pools and enhancing ROS scavenging—makes GhGLDH35A a promising target for the development of stress-tolerant crops. 

Alkaline stress impairs photosynthetic efficiency, in part through the degradation of chlorophyII, a hallmark of disrupted light harvesting and photochemical reactions [76]. Our results sup-

port this concept, demonstrating that alkaline stress significantly reduces chlorophyII content compared to salinity (Fig. 3E–G), which aligns with oat studies suggesting that chlorophyII retention can serve as a alkali tolerance biomarker [77]. Notably, silencing of GhGLDH35A exacerbates chlorophyII loss in cotton (Fig. 6V–X), while its overexpression helps preserve chlorophyII pools in Arabidopsis under stress (Fig. 7T–V), indicating that GhGLDH35A plays a critical role in modulating photosynthetic stability under alkaline conditions. It is reported that AsA acts as a key antioxidant molecule for sustained photosynthesis [78]. As our data show, GhGLDH35A regulates chlorophyII content and AsA/DHA synthesis in both cotton and Arabidopsis (Fig. 6R–T, Fig. 7O–Q), although the direct causal relationship between AsA and chlorophyII maintenance remains unclear. Importantly, DHAR − an AsA-recycling enzyme − has been shown to support chlorophyII retention by stabilizing light-harvesting complexes and Rubisco (RbcL), resulting in delayed leaf aging [79]. Consistent with this, we found that suppressed DHAR activity led to reduced chlorophyII content (Fig. 6Q and V–X), while enhanced DHAR activity restored chlorophyII levels (Fig. 7S–V). These findings suggest that GhGLDH35A may preserve chlorophyII indirectly by maintaining AsA-DHAR redox cycles, thus mitigating oxidative damage to the photosynthetic machinery. It remains to be determined whether AsA directly stabilizes chlorophyII biosynthesis enzymes or regulates chloroplast retrograde signaling. Future studies involving exogenous AsA application or targeting chlorophyII-specific pathways, such as tetrapyrrole synthesis, could provide further insights into its role. 

Stomatal regulation is a critical mechanism employed by plants to balance photosynthetic efficiency with water conservation under abiotic stresses [80]. In this study, alkaline stress induced stomatal closure in both cotton and Arabidopsis, resulting in a marked reduction in stomatal aperture and area (Fig. 6H and I, Fig. 7M and N), similar to responses observed under salt and drought stress [81]. While the role of ROS, particularly $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ , in mediating stomatal closure has been well-documented in species like maize (via ZmRBOHC-dependent ROS biosynthesis [82] and Arabidopsis (through BAK1-AHA2 signaling) [83], the involvement of ROS in cotton stomatal regulation had not been characterized. 

Our results demonstrate that exogenous $\mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ application directly triggers stomatal closure in cotton (Fig. 5C), aligning with conserved ROS signaling mechanisms observed in Arabidopsis [84]. Importantly, we identified GhGLDH35A as a novel regulator of stomatal behavior under alkaline stress. Silencing GhGLDH35A in cotton resulted in larger stomatal apertures and areas under stress (Fig. 6H and I), while its overexpression in Arabidopsis enhanced stomatal closure (Fig. 7M and N). This functional conservation mirrors the roles of GLDH homologs in modulating stomatal traits to enhance drought tolerance in wheat [16] and Arabidopsis [48], while extending their function to alkaline stress adaptation in cotton. The influence of GhGLDH35A on stomatal size highlights its potential role in regulating gas exchange during stress, a critical factor for improving crop resilience [85–87]. Although our study establishes GhGLDH35A’s involvement in stomatal regulation, the underlying molecular mechanisms remain to be fully elucidated. For instance, GLDH homologs in other species interact with NADPH oxidases (e.g., RBOHs) [82] or antioxidant systems [48], raising the possibility that GhGLDH35A might similarly influence ROS homeostasis in guard cells. Future investigations should explore whether GhGLDH35A directly modulates ROS production or scavenging, or interacts with ABA signaling components, as seen in pepper (CaDHN3) [87] and cotton (GhTULP30) [88]. By linking stomatal behavior to alkaline stress tolerance, our findings extend the functional repertoire of GLDH genes and highlight their potential as targets for the development of stress-resilient crops. 

In this study, we identified GhGLDH35A, a mitochondrially localized gene that is regulated by alkaline stress, and demonstrated its role in enhancing tolerance to alkaline stress in cotton, Arabidopsis, and yeast. Based on these findings, we propose a model in which GhGLDH35A stabilizes ROS homeostasis and photosynthesis by modulating the content of AsA, thereby regulating stomatal movement under alkaline stress (Fig. 8). Under normal conditions, both cotton and Arabidopsis maintain a balance between AsA and ROS levels, supporting a moderate degree of stomatal opening. Mild alkaline stress promotes AsA synthesis, which scavenges excess ROS, leading to a reduction in stomatal conductance and aperture.
