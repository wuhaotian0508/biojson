# P L A N T S C I E N C E S

# Induction of collective behavior by β-1,3-glucans in microalgae

Lou Lambert1,2 , Richard G. Dorrell1,2 , Antoine Danon1,2 * 

$\mathbf { \beta }$ -1,3-glucans are multifaceted molecules, playing a key role in aquatic ecosystems where they store atmospheric $\mathsf { C O } _ { 2 } ,$ as well as in human health, where they help fight pathogens and have anticancer properties. Until now, no role other than sugar storage had been attributed to $\beta$ -1,3-glucans in algae. We show here that in Chlamydomonas reinhardtii, β-1,3-glucans control aggregation, a collective behavior in response to stress. We have identified the enzymes specifically involved in β-1,3-glucan synthesis and degradation during aggregation. The transcriptome of $\mathbf { \beta }$ -1,3-glucan–induced aggregation revealed an early effect of this elicitor. A comparative genomic analysis allowed us to envisage a potential role for $\mathbf { \beta }$ -1,3-glucans in the transition to multicellularity in Volvocales. The finding of the function of $\mathbf { \beta }$ -1,3-glucans in the stress response of the unicellular model organism Chlamydomonas sheds light into their mode of action. This is particularly relevant given their potential roles combatting pathogenic fungi in plants and animals.

# INTRODUCTION

$\beta$ -1,3-glucans are polysaccharides composed of 20 to $3 0 \beta$ -d-glucose residues that are necessary for the functioning of organisms such as bacteria, fungi, or plants (1). Principally, $\beta$ -1,3-glucans are known to function in carbon storage in eukaryotic algae, either as laminarin (in brown algae) or chrysolaminarin (in diatoms), analogous to the role of starch (α-1,4- and $\alpha$ -1,6-glucan) in plants. They can form a substantial proportion of algal biomass, with estimates of laminarins forming $3 5 \%$ of the dry weight in the brown algae Laminaria digitata (2), and chrysolaminarins forming up to $5 5 \%$ of that of diatoms (3). At a more global level, $\beta$ -1,3-glucans have been estimated to account for more than a quarter of all organic carbon, with an estimated annual production of 12 Gt (4). Given that diatom algae alone account for up to $2 0 \%$ of the planet’s total annual carbon fixation (5), $\beta$ -1,3-glucans are likely to have fundamental role in the biological carbon pumps (6). 

Alongside their roles in storage, $\beta$ -1,3-glucans are also elicitors, allowing dialogs to be established between fungi, plants, and animals. They are essential components of the cell wall of some pathogenic fungi and are important for immune responses. $\beta$ -1,3-glucans within fungal cell walls can be perceived as a pathogen-associated molecular pattern (PAMP) by animal or plant cells (7). In plants, it has been shown on numerous occasions that $\beta$ -1,3-glucans–induced immune responses enable greater resistance to pathogenic attack (8, 9) and may even have biostimulating functions for their growth (9–12). In the medical sector, there is increasing use in the development of $\beta$ -1,3-glucans for their ability to trigger immune responses against pathogenic fungi (13), which can be the cause of up 2.5 million annual deaths worldwide (14). The therapeutic potential of $\beta$ -1,3-glucans extend to antioxidant and anti-infectious properties and even anticancer activity (15). 

Chlamydomonas reinhardtii (hereafter referred to as Chlamydomonas) is a unicellular green algae, which is a model for study in these fundamental areas as photosynthesis, flagellum function, and the cell cycle (16). Chlamydomonas is a terrestrial single-celled species, although it sits in a family (the Chlamydomonadales) that includes marine, aggregating, and colonial members (in the subfamily Volvocales). Chlamydomonas is an 

excellent chassis for the production of molecules of interest such as biofuels (17). Similar to plants, and unlike brown algae or diatoms, Chlamydomonas stores sugars produced during photosynthesis as starch, and to our knowledge, the function that $\beta$ -1,3-glucans might play in this alga remains unknown. In a previous study, we demonstrated that cell aggregation may enable Chlamydomonas to adapt to hostile environmental conditions (18). Quantitative proteomics studies of proteins secreted by Chlamydomonas coupled with a reverse genetic strategy have enabled us to identify the first regulators of aggregation. Some of these regulators are pherophorins, hydroxyproline-rich glycoproteins that mainly constitute the cell wall of Chlamydomonas. The analysis of the aggregation secretome has also allowed us to envisage that $\beta$ -1,3- glucans may be involved in this process. $\beta$ -1,3-glucans are synthesized by $\beta$ -1,3-glucan synthase (BGS) and can be degraded by $\beta$ -1,3-glucanase (BGL). In the aggregation secretome, two BGL-like proteins were found to be strongly down-regulated (18). 

In this study, we demonstrate that $\beta$ -1,3-glucans can play a role as elicitors of cell aggregation in Chlamydomonas. We show that the mutation of the two BGLs mentioned above leads to the accumulation of $\beta$ -1,3-glucans and cell aggregation and that this can also be specifically induced by treatment with $\beta$ -1,3-glucans. We further show that $\beta$ -1,3- glucans are produced in early stage during stress-induced aggregation, predominantly by one of the BGS-like proteins in Chlamydomonas. Last, using comparative genomics approaches, we infer a probable association between the presence of these protein families and the emergence of colonial life strategies within the Volvocales (19). Our study reveals role that $\beta$ -1,3-glucans can play in algae beyond storing carbon and opens up previously unexplored avenues for research on the role this molecule plays in plants, animals, and the planet’s ecosystem.

In the list of secreted proteins, we have identified two representatives of a particular protein class, BGL that we called BGL1 (Cre16.g693601) and BGL2 (Cre16.g693600) (18). This spontaneous aggregation phenotype in bgl1 and bgl2 mutants is consistent with the fact that the corresponding proteins are down-regulated in aggregating saz mutants (18). 

# $\beta$ -1,3-glucans are specific elicitors of aggregation

The results obtained with bgl1 and bgl2 mutants led us to hypothesize that Chlamydomonas cell aggregation was dependent on the accumulation of $\beta$ -1,3-glucans (which would otherwise be degraded by BGLs) from the culture medium. To test this, we assessed the effect of a commercially available $\beta$ -1,3-glucan (laminarin) on Chlamydomonas aggregation. The laminarin concentrations chosen for this experiment were determined on the basis of experiments conducted on plants (12, 21, 22). The exposure of wild-type strains to $\beta$ -1,3-glucans resulted in the formation of aggregates visible to the naked eye, proportional to concentration (Fig. 1E). Aggregate surface quantification confirmed that $\beta$ -1,3-glucans induced aggregation in a dose-dependent manner (Fig. 1E). Previously, we have shown that Chlamydomonas exposed to rose bengal or to heat stress undergo programmed cell death (PCD) and aggregate (23, 24). In contrast to these stressors, $\beta$ -1,3-glucans have no lethal effect on Chlamydomonas cells, implying that they specifically induce aggregation without cell death (Fig. 1F). Last, we tested the effect of $\beta$ -1,3-glucans from bacteria (curdlans) or from yeast (zymosans), which differ from laminarins in terms of structure, size, and branching (25, 26). These $\beta$ -1,3-glucans were unable to efficiently induce aggregation in Chlamydomonas, suggesting that aggregation is specific to laminarins or molecules with a similar structure (fig. S1). 

# 1,3- $\mathbf { \sigma } \cdot \mathbf { \{ \beta \phi } $ -glucan synthases are necessary for aggregation to occur

Having established that $\beta$ -1,3-glucans are capable of inducing aggregation, probably in conjunction with the BGLs responsible for their degradation, we next investigated which enzymes might be responsible for their synthesis. As no 1,3- $\beta$ -glucan synthase (BGS) gene has been described in Chlamydomonas, we used that of another model unicellular alga, the diatom (Phaeodactylum tricornutum), PtBGS, to identify putative homologs. Three genes with the highest degree of homology with PtBGS—here termed BGS1 (Cre13.g574900), BGS2 (Cre03.g198200), and BGS3 (Cre03.g183700)—were selected, and the corresponding mutants from the CLiP library were analyzed. All three mutants (bgs1, LMJ.RY0402.059883; bgs2, LMJ.RY0402.252840; and bgs3, LMJ.RY0402.053530 mutants) were verified to contain insertions at the predicted loci (Fig. 2, A and B) and to no longer express the corresponding genes (Fig. 2C). 

Next, we treated the bgs mutants and the wild-type strain with aggregation-inducing treatments (rose bengal and heat shock). All three mutant lines were observed to have aggregation phenotypes, albeit 

with different intensities. The bgs1 mutant showed absolutely no aggregation under all conditions tested (Fig. 2D). By quantifying the surface area of the aggregates generated in the different strains, the bgs2 and bgs3 mutants were in contrast found still able to aggregate partially (Fig. 2D). This indicates that BGS1 is an essential component of aggregate formation, potentially via $\beta$ -1,3-glucan synthesis. To check whether the inability of the bgs1 mutant to aggregate in response to stress was indeed linked to $\beta$ -1,3-glucans, we treated bgs1 with laminarin and found that the mutant had indeed acquired the ability to aggregate partially (Fig. 2E). This inability of the bgs1 mutant to form aggregates at similar level to that of the wild type may indicate that the nature of the $\beta$ -1,3-glucans used (from L. digitata) or the quantity entering the cell is not optimal for Chlamydomonas. 

# Detection and quantification of $\beta$ -1,3-glucan in the context of aggregation

Since our results point to a role for $\beta$ -1,3-glucans in aggregate formation, we investigated the possibility that they are produced in Chlamydomonas during this process. To achieve this, we used a $\beta$ -1,3-glucan–specific marker, aniline blue (27). In addition to aniline blue, we used another marker, concanavalin–fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC), which binds to the common sugars d-mannose and d-glucose, emitting green fluorescence in the cell wall of Chlamydomonas. We initially found that in the wild-type strain, under standard culture conditions, cell wall sugars are indeed labeled by concanavalin-FITC, but no $\beta$ -1,3-glucan was detected in isolated cells (Fig. 3A). We then looked for the presence of $\beta$ -1,3-glucan in the aggregates formed in bgl1 and bgl2 mutants and found that they could be detected, probably at the level of the cell wall, and often at the intersection between different cells (Fig. 3A). Fluorescence levels were quantified in cell extracts from each line, confirming the accumulation of $\beta$ -1,3-glucan in bgl mutants and not in bgs mutants compared with the wild-type strain (fig. S2). To determine whether $\beta$ -1,3-glucans could also be produced under conditions of abiotic stress inducing aggregation, we treated the wild-type strain with heat shock (18). This analysis enabled us to show that very soon after heat shock (4 hours), when the aggregates had not yet formed, $\beta$ -1,3-glucans were detected almost always at the base of the flagella (Fig. 3B). By the time the aggregates are formed (48 hours), which corresponds roughly to the stage of bgl mutants, $\beta$ -1,3-glucans were also present within the aggregates, in the cell wall, and often at the intersection between several cells (Fig. 3B). 

# Transcriptomics identify probable cellular consequences of β-1,3-glucan–induced aggregation

To gain a better understanding of the mechanisms involved in the induction of aggregation by $\beta$ -1,3-glucans, we implemented a global analysis of gene expression by RNA sequencing. Reflecting that the production of $\beta$ -1,3-glucans is an early event in the course of aggregation (Fig. 3B), our analysis was carried out before (2 hours) and after (8 hours), the formation of the first aggregates, which are detectable after four hours of treatment. Following 2 hours of treatment with $\beta$ -1,3-glucans (laminarin), we found 54 genes overexpressed and 75 genes underexpressed at least 2.5-fold compared with the untreated sample. # Involvement of light and singlet oxygen signaling in aggregation responses

As the transcriptome of $\beta$ -1,3-glucan–induced aggregation revealed that CCM was precociously altered during this process, we sought to 

understand what consequences this might have. The CCM enables the Calvin-Benson-Bassham cycle (CBBC) to function optimally by ensuring that the ribulose bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase (RUBISCO) fixes $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ as efficiently as possible. A reduction in CCM should therefore reduce the efficiency of CBBC, which supplies nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate, the final acceptor in the thylakoid electron transfer chain. With the electron transfer rate unaltered, the components of the transfer chain will tend to be hyperreduced, which should result in a redirection of electron flow toward $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 } \mathrm { { : } }$ , to generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) (28). To test this hypothesis, we measured the proportion of cells generating singlet oxygen $( ^ { 1 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } )$ at different times after treatment with $\beta$ -1,3-glucans. We identified a discontinuous distribution of homologs of Chlamydomonas $\beta$ -1,3-glucan metabolism proteins across the tree of life (Fig. 6). All three BGL subgroups were detected across the Volvocales, and more infrequently in other green algae and (in the case of BGL1 and BGL2 and BGL3 to BGL5) marine algal and protist groups such as Ochrophyta, Haptista, and Excavata. Many of the BGLs were further inferred to harbor N-terminal signal peptides, suggesting secretory localizations and reflecting the detection of BGL1 and BGL2 in the aggregation secretome (18). The BGS proteins showed more widespread distributions across the tree of life. Of the seven Chlamydomonas BGSs, four (BGS2, BGS5, BGS3, BGS1, and considering reciprocal BLAST best hits) found widespread homologs in other algal and plant groups, while three (BGS6, BGS4, and BGS7) rarely retrieved BLAST best hit homologs. In contrast to the BGLs, the BGS homologs rarely encoded N-terminal signal peptides. 

A more detailed view of the taxonomic distribution of species having BGL and BGS proteins indicated a stepwise origin of these proteins in Chlamydomonas (fig. S4). The BGS proteins likely originated in the common ancestor of the green lineage, including plants, nonaggregating members of the Chlorophyta (e.g., Prasinophyceae) basally divergent groups (Prasinodermophyceae) and the sister lineage (Glaucophyta) (fig. S4A). BGLs were inferred to have been acquired later, with only four Volvocalean s p e c i e s (C. reinhardtii, Gonium pectorale, Tetrabaena socialis, and Volvox carteri), inferred to have all three BGL classes (fig. S4B). 

As these four species all have either aggregative responses or colonial lifestyles, these enzymes may be associated to the evolution of cell aggregation in the Volvocales. 

Last, we chose to assess the probable evolutionary origins of BGL and BGS proteins, for which sufficient homologs were identified to construct phylogenetically meaningful trees. Trees were not attempted for BGL1 and BGL2 and BGL6 and BGL7 due to the limited number of identifiable homologs and low reciprocal conservation/highly disordered structure observed between species. The Volocalean BGL3 to BGL5 proteins formed a monophyletic and robustly supported $( > 8 5 \%$ RAxML bootstrap support) clade with Xanthophyte algae and Agrobacterium sequences (fig. S5A, clade shaded in yellow), suggesting a probable horizontal transfer of this gene family from either brackish-water algae or bacteria recently in their recent evolutionary history. The Volvocalean BGS sequences formed a monophyletic clade with homologs from other Chlorophyta and Glaucophyta (fig. S5B, green branches). Nonetheless, by considering both tree topology and reciprocal BLAST best hits, we could identify four distinct subfamilies of BGS (fig. S5B, clades shaded in blue, green, red, and violet) that each contained Chlamydomonas sequences. We therefore infer probable duplications or diversifications of the BGS family during recent Chlamydomonadalean evolution (fig. S5). 

# DISCUSSION

$\beta$ -1,3-glucans are fascinating molecules with crucial roles in the ecological equilibrium of the planet and in the molecular dialog between pathogens and plant or animal cells (8, 31). Until now, their only known role in macro- and microalgae was to store $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ fixed during photosynthesis (4). However, for all the algae that store $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ in the form of starch, the role of $\beta$ -1,3-glucans remained completely unknown. In Chlamydomonas, only one article mentioned $\beta$ -1,3-glucans nearly 40 years ago, referring to their presence in the cell wall during sexual reproduction (32). The characterization of aggregation as a collective stress-response behavior in Chlamydomonas, revealed the possible role of $\beta$ -1,3-glucans in this process (18), explored further in this study. Direct treatment with $\beta .$ - 1,3-glucans induces dose-dependent aggregation, without any toxicity that could lead to cell death (Fig. 1, E and F). We also show that this activity is dependent on the presence of endogenous βGSs, particularly BGS1 (Fig. 2). 

In bgl1 and bgl2 aggregates, $\beta$ -1,3-glucans are localized in the cell wall, as previously described (32), since they colocalize with concanavalin-A FITC, which marks cell wall sugars (Fig. 3A). We also 

frequently found in bgl mutants $\beta$ -1,3-glucans at the junction between two or more cells, suggesting that this polysaccharide may be located at the anchor point between these cells. This leads us to believe that one of the mechanisms leading to the aggregation of cells between themselves could stem from the attachment effect of $\beta$ -1,3-glucans, perhaps by means of specific receptors such as those described in animals (33). We investigated the early mechanisms of aggregation using heat shock and found that 4 hours after treatment, when aggregates had not yet formed, $\beta$ -1,3-glucans were visible, almost always at the same cellular location, at the base of the 

flagella (Fig. 3B). Forty-eight hours after heat shock, when the aggregates have formed, the $\beta$ -1,3-glucan signal is found in the cell wall, as in the bgl mutants (Fig. 3B). This suggests an early role for $\beta$ -1,3-glucans before aggregate formation, which would be consistent with the adhesion mechanisms discussed above. 

The analysis of the transcriptomic data confirmed early signaling by $\beta$ -1,3-glucans, which is detectable after 2 hours of treatment and which fades after 8 hours. Notably, genes linked to the Chlamydomonas CCM are very strongly down-regulated after 2 hours (Fig. 4), but this is no longer detectable after 8 hours of treatment (fig. S1). The CCM enables the CBBC of photosynthesis to function optimally. Typically, genes involved in CCM are overexpressed at low $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ concentrations or underexpressed in the absence of light and therefore of photosynthesis (34, 35). However, if no other environmental conditions change, apart from treatment of the cells with $\beta$ -1,3-glucans, the underexpression of the CCM genes should result in diminished CBBC and photosynthetic efficiency. We note an early production of ROS $( ^ { 1 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } )$ , with a peak between 3 and 4 hours after treatment with $\beta$ -1,3-glucans and which persisted to 6 hours posttreatment (Fig. 5A). This may relate to changes in photosynthetic efficiency, e.g., hyperreduction of the thylakoid electron transfer chain and the formation of ROS, including $^ \mathrm { i } _ { \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } }$ in photosystem II (28). The signaling specifically induced by $^ \mathrm { I } _ { \mathrm { O } _ { 2 } }$ and the genes induced by this form of ROS are becoming better known in plants (29). Our hypothesis is that the small proportion of cells affected by the production of ${ } ^ { 1 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ would not be sufficient to induce death, which is not actually detected, but could be sufficient to induce early signaling allowing the later formation of aggregates. The signaling specifically induced by ${ } ^ { 1 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ may be linked to the fact that we have previously shown that communication between cells via the culture medium was crucial to induce aggregation (18). Given that ${ } ^ { 1 } \mathrm { O } _ { 2 }$ production is light-dependent, the fact that we have shown that the formation of aggregates by $\beta$ -1,3- glucans is attenuated in the dark reinforces our hypothesis (Fig. 5B). However, we cannot rule out the possibility that CCM-related genes are down-regulated following the addition of $\beta$ -1,3-glucans due to a more general modification of cellular metabolism (36), which might include a general reallocation of cell resources away from the CBBC toward the aggregation response. 

Our transcriptomic analysis also revealed that many genes involved in intracellular iron transport were overexpressed after treatment with $\beta$ -1,3-glucans. While there is no obvious link between iron metabolism and the formation of aggregates, the knowledge we have gained about the interaction between animal cells and pathogenic fungi may give us something to investigate. $\beta$ -1,3-glucans of the cell wall have a fundamental function as PAMPs in the recognition and destruction of pathogenic fungi by animal cells. During this interaction, iron plays an important role, since one of the responses of the host cell, having recognized the pathogen, is to establish a nutritional immunity by depriving the fungus of iron, an element essential to its functioning and virulence (37). One of the fungal adaptations to counter this iron starvation strategy was to shave detectable $\beta$ -1,3-glucans from the cell wall by activating specific BGLs (38). Similar BGL activities have also been demonstrated during interactions between fungi and plants (39), which are themselves capable of secreting BGL to attack the pathogen’s cell wall (Fig. 7) (40). We speculate that the overexpression of iron uptake genes by Chlamydomonas that allow iron to be pumped from the culture medium in response to $\beta$ -1,3-glucans could therefore also correspond to a nutritional immunity strategy aimed at combating pathogens. The role of $\beta$ -1,3-glucans in inducing aggregation in 

response to abiotic stress could therefore be an adaptation of a pathogen resistance strategy to a broader resistance to environmental stresses. Another possibility concerning the increase in iron uptake by cells could be linked to ROS production (29), which we have shown occurs early after treatment with $\beta$ -1,3-glucans and could be linked to aggregate formation (Fig. 5). 

Regarding the link between aggregation and interorganism relationships, it has been shown, for example, that the formation of multicellular structures prevents Chlamydomonas from being absorbed by predators (41). Relationships between different species in an aquatic environment may involve sending out molecule signals, this was recently illustrated during the interaction between choanoflagellates and algae (42). The finding of the role played by $\beta$ -1,3-glucans in aggregation in Chlamydomonas opens perspectives on a larger scale. It is indeed possible to envisage that, in the context of competition for resources between different species of algae, the local release of $\beta$ -1,3-glucans could induce aggregation of a competing algae such as Chlamydomonas, causing it to sink to depths. The BGLs secreted by Chlamydomonas could therefore play a defensive role, eliminating the allelophatic $\beta$ -1,3-glucans and preventing themselves from sinking. The use of $\beta$ -1,3-glucans would not only eliminate a competitor by sinking, but at the same time, it would also cause it to enter the biological carbon pump more quickly. These phenomena of amplification of the biological carbon pump have already been described during the secretion of fucan by diatoms during blooming (43). 

A role of $\beta$ -1,3-glucans in the control of collective behavior in a single-cell organism, therefore, reveals several important aspects. First of all, cell aggregation is described as an intermediate stage that can lead to the transition to multicellularity over the course of evolution, and $\beta$ -1,3-glucans may have played a role in this process (Fig. 7). This is particularly true of the Volvocales, which include aggregating, gregarious, and colonial members, for which our comparative genomic analysis shows a recent and specific evolution of BGLs. While algal $\beta$ -1,3-glucans were previously thought to only serve as $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ storage molecules, this aggregation role may have broader functional relevance to the global oceans, where they are produced on a very large scale. In addition, the specific aggregation regulator role that we found for $\beta$ - 1,3-glucans could open doors for the use of Chlamydomonas as a chassis to produce molecules of interest, e.g., biofuels. One of the current limits to the use of single-cell organisms is the inherent cost of harvesting these small cells. Aggregating cells would solve this problem, allowing harvesting by simple filtration or even sedimentation. Until now, techniques for inducing aggregation have always shown a minimal degree of toxicity for cells, and here we show that $\beta$ -1,3-glucans could perfectly fulfil this role. Last, reflecting that it is a unicellular model organism, dissecting the cell biology mechanisms induced in Chlamydomonas by $\beta$ -1,3-glucans may have repercussions for our understanding of its biological roles in plants and animals.

# Quantification of cell death and aggregation

All aggregation and stress treatment were performed in 24-well plates (Caplugs Evergreen) in a culture volume of 1 ml at a cellular concentration of $4 \times \overline { { 1 0 } } ^ { 6 }$ to $\mathrm { 8 \times 1 0 ^ { 6 } \ c e l l s / m l , }$ , measured by cell count. Rose bengal (330000, Sigma-Aldrich) and laminarin (L9634, Sigma-Aldrich) were used at concentrations of $4 \mu \mathrm { M }$ and $4 \mathrm { m g / m l }$ , respectively. Curdlan (16129760, Invivogen) and Zymosan A (Z4250, Sigma-Aldrich) where used at $\mathrm { 1 ~ m g / m l }$ . Heat shock was performed with 1-ml culture in a $1 . 5 { \mathrm { - m l } }$ eppendorf tube at $5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 min using a thermomixer (ThermoMixer F1.5, Eppendorf), with shaking at $7 5 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ . Cell death quantification was performed using Evans Blue according to previously published method (45). Aggregation was quantified in a 24-well plate 3 days after treatment, and the image of the plate was obtained using a scanner (Epson Perfection V850 pro) at a resolution of 800 pp. Each of the 24 images wells was individually cut using Fiji/ImageJ (v.1.54.f) to create 24 unique files. Aggregate 

surface area in each file was measured using the Fiji macro (github. com/fdecarpentier/ParticleWell). All aggregates detected in a file were plotted using GraphPad Prism 8.0.1. 

# Genotyping mutant strains

Mutants from the CLiP library were genotyped by polymerase chain reaction (PCR) as described in (20). Briefly, DNA from each mutant and the wild-type strain was purified using the protocol described in (46). For each mutant and the wild-type strain, a PCR was performed using primers located on either side of the insertion site. PCR was performed using Quick-Load Taq $2 \mathbf { x }$ Master Mix (M0271L, New England Biolab) according to the manufacturer’s recommendation with primers described in table S1. The PCR products were then loaded in $1 \%$ tris-borate-EDTA agarose gel electrophoresis. Reverse transcription (RT-PCR) was used to check that the genes were no longer expressed in the corresponding mutants compared to the 

wild type using the primers describes in table S1. RNAs were extracted using the Quick-RNA MiniPrep Kit (ZR1054, Zymo Research), and RT was performed using the LunaScript RT Supermix Kit (E3010L, New England Biolabs). 

# Detection and quantification of cell wall sugars

$\beta$ -1,3-glucans were detected using aniline blue (415049, Sigma-Aldrich), which was used at a final concentration of $1 \%$ (w/v). Concanavalin-A FITC (C11252, Thermo Fisher Scientific) was used at a final concentration of $5 0 \mu \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { m l }$ . In both cases, cells are observed directly after labeling using an epifluorescence microscope (Nikon Eclipse Ti) with excitation and emission wavelengths of 360/460, $4 8 0 / 5 1 0 \mathrm { m }$ and 637/670 nm for aniline blue, concanavalin-A FITC, and chlorophyll, respectively. $\beta$ -1,3 glucan quantification was performed on cellular extract as described in (47). Briefly, $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of cell suspension was centrifuged at $2 5 0 0 g$ for 5 min. Cell pellet were rinsed two times with TE buffer [10 mM tris-HCl (pH 8.0), and $1 \mathrm { m M }$ EDTA], followed by a 30-min incubation at $8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in $0 . 5 \mathrm { m l }$ of TE and $0 . 1 \mathrm { m l }$ of 6 M NaOH. A total of $9 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ of $0 . 1 \%$ aniline blue was added to each sample. Samples were incubated for $3 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ at $5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and then 30 min at room temperature before fluorescent detection at $4 0 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (excitation) and $4 9 0 \mathrm { n m }$ (emission). 

# Transcriptomic analysis

Cells were treated with laminarin for 2 and 8 hours, with three biological replicates for both time points. To extract RNA, 4 ml of culture was used using the Quick-RNA MiniPrep Kit (ZR1054, Zymo Research). After deoxyribonuclease treatment, library construction and sequencing was performed by BMKGENE company with the following procedure: mRNA was isolated using polyadenylate selection and then fragmented. The cDNA library was synthetized using random hexamer primers. Adenosine and adaptors were added to the end of each fragments that were 300– to 400–base pair long. Libraries were completed by PCR amplification and quality checked using Qubit 2.0, an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer, and by qPCR. Sequencing was performed using an Illumina paired-end technique. At least 6.53-Gb clean data were generated for each sample with $9 4 . 4 0 \%$ achieved a Q30 quality score. The reads were aligned on the C. reinhardtii genome v6.1 (48) using HISAT2 (49). Transcript assembly and quantification were performed using StringTie (50). Fragments per kilobase of transcript per million fragments mapped (FPKM) was calculated in StringTie to measure the relative expression level of transcripts. A Pearson’s correlation coefficient was used to estimate reproducibility between experimental replicates. FPKM values for each gene with gene annotation are available in dataset S1. Differential gene expression was determined using DESeq2 (51) for a fold change greater or equal to 2.5 and a $P$ value $< 0 . 0 1$ . 

# Phylogenetic analysis

Homolog detection was performed using methodology adapted from previous studies (30). Primary BLASTp searches were conducted using uniref (52), UniEuk (53), JGI Phycocosm genomes (54), decontaminated versions of the MMETSP (55), and OneKp transcriptomes (56). Conceptual peptide translations for 18 proteins (seven putative BGLs, seven 1,3- $\cdot \beta$ -glucan synthases, and four control proteins) were searched against this library by BLASTp with threshold e value of $1 0 ^ { - 5 }$ (57), and putative homologs were extracted. Homologs were reciprocally searched against the complete v6 Chlamydomonas genome annotation peptide models by BLASTp 

with -max_target_seqs 1 condition, and only sequences that retrieved one of the query proteins as a best-scoring hit were retained for further analysis (30, 48). Peptides were additionally screened for the presence of relevant PFAMs found in each query protein using hmmsearch and threshold e value of ${ 1 0 } ^ {  { - } 0 5 }$ (58, 59). For BGL1 and BGL2, no clear PFAM was identifiable, so an alternative threshold of ${ > } 1 0 \%$ proline in the total peptide sequence was erected. For BGL6 and BGL7, two different gene models in Chlamydomonas genomes were found to contain separate PFAMs (geneID 708950: PF018222.22; geneID 669200: PF1322.9 or PF12708.10) and were treated as independent datasets. Putative peptide localizations were considered using a cocktail of signalp v 5.0 using eukaryotic matrices (60); targetp v 2.0 using plant matrices (61); MitoFates using plant matrix (62); and HECTAR, ASAFind v 2.0, and PredAlgo under default conditions (63–65). Consensus decisions were made relative to targeting predictors and taxonomy (Archaeplastid sequences: consensus of TargetP, PredAlgo, and SignalP; secondary plastid-containing organisms: consensus of HECTAR, ASAFind, and MitoFates; heterotrophic SARP clade members: HECTAR, MitoFates, and SignalP; Amorphea: consensus of SignalP, MitoFates, and TargetP; bacteria: consensus signal peptide detection only using SignalP, TargetP, PredAlgo, and HECTAR), and sequences were grouped into taxonomic divisions based on recognized relationships identified in previous concatenated multigene trees. 

For BGL3 to BGL5 and BGS sequences, single gene trees were performed using a previously defined pipeline based on mafftv7 alignment under --auto setting, removal of fast-evolving sequences in GeneIOUS v 10.0.9, alignment trimming with trimal under -gt 0.5 setting, and phylogenetic inference using IQtree with automated model selection (BGL- $\mathrm { L G } + \mathrm { R } 1 0$ ; BGS: $\mathrm { J T T + F + R } 1 0 \rangle$ ). Phylogenies were run until the log-likelihood had reached a plateau (BGL, 763116.067; BGS, 1257403.006), and consensus tree topologies were used to infer phylogenetic relationships. Equivalent alignments for BGL1 and BGL2, BGL6, and BGL7 were constructed but were not used for IQtree inference due to the limited number of sequences and limited internal homology between sequences identified. Complete outputs are provided in fig. S5.
