# Summary

The tomato ripening process contains complex changes, including ethylene signalling, cell wall softening and numerous metabolic changes. So far, much is still unknown about how tomato plants precisely coordinate fruit maturation and metabolic regulation. In this paper, the ERF family transcription factor SlERF.G3-Like in tomato was found to be involved in the regulation of ethylene synthesis, cell wall degradation and the flavonoid pathway. We show that the master ripening regulator SlRIN was found to directly bind to the promoter region of SlERF.G3-Like to activate its expression. In addition, we managed to increase the production of resveratrol derivatives from ${ \sim } 1 . 4 4 ~ \mathrm { m g / g }$ DW in E8:VvStSy line to ${ \sim } 2 . 4 3 \ \mathrm { m g / g }$ DW by crossing $p 3 5 S$ : 

SlERF.G3-Like with the E8:VvStSy line. Our data provide direct evidence that SlERF.G3-Like, a hierarchical transcriptional factor, can directly manipulate pathways in which tomatoes can coordinate fruit maturation and metabolic changes. We also attest that SlERF.G3-Like can be used as an effective tool for phenylpropanoid metabolic engineering. 

# Introduction

The ripening of tomatoes, the representative climacteric fruit, is controlled by the plant hormone ethylene, a range of transcription factors and other developmental cues (Bapat et al., 2010). During tomato fruit ripening, ethylene production and signalling, fruit softening and metabolic changes take place under the tight control of a regulatory network (Giovannoni, 2004; Seymour et al., 2013). This regulatory network of transcription factors collectively determines the expression of downstream ripeningrelated genes (Li et al., 2019). 

During tomato ripening, various genes are differentially expressed and the fruit undergoes several physiological and biochemical changes, including hardness, flavour, colour and aroma (Giovannoni, 2004; Karlova et al., 2014; Qin et al., 2016). At the onset of ripening, like other climatic fruits, tomato fruit experiences a burst of respiration and a surge in ethylene production (Alexander and Grierson, 2002). The ethylene biosynthetic pathway has been well characterized and involves the action of two key enzymes, ACC synthase (ACS) and ACC oxidase (ACO) (Hoffman, 1984). Researchers demonstrated that SlACS2 and SlACS4 were consistently expressed in increasing amounts during ripening and were the central genes expressed during tomato ripening and that, among the six ACOs, SlACO1 and SlACO4 appeared to be more important (Liu et al., 2015). To date, a rich literature confirms the involvement of transcription factors in the regulation of ethylene biosyntheses, such as RIPENING-INHIBITOR (RIN) (Vrebalov et al., 2002), COLORLESS NONRIPENING (CNR)(Manning et al., 2006) and NON-RIPENING 

(NOR) (Giovannoni, 2004). There are certain differences between SlRIN mutants induced by CRISPR/Cas9 and natural mutant rin, but SlRIN is still essential for the maturation process (Ito et al., 2017; Li et al., 2018a, 2019; Vrebalov et al., 2002). SlRIN significantly impacts genes involved in maturation within this regulatory network. Master regulator modulation often involves direct regulation, interactions and feedback regulation. A better understanding of the function of master transcription factors in tomato is gained through the study of hierarchical transcriptional cascades. 

Fruit softening is also a prominent feature during the ripening of climacteric fruit (Karlova et al., 2014; Shi et al., 2022). Hemicellulose and pectin in the cell wall are solubilized and depolymerized by various cell wall-related enzymes, leading to softening fruits (Karlova et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2018a). The early report showed that polygalacturonase (PG) activity in tomatoes could be reduced by antisense RNA (Smith, 1988). After silencing SlPG, no difference in the firmness of tomato fruit was detected by vertical pressing compared with controls, although the inhibition of the expression of SlPG did improve its storage life (Schuch et al., 1991). However, simultaneous inhibition of LePG and LeEXP1 expression caused significantly firmer tomato fruit (Powell et al., 2003). Also, silencing SlPL enhanced fruit firmness (Yang et al., 2017). These studies suggest that softening is a complex process involving the synergistic action of multiple enzymes. 

In addition, the ripening process involves many metabolic changes that affect not only the texture of the fruit but also its taste, colour and aroma (Gao et al., 2022; Qin et al., 2016; Shi 

et al., 2021). The change in carotenoids has been thoroughly studied, and it significantly increases during the maturation process (Klee and Giovannoni, 2011; Seymour et al., 2013). In addition to carotenoids, phenylpropanoids, such as flavonoids, are also involved in the process of colour change in tomato fruit (Zhang et al., 2015a), but little has been reported on the relationship between the ripening process and flavonoid metabolism. 

Numerous studies have reported transcription factors involved in the regulation of ripening. To comprehend the process of fruit ripening regulation, it is crucial to investigate the connections between higher level regulatory genes, intermediate regulators and downstream effectors. Ethylene Responsive Factor (ERF) is conservatively widespread in plants. It is involved in the control of plant growth and developmental programmes, response to abiotic stress and plays a dynamic role in primary and secondary metabolic regulation (Bailey-Serres et al., 2012; De Boer et al., 2011; Licausi et al., 2013; Yang et al., 2005). The crucial role played by ERF TFs in the ripening of tomato fruit was revisited and defined (Julien Pirrello et al., 2012). Increasing numbers of ERF TFs have been characterized by fruit ripening regulators and are involved in regulating a specific aspect of fruit ripening (Cao et al., 2020; Deng et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2012; Liu et al., 2013, 2014). 

Due to its high yield and high contents of various metabolites, tomato is an ideal chassis for metabolic engineering (Fan et al., 2021; Li et al., 2018b, 2022). In addition to introducing biosynthetic genes, the use of transcription factors can significantly enhance the production of valuable compounds in tomato fruit (Fu et al., 2018; Jian et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2020). For example, overexpression of CYP76AD6 in the background of AtMYB12 results in further accumulation of L-DOPA (Breitel et al., 2021). And simultaneously, overexpression of SlWRKY35 and SlLCYE further enhances lutein production from 290 to $4 2 2 . 6 ~ { \mu \ g } ~ { \ g } ^ { - 1 }$ DW in tomato (Yuan et al., 2022). Resveratrol (3,40 ,5-trihydroxystilbene) is a non-flavonoid polyphenolic compound naturally synthesized in only a few plant species (Vannozzi et al., 2012). Its potential value in the nutraceutical, medicinal and cosmeceutical fields is extensively characterized (Valletta et al., 2021). The master transcription factor AtMYB12, which regulates the flavonoid pathway, was reported to obtain tomatoes enriched with high resveratrol by crossing with the transgenic line overexpressing VvStSy (stilbene synthase) (Zhang et al., 2015a). Given the high demand for the production of valuable compounds, new metabolic engineering tools are needed. 

In this study, we comprehensively characterize the roles of SlERF.G3-Like in regulating tomato ripening and metabolism. SlERF.G3-Like is expressed explicitly during tomato fruit ripening, which also predicts its vital role in the ripening process. Stable knockout mutants of slerf.g3-like by CRISPR/Cas9 and stable overexpressing transgenic lines under a constitutive promoter of 35S were generated. Phenotypical characterization indicated that SlERF.G3-Like also participates in regulating the ripening process in tomatoes. Comprehensive transcriptomic and metabolic analyses showed that SlERF.G3-Like could promote fruit ripening as well as affect fruit softening by regulating the expression of ripening-related and cell wall-related genes. Our study complements the link between the ripening process and flavonoid metabolism. Furthermore, our findings show that SlRIN, a master regulator during tomato fruit ripening, can bind to two 

CArG motifs upstream of the promoter of SlERF.G3-Like and induce its expression. SlERF.G3-Like is a key TF responding to master regulator RIN and controls multiple ripening-related processes. In addition, co-expression of SlERF.G3-Like with the resveratrol biosynthetic gene VvStSy can significantly enhance resveratrol production in tomato fruit. Altogether, this study reveals the coordinated maturation and metabolic model of SlERF.G3-Like as a hierarchical transcriptional factor in fruit ripening that can be applied to future breeding. 

# Results

# SlERF.G3-Like positively regulates tomato fruit ripening

We previously screened SlERF.G3-Like as a potential flavonoid biosynthesis regulator in tomato fruit (Li et al., 2020b). To further investigate the function of SlERF.G3-Like in tomatoes we generated slerf.g3-like knockout mutants in the MicroTom background using CRISPR/Cas9, as well as overexpression lines driven by the cauliflower mosaic virus (CaMV) 35S promoter. 

We genotyped 14 gene-edited seedlings by PCR amplification and sequencing over the edited site in the ${ \sf T } _ { 0 }$ generation. In total, we obtained two homozygous lines named CR-slerf.g3- like-3 (CR-3) and CR-slerf.g3-like-8 (CR-8), respectively. CR-3 harboured a 4-bp deletion, while CR-8 carried an 8-bp deletion (Figure S1). Both lines have premature stop codons compared with the WT. We grew the CR-3 and CR-8 into the $\mathsf { T } _ { 2 }$ generation. Individual plants containing the homozygous mutation but no T-DNA insertions were selected for subsequent analyses (Figure S1d). 

On the other hand, we obtained 16 independent 35S: SlERF.G3-Like-OE lines in the ${ \sf T } _ { 0 }$ generation (Figure S2a). Two lines, OE-SlERF.G3-Like-K (OE-K) and OE-SlERF.G3-Like-S (OE-S), with representative phenotypes and expression levels, were selected for further phenotypic and molecular analyses (Figures 1a,b and S2). 

To analyse SlERF.G3-Like related phenotypes, knockout mutants, overexpression lines and WT were sown and grown together. There was no significant difference between CR and WT in the period of vegetative growth (Figure S3c). However, significant differences were observed during the fruit ripening process (Figure 1a). Compared with the WT fruits, which reached the breaker stage at 39 days post anthesis (DPA), the CR-slerf.g3- like fruits displayed a significant delay in ripening (breaker stage at 41 DPA), while the OE fruits were significantly earlier (breaker stage at 36 DPA) (Figure 1a,c). 

In addition, we assessed the fruit hue angle, which indicates colour saturation. Compared with WT, the fruit hue angle was significantly increased in OE-K and OE-S fruits, which eventually show an orange phenotype in OE lines (Figures 1d and S3a,b). The fruit hue angle was not significantly different between CR and WT fruits at the Br + 15 stage (Figures 1d and S3a). All these results indicate that SlERF.G3-Like is a positive regulator of tomato fruit ripening.

# SlERF.G3-Like directly regulates the softening of tomato fruit by regulating the expression of SlPG2a and SlPL

The softening of tomato fruit is a significant physiological change during the ripening process (Giovannoni, 2004; Seymour et al., 2013). To evaluate the effect of the SlERF.G3-Like on tomato fruit softening, the firmness of CR, OE and WT fruits at the $\mathsf { B r } + 1 5$ stage was measured by a texture analyser. CR-K and CR-S mutant fruits were significantly firmer than the WT at the $\mathsf { B r } + 1 5$ stage examined. In contrast, the overexpressed lines showed the opposite trend (Figure 4c). Furthermore, histological sectioning from the fruit epicarp indicated compact cells and increased cell numbers in the exocarp of CR fruit compared with WT fruit. In comparison, the exocarp cells are more loosely arranged in OE fruit (Figure 4a,e,f). On the other hand, the cuticle thickness of the OE lines was also significantly thinner than the WT lines (Figure 4b,d). 

Softening is one of the irreversible features of ripening fruits. Extensive studies on softening during fruit ripening in tomatoes showed that softening is a complex process with multiple enzymes working consecutively and synergistically, contributing to altering fruit texture (Wang et al., 2018a). Silencing SlPL, the only gene confirmed in tomatoes, was shown to increase fruit firmness and improve fruit shelf life (Yang et al., 2017). GO enrichment analysis of the DEGs showed that SlERF.G3-Like regulates cell wall organization or biogenesis pathways (Figure S3d). To investigate the molecular mechanism of SlERF.G3-Like in regulating tomato fruit softening, transcriptome analysis was performed. Functional annotation of the DEGs indicates that the expression levels of SlPG2a and SlPL were downregulated in CR-slerf.g3-like fruits but upregulated in OE-SlERF.G3-Like lines (Figure 4g). To confirm the regulatory role of SlERF.G3-Like in the expression of cell wall-related genes, we assessed their transcript levels in CR, OE and WT fruits by RTqPCR (Figure S7). 

A dual-luciferase assay was performed to detect whether SlERF.G3-Like could directly regulate the promoter activity of SlPG2a and SlPL. The results showed that SlERF.G3-Like could 

bind to the promoters of SlPG2a and SlPL in vivo and activate their expression (Figure 4h). Furthermore, a total of 14 cell wallrelated genes, including endo-glucanase 2 (SlCEL2), expansin1 (SlEXP1), $\beta$ -galactosidases (SlTBG), xyloglucan endotransglycosylase/hydrolase (SlXTH), endo- $\beta$ -mannanase (SlMAN), xylosidase (SlXY) and xylanase (SlXyn) were downregulated in both CR-K and CR-S fruits (Figure 2c). Collectively, these results imply that SlERF.G3-Like positively regulates the cell wall by targeting SlPG2a and SlPL. 

# SlERF.G3-Like positively regulates the phenylpropanoid pathway

The phenylpropanoid metabolic pathway is an essential secondary metabolic pathway in plants and is synthesized from phenylalanine produced by the shikimate pathway as a substrate (Jacobowitz and Weng, 2020). Many important phenylpropanoids, such as coumarins, anthocyanins, flavonoids and lignins, have vital roles in plant growth and development, disease and antioxidant capacity and pigment formation (Dong and Lin, 2021; Jacobowitz and Weng, 2020; Zhang et al., 2015b). The GO terms of the secondary metabolic process were dominantly enriched in the biological process group, especially the phenylpropanoid metabolic process. Our previous study suggests that SlERF.G3-Like can directly regulate the expression of SlCHS1 (chalcone synthase 1), SlFLS (flavonol synthase) and SlF3H (flavanone-3-hydroxylase) (Li et al., 2020b). SlCHS1 is responsible for the first committed step in the flavonoid pathway, and most of the downstream genes were upregulated in OE lines and downregulated in CR lines, suggesting enhanced activity of this branch of the pathway (Figure 2d). On the other hand, overexpression of SlERF.G3-Like in tomatoes increased transcript levels of genes involved in primary metabolism. Compared with WT fruit, nearly all of the genes encoding enzymes of glycolysis and the shikimate pathways were expressed more highly in p35S: SlERF.G3-Like tomato fruit than control fruit. And both primary and secondary metabolisms promote flavonoid biosynthesis in tomatoes (Figure 2d). 

Lignin is a major component of the secondary cell wall and can provide mechanical strength for plants. Formation, movement and polymerization of monolignols are all aspects of lignification and this process results from the coordinated expression of several enzymes (Boerjan et al., 2003; Boudet et al., 2003). Monolignols are derived from the general phenylpropanoid pathway, and the related enzymes have been well-characterized in Arabidopsis and poplar (Shi et al., 2022; Vanholme et al., 2019). It is worth mentioning that $p$ -coumaric acid acts as the common precursor for both flavonoid and monolignol biosynthesis (Figure 5). By sequence alignment of key enzymes with verified functions, it was found in our data that cinnamyl CoA reductase (CCR), caffeic acid O-methyltransferase (COMT) and cinnamyl alcohol dehydrogenase (CAD) showed significant upregulation in CR lines and downregulation in OE lines. The last major steps in lignin synthesis are catalysed by laccase (LAC) or peroxidase (PRX). Putative genes, including 10 PRXs and two LACs, showed the same trend as other monolignol biosynthesis genes. And all of them offer an opposite trend to the key genes of the flavonoid pathway. SlERF.G3-Like is involved in regulating the flavonoid pathway, and increased flavonoid biosynthesis reduces the availability of these precursors, thereby directing a change in the direction of metabolic flow and thus reducing lignin biosynthesis (Figure 5). The redistributed metabolic flow also contributes to the softening phenotype of p35S:SlERF.G3-Like fruits.

# SlRIN directly activates the expression of SlERF.G3-Like during tomato ripening

Our data indicate SlERF.G3-Like can regulate multiple ripeningrelated pathways. Interestingly, the expression level of SlERF.G3- Like is significantly co-expressed with the master ripening regulator RIN in the Tomato Expression Atlas (TEA; http://tea. solgenomics.net/) database (Figure S8a). Previously, analysis data revealed that MADS-box proteins could bind to the specific DNA sequences known as CArG-box, including C(C/T)(A/T)6(A/G)G, C (A/T)8G or C(C/T)(A/T)G(A/T)4(A/G)G (Fujisawa et al., 2013; Ito et al., 2008). As shown in Figure 6a, the $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { k b }$ promoter of SlERF.G3-Like contains five CArG-box motifs, which are putative binding motifs of MADS proteins. A ChIP-seq dataset that predicted direct RIN targets showed significant fragment enrichment at the promoter of SlERF.G3-Like (Zhong et al., 2013) (Figure S8b). Furthermore, the expression level of SlERF.G3-Like was markedly reduced by $9 8 \%$ in the rin mutant compared with 

WT (Zhong et al., 2013) (Figure S8c). RT-qPCR data showed the same result (Figure S8d). Based on these results, SlRIN is a potential upstream factor of SlERF.G3-Like.

These results predict that, in addition to directly activating the expression of key structural genes of the flavonoid pathway, SlERF.G3-Like may be involved in the regulation of the flavonoid pathway by interacting directly or indirectly with FUL1 in fruit ripening. 

SlERF.G3-Like is also found to be involved in the regulation of ethylene synthesis and the hardness of cell walls during tomato fruit ripening. Overexpression of SlERF.G3-Like under constitutive promoter 35S activated the expression of SlACO1 and SlACS2, which play a significant function in the ethylene synthesis pathway (Liu et al., 2015), resulting in increased ethylene production. The cell wall phenotype and DEGs suggest that SlERF.G3-Like directly affects cell wall texture. The promoter of SlPL, which by far has the most determined impact on the hardness of the cell wall, is positively regulated by SlERF.G3-Like and can be activated in vitro (Yang et al., 2017). SlPG2a is another gene involved in the cell wall that SlERF.G3-Like can directly activate. Other reported cell wall-associated enzymes, such as SlEXP1, SlTBG, SlMAN, SlXY and SlXyn are significantly increased in OE lines while showing the opposite trend in CR lines. Whether these genes are direct targets of SlERF.G3-Like needs further confirmation. SlLOB1 was reported to target SlEXP1 in tomato directly, and the transcript and protein levels of SlEXP1 were strongly repressed in RNAi lines (Shi et al., 2021). SlLOB1 was a DEG in RNA-seq data, with decreased expression in CR lines and increased expression in OE lines. In addition, these two genes have different tissue expression profiles and functions. SlLOB1 was initially induced at the mature green (MG) stage, is highly expressed during the BR stage and has the highest nonfruit expression in stems (Shi et al., 2021). SlERF.G3-Like expression occurs specifically at the fruit ripening stages. Unlike SlERF.G3-Like, SlLOB1 did not affect other tomato ripening phenotypes, including the maximum ethylene production and the onset of ripening. According to our findings, p35S:SlERF.G3-Like has a significantly shorter time to induce ethylene production and a higher maximum ethylene production than WT. This result suggests that SlLOB1 has more specific functions for cell wall changes, while SlERF.G3-Like has a broader pleiotropic effect on the maturation process. 

In the wild-type tomato fruit, the peel is orange predominantly due to flavonoids, while the flesh is red mainly due to carotenoids (Adato et al., 2009; Fernandez-Moreno et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2018b). However, when TFs regulating flavonoid biosynthesis were overexpressed throughout the fruit, the tomato fruit pericarps were enriched with flavonoids and turned yellow (Luo et al., 2008; Ying et al., 2020). Our data support the idea that overexpression of SlERF.G3-Like affects the onset of ripening. Eventually, OE fruits show an orange colour mainly due to the enrichment of flavonoids. We examined the carotenoid content (mainly lycopene, lutein, zeaxanthin, a-carotene and $\beta$ -carotene) in fruits at the Br + 10 stage, and there was no change in the total carotenoid content in OE and WT lines (Figure S15). In contrast, there was a tendency for the total carotenoid content to increase in the CR line compared with WT, but the changes were different for each compound specifically. For example, lycopene was elevated in CR fruits relative to WT fruits, but $\beta$ -carotene was decreased in CR lines and increased in OE lines (Table S2). The 

increasing carotenoid contents in CR fruit could be due to the reduction of flux into flavonoid biosynthesis. The previous data indicate that the induction of carotenoid biosynthesis by SlWRKY35 could lead to decreased flavonoids, suggesting that both pathways could compete for substrates (Yuan et al., 2022). Although carotenoid accumulation was present in the final fruits of CR lines, unlike direct targeting of SlPSY1 and thus affecting carotenoid content, this could be a cascade change resulting from the redistribution of metabolic flow caused by the induction of flavonoid biosynthetic genes by SlERF.G3-Like (Fu et al., 2018). We previously analysed the expression profile of carotenoid biosynthesis genes in transcriptomic data of E8:SlERF.G3-Like lines, and no significant changes were observed among those lines (Li et al., 2020b). In addition, the Dual-Luc assay verified that SlERF.G3-Like does not activate the promoters of SlPSY1 and SlPDS (Figure S16). All these data suggest that SlERF.G3-Like does not directly affect carotenoid biosynthetic genes. 

As a climacteric fruit, the ripening process of tomato fruit involves complex changes in morphology and metabolites, triggered by the strong ethylene production during the ripening process (Seymour et al., 2013). Several master regulators have been identified, such as RIN, a MADS-box transcription factor identified and characterized by an originally cloned rin tomato mutation (Vrebalov et al., 2002). The rin mutant led to a ripening-inhibition phenotype, indicating the central role of RIN during tomato ripening (Fujisawa et al., 2011, 2013; Vrebalov et al., 2002). Recent studies showed that rin is a gain-of-function mutant, and there are differences between the phenotypes of the RIN knockout and rin mutant (Ito et al., 2017; Li et al., 2018a). However, there is no doubt that RIN is required for the progression of ripening and plays a central role in tomato fruit ripening (Li et al., 2020a). 

Considering the central role of RIN during ripening, there should be a hierarchical transcriptional cascade for the accurate regulation of various downstream morphologic and metabolic changes. Similar regulatory networks have been established in other plant hormone signalling pathways. For example, OsDREB1C, a transcriptional regulator belonging to the AP2/ERF family, can be involved in the regulation of both photosynthesis and nitrogen utilization (Wei et al., 2022). In the JA signalling pathway, the master regulator MYC2 and its downstream MYC2- targeted transcription factors (MTFs) form various MYC2-MTF modules, which initiate and amplify specific aspects of the JAsignalling transcriptional output: the MYC2-JA2L module mediates the wound response while the MYC2-ERF.C3 module regulates the pathogen response (Du et al., 2017). 

Transcriptomic data and phenotypes suggest that SlERF.G3-Like is an intermediate regulator influencing the most physiological subdomain of the maturation transition. Combining Dual-Luc, Y1H and EMSA assays, we verified that SlRIN is an upstream regulator of SlERF.G3-Like. SlERF.G3-Like is an essential intermediate component of the SlRIN regulatory network. Systematic elucidation of regulatory networks at all levels contributes to further understanding of the molecular mechanisms of master regulators. 

Notably, many known intermediate TFs in the RIN hierarchical transcriptional cascade regulate a single aspect of ripening. For instance, our previous studies indicate that SlRIN can directly activate the expression of SlWRKY35, an inducer of SlDXS1, to guide carbon flux towards the MEP pathway and enhance downstream carotenoid biosynthesis (Yuan et al., 2022). In the meantime, SlRIN can also inhibit the expression of SlGCR, an inducer of SlLCYE, to suppress the production of lutein from the main carotenoid pathway (Ren et al., 2022). Together, the SlRIN-

WRKY35 and SlRIN-SlGCR modules accurately regulate the significant induction of carotenoid biosynthesis during tomato ripening. The current SlRIN-SlERF.G3-Like module, however, is at least associated with ethylene production, cell wall degradation and phenylpropanoid metabolism. The hierarchical transcriptional cascade of plant hormone signalling seems more complicated than expected. More intermediate TFs are yet to be identified. 

Due to their medicinal functions, tomato fruit enriched with specialized metabolites is suitable for processing purposes rather than fresh consumption (Li et al., 2018b). Therefore, normal, even fast, softening is an ideal trait for such types of varieties. However, when TFs such as AtMYB12 and Del/Ros1 were used as metabolic engineering tools, fruit ripening and over-ripening were significantly delayed due to the increased antioxidant capacity by flavonoid enrichment (Zhang et al., 2013, 2015b). Our study indicates SlERF.G3-Like can positively regulate tomato ripening by activating the expression of genes involved in ethylene synthesis, cell wall degradation and phenylpropanoid pathway. Therefore, it can be an ideal tool for engineering high antioxidant compounds in tomato fruit while maintaining the ordinary ripening and over-ripening processes. 

To summarize, our results suggest that SlERF.G3-Like drives functionally diverse transcriptional programmes that regulate ethylene synthesis, cell wall degradation and the phenylpropanoid pathway during tomato ripening. And SlRIN, which plays a global regulatory role in tomato fruit ripening, can control its expression. The conservative role of the SlERF.G3-Like remains to be further explored. Overall, our data revealed the comprehensive roles of SlERF.G3-Like, and, in particular, provided new evidence linking the ripening and the phenylpropanoid pathway during the maturation of tomato fruits. This work offers new insights into how plants control intricate biological processes despite limited genomic information. Since targeting global regulators like SlRIN causes many phenotypic changes, including some adverse effects such as flavour (Osorio, Carneiro et al. 2020), these hierarchical transcriptional regulators can act as better breeding targets and offer a theoretical foundation for precision breeding. We also attest that SlERF.G3-Like can be an effective tool for phenylpropanoid metabolic engineering in processing tomatoes. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

A tomato (Solanum lycopersicum cv. MicroTom) was purchased from PanAmerican SeedTM. Tomato plants were grown in a greenhouse with standard conditions (16/8 h of light/dark cycle at $2 4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ in $60 \%$ humidity and under $2 5 0 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } / \varsigma ^ { - 1 }$ intense luminosity) and watered daily. Nicotiana Benthamiana was grown in appropriate greenhouses for the dual-luciferase reporter assay. 

# Plasmid construction and plant transformation

The overexpression vector was assembled by the GoldenBraid2.0 cloning strategy (Sarrion-Perdigones et al., 2013). The full length of thecoding sequence (CDS)of SlERF.G3-Like was introducedinto the entry vector PUPD2, and one transcriptional unit was constructed using the 35S promoter-transcription factor 35S terminator and the kanamycin resistance gene under the control of NOS promoter and NOS terminator as an additional transcriptional unit. The CRISPR/ Cas9 vector was kindly provided by Dr. Li Zhengguo (College of Life Sciences, Chongqing University). A target site was designed for the SlERF.G3-Like based on the online tool CRISPR-P 2.0 (http://cbi. hzau.edu.cn/crispr/). These constructed vectors were used for 

Agrobacterium-mediated tomato plant transformation and were introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105 (Ying et al., 2020). 

# RNA extraction and RT-qPCR analysis

Total RNA was extracted from tomato fruit using the Fastpure Plant Total RNA Isolation Kit (Vazyme Nanjing, China; RC401-01) following the instructions. First-strand cDNA was reverse transcribed from $1 ~ \mu \ g$ of total RNA using the PrimeScriptTM RT reagent kit (AK4201; Takara Bio, Kusatsu, Japan) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. RT-qPCR was performed using AceQ Universal SYBR qPCR Master Mix (Vazyme, Nanjing, China; Q511- 02) on a Bio-Rad CFX384 Real-Time PCR System (Initial incubation at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 3 min, followed by 40 cycles of 95 °C for 10 s and $6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 30 s, and added melting curve analysis during the $6 5 { - } 9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C )$ ). The primer sequences used in this study are listed in Table S1. 

# Transcriptome profiling

Total RNA was extracted from the fruits at 39 DPA for WT, overexpression and gene-edited lines. RNA-Seq library construction and high-throughput sequencing were conducted by Novogene (Beijing, China). The library preparations were sequenced on an Illumina Novaseq 6000 platform, and 150 bp paired-end reads were generated. Transcriptome analysis was carried out as previously described (Yuan et al., 2022). DEGs were found using a significance threshold of a $\log _ { 2 }$ fold change of $\pm \harpoonleft$ . RNA seq data reported in this paper have been deposited in the Genome Sequence Archive (Chen et al., 2021) in National Genomics Data Center, China National Center for Bioinformation/Beijing Institute of Genomics, Chinese Academy of Sciences (GSA: CRA009040) and are publicly accessible at https://ngdc. cncb.ac.cn/gsa. 

# Ethylene production measurements

Ethylene production was measured as specified previously (Deng et al., 2022) with a few minor adjustments. Before measurement, fruits gathered at different stages were placed in 120 mL open jars. After0.5 h,thejarsweresealedandincubated atroomtemperature for 2 h until measurement (Agilent 7890B gas chromatography). The final ethylene production was obtained by comparing it with the peak area of ethylene at known concentrations. 

# Fruit firmness

More than twenty fruits from each line were harvested at the $\mathsf { B r } + 1 5$ stage, and the firmness was measured with a TA.XTC-18 texture analyser (BOSIN, Shanghai) with a P/2 columnar probe $( 2 \mathsf { m m }$ diameter). The test parameters were set as follows: pretest speed $5 ~ { \mathsf { m m } } ~ { \mathsf { s e c } } ^ { - 1 }$ ; test speed $1 \ \mathsf { m m } \ \mathsf { s e c } ^ { - 1 }$ ; post-test speed $5 ~ \mathsf { m m } ~ \mathsf { s e c } ^ { - 1 }$ ; penetration depth $5 \textrm { m m }$ ; trigger force 5 g. The value of firmness was calculated by BosinTech TAS. 

# Colour measurement

The colour values are measured with a Konica Minolta chromameter (CR-400) at various stages of ripening. The exact calculation method has been described in a previous study (Deng et al., 2018). At least 20 fruits were measured for each line. 

# Analysis and identification of carotenoids and resveratrol

For LC–MS analysis of compounds, fruits were harvested at the $\mathsf { B r } + 1 0$ stage and immediately placed in liquid nitrogen, then lyophilized and ground into a fine powder for extraction. 

The carotenoids were extracted as described previously. All the above operations must be carried out away from bright light. And the carotenoids were determined by LC–MS (the Sciex TRIPLE QUADTM 5500 platform in Life Science Core Facilities, College of Life Sciences, Sichuan University) using a YMC C30 chromatographic column (Thermo AccucoreTM C30 chromatographic column, USA) and controlled by Analyst 1.6.3 software (AB Sciex). Mass spectrometry was performed using an atmospheric pressure chemical ionization (APCI) source operating in a negative ion mode. The carotenoids were identified with standards and quantified by integrating the peak areas. Standards were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St Louis, MO; https://www.sigmaaldrich.com/). The detection was performed for lutein, zeaxanthin, $\alpha \cdot$ -carotene, $\beta$ -carotene and lycopene. Three independent replicates were used for the analysis. 

Resveratrol was extracted as previously described (Zhang et al., 2015a) with slight modifications. An amount of $0 . 0 1 \ 9$ of dry powder was extracted with $1 \mathrm { \ m L } 8 0 \%$ MeOH at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ by ultrasonic extraction. The sample was then centrifuged at $3 0 0 0 \ { \mathfrak { g } }$ at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ gfor 10 min, and the supernatant was taken. The supernatant was then centrifuged at $1 8 \ 0 0 0 \ g$ at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for g10 min. The supernatant was further diluted 20 times with $80 \%$ MeOH. The samples were filtered through a $0 . 4 5 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ filter before injection. For each line, three biological replicates were analysed. Moreover, resveratrol was determined by LC–MS using a C18 chromatographic column (Thermo ScientificTM). Mass spectrometry was performed using an Electron Spray Ionization (ESI) source operating in a negative ion mode. 

# Cytological assessment of fruit pericarp

The fresh fruits were trimmed from the target tissue with a scalpel and put into fixative (G1101, Wuhan Servicebio Technology Co., Ltd, China) for more than $2 4 ~ \mathsf { h }$ . After dehydration with gradient alcohol, the tissue is embedded in paraffin wax at $6 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . The slice transverse sections of $4 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ thickness were cut with the paraffin slicer and stained with Safranin O-Fast Green staining solution (G1031, Servicebio). Oil-Red solution (G1015, Servicebio) stained slices were chosen for cuticle observation. Multiplex slice images were acquired through a whole slide imaging system (Olympus VS200) at $2 0 \times$ objective. Cytological assessment, including cuticle thickness, cell size and cell number, was performed using ImageJ software. 

# Luciferase reporter assay

The full-length CDS of SlERF.G3-Like and SlRIN under the control of the 35S promoter were used as effectors and constructed into the destination vector pEAQ-HT-DEST1 using Gateway Cloning. The remaining promoters involved the promoters of SlACS2, SlACO1, SlPG2a, SlPL and SlERF.G3-Like; each promoter fragment was cloned upstream of the LUC (luciferase) CDS using the GoldenBraid 2.0 cloning strategy. The internal control REN (Renilla luciferase), driven by the 35S promoter, was also constructed to act as an internal reporter at the same level as the transcriptional unit. The pEAQ-HT-DEST1 empty vector was used as a negative control (CK), and specific steps were performed as described previously (Ying et al., 2020). The LUC and REN activities were assayed using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (Promega, Madison, WI, USA; E1910) by a SynergyTM H1 hybrid multimode microplate reader (BioTek). At least 3 independent biological replicates were performed for each experiment. Results were expressed as relative values of 

LUC/REN. For the Luciferase reporter assay, two different detection methods were used in this study. Another transactivation assay to test the regulation of SlERF.G3-Like on the SlACS2 and SlACO1 promoters, using D-Luciferin (Solarbio, D9390) as substrate, was performed using an IVIS Lumina Series III Preclinical In Vivo Imaging system (PerkinElmer), and the luminescence intensity was detected by a luminometer. 

# Yeast One-Hybrid assays

The yeast onehybrid assay was performed as described previously (Ying et al., 2020) with modifications. DNA fragments consisting of three copies of the proSlERF.G3-Like ( 1214 to 1184 bp) were chemically synthesized and cloned into the pHis2-Leu-GW through Gateway Cloning. And another upstream fragment ( 688 to 589 bp) of the SlERF.G3-Like was cloned into the pHis2-Leu-GW reporter vector. A full-length CDS of SlRIN was introduced into the pDEST22 vector. The pDEST22-EV (empty vector) was used as a negative control. Then, different combinations of effector and reporter were co-transformed into the Y187 yeast strain. The transformants were grown on the SD/ Leu/ Trp selection medium, and the positive yeast cells were diluted with a $1 0 ~ \times$ dilution series and dotted on SD/-Leu/-Trp/- His medium with different concentrations of 3-AT as screening pressures. 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA)

The full-length coding sequence of SlRIN was amplified by PCR and cloned into pGEX-4 T-1–GST named pGEX-4 T-1–GST–SlRIN (Yuan et al., 2022). Oligonucleotide probes were synthesized and labelled with $5 ^ { \prime }$ biotin (Sangon Biotech, China). EMSAs were performed as previously described (Hellman and Fried, 2007; Yuan et al., 2022). Briefly, biotin-labelled probes were incubated with GST-fusion proteins at room temperature for 20 min, and free and bound DNAs were separated in an acrylamide gel. Unlabelled wild-type probes were used as competitors. Electrophoretic mobility shift assay binding reactions were detected according to the manufacturer’s instructions (Chemiluminescent Nucleic Acid Detection Module Kit, Thermo Scientific). The probes used for EMSA are listed in Table S1.
