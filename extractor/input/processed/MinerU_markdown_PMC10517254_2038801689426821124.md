# Abstract

Molecular mechanisms that distinguish the synthesis of semi-crystalline $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan polymers found in plant starch granules from the synthesis of water-soluble polymers by nonplant species are not well understood. To address this, starch biosynthetic enzymes from maize (Zea mays L.) endosperm were isolated in a reconstituted environment using yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) as a test bed. Ninety strains were constructed containing unique combinations of 11 synthetic transcription units specifying maize starch synthase (SS), starch phosphorylase (PHO), starch branching enzyme (SBE), or isoamylase-type starch debranching enzyme (ISA). Soluble and insoluble branched $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucans accumulated in varying proportions depending on the enzyme suite, with ISA function stimulating distribution into the insoluble form. Among the SS isoforms, SSIIa, SSIII, and SSIV individually supported the accumulation of glucan polymer. Neither SSI nor SSV alone produced polymers; however, synergistic effects demonstrated that both isoforms can stimulate $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan accumulation. PHO did not support $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan production by itself, but it had either positive or negative effects on polymer content depending on which SS or a combination thereof was present. The complete suite of maize enzymes generated insoluble particles resembling native starch granules in size, shape, and crystallinity. Ultrastructural analysis revealed a hierarchical assembly starting with subparticles of approximately $5 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ diameter that coalesce into discrete structures of approximately $2 0 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ diameter. These are assembled into semi-crystalline α-glucan superstructures up to $4 \mu \mathsf { m }$ in length filling most of the yeast cytosol. ISA was not essential for the formation of such particles, but their abundance was increased dramatically by ISA presence. 

# Introduction

Starch metabolism has been investigated since the late nineteenth century because of its importance in food and fuel production, as well as its usefulness as a model genetic 

system (Smith et al. 1997; Jeon et al. 2010; Keeling and Myers 2010; Zeeman et al. 2010; Ball et al. 2015; Nakamura 2015; Pfister and Zeeman 2016; Hannah and Boehlein 2017; Seung and Smith 2019; Smith and Zeeman 2020; Tetlow and Bertoft 2020). Much has been learned from forward 

and reverse genetic analyses that compromise or eliminate one or more system components, as well as phylogenomic and biochemical analyses. Exhaustive studies of cereal endosperms, Arabidopsis thaliana leaves, unicellular green algae, potato (Solanum tuberosum) tubers, pea (Pisum sativum L.) embryos, etc., have revealed a generally conserved suite of starch metabolism enzymes present in all Chloroplastida species, i.e. chloroplast-containing organisms. These enzymes are all present as multiple isoforms that in most instances are broadly conserved throughout the Chloroplastida lineage (Deschamps et al. 2008). 

Numerous fundamental questions remain unanswered about how the $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan polymers that constitute starch are assembled. These polymers are amylopectin, which contains an $\mathbb { a } ( 1 \to 6 )$ branch point on approximately $5 \%$ of the $\mathfrak { a } ( 1 \to 4 )$ linked glucose residues, and amylose, which is primarily an $\mathfrak { a } ( 1  4 )$ -linked linear polymer with only rare branches (Tetlow and Bertoft 2020). Among the questions are (i) How is starch granule initiation accomplished and regulated? (ii) What are the roles of individual enzymes among the multiple isoforms present? (iii) What are the precise molecular structures of the branched α-glucan polymers and why do some partially crystallize and assemble into starch granules whereas others remain soluble, and (iv) Why do plants lack soluble $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucans and instead contain exclusively insoluble granules? The complexity of these processes complicates the interpretation of mutant phenotypes such that important details will remain obscure when only genetics approaches are applied. 

Reconstitution with defined components is a complementary approach for investigating a metabolic system that enables the evaluation of the effects of individual factors. Such experiments can be performed in vitro, and also synthetic biology methods can reconstitute metabolic systems in heterologous host cells. Previous studies made use of the ease by which the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae can be genetically engineered to express multiple transgenes to reconstitute starch metabolism from Arabidopsis leaves (Pfister et al. 2016, 2022). In these studies, the host strain genome lacked the elements needed to produce glycogen, the soluble, branched $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan polymer native to yeast and most nonphotosynthetic eukaryotes. The host was further modified to express a prokaryotic enzyme that produces ADP-glucose (ADPGlc), the glucosyl unit donor used by Chloroplastida species to produce starch granules. Simultaneous expression in yeast of a complex suite of enzymes native to Arabidopsis leaf cells resulted in the production of insoluble $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan particles that resemble native starch granules in many respects, as well as soluble $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucans (Pfister et al. 2016). 

The current study used the same heterologous system to reconstitute the maize (Zea mays L.) endosperm starch biosynthetic pathway in yeast (S. cerevisiae). Combinations of 11 different transgenic enzymes were expressed in addition to the prokaryotic ADPGlc pyrophosphorylase (AGPase) that produces ADPGlc. These include starch synthases (SS; ADP-glucose: 

1,4-α-D-glucan 4-α-D-glucosyl transferase), starch branching enzymes (SBE; 1,4-α-D-glucan: 1,4-α-D-glucan 6-glucosyl transferase), isoamylase-type starch debranching enzyme (ISA; amylopectin 6-α-D-glucohydrolase), and starch phosphorylase (PHO; 1,4-α-D-glucan: phosphate $\mathfrak { a }$ -D-glucosyltransferase; PHO). SS catalyzes the synthesis of ${ \mathfrak { a } } ( 1 {  } 4 )$ -linked “linear” glucan chains using ADPGlc as substrate, SBE cleaves part of a linear chain and transfers it to a C6 hydroxyl group to form an $\operatorname { \ a u } ( 1 \to 6 )$ glycoside bond branch point, ISA hydrolyzes a subset of $\operatorname { \ a u } ( 1 \to 6 )$ bonds to affect polymer architecture, and PHO is able to either elongate or shorten linear $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan chains by reversible phosphorolysis. In vivo, these enzymes function together to produce branched polymers that undergo partial crystallization and assemble into insoluble starch granules. 

Part of the difficulty in unraveling starch biosynthetic mechanisms stems from the presence of multiple isoforms of each enzyme in the pathway (Supplemental Table S1). Maize endosperm expresses genes encoding 6 SS isoforms denoted SSI, SSIIa, SSIIIa, SSIV, SSV, or granule-bound starch synthase (GBSS), all of which were included in this reconstitution study. Each of these is broadly conserved as a unique isoform in chloroplast-containing species, which indicates a function selected in evolution. Three SBE genes were expressed, including 2 broadly conserved isoforms (SBEI and SBEII) (Deschamps et al. 2008), the latter present in maize in 2 homologous copies (SBEIIa and SBEIIb) as the result of relatively recent gene duplication (Fisher et al. 1996). The ISA1 isoform of isoamylase-type DBE was included in the study set, as was the PHO1 isoform of starch phosphorylase. These enzymes are located within amyloplasts of endosperm cells and several of them co-purify in stable multi-subunit complexes (Tetlow et al. 2004, 2008; Hennen-Bierwagen et al. 2008, 2009). In several instances (SSIIa, SSIIIa, GBSS, ISA1, SBEIIb), genetic analysis has shown that the loss of a specific isoform causes major changes in starch content and/or structure (Supplemental Table S1). In other instances, loss-of-function mutations do not obviously affect starch properties despite strong evolutionary conservation of the isoform. 

The fully reconstituted system as defined here synthesizes insoluble particles of α-glucan ranging up to 4 μm in length, having filled a large proportion of the cytoplasmic space of the host cell. The particles resemble native starch granules in morphology and crystallinity characteristics. High-resolution imaging of these structures revealed hierarchical assembly starting with subparticles of approximately $5 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ diameter. In vivo reconstitution assays tested the effects of individual enzymes and demonstrated specific roles for ISA1, PHO1, and various SS isoforms. 

# Results

# Yeast metabolic engineering

The host organism for metabolic engineering was yeast strain 504 in which the glycogen synthase genes GSY1 and GSY2, the glycogen branching enzyme gene GLC3, and the 

glycogenin genes GLG1 and GLG2 were deleted (Pfister et al. 2022). Host genes encoding the $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucosidases that hydrolyze maltose also were inactive, from nonsense mutations created near the initiation codon, and an Escherichia coli glgC gene that encodes a variant AGPase insensitive to feedback regulation (Sakulsingharoj et al. 2004) was expressed from the yeast GAL1 promoter (Pfister et al. 2022). The endogenous host genes GPH1 encoding glycogen phosphorylase and GDB1 encoding indirect glycogen debranching enzyme are intact in strain 504 and were used as homologous recombination target sites for replacement by synthetic maize genes. 

Maize genes encoding SS, SBE, PHO, or ISA were selected for expression in yeast based on known effects in planta on starch content and/or structure, and on expression at the mRNA level in developing endosperm (Supplemental Table S1). Codon-optimized coding sequences lacking the known or predicted plastid targeting peptide (Supplemental Fig. S1) were coupled to promoters of the yeast genes GAL1 or CWP2, both of which are active during growth in galactose media (Keren et al. 2013; Pfister et al. 2022), and yeast terminators (Supplemental Table S2). Resulting synthetic transcription units (TUs) were integrated individually or in tandem arrays (Supplemental Table S3) into 1 of 3 locations in the yeast genome: the GPH1 locus, the GDB1 locus, or chromosome XII site 5 (Mikkelsen et al. 2012) (Supplemental Table S4). In some strains, all 3 integration sites were utilized so that GPH1 and GDB1 were both deleted, whereas in others 1 or both of those loci remained intact. 

# Maize ISA1 functions in yeast cells in the absence of ISA2

The reconstituted system was used initially to examine the effects of individual enzymes on the accumulation of solubleand insoluble α-polyglucans (IPG and SPG, respectively). This was first applied to test maize ISA1 for the ability to stimulate the accumulation of IPG. This is of interest because mutations affecting ISA in various plants and green algae result in decreased starch content and the appearance of hydrosoluble glycogen-like polymers referred to as phytoglycogen (Zeeman et al. 2010; Hennen-Bierwagen et al. 2012). In Arabidopsis leaves, normal function requires both ISA1 and the conserved, paralogous protein ISA2 (Zeeman et al. 1998; Delatte et al. 2005; Wattebled et al. 2005). In cereal endosperms, however, ISA1 provides an apparently normal function in the absence of ISA2 (Kubo et al. 2010; Utsumi et al. 2011). The ability of the ISA1 and ISA2 combination to stimulate IPG accumulation was recapitulated for the Arabidopsis genes in metabolically engineered yeast strains (Pfister et al. 2016). Here, the activities of maize ISA1 and Arabidopsis ISA were compared using the same strategy. 

Previous applications of the synthetic biology system generated Strains 28 and 29 that express a suite of Arabidopsis enzymes including SBE2, SBE3, SS1, SS2, SS3, and SS4 (Pfister et al. 2016). Strain 29 also expresses Arabidopsis 

ISA1 and ISA2, whereas Strain 28 is devoid of any debranching enzyme. The TU encoding maize ISA1 was integrated into the genome of Strain 28 to form Strain 503. The 3 strains were precultured in rich glucose medium (YPD), transferred to rich galactose (YPGal) medium at approximately $0 . 3 \mathsf { A } _ { 6 0 0 } /$ mL to induce transgene expression, and incubated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 5.75 h. Preliminary growth curves indicated that culture density increased 2- to 3-fold during this period (Supplemental Fig. S2), indicating continued cell growth and division. Carbohydrates in cell lysates were separated into (i) the water-insoluble IPG fraction, into which starchlike glucans would partition, (ii) the water-soluble, methanol-insoluble SPG fraction, into which glycogen- or phytoglycogen-like glucans would partition, and (iii) the water-soluble, methanol-soluble fraction containing maltooligosaccharides (MOS), i.e. glucose polymers with a low degree of polymerization (DP) (Supplemental Fig. S3). Glucan content in the IPG, SPG, and MOS fractions was then quantified relative to cell wet weight (Table 1). Total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan content in Strains 28, 29, and 503 was not significantly different. In Strain 28 that lacks ISA, only $10 \%$ of the total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan was in the IPG fraction, similar to previous results (Pfister et al. 2016). As seen in the previous study, expression of Arabidopsis ISA1 and ISA2 in Strain 29 resulted in significantly increased IPG content and significantly decreased SPG content, so that insoluble polymer increased to approximately $2 8 \%$ of the total. Strain 503 containing maize ISA1, but lacking ISA2, also exhibited significantly elevated IPG content compared to Strain 28, increasing insoluble polymer to approximately $2 2 \%$ of total α-glucan. This result demonstrates that the TU encoding maize ISA1 generates a functional enzyme in yeast cells. Further, maize ISA1 functioned to stimulate the formation of IPG, in cooperation with SSs and SBEs native to Arabidopsis, without the requirement for ISA2. Analogous results were observed when maize ISA1 was expressed in Arabidopsis leaves lacking the endogenous ISA1 and ISA2 proteins (Facon et al. 2013). 

The ${ \mathfrak { a } } ( 1 {  } 6 )$ -glucosidase activity of ISA is predicted to release certain ${ \mathfrak { a } } ( 1 {  } 4 )$ -linked linear chains from branched precursor glucan polymers during their biosynthesis, which potentially could accumulate as MOS in the methanolsoluble fraction of yeast cell lysates. In agreement with this expectation, MOS was detected in Strains 29 and 503 that contain ISA, whereas $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucans in this fraction were undetectable in Strain 328 that lacks such enzymatic activity (Table 1).

# Maize ISA1 stimulates IPG formation when combined with maize SBEs

Maize ISA1 function was examined in combination with each maize SBE individually and with all 3 SBEs in combination. These experiments again included AtSS3 in the reconstituted system to generate linear $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan precursors. When all 3 SBEs are present, in the absence of ISA1, the distribution between IPG and SPG is approximately equal (Fig. 1B, Strain 526). The addition of ISA1 resulted in significantly increased IPG content and significantly decreased SPG content, resulting in approximately $7 5 \%$ IPG (Fig. 1B, Strain 527). Thus, the ability of ISA1 to affect the partitioning of $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan into the insoluble phase, in the absence of ISA2, was again demonstrated. In this genetic background, the effect of ISA1 on ${ \mathfrak { a } }$ -glucan partitioning was quantitatively more pronounced than in strains containing SBE and SS both from Arabidopsis (Table 1). 

In contrast to the condition when all 3 SBEs are present, the inclusion of any individual SBE conditioned a preponderance of α-glucan in the insoluble phase even in the absence of ISA1, ranging from $76 \%$ to $9 3 \%$ IPG (Fig. 1B, Strains 530, 532, 

and 534). ISA1 in the presence of SBEIIa or SBEIIb caused a significant reduction in SPG content and thus shifted the distribution even further to the insoluble phase (Fig. 1B, Strains 533 and 535). This effect was not observed when ISA1 was combined with SBEI (Fig. 1B, Strain 531). 

MOS accumulation was quantified in strains containing maize SBEs (Supplemental Table S5). MOS was not detected in any strain lacking ISA1. Two strains expressing ISA1 accumulated MOS, specifically those expressing SBEI alone (Strain 531) or all 3 maize SBEs (Strain 527). MOS was not detected in the strains expressing ISA1 together with maize SBEIIa (Strain 532) or SBEIIb (Strain 534), nor in the ISA1- expressing strain lacking any SBE (Strain 536).

The activity of each maize SS was examined by iodine staining in strains containing all 3 maize SBEs or lacking any gene encoding that enzyme. In SBE-deficient strains, the inclusion of the TU encoding SSIIa or SSIIIa resulted in iodine staining of colonies grown on YPGal medium, although at a relatively low level (Fig. 2, Strains 613 and 559). # SSI/PHO1 interaction

As noted, neither SSI nor PHO1 alone supports $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan accumulation, whereas combining these 2 enzymes results in a synergistic effect as detected by iodine stain. Glucan quantification confirmed this result by detecting greater than 20-fold increased total glucan content in the SSI/PHO1 strain compared to that containing SSI alone (Figs. 2, 3A, Strains 585 vs. 599). Synergism was also observed when ISA1 was included in the strain background, in this instance approximately 5-fold elevation in total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan content when PHO1 was added (Figs. 2, 3A, Strains 586 vs. 600). Synergism was also observed between PHO1 and the combination of SSI and SSV. The strain containing those 2 SSs together with PHO1 accumulated approximately 8-fold more total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan than the comparable strain lacking PHO1 (Fig. 3F, Strains 634 vs. 631). When ISA1 was included in 

that strain background, the addition of PHO1 resulted in approximately 3-fold increase in total α-glucan (Fig. 3F, Strains 640 vs. 637). These results raise the possibility PHO1 provides glucan primers that stimulate the activity of SSI. Despite the increases, however, the total glucan generated by PHO1combined with SSI, or SSI plus SSV, remains 5- to 10-fold less than the maximal level detected in strains containing SSIIa or SSIIIa (Fig. 3).

# SSIIIa/PHO1 interaction

SSIIIa as the sole SS enzyme present generated substantial amounts of total glucan, predominantly in the soluble phase (Fig. 3C, Strain 589). As with SSIIa, PHO1 caused a significant reduction in total polymer accumulation (Fig. 3C, Strains 589 vs. 603). The magnitude of the PHO1 effect was less with SSIIIa than with SSIIa, amounting to $23 \%$ less total glucan. A comparable reduction in total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan also resulted from the inclusion of PHO1 with SSIIIa when ISA1 was present (Fig. 3C, Strains 590 vs. 604). 

# SSIV/PHO1 interaction

In the absence of other enzymes, total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan accumulation generated by SSIV together with the SBEs was approximately 3- to 4-fold less than that in strains utilizing SSIIa or SSIIIa (Fig. 3, B to D). Adding PHO1 to the SSIV strain resulted in a significant $2 2 \%$ reduction in total glucan accumulation (Fig. 3D, Strains 621 vs. 624), similar to the effect of adding PHO1 to SSIIIa. The same effect of PHO1 was observed when ISA1 was included in the strain background along with SSIV (Fig. 3D, Strains 677 vs. 680). 

# Interaction of PHO1 with suites of multiple SSs

A combination of 5 or 6 SSs in the same strain, in the absence of PHO1 or ISA1, resulted in approximately the same level of total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan observed in the comparable SSIIa strain (Fig. 3, B, G, and H). The inclusion of PHO1 in a background containing all 5 presumably soluble SSs (SSI, SSIIa, SSIIIa, SSIV, and SSV) did not alter the total α-glucan level (Fig. 3G, Strains 644 vs. 647). Slight changes were observed in both SPG and IPG content, but these compensated for each other so that the total amount was not significantly different. The same result was observed when ISA1 was included in the strain background, i.e. no significant difference in total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan accumulation in the presence or absence of PHO1 (Fig. 3G, Strains 650 vs. 653). The addition of PHO1 when all 6 SS genes were present, in the absence of ISA1, again resulted in a slight change in IPG and SPG content but did not significantly alter total glucan accumulation (Fig. 3H, Strains 699 vs. 701). Yet another instance of PHO1 failing to influence total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan level was detected when SSI and SSIV, in the absence of ISA1, were combined in the strain background (Fig. 3E, Strains 630 vs. 633). 

In summary, varying effects of PHO1 on α-glucan content were observed depending on the SS(s) responsible for glucan synthesis. The combination of PHO1 with SSI resulted in the stimulation of total α-glucan content, whereas PHO1 conditioned decreased content when present together with SSIIa, SSIIIa, or SSIV. In yet other instances, specifically when multiple SSs were combined in the strain background, the addition of PHO1 had no obvious effect on total glucan accumulation. 

# Distinct functional interactions of specific maize SSs with ISA1

The ability of ISA1 to condition redistribution from the SPG fraction into IPG was observed again in multiple instances involving maize SBEs and maize SSs. The SSIIa strain lacking PHO1 contained approximately $4 4 \%$ IPG (Fig. 3B, Strain 587). The addition of ISA1 to that genetic background did not significantly change total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan accumulation but caused a significant reduction in SPG content and significantly elevated IPG abundance, shifting the distribution to approximately $6 3 \%$ insoluble material (Fig. 3B, Strain 588). The effect on $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan partitioning was more pronounced in the strain background containing the 5 presumably soluble SSs. In the pair of strains lacking PHO1, the addition of ISA1 shifted the distribution from $2 8 \%$ IPG to $7 2 \%$ IPG (Fig. 3G, Strains 644 vs. 650). When PHO1 was included in this genetic background, the same effect was observed, with the distribution increasing from $1 6 \%$ IPG in the absence of ISA1 to $8 2 \%$ IPG when ISA1 was included (Fig. 3G, Strains 647 vs. 653). In both instances, there was no significant difference in the amount of total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan accumulated. The same result was observed qualitatively when all 6 SSs were present in the strain background. In the absence of PHO1, the distribution was increased from $34 \%$ IPG in the absence of ISA1 to $7 2 \%$ when ISA1 was present (Fig. 3H, Strains 699 vs. 700). When PHO1 was included, the addition of ISA1 increased the distribution from $5 2 \%$ IPG to $8 4 \%$ IPG (Fig. 3H, Strains 701 vs. 702). Again, the total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan accumulation did not vary significantly in the strains containing or lacking ISA1.

# Synergistic interactions of maize starch synthetic enzymes

Comparing the total α-glucan levels generated by individual SS isoforms to the amount of polymer accumulated when multiple SSs were present allowed the identification of synergistic effects. Including multiple SS isoforms in addition to SSIIa in the same strain, in genetic backgrounds lacking PHO1 and ISA1, failed to increase the total α-glucan level substantially above the amount generated by SSIIa acting alone (Fig. 3, B, G, and H, Strains 587, 644, and 699). In this instance, therefore, synergistic effects among SSIIa and other isoforms were not evident. In contrast, coupling SSI with other SSs in the same strain resulted in total α-glucan levels substantially greater than the sum of those SSs acting individually. SSI and SSIV as individuals produced the lowest amount of α-glucan among the isoforms that yielded positive iodine stains (Fig. 3, A to D). The total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan level in the strain containing both SSI and SSIV, in the genetic background lacking PHO1 and ISA1, was elevated 2-fold compared to the sum of the total levels produced by SSI or SSIV alone (Fig. 3, A, D, and E, Strains 585, 621, and 630; Table 2). Similar synergistic effects were observed in backgrounds containing PHO1 and/or ISA1, ranging from 2- to 6-fold increases compared to the sum of the α-glucan levels generated by the 2 SSs acting individually (Table 2). These results indicate that SSI activity is stimulated by the presence of SSIV, or vice versa. 

Synergistic action of SSI and SSV was also detected. Adding SSV to the strain containing SSI and PHO1 in addition to the 3 SBEs resulted in a statistically significant 1.6-fold increase in the SPG and total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan levels (Fig. 3, A and F, Strain 634 vs. 599) (P value $< { 1 0 } ^ { - 7 }$ ). However, the addition of SSV in other genetic backgrounds with respect to PHO1 and/or ISA1 did not elevate the total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan level produced by SSI acting alone.

# Structures of α-glucans produced in yeast in comparison to native polymers

Branched α-glucan polymer structures were characterized at the level of linear chain length frequencies. Polymers were converted to populations of ${ \mathfrak { a } } ( 1 {  } 4 )$ -linked linear chains by hydrolyzing branch linkages using a bacterial ${ \mathfrak { a } } ( 1 \to 6 )$ -specific glucosidase. These were separated by anion exchange chromatography at high pH based on the number of glucosyl units in each linear chain, i.e. DP. The apparent abundance of the fragment with each DP was quantified by pulsed amperometric detection and normalized to the total number of chains detected, yielding a linear chain length distribution (CLD) profile. CLD profiles were then compared between different polymer samples (Fig. 4).

# Ultrastructure of α-glucan particles

After demonstrating the activity of maize SSs, DBEs, ISA1, and PHO1 in the reconstituted system, all of the enzymes were expressed together to test whether they are capable of generating semi-crystalline particles resembling native starch granules. The analyses focused on Strain 702 that contains all 11 of the maize coding regions used in this study and Strain 699 that is identical except for the absence of ISA1 and PHO1 (Supplemental Table S4). Included for comparison was strain 29, which contains Arabidopsis SS, SBE, ISA1, and ISA2, and was analyzed similarly in a previous study (Pfister et al. 2016). The host genes GPH1 and GDB1 are both deleted in all 3 strains. Strains 699 and 702 accumulate about the same amount of α-glucan, but differ substantially in the proportions of IPG and SPG (Fig. 3H). 

The nature of the $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucans produced in Strains 699 and 702 was examined by light microscopy (LM) imaging of live cells stained with $\mathsf { I } _ { 2 } / \mathsf { K } \mathsf { I }$ (Fig. 6; Supplemental Fig. S5). As expected, an iodine stain was not detected in host Strain 504. Nearly every cell in YPGal cultures of Strains 699, 702, or 29 stained with iodine. In Strain 699, many mother cells were apparently filled with stainable α-glucan, and buds frequently exhibited irregularly stained areas in a background devoid of stain. Strain 702 cells also stained readily with $\mathsf { I } _ { 2 } /$ KI, although the color varied from that of Strain 699, consistent with the colony stain result (Fig. 6). $\mathfrak { a }$ -Glucans in Strain 702 were often concentrated in the cell interior, and material in the buds was diffuse as opposed to the irregular pattern in Strain 699. The staining patterns were generally similar to that of the previously analyzed Strain 29 (Pfister et al. 2016). 

# Particle shape and size

Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) revealed that IPG from Strains 699 and 702 both contained discrete particles that resemble starch granules in outward appearance and reproduced this result for Strain 29 (Pfister et al. 2016; Fig. 7). As expected, IPG was not recovered from host strain 504 which 

lacks any plant enzyme. The IPG particles made in yeast ranged in size from approximately 2 to $4 \mu \mathsf { m } _ { i }$ , which is smaller than maize starch granules but within the size distribution from plant species in general (Jane et al. 1994; Pérez and Bertoft 2010). The size and shape of these structures varied between strains, with spherical particles in Strain 702 and larger oblate spheroids in Strain 699 (Fig. 7). IPG particles generated by Arabidopsis enzymes in Strain 29 were generally smaller than those made by maize enzymes in Strain 702 or 699. 

Two types of particle appearance were observed, designated as smooth or rough (Fig. 7). Both particle types retained the general shape and size of the yeast cells in which they formed, including mother–bud pairs in some instances. Strains 699 and 702 both produced mixtures of rough and smooth IPG particles, whereas Strain 29 produced exclusively rough particles. High magnification images showed both rough and smooth particles to be composed of smaller subparticles (Fig. 7B). This suggests a continuum between rough and smooth IPG particles, rather than 2 distinct forms as if the smooth particles are packed more densely than the rough particles. Differences in overall appearance likely arise from variations in the concentration of subparticles within the cell. These hypotheses are supported by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) data that follow. 

Comparing IPG particle shape from Strain 699 to live cells suggests that $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan fills the entire cytoplasm, and all of this polymer assembles into a structure that remains intact through the extraction process. The spherical shape of particles from Strain 702 suggests these structures arise from glucans made in a subdomain of the cellular cytoplasm and is consistent with LM images of iodine-stained live cells (Fig. 6). The reason for the distinct IPG particle shapes must originate from the functions of ISA1 and/or PHO1, which are the only genetic variables between Strains 702 and 699.

# Crystallinity

Purified IPG particles from Strains 699 and 702 were tested for crystallinity by wide-angle X-ray scattering (WAXS). This method distinguishes between the A- and B-type allomorph crystal packing forms observed in native starch granules from various plant species (Imberty et al. 1991). The particles from either yeast strain yielded a diffraction pattern clearly indicative of semi-crystalline structure (Fig. 9). The crystal packing form, however, was in the B-type allomorph rather than the A-type allomorph typical of native maize starch granules. Semi-crystalline particles with B-type crystal packing were also observed previously from yeast cells expressing Arabidopsis starch biosynthetic enzymes (Strain 29; Pfister et al. 2016). 

Small-angle X-ray scattering (SAXS) tested for potential periodicity of ordered lamellar structures, as is typically observed with a 9 to 10 nm repeat distance in native starch from any botanical source (Jenkins et al. 1993). The SAXS diffraction pattern revealed a weakly organized repeating structure with a repeat distance of approximately $1 3 . 1 \ \mathsf { n m }$ in IPG particles from either Strain 699 or Strain 702 (Fig. 9). This result again closely resembled that seen previously in particles produced by Arabidopsis enzymes expressed in the yeast system (Strain 29; Pfister et al. 2016). 

# Discussion

# Granule formation in yeast cells

The synthetic biology system enabled high-resolution imaging of $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan particles as they form. The end product 

output of the maize enzyme system is IPG particles that resemble starch granules in overall appearance and size. Semi-crystallinity was demonstrated and lamellar repeats similar in nature to native starch were detected. Thus, the process by which branched $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan polymers, synthesized as hydrosoluble molecules, develop into semi-crystalline particles in yeast resembles native starch biosynthesis in fundamental aspects. 

TEM imaging of cross sections of yeast cells containing IPG revealed discrete $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan structures that range up to approximately $2 0 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ in diameter, referred to as $2 0 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ subparticles. In some instances, these are dispersed within the cytosol, whereas in others, they are tightly packed together. The mat appearance of IPG in cell sections apparently results from tight packing of the $2 0 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ subparticles, whereas in cells with particulate appearance, those subparticles remain as discrete units. Similarly, variation in the appearance of IPG particles, i.e. rough or smooth surfaces observed by SEM, likely is explained by the packing density of the $2 0 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ subparticles from which they form. Thus, it appears that the $2 0 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ subparticles coalesce to form full-sized IPG particles. 

Higher magnification TEM reveals a second hierarchical level of assembly. The $2 0 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ subparticles are not homogeneous in appearance, but instead appear to be composed of smaller units. Some cells displayed discrete structures of approximately $5 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ diameter that appear to coalesce with each other. Taken together, the TEM and SEM data suggest that the first order of α-glucan assembly creates $5 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ subparticles, then these join together to form the $2 0 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ structures, and finally, these assemble into the full-sized IPG particles that fill much of the cytosol and attain dimensions of several microns. 

The ultrastructure of the IPG particles made in yeast corresponds well with atomic force microscopy (AFM) imaging of the surface of native starch granules from wheat endosperm or potato tuber (Gallant et al. 1997). The $5 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ subparticles imaged in yeast cross sections by TEM, and on the surface of IPG particles by SEM, are consistent with the repeating structures observed on native starch granule surfaces by AFM that were referred to as “blocklets.” Hierarchical assembly of the $5 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ subparticles observed in yeast into $2 0 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ structures is also consistent with AFM and TEM analyses of native starch granules (Gallant et al. 1997). 

The question remains of how the $5 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ subparticles are formed. A likely explanation is the spontaneous crystallization of a branched $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan, as indicated by the crystalline repeat distance of approximately 13 nm revealed by SAXS. Several crystalline layers, in 3 dimensions, could make up the IPG concentrations that appear in cross sections as $5 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ subparticles. The frequency of the initial crystallization event that forms 50 nm subparticles may be a determining event in the distribution between IPG and SPG. 

Whether the honeycomb-shaped mesh background of electron-opaque fibers observed in Strains 702 and 699 plays a role in the formation of $5 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ subparticles or any other aspect of IPG particle formation remains to be determined. The 

α-glucans synthesized by the maize enzymes likely interact with the endomembrane system of the host cell. The background network of electron-opacity in which the $5 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ particles form resembles the reticulate network of the host strain presumed to be ER or Golgi membrane. A passive explanation may be that $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan simply fills in the soluble spaces between internal membranes. Alternatively, there may be an interaction between carbohydrate fibers and membrane lipids or endomembrane proteins. In any event, variation in the structure of the endomembrane system could affect particle assembly and eventually overall granule size and shape. Future studies involving synchronized yeast cell populations and temporal analysis of IPG accumulation may provide further insight into these questions. 

Polymer initiation was not well defined in this study, but in general was accomplished by a variety of SS isoforms. The reconstituted system as defined here did not incorporate any of the additional factors known to affect starch granule initiation in plant cells, which include PTST2, PTST3, MFP1, and MRC/PII1 (Seung and Smith 2019). The fully reconstituted system did include SSIV, known to control granule initiation (Roldán et al. 2007; Crumpton-Taylor et al. 2013; Hawkins et al. 2021), and SSIII, which in Arabidopsis stimulates granule initiation in the absence of SSIV (Szydlowski et al. 2009). However, SSIIa as the sole isoform generated as much total α-glucan as any other strain, so this isoform apparently has adequate initiation capability as well. Further experiments varying SS isoforms and adding accessory factors into the reconstituted system can test how specific granule initiation functions affect the IPG particle output. 

A final note regarding IPG particle synthesis in yeast is that semi-crystalline structures can form from polymers with linear CLDs far different from those in native amylopectin. Similar results were obtained previously using Arabidopsis enzymes in the yeast system (Pfister et al. 2016). 

# ISA1 function

The role of ISA1 to stimulate α-glucan crystallization was amply demonstrated in the reconstituted system. The addition of ISA1 in several instances conditioned a switch from approximately $2 5 \%$ IPG to approximately $7 5 \%$ IPG. This included strains with multiple SS isoforms, as well as a strain containing exclusively SSIIa (Fig. 2, B, G, and H). Maize ISA1 function does not require the presence of ISA2, in contrast to the Arabidopsis enzyme. All of these observations correspond with genetic analyses of ISA1 and ISA2 knockout mutants in maize and Arabidopsis (Delatte et al. 2005; Wattebled et al. 2005; Kubo et al. 2010; Facon et al. 2013). 

The precise function of ISA that stimulates the formation of semi-crystalline $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan particles remains elusive. IPG particles clearly form in the absence of any ISA in the reconstituted system, and linear CLDs of the output IPG are not affected by the presence or absence of ISA1. There is substantially less IPG per cell mass, however, when ISA1 is absent, even though total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan levels are not reduced. Thus, ISA1 function affects the distribution between soluble- and 

insoluble α-glucan in the heterologous yeast system, just as it does in planta. IPG particle morphology varies in the presence or absence of ISA1, specifically, the spherical shape when ISA1 is present and the oblate shape in its absence (Fig. 4). Whether this is due directly to ISA1 cannot be determined because PHO1 was also absent in the strain with the oblate-shaped IPG particles. Regardless, the enzyme composition clearly affects IPG particle shape and size. A possible explanation is that ISA1 stimulates the frequency of an early initiating event in the overall crystallization process. This event may occur spontaneously at a relatively low rate, explaining the formation of insoluble $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan in the absence of ISA activity both in the reconstituted system and in planta. The presence of ISA1 may increase the frequency with which the nucleating event occurs, and this in turn would affect the subsequent formation of IPG particles. 

The proposed nucleating event may relate to the formation of $5 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ subparticles. The density of such particles as they begin to coalesce (Fig. 8) could thus be affected by ISA1, and this in turn may be the determining factor for the overall distribution between semi-crystalline and hydrosoluble forms. This hypothesis is consistent with the diffuse appearance of $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucans detected by TEM only in Strain 699. Some of the cells of this genotype are apparently filled with the diffuse $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan form, and in high magnification images, these appear to be devoid of $5 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ subparticles. The diffuse $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan appearance is specific to the strain lacking ISA activity, thus suggesting a role of ISA in increasing the frequency at which $5 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ subparticles are formed. How such a stimulatory function might be accomplished remains unknown, considering that substantial changes in the CLD profile were not detected when ISA1 was omitted from the reconstituted system. One possibility is that ISA1 activity is necessary only in isolated locations in the overall 3-dimensional structure of a precursor polymer in order to stimulate crystallization. Another possibility is that structural changes not revealed by CLD data are mediated by ISA activity. 

ISA function in the reconstituted system is apparently not limited to stimulating the formation of IPG. In some genetic backgrounds including individual SS isoforms, the addition of ISA1 caused less total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan to accumulate. This was noted in strains containing only SSIIIa or only SSIV (Fig. 3, C and D). Reduced SPG content in those 2 instances accounts for the decrease in total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan. This effect was not observed when SSIIa was responsible for the synthesis of the ${ \mathfrak { a } } ( 1 \to 4 )$ -linked linear chains (Fig. 3B). Thus, it appears that ISA1 has the ability either to degrade SPG or to facilitate the conversion of that material into IPG, depending on which SSs have constructed the precursor polymer. A similar effect of ISA activity on SPG degradation was observed previously when the reconstituted system was applied to Arabidopsis starch biosynthetic enzymes (Pfister et al. 2016). 

# Synergistic interactions and SSV function

SSI and SSV were shown to be synergistic regarding total $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan produced in the reconstituted system. Maize SSV 

was found via genome gazing (Liu et al. 2015) and named as SS based on sequence similarity to SSIV. Multiple sequence alignments including all known SS isoforms show that highly conserved sequence motifs typical of this enzyme family are absent from the SSV group (Helle et al. 2018; Abt et al. 2020), thus defining SSV as a noncatalytic putative paralog. Despite the lack of catalytic activity, the SSV isoform is conserved in plant species. SS5 from Arabidopsis was shown to be a noncanonical isoform lacking glucosyltransferase activity (Abt et al. 2020). SS5 mutants in Arabidopsis have fewer starch granules than normal; thus, a role in initiation was proposed. The current study confirmed that SSV by itself does not support $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan accumulation (Fig. 1B), yet the addition of SSV to a strain background containing SSI and PHO1 resulted in the significantly increased total polymer. Hence, like Arabidopsis SS5, the maize ortholog appears to lack enzyme activity but may still be important in glucan biosynthesis, potentially in granule initiation. 

SSI also exhibited synergism with SSIV or PHO1, again regarding total α-glucan accumulated. These observations likely are explained by the requirement of SSI for a maltooligosaccharide primer that potentially can be provided by PHO1 or SSIV. Providing a primer is unlikely to explain the stimulation of SSI action by SSV because, as noted, the latter protein lacks enzymatic activity. 

# Variable effects of PHO1

The role of the plastid localized starch phosphorylase in starch metabolism has long been a topic of debate, as summarized in a recent review (Hwang et al. 2020). Relative amounts of glucose-1-phosphate and phosphate in cells favor a degradative role of PHO; however, loss of this enzyme in colder conditions reduced starch content in rice endosperm (Satoh et al. 2008). In maize endosperm, PHO activity peaks developmentally coincident with the time of rapid starch accumulation (Tsai et al. 1970). Data reported here provide direct evidence for PHO functioning in both α-glucan synthesis and degradation. When $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucans are synthesized by SSI, the presence of PHO1 enhances SPG and IPG content 25-fold each. In contrast, the presence of PHO1 decreases the abundance of both SPG and IPG when glucans are synthesized by SSIIa. SPG is particularly sensitive to the presence of PHO1; reductions of at least $60 \%$ occur when SSIIa or the complete suite of 6 SSs produce the $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucans. The presence of ISA1 does not influence this reduction in SPG content conditioned by the presence of PHO. In yet other instances, for example, when glucans are synthesized by SSI and SSIV or by all 5 soluble SSs, PHO1 does not affect glucan accumulation. These results may underlie some of the conflicting results concerning the role of starch phosphorylase extant in the literature. The fact that the absence of PHO produces mutant seed when rice plants are grown at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ but not at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ (Satoh et al. 2008) highlights the complexity of the role of PHO in cereal seed starch biosynthesis. 

# Models for the role of individual SSs in determining amylopectin structure

A prevailing general model for the roles of the multiple conserved SS isoforms has suggested that the various enzymes are each responsible for producing a given range of linear chain lengths (James and Myers 2011; Fujita and Nakamura 2012; Nakamura 2015; Crofts et al. 2017). These models are based in part on the effects of mutations that eliminate individual isoforms. For example, in multiple species genetically eliminating either SSI or SSII results in decreased abundance of specific ranges of chain lengths in the mutant amylopectins. Certain in vitro data are consistent with this hypothesis, including a study showing the activity of SSI diminishes when the substrate chain length being elongated approaches 20 glucosyl units (Commuri and Keeling 2001). To the contrary, however, analysis of recombinant isoforms from Arabidopsis found that SSI, SSII, SSIII, and SSIV acting in isolation could each synthesize chains extending to greater than 40 glucosyl units (Brust et al. 2014). The current study using yeast to isolate individual isoforms confirmed this result, showing that SSIIa and SSIIIa from maize could each generate linear chains of DP 40 or greater. Thus, distinct ranges of products generated by the activity of each conserved isoform cannot fully explain the final CLD that occurs in mature amylopectin in starch granules. Additional interactions in vivo must explain this structural feature of the mature storage α-glucan and the effects of genetically removing each SS isoform. 

# Conclusions

This study achieved expression in yeast of an $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan biosynthetic system including all the known SSs and SBEs of maize, together with functional ISA1 and PHO1. With the exception of GBSS, all the exogenous TUs were proven by phenotypic effects to generate active product proteins. Nonetheless, the resultant $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucans, either in the soluble or insoluble phase, were markedly different in linear CLD compared to native amylopectin from nonmutant maize endosperm or phytoglycogen from mutant maize lines defective in ISA function. Thus, some aspects of the native system were not reproduced in the in vivo reconstitution system. These factors may derive from additional proteins that are required, potential post-translational modifications, or protein–protein interactions that do not occur in yeast, the organellar context of the native tissue compared to the yeast cytosol, enzyme activity ratios at the level of individual isoforms (Pfister et al. 2022), etc. Additional application of the reconstitution approach will seek to fully reconstruct aspects of native starch granule biosynthesis. 

# Materials and methods

# Growth media

Growth media for yeast (S. cerevisiae) were as follows. Complete media contained $2 \%$ (w/v) Bacto Peptone, $1 \%$ 

$( \boldsymbol { \mathsf { w } } / \boldsymbol { \mathsf { v } } )$ Bacto Yeast Extract with either $2 \%$ (w/v) glucose (YPD) or $2 \%$ (w/v) galactose (YPGal). Glucose and galactose solutions were filter sterilized and added to the remaining media components after those had been autoclaved. Agar plates containing $2 \%$ Bacto Agar were prepared in the same way. Hygromycin was used at $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g / m l }$ for transformant selection and $2 0 0 \ : \mu \mathrm { g / m l }$ for strain maintenance. Minimal media contained yeast nitrogen base without amino acids (Difco no. 0919-15-3 $\left[ 6 . 7 \ g / \mathrm { L } \right] $ ). Synthetic dropout media supplements without uracil (Sigma no. Y1501) were added at $0 . 9 6 \ : \mathrm { g } / \mathrm { L }$ . 5-Fluoroorotic acid (5-FOA) was used at $1 ~ \mathrm { m g / m L }$ to counter-select URA3. Carbenicillin was used at $1 0 0 \ : \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ . G418 was used at $5 0 \ : \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ . 

# Expression of maize starch biosynthetic enzymes in S. cerevisiae

The yeast genome was engineered to express multiple maize (Z. mays L.) starch biosynthetic enzymes following a strategy previously described for Arabidopsis (A. thaliana) enzymes (Pfister et al. 2016, 2022). Recombinant plasmids for this purpose were generated using Golden Gate assembly and a modular cloning strategy implemented for yeast (Werner et al. 2012; Lee et al. 2015). A set of yeast integration vectors was constructed by BsaI-mediated assembly of component DNA fragments obtained from previously described plasmids or domesticated de novo by cloning between properly configured BsaI sites (Lee et al. 2015). These vectors contain target site sequences for homologous recombination into the host genome, a selectable marker for yeast, sequences for plasmid maintenance in E. coli, and a GFP dropout marker that is replaced during subsequent BsaI-mediated assembly (Supplemental Fig. S6 and Table S7). Maize TUs were generated by BsaI-mediated assembly of 3 domesticated fragments within one of the yeast integration vectors, thus replacing the GFP marker and linking a yeast promoter, a synthetic coding sequence, and a yeast terminator (Supplemental Fig. S1 and Table S2). The promoters employed were from the yeast genes CWP2 (gene identifier YBR020W) or GAL1 (gene identifier YKL096W-A). The CWP2 promoter is expressed during growth in either galactose- or glucose-containing media, whereas the GAL1 promoter is expressed only at low levels in glucose media and is strongly induced in galactose media (Keren et al. 2013). In galactose medium, the GAL1 promoter is expressed to a level approximately 2.4-fold higher than that of the CWP2 promoter (Keren et al. 2013). 

In some instances, maize TUs were integrated into the yeast genome as individual elements. In addition, arrays of up to 4 maize TUs were generated in a second-stage assembly into yeast integration vectors designed for this purpose. Single maize TUs were excised by cleavage with BsmBI from the individual plasmids in which they were created and then joined in tandem in a derivative plasmid by BsmBI-mediated assembly (Supplemental Table S3 and Fig. S7). When less than 4 TUs were included, spacer sequences were used to generate the $5 ^ { \prime }$ - and $3 ^ { \prime }$ - overhang sequences 

necessary for BsmBI-mediated assembly (Supplemental Table S7). 

Strain construction is detailed in Supplemental Table S4. Yeast host strain 504, which lacks a functional glycogen biosynthesis pathway, is described by Pfister et al. (2022). The host genes GPH1 encoding glycogen phosphorylase and GDB1 encoding glycogen debranching enzyme are intact and were used as target sites for replacement by exogenous TUs. Homologous integration also was targeted to chromosome XII site 5 (Mikkelsen et al. 2012). Selectable markers for yeast transformation included in the integration vectors were URA3 or the hygromycin resistance gene HygR (Goldstein and McCusker 1999). In some instances, the URA3 prototrophic marker was removed from the genome by homologous recombination at flanking direct repeats, which was selected by the resistance of uracil auxotrophs to 5-FOA (Alani et al. 1987). This enabled the use of the URA3 marker in a second sequential integration at another locus. The genomes of all strains were sequenced using an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 instrument to verify the integrity of all exogenous genes. Short-read sequences were filtered for quality and aligned both to the yeast genome and plasmid sequences. Integrity was verified by the presence of shortread sequences covering all the exogenous genes. 

# Glucan quantification

YPgal media was inoculated from YPD cultures grown overnight to reach a starting $\mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 0.275 to 0.325. Cultures were grown with shaking (240 rpm) for $5 . 7 5 \mathrm { h } $ , and then, cells were collected by centrifugation at $7 { , } 0 0 0 \times g$ for 3 min. Pellets were washed with 20 mL ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } ,$ , centrifuged, brought up in $1 \mathrm { \ m L \ H } _ { 2 } { \ O } ,$ and transferred to a preweighed $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ tube. Tubes were centrifuged, water removed, and the wet weight of the yeast pellet determined. 

Carbohydrates extracted from the cell pellets were separated into 3 components, namely (i) water-insoluble; (ii) watersoluble, MeOH-insoluble; and (iii) water-soluble, MeOH-soluble (Supplemental Fig. S3). The water-insoluble fraction (IPG) potentially contains glucans that have crystallized within the cell, analogous to starch granules in chloroplasts. The watersoluble, MeOH-insoluble fraction (SPG) contains soluble glucan polymers, potentially analogous to plant phytoglycogen. The water-soluble, MeOH-soluble fraction contains short-chain maltooligosaccharides (MOS) and free glucose. 

Carbohydrate extraction and quantification were performed according to Pfister et al. (2016) with the following modifications. Yeast cell pellets from $4 2 - \mathsf { m l }$ cultures were brought up into $3 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $1 . 2 ~ m$ perchloric acid (PCA), and approximately $2 0 0 \mu \ g$ of $4 0 0 \mu \mathrm m$ silica beads were added to the suspension. Cell disruption was accomplished after 10 min at high speed in a 1,600 Mini G homogenizer. Greater than $9 8 \%$ of the yeast cells were broken as visualized by LM. Lysates were centrifuged at $5 { , } 0 0 0 \times g$ for 5 min. The pellet from this separation constituted IPG, the insoluble glucans. This material was washed with 1 mL ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O }$ and then dissolved by boiling for 30 min in 300 μL ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O }$ . Glucan polymers 

were enzymatically converted to free glucose as follows. After centrifugation to remove undissolved residue, 8 μL of the supernatant was combined with $3 9 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of sodium acetate buffer (100 mM NaOAc, pH 5, 5 mM CaCl2) containing 1 U of thermostable α-amylase. Samples were incubated in a boiling water bath for 15 min and then cooled to room temperature. Amyloglucosidase (AMG) was added (3.3 U, Megazyme), and the sample was incubated for $^ { 1 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ at $5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . After centrifugation to remove remnant undissolved residue, free glucose in the supernatant was quantified using the glucose oxidase method (Megazyme K-TSTA). 

To prepare the SPG and MOS fractions, the supernatant from the PCA extraction was neutralized with 175 μL of neutralization buffer (2 M KOH, 0.4 M 2-(N-morpholino)ethanesulfonic acid, and $0 . 4 \mathrm { ~ } M \mathrm { ~ } \mathsf { K C I }$ ) and centrifuged at maximum speed in a microfuge for 5 min to remove salts. The pH of the supernatant was checked to ensure it was between 6 and 7. The supernatant was adjusted to $80 \%$ (v/v) MeOH and stored at $- 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ overnight and then centrifuged at maximum speed in a microfuge for 5 min. The pellet from this separation constituted SPG, while the supernatant constituted the MOS fraction monosaccharides and maltooligosaccharides. The SPG pellet was dissolved by boiling for 30 min in 200 μL ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O }$ . After centrifugation to remove undissolved residue, 8 μL of the supernatant was mixed with $3 9 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of sodium acetate buffer containing $_ { 0 . 6 \ : \mathrm { U } }$ of thermostable $\mathfrak { a }$ -amylase. The sample was incubated at $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 15 min and then cooled prior to the addition of 3.3 U AMG and incubation for $^ { 1 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ at $5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Free glucose was then quantified by the glucose oxidase method. The soluble fraction from the methanol precipitation was evaporated for 4 h in a speedvac to yield the MOS fraction. The dried material was dissolved by boiling for 15 min in 200 μL ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O }$ . After brief centrifugation to remove undissolved residue, 3.3 U AMG was added, and the sample was incubated at $5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 1 h. Free glucose was then quantified by the glucose oxidase method. 

# Linear CLD in branched glucan polymers

Samples of the final preparations described in the previous section containing 200 to $1 { , } 0 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g }$ glucose equivalents were incubated overnight at $5 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in a final volume of $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of 50 mM NaOAc, pH 4.8, containing 0.4 U Pseudomonas isoamylase (Megazyme catalog no. E-ISAMY). This activity converts α- $( 1  6 )$ -branched polymers to populations of $\mathfrak { a } \mathfrak { - } \bigl ( 1 \to 4 \bigr )$ -linked linear chains. Samples were then boiled for 5 min, centrifuged briefly to remove undissolved residue, and passed through a $0 . 2 2 \ : \mu \mathrm { m }$ syringe filter. Linear glucans were separated by high-pressure anion exchange chromatography and quantified by pulsed amperometric detection. Separation was on a Dionex CarboPac PA-100 4 × $2 5 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ column with a CarboPac PA-100 4 × 50 mm guard column at a flow rate of $1 \mathrm { m L / m i n }$ . Elution was in $_ { 0 . 1 ~ M }$ NaOH for 4 min followed by a 40 min gradient of 0 to $0 . 4 ~ M$ sodium acetate in 0.1 M NaOH. Peak areas for each DP were normalized to the total peak area, and the normalized values for each chain length were compared between 

samples. Control samples were treated identically with the exception that isoamylase treatment was omitted. 

# Absorption spectra of purified glucans complexed with iodine

Iodine absorption spectra of the soluble (Component 2) and insoluble (Component 1) glucan polymer fractions were obtained using a scanning plate reader. One volume of insoluble $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan adjusted to $1 . 4 ~ \mathrm { m g / m L }$ or soluble $\mathfrak { a }$ -glucan adjusted to $2 . 8 ~ \mathrm { m g / m L }$ was mixed with 7 volumes of iodine solution $( 2 \mathrm { \Delta } \mathrm { M } \mathsf { C a C l } _ { 2 } , 3 . 1 \mathrm { \Delta } \mathsf { m } \mathrm { m } \mathsf { K l } , 0 . 2 \mathrm { \ m } \mathsf { M } \mathsf { l } _ { 2 } )$ ) in a 96-well microtiter plate. Absorption spectra were recorded from 450 to $7 0 0 \ \mathsf { n m }$ in 1 nm increments.

Three microliters of an overnight yeast culture grown in YPD was spotted on a synthetic complete plate containing $2 \%$ (w/ v) galactose. Plates were incubated overnight at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . Staining was performed by exposing plates to vapor from solid iodine crystals for 1 min. 

# X-ray scattering

IPG from yeasts grown for α-glucan quantification were purified as described previously (Pfister et al. 2016). Normal Arabidopsis starch is leaf starch purified from wild-type (Col-0) A. thaliana (Liu et al. 2023). Arabidopsis amylopectinfree starch is leaf starch purified from A. thaliana gbss mutants (Seung et al. 2015). Normal maize starch was obtained from the Total Starch Assay Kit (AA/AMG) from Megazyme (Product code: K-TSTA-100A). Maize amylopectin was purchased from Sigma (Product code: 10120). For SAXS measurements, purified glucans and starches were kept as suspensions in water. SAXS data acquisition and analysis were conducted as described previously (Liu et al. 2023). 

For WAXS measurements, purified glucans were first dried and then rehydrated to obtain a defined degree of hydration. Therefore, the glucans in water suspensions were once washed with $80 \%$ (v/v) ethanol, partially dried at 200 mbar for $^ { 2 \mathsection }$ , ground to a fine powder, and dried at 200 mbar overnight. The glucans were then rehydrated for 10 days in a desiccator chamber containing an open container of saturated ${ \sf K } _ { 2 } { \sf S } { \sf O } _ { 4 }$ solution (providing a relative humidity of $9 7 \%$ at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } )$ . Rehydrated starch powder was transferred into quartz glass mark tubes (Hampton Research) with an outer diameter of 1 mm and a wall thickness of $0 . 0 1 \ \mathrm { m m }$ and sealed with Duco cement. The capillaries were mounted into a laboratory SAXS system (Xenocs Xeuss 3.0) and the scattering signal was recorded under vacuum using $\mathsf { C u K a X }$ -ray radiation and a 2D detector (Dectris EIGER2 1 M) positioned $1 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ away from the sample, with the distance calibration being performed using a silver behenate standard, the virtual detector set to the E1M-x3-3-1 setting and the line eraser function turned on. Six images per specimen were acquired, each with an exposure time of 1,000 s and a shifted detector position, to collect the scattering signal up to $2 7 \ : \mathrm { n m } ^ { - 1 }$ . The 1D signal (intensity as a 

function of the scattering vector q) was calculated by azimuthally integrating the recorded 2D signal. To obtain the signal from the starch powder alone, the background scattering from an empty capillary was scaled and subtracted from the recorded signals. Data were analyzed using Xenocs XSACT software and repeat distances $( d )$ were calculated from the scattering vectors using the formula $d = ( 2 \times \pi ) / q$ . 

# Scanning and TEM and LM

For TEM imaging of yeast cells, yeasts grown for glucan quantification were embedded and imaged as described previously (Liu et al. 2023). For SEM imaging, IPG from yeasts grown for glucan quantification were purified as described previously (Pfister et al. 2016) and stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ until use. Silicon wafers were cut into small chips, labeled, cleaned, glow discharged, and coated with Poly-L-Lysine. The insoluble glucans were allowed to attach for 15 min on individual chips, before unattached particles were removed by dipping the chips into distilled water. The chips were air-dried, mounted to SEM stubs with silver paint, and sputter coated with a $5 \mathsf { n m }$ layer of platinum/palladium. Samples were imaged top-down and tilted at $2 \kappa \nu$ in a TFS Magellan 400 SEM by secondary electron detection. The method for LM imaging of live yeast cells stained with $\mathsf { I } _ { 2 } / \mathsf { K I }$ solution was described previously (Pfister et al. 2016).
