# Summary

Pomegranate is an important perennial fruit tree distributed worldwide. Reference genomes with gaps and limit gene identification controlling important agronomic traits hinder its functional genomics and genetic improvements. Here, we reported a telomere-to-telomere (T2T) gap-free genome assembly of the distinctive cultivar ‘Moshiliu’. The Moshiliu reference genome was assembled into eight chromosomes without gaps, totalling ${ \sim } 3 6 6 . 7 1$ Mb, with 32 158 predicted protein-coding genes. All 16 telomeres and eight centromeres were characterized; combined with FISH analysis, we revealed the atypical telomere units in pomegranate as TTTTAGGG. Furthermore, a total of 16 loci associated with 15 important agronomic traits were identified based on GWAS of 146 accessions. Gene editing and biochemical experiments demonstrated that a 37.2-Kb unique chromosome translocation disrupting the coding domain sequence of PgANS was responsible for anthocyanin-less, knockout of PgANS in pomegranate exhibited a defect in anthocyanin production; a unique repeat expansion in the promoter of PgANR may affected its expression, resulting in black peel; notably, the $\mathsf { G } \to \mathsf { A }$ transversion located at the 166-bp coding domain of PgNST3, which caused a E56K mutation in the PgNST3 protein, closely linked with soft-seed trait. Overexpression of $P g N S T 3 ^ { A }$ in tomato presented smaller and softer seed coats. The E56K mutation in PgNST3 protein, eliminated the binding ability of PgNST3 to the PgMYB46 promoter, which subsequently affected the thickness of the inner seed coat of soft-seeded pomegranates. Collectively, the validated gap-free genome, the identified genes controlling important traits and the CRISPR-Cas9-mediated gene knockout system all provided invaluable resources for pomegranate precise breeding. 

# Introduction

Most perennial crops have a long juvenile phase that prolongs the genetic improvement process and increases input costs. The use of a molecular breeding system that incorporates marker-assisted selection (MAS) and gene-editing technologies (GETs) has emerged as a crucial strategy for increasing breeding efficiency (Li et al., 2024a). Owing to recent advances in sequencing and GETs, complete genome information (Han et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024b), gene discoveries of important agronomic traits (Chen et al., 2023b; Guo et al., 2020) and the application of GETs (Huang et al., 2023; Rocafort et al., 2022; 

Xing et al., 2020) have been reported for many perennial crop species; these advances accelerated the genetic innovation of important agronomic traits for using a molecular breeding system. 

Pomegranate (Punica granatum L.) is native to Iran and its neighbouring regions (Holland et al., 2009) and is currently cultivated globally. Its nutritional, medicinal, economic and ornamental values have attracted worldwide interest, and it is known as a super-food (Sharma et al., 2017). Moreover, owing to its small genome size, multi-seeded nature and relatively short juvenile period, pomegranate is a potential model plant for perennial fruit plants. Up to now, there is no a T2T genome that 

has been assembled, although four pomegranate genomes have been reported (Luo et al., 2020; Qin et al., 2017; Roopa et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2018). One of the best of these genomes, which serves as a long-range contiguity chromosome-scale genome, still has 188 gaps and lacks complete centromere and telomere structures. Additionally, only a limited number of genes associated with agronomic traits have been identified (Harel-Beja et al., 2015; Trainin et al., 2021), and the application of GET in pomegranate has not yet been reported. 

Fruit colour and seed hardness are important pomegranate fruit quality traits. Fruit colour in the genus Punica accesses widely from white fruits to black (purple). White pomegranates show anthocyanin-less accumulation in all plant tissues, including the leaves, flowers and fruit (both skin and arils) (Ben-Simhon et al., 2015). In contrast, black pomegranates contain exceptionally high levels of anthocyanins in leaves, flowers and fruit skin (Trainin et al., 2021). Gaining insight into the genetic factors affecting natural variation in skin and flesh fruit colour is crucial for the efficient modification of fruit colour. The expression of genes related to anthocyanin synthesis participates in pomegranate fruit colour (Zhao et al., 2015), while the major determining genomic variations are still indefinable. Pomegranate seeds, which include the outer seed coat (aril), inner seed coat and embryo, are edible and rich in sugars, organic acids, anthocyanins, phenolic acids and ellagic acid (Tezcan et al., 2009). The texture of the inner seed coat, whether hard or soft, is the main factor affecting fruit edibility, with consumers generally preferring soft-seeded varieties. Cell wall thickness and rigidity of the inner seed coat were the main factors determining seed hardness. Some genes related to lignin monomer synthesis (Qin et al., 2020), cellulose synthesis (Zarei et al., 2016), sucrose transport (Luo et al., 2020) and ABC transporters (Yu et al., 2022) were identified by gene expression analysis among soft and hard pomegranate seed different development stages, while the genetic mechanism of pomegranate seed hardness is still underexplored. 

In this study, we assembled the first T2T genome of the distinctive cultivar pomegranate ‘Moshiliu’ via a combination of Oxford Nanopore Technology (ONT), PacBio HiFi reads and Hi-C technology, along with a GWAS of 146 pomegranate accessions to identify several genomic loci associated with 15 important agronomic traits. Furthermore, three genes related to seed hardness and anthocyanin synthesis were cloned, and their functions were validated via using of an overexpression and gene-editing system. The genetic markers associated with important agronomic traits were also mined for seedling early prediction. Our findings provide valuable genetic resources for pomegranate molecular breeding and enhance the understanding of peel colour variation and secondary cell wall thickening in seed coats. 

# Results

# Genome assembly, validation and annotation

The distinctive pomegranate cultivar ‘Moshiliu’, which is characterized by a black peel, was selected for telomere-to-telomere (T2T) reference genome assembly (Figure S1a). Karyotype analysis indicated that the chromosome number was $2 n = 2 x = 1 6$ (Figure S1b). 

To increase the quality of the genome assembly, 37.51 Gb $( \sim 1 0 1 ~ \times$ coverage) of HiFi reads, 21.9 Gb ${ \mathrm { \Omega } } ^ { \prime } { \sim } 6 0 \ \times$ coverage) of ONT ultralong reads, and 37.96 Gb $( \sim 1 0 3 ~ \times$ coverage) of Hi-C 

clean reads with $7 9 . 6 1 \%$ valid interaction pairs (Table S1) were generated. The N50 length of the HiFi reads and ONT reads was $1 6 . 7 2 \ \mathrm { k b }$ and $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { k b } _ { \iota }$ , respectively (Table S2). Owing to the accuracy of HiFi long reads, the genome of Moshiliu was assembled with a contig N50 of 46.25 Mb, which was higher than those of previously reported assemblies (Table 1). A total of $9 8 . 5 3 \%$ (366.69 Mb) of the total assembly was anchored onto eight chromosomes via Hi-C cross-linking (Table S3; Figure S2), while there were still two gaps (Table S3). NextDenovo was used to assemble the ONT data; the assembled ONT genome was used to fill the gaps. Finally, a gap-free reference genome named Moshiliu was generated, containing eight chromosomes with a total length of 366 710 298 bp (Figure 1a, Table 1). A total of 16 telomere regions and 8 centromere regions were also detected, respectively (Figure 1a). 

We performed extensive validations of the gap-free genome. First, we evaluated the reliability of the Moshiliu sequences corresponding to the 188 gaps in the Tunisia genome. A total of 376 pairs of primers (Table S4) were designed to target the both $5 ^ { \prime }$ and $3 ^ { \prime }$ sides of the closed gaps for PCR amplification, of which 308 boundary sequences were successfully amplified (Figure S3), and 40 boundary sequences were further confirmed through Sanger sequencing, which validated its accuracy assembly and eliminated all the gaps. In addition, we employed the Core Eukaryotic Genes Mapping Approach (CEGMA) to evaluate the completeness of the genome, with $100 \%$ of the 458 core eukaryotic genes (CEGs) and $9 7 . 5 8 \%$ of the 248 highly conserved CEGs detected in the assembly. By using Embryophyta databases, we achieved a BUSCO score of $9 8 . 6 4 \%$ , surpassing the $9 3 . 3 3 \%$ score for Tunisia and the $9 4 . 3 \%$ score for Taishanhong (Table 1). High mapping rates were observed for both Illumina short reads $( 9 8 . 0 1 \% )$ and HiFi reads $( 9 9 . 8 6 \% )$ ), respectively (Figure S4). These results collectively confirmed the high quality of the ‘Moshiliu’ genome assembly. 

A total of 32 158 protein-coding genes were predicted in ‘Moshiliu’ (Table 1), among which 31 895 $( 9 9 . 1 8 \% )$ ) (Figure S5, Table S5) were functionally annotated (Table 1). # Genome structure of assembled telomeres, rDNA and satellite arrays

Notably, all 16 telomere regions in pomegranate presented the atypical TTTTAGGG motif (Figure 1a) and a T insertion existed compared with other plants that have the conserved TTTAGGG motifs. Fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) analysis using (TTTTAGGG) probes also confirmed the distribution of telomeric 

repeats (TTTTAGGG)n (Figure 1b). The average length of the 16 telomeres was 5.1 kb (Figure 1c), and the average number of telomeric repeat copies was 582 (Figure 1d). To investigate whether other Lythraceae species also presented unusual telomere motifs, we checked the telomere motifs of Sonneratia alba, Lagerstroemia speciosa and Ammannia multiflora, all of which presented the unique TTTTTTAGGG motif (Figure 1e). In contrast, other Myrtales species presented the typical telomere sequence TTTAGGG (Figure 1e), suggesting that these unusual telomere motifs may have significantly impacted the evolution of Lythraceae species. 

Ribosomal DNAs (rDNAs), which encode the RNA components of ribosomes (rRNAs), are highly repetitive across all eukaryotic genomes, and the number of rDNA loci is dynamic in different species (Nelson et al., 2019). In pomegranate, the 5S rRNA is generated mainly via the transcription of 5S rDNA (Figure 2a), and the 18S, 5.8S and 28S rRNAs result mainly from the splicing of one 45S transcript (Figure 2b). Consistent with the FISH results (Figure S7), we identified a predominant 2.65-Mb array of 45S rDNA and a $6 2 5 . 6 \mathrm { - } \mathrm { k b }$ array of 5S rDNA located on acrocentric Chromosomes 1 (Figure 2c) and 2 (Figure 2d), respectively. The clusters of 45S rDNA and 5S rDNA encompass approximately 835 and 2006 gene units, respectively (Figure 2a–d). The 45S rDNA array contained 195.8-kb TEs; the 5S rDNA array harboured 14.4-kb TEs (Figure 2d). 

Approximately $3 3 \%$ of the satellite repeats located in centromeric regions, with no apparent correlation between chromosome length and centromere positioning (Figure 2e, Table S9). Two satellite repeats (CL2 and CL6) were predominantly located in centromeric regions, accounting for $7 0 . 7 \%$ of these sequences (Figure 2e). Centromeres can be categorized into three structural types on the proportions of CL2 and CL6 satellite repeats. For Chromosomes 1, 2, 5, 6 and 8, nearly all centromeric regions were covered by CL2 (proportion of $5 1 . 1 \% -$ $9 9 . 3 \%$ ) and CL6 (proportion of $2 1 . 9 \% - 9 9 . 8 \%$ ) repeats (Figure 2e). In contrast, Chromosome 7 exhibited complete coverage $( 1 0 0 \% )$ by CL7, and Chromosome 4 was composed of CL16 (Figure 2e). 

# Comparative genomic analysis of pomegranate and other plant species

Whole-genome duplication (WGD) events have been pivotal in the evolution of angiosperms (Van de Peer et al., 2017). The Lythraceae species S. alba and L. speciosa have been reported as model species for understanding the polyploidization– rediploidization process (Feng et al., 2024). Estimated divergence analyses suggested that the common ancestor of Lythraceae species (P. granatum, S. alba and L. speciosa) diverged from the ancestor of Melastomataceae (M. candidum) and Myrtaceae (E. grandis and P. guajava) species ${ \sim } 8 3 . 7 8$ Mya (Figure 2f). The WGD event in E. grandis occurred before the divergence time of 

P. granatum and E. grandis (Figure 2h). The whole-genome triplication (WGT) event in L. speciosa (Ks value of 0.42) occurred slightly after it diverged from P. granatum (Ks value of 0.45) (Figure 2g,h). However, no obvious WGD event was observed in P. granatum, which was further supported by 4DTv analysis (Figure S8) and collinearity assessments (Figure S9). Additionally, the distributions of the insertion times of long terminal repeat retrotransposons (LTR-RTs), which play a role in plant evolution (Kijima and Hideki, 2010), also corroborate the obtained divergence timeline of P. granatum (Figure 2i). 

# A variation map of 146 extensive pomegranate accessions

We generated the genome resequencing data for 142 pomegranate landraces and improved cultivars, which were utilized for GWAS (Table S10). The data for another four cultivars (Bhagwa, PGM and Taishanhong) were obtained from NCBI. After removing adaptors and low-quality sequences, we generated a total of 1.79 Tb of clean data, achieving an average coverage depth of $3 3 \times$ for each sample (Table S10). Mapping to the Moshiliu reference genome revealed 1.15 million single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), with an average mapping rate of $9 8 . 5 \%$ (Table S10). 

By using high-quality SNPs with a missing rate of no more than $10 \%$ and a minor allele frequency $( \mathsf { M A F } ) \geq 0 . 0 1$ , we inferred the phylogenetic relationships among the 146 pomegranate accessions, identifying six major clusters. Group I included accessions mainly from Israel, Italy, the United States and Turkmenistan; Group II included accessions from China; Group III included accessions from India, Saudi Arabia and China; Groups IV, V and VI mainly included accessions from China and the USA (Figure 3a). These findings were largely corroborated by population structure analysis and principal component analysis (PCA) (Figure 3b,c).

# A unique chromosome translocation disrupting PgANSexpression leads to anthocyanin defects in pomegranate

In contrast, black pomegranates contain exceptionally high levels of anthocyanins (Figure 4a). # Discussion

# T2T genome assembly and GWAS identified 16 loci associated with 15 important agronomic traits

A high-quality genome is an essential tool for functional genomic and phylogenetic analysis. This is, to the best of our knowledge, 

the first T2T gap-free genome assembly in pomegranate. The T2T genome assembly in this study filled all the gaps in previous assemblies, completed the assembly of centromeres and telomeres and reported higher percentages and numbers of annotated genes, which allow us to gain comprehensive insight into complex genome regions. Although numerous candidate genes associated with pomegranate fruit quality traits have been reported, including those linked to seed hardness (Luo et al., 2020; Qin et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2022; Zarei et al., 2016), fruit peel colour (Ben-Simhon et al., 2015; Trainin et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2015) and organic acid content (Feng et al., 2022), most studies focus solely on gene expression patterns. The causal genomic variations underlying these traits remain poorly investigated. Most of them were identified only at the expression level, with limited investigations into the underlying causal genome variations. GWAS was determined to be an efficient way to identify key genes and variant alleles associated with important traits in many perennial crop species, while its application in pomegranate remains limited. Based on T2T genome assembly and comprehensive GWAS of pomegranate important agronomic traits, we identified 16 loci associated with 15 important agronomic traits. Among these, 14 loci were newly identified, including those associated with seed hardness, peel colour, peel firmness, aril weight, flower type and acidity. 

# Atypical telomere motifs may impact the evolution of Lythraceae species

Telomeres play a critical role in whole-genome stability (Peska and Garcia, 2020; Van Steensel and de Lange, 1997). Even minor alterations in telomeric motifs can disrupt the binding of sequence-specific telomere proteins, thereby influencing evolutionary trajectories (Peska and Garcia, 2020). Typically, telomere repeat units are always conserved across diverse plant species, with the conserved plant telomere sequence being TTTAGGG (Richards and Ausubel, 1988). We found the atypical telomere units in pomegranate as TTTTAGGG; only four species, including the green algae Chlamydomonas reinhardtii (Petracek et al., 1990) and Yamagishiella unicocca (Lycka et al., 2024), the protist parasite Theileria annulata (Hall et al., 1990) and the clubroot pathogen Plasmodiophora Brassicae (Javed et al., 2024), were reported with these telomere units; pomegranate is the only angiosperm. We also found the atypical telomere sequence TTTTTTAGGG in other Lythraceae species, which further suggested the critical roles of telomeres in the evolution of Lythraceae species.

# The E56K mutation in  specifically affect seed Pghardness in pomegranate

Seed coats as by-products in seed and nut crops usually require waste disposal, which also affects the eating quality of some economic crop such as pomegranate, grape, watermelon, strawberry and passion fruit. The emergence of a natural variation in seed coat thickness exhibiting soft-seeded pomegranate allows us to eat directly without seed spitting, which promotes the eating quality and reduces nutrition waste. Soft-seeded pomegranate is a perfect material for studying secondary cell wall development in the seed coat. Here we found the thickness of the secondary cell wall in the SCL was the primary determinant of pomegranate seed hardness (Figure 5); the soft-seed trait in pomegranates may be controlled by a single recessive allele (Table S15). PgNST3 was identified as the candidate gene controlling seed hardness in pomegranate by multi-omics analysis. The nonsynonymous SNP (G-to-A) located at the 166- bp coding domain of PgNST3caused an E56K mutation in the PgNST3 protein, which is closely linked with the soft-seed trait. 

NST3, also named as SND1 and NAC012, is a member of the specific NAC transcription factor family. The NAC TFs were determined to be transcriptional activators for secondary cell wall thickening (Taylor-Teeples et al., 2015; Zhong et al., 2008). The nst1-3 mutant in Medicago truncatula showed no lignification in interfascicular fibres (Wang et al., 2011), while overexpression of NST3 in Arabidopsis dramatically suppressed secondary wall deposition in the xylary fibre (Ko et al., 2007). The expression of the NAC master switch itself is under both positive (autoregulatory) and negative control; the T94K mutation in NST1 and NST3 in Arabidopsis and Medicago truncatula broke the balance of the regulation of NST3 expression and led to their higher expression level, eliminating its trans-activation activity to downstream TFs, then resulting in no lignification (Wang et al., 2011). Intriguingly, in pomegranate, we found the E56K mutation in the PgNST3 protein existing in soft-seed accessions (Figure 5); the expression of PgNST3 was up-regulated in the inner seed coat of soft-seed pomegranate (Figure 5), and less lignification in the inner seed coat was observed in soft-seed pomegranate (Figure 5). Furthermore, which is similar to the T94K mutation in Arabidopsis, E56 of PgNST3 is conserved in NAC domain TFs (Figure S17), E56K mutation effected its binding ability to its own promoter (Figure S21) and the promoter of PgMYB46 (Figure 6). These results suggest that E56, as a conserved amino acid, is critical for NST3 function, E56K mutation in PgNST3 protein, which may be similar to the T94K mutation in Arabidopsis, may have broken the balance of the regulation of the expression of PgNST3, then resulting in its higher expression level in soft-seeded pomegranate. While the E56K mutation also 

eliminated the trans-activation activity of PgNST3 to the PgMYB46 promoter, which subsequently affected the thickness of the inner seed coat of soft-seeded pomegranates. 

# Experimental procedures

# Plant materials and phenotypes

‘Moshiliu’ and 142 pomegranate accessions that were used for the T2T genome assembly and whole-genome sequencing were cultivated at the National Horticulture Germplasm Resources Center of China (NHGRC). 

The phenotypic values of 15 agronomic traits, including seed hardness (SH), peel colour (PC), hundred-grain weight (HGW), aril colour (AC), petal colour, sepal colour (SC), total soluble solid (TSS), acid, calyx length (CL), peel firmness (PF), calyx state (CS), flower type (FT), stem thorn (ST), pericarp thickness (PT) and tender leaves colour (TLC), were measured in terms of the available descriptions and data standards for pomegranate (Cao and Hou, 2013). The SH, TSS, acid, HGW and PT phenotypes of 146 accessions were detected by at least 2 years of repetition. CL and PF were measured in 2022. 

# SH

More than 15 seeds from mature fruits were obtained from three trees as one biological sample, and three biological samples $( \ge 4 5$ seeds in total) were generated for detecting seed hardness via a TA-XT texture analyser (SMS, UK). A P2 probe (with a displacement of $1 . 3 { - } 2 . 0 ~ \mathrm { m m } )$ was selected, with a front and back speed of $5 \ : \mathrm { m m } { \cdot } 5 ^ { - 1 }$ and a measured middle speed of $1 \ : \mathrm { m m } { \cdot } 5 ^ { - 1 }$ . 

# PF

Peel firmness was also evaluated with a TA-XT texture analyser (SMS, UK), and four replicate measurements in different directions of the fruit were regarded as one biological sample. At least three biological samples were generated for peel firmness evaluation. 

# HGW

One hundred fresh seeds with integrated embryos, inner and outer seed coats were selected for weighing with an electronic balance (FA2204). 

# TSS and acid

TSS and acid were measured with a handheld refractometer (ATAGO, PAL-29S), and the acidity was measured for 50-fold dilutions of the pomegranate juice. 

# PC

The study included fruits with anthocyanin deficiency (white) and colour (yellow-red, green, pink, red, deep red and dark purple (black)). Anthocyanin deficiency was recorded as $' 0 ^ { \prime }$ , and other peel colours were recorded as $" 1 "$ in the GWAS of white peel colour. 

# TLC

The observed TLC included green, red and deep red. Green was recorded as $' 0 ^ { \prime }$ , and other colours of tender leaves were recorded as $" 1 "$ in the GWAS. 

# SC

The study included fruits with anthocyanin deficiency (light yellow) and coloured sepals (yellow-pink, orange-red and deep 

red) (Figure S11). Light yellow was recorded as $' 0 ^ { \prime }$ , and other sepal colours were recorded as ‘1’ in the GWAS. 

# Petal colour

The observed petal colours included white, pink, orange-red and variegated (Figure S11). 

# AC

The observed ACs included white, pink, red and deep red (Figure S11). White was recorded as $' 0 ^ { \prime }$ , and other colours were recorded as ‘1’ for the GWAS of aril colour. 

# FT

A single-flowering trait was recorded as $' 0 ^ { \prime }$ , and a doubleflowering trait was recorded as ‘1’ (Figure S11). 

# ST

A ‘yes’ was recorded as $' 0 ^ { \prime }$ , and a ‘no’ was recorded as ‘1’ (Figure S11). 

# CS

The observed CS included closed, semi-open and open (Figure S11). The closed accessions were recorded as $' 0 ^ { \prime }$ , and the semi-open and open accessions were recorded as ‘1’ in GWAS. 

# CL and PT

Calyx length and pericarp thickness were measured with a Vernier calliper. Three replicates in different directions were regarded as one biological sample, and at least three biological samples were generated for CL and PT. 

# FISH

The seeds of ‘Moshiliu’ were cultured in damp sterile gauze for 2 weeks to obtain the radicle, and the root tips were obtained when the radicle grew to $1 . 5 { - } 2 ~ \mathsf { c m }$ . The root tips were soaked in $0 . 0 0 2 ~ \mathrm { m o l / L }$ 8-hydroxyquinoline for $4 \textrm { h }$ to induce mitosis. The treated root tips were digested in $0 . 3 \%$ pectolyase (Yakult, Japan) and $3 \%$ cellulase (SCR, Shanghai, China) at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $3 . 5 ~ \mathsf { h }$ and then gently rinsed with Carnoy’s solution (Vacetic acid: $V _ { e t h a n o I } = 2 { : } 1 $ ) to collect the cells. Finally, the apical meristems were dispersed on glass slides and dried over the flame of an alcohol burner to observe the target cells in the mitosis metaphase (Dang et al., 2019). 40 , 6-Diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) was used for fluorescence staining (Kolano et al., 2013). Fluorescence in situ hybridization was used to confirm an accurate karyotype (Jiang, 2019). The telomere-specific probe $( \mathsf { T I T F A G G } ) _ { 4 }$ was used to confirm the intact chromosomes and unusual telomere motif of pomegranate; additionally, 18S rDNA and 5S rDNA repeat sequence probes were used to identify repeat arrays of chromosomes. The chromosomes were observed via high-resolution fluorescence microscopy (Olympus BX53) and a digital imaging system (Olympus DP70). 

# Genome sequencing

Tender fresh leaf tissue of ‘Moshiliu’ was collected from the apex of annual branches in 2022. High-molecular-weight genomic DNA was extracted via the nuclear method (Zhang et al., 2012). High-molecular-weight genomic DNA was purified by magnetic beads, after damage and end repair (NEB, M6630L and E7546L), sequencing adapters ligation (NEB, E6056S), ONT ultralong sequencing libraries were prepared with the ligation kit (SQK-

LSK110) following the default protocol. The purified library was loaded onto primed R9.4 Spot-On Flow Cells and sequenced using a nanopore promotion sequencer Nanopore PromethION. 

For PacBio HiFi sequencing, high-quality genomic DNA was prepared via the CTAB method. The DNA in the nuclei was digested with 200 U of MboI (NEB) and purified via the QIAamp DNA Mini Kit (Qiagen) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The purified DNA was utilized to construct a Hi-C library, which was prepared with the NEBNext- UltraTM II DNA library Prep Kit for Illumina (NEB) following a standard Hi-C protocol (Belton et al., 2012). Fragments ranging from 400 to 600 bp were paired-end sequenced on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform using the 150 PE mode. 

# Genome assembly

For the 37.51 Gb of PacBio HiFi data, hifiasm (v0.19.8-r603) (Cheng et al., 2021) was utilized for whole-genome assembly via default parameters. The 32.83 Gb of Hi-C sequencing data were mapped to the polished HiFi assembly genome using BWA (bwa-0.7.17) with default settings. Paired reads mapped to different contigs were used to anchor the HiFi assembly contigs via 3D-DNA (v201008) with default parameters (Dudchenko et al., 2017). JuicerBox (Durand et al., 2016) was employed to adjust the order and orientation of the clustered contigs. Nextdenovo was used for the assembly of ONT data with the parameters genome_size $= 3 6 0 ~ \mathsf { M }$ , read_cutoff = 50 000, seed_depth $= 3 0$ , sort_options $= - \mathsf { m }$ 20 g -t 10 (Hu et al., 2024). Gaps were filled using QuarTeT v1.2.0 (Lin et al., 2023) with the parameters $\therefore \pm 2 0$ --minimapoption’. 

# Genome quality assessment

The quality of the assembly was evaluated via CEGMA (Parra et al., 2007) and BUSCO (v5.2.1) using the embryophyta_odb10 database (Sim~ao et al., 2015). HiFi and Illumina data were also mapped to the assembled genome via bwa (Li and Durbin, 2009) and Minimap2 (v2.24) (Li, 2018), respectively, to evaluate the quality of the HiFi assembly. The primers used for PCR amplification to validate the quality of the genome assembly are listed in Table S4. Centromics and quarTeT were used for centromeric repeat identification. FindTelomeres was used for telomere motifs. 

# Repeat annotation and TE analysis

TEs and tandem repeats were annotated via a combination of the universal Repbase database and a de novo repeat library customized with RepeatModeler. The final TE elements were identified and classified using RepeatMasker using -nolow -e rmblast -pa 60 (v4.1.2) (Tarailo-Graovac and Chen, 2009). Long terminal repeat retrotransposons (LTR-RTs) were identified with LTR_Finder (v1.2) (Xu and Wang, 2007), LTRharvest (v1.6.2) (Ellinghaus et al., 2008) and LTR_retriever (v2.9.0) (Ou and Jiang, 2018). Tandem repeats were annotated using Tandem Repeats Finder (TRF 409) (Benson, 1999) and the microsatellite identification tool (MISA v2.1) with default parameters (Beier et al., 2017). 

# Annotation of protein-coding genes

Protein-coding genes were annotated via three approaches: (a) de novo prediction with Augustus (v3.1.0) (Stanke et al., 2006) and SNAP (2006-07-28) (Korf, 2004); (b) homology searching via GeMoMa (v1.7); and (c) transcript-based assembly, employing Hisat (v2.1.0) and Stringtie (v2.1.4) (Pertea et al., 2016) for RNA-

sequencing data mapping and assembly. GeneMarkS-T (v5.1) was used for gene prediction according to the assembled transcripts. PASA (v2.4.1) (Haas et al., 2008) software was used to predict genes from unigenes and full-length transcripts assembled via Trinity (v2.11) (Grabherr et al., 2011) with default parameters. Gene models from these approaches were combined using EVM software (v1.1.1) with weight ratio: abinitio prediction 0.3; protein GeMoMa 50; protein other 50; alignassembly-PASA 50; RNAseq 20. Gene functions were inferred based on alignments to the NCBI Nonredundant (NR), EggNOG, KOG, TrEMBL, InterPro and SwissProt protein databases via diamond BLASTP (diamond v0.9.29.130) and the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) database, with an E-value threshold of $1 \mathrm { e } ^ { - 3 }$ . Protein domains were annotated using the InterProScan (v5.34–73.0) (Jones et al., 2014) and PFAM databases. Gene Ontology (GO) IDs for each gene were obtained from TrEMBL, InterPro and EggNOG. 

# Annotation of noncoding RNA genes

tRNAscan-SE (v1.3.1) (Lowe and Eddy, 1997) was used for tRNA gene identification. rRNA genes were detected with barrnap (v0.9). MicroRNAs (miRNAs) and small nuclear RNAs (snRNAs) were identified using Infernal (v1.1) (Nawrocki and Eddy, 2013) to search against the Rfam (v14.5) (Griffiths-Jones et al., 2003) database. Pseudogenes were predicted using GenBlastA (v1.0.4) and GeneWise (v2.4.1). 

# Comparative genomic analysis

Genome alignments between Moshiliu and Tunisia were performed with the NUCmer3.0 program of MUMmer4.0.0 (Marcais et al., 2018) with the parameter setting ‘-mum -g $1 0 0 0 \ \_ \textmu \scriptsize { \cdot } \ 9 0 \ \ - 1 \ 4 0 ^ { \prime }$ , and a delta filter was used to filter the alignment file produced by nucmer with the parameter setting ‘- 1’. SNPs and INDELs were detected with the program show-snps with the parameter setting ‘-Clr -H -x 1’ (Zhang et al., 2016). The effects of the SNPs and InDels were annotated via snpEff (Cingolani et al., 2012). We calculated the SNP density via VFtools with a sliding window of $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { k b }$ . Additionally, structural variations (SVs) were identified via Assemblytics (Nattestad and Schatz, 2016). 

# Genome resequencing

DNA of 142 pomegranate accessions (Table S10) was extracted via the CTAB method. DNA purity and integrity were evaluated with a Nanodrop spectrophotometer and $1 \%$ agarose gels. The DNA concentration was measured with a Qubit- DNA Assay Kit with a Qubit Fluorometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Libraries were prepared following the manufacturer’s instructions (Illumina Inc.). The Illumina NovaSeq 6000 system was used to sequence the DNA libraries (paired-end sequencing libraries with an insert size of approximately 300 bp). Clean data were obtained after removing adaptors and low-quality bases from the raw data. The data for Bhagwa (SRR6236886 and SRR6917660), PGM (SRR5812494) and Taishanhong (SRR5110055) were downloaded from NCBI (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/). 

# Measurement of anthocyanin concentration and composition

Total anthocyanin and oligomeric proanthocyanidins (OPC) were detected by the plant Anthocyanin Content Assay Kit (AKPL021M) and Oligomeric Proantho Cyanidins (OPC) Content Assay Kit (AKPL017M) via ELISA (SpectraMax-i3x), respectively. 

Contents of delphinidin, cyanidin, procyanidin B4, petunidin, procyanidin B2, pelargonidin, peonidin, malvidin, rutin, luteolin, quercetin, kaempferol and isorhamnetin were detected by Shanghai Biotree Biotech. Approximately 100 mg of each sample was subjected to dual extraction using 1 mL of methanol:water: formic acid (70:30:1, v/v/v) to ensure complete anthocyanin dissolution. Following vortex mixing and 20-min sonication, the samples were centrifuged at $2 0 0 0 \times \pmb { g }$ for 10 min in a gmicrocentrifuge. The resulting supernatants were filtered through $0 . 2 2 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ nylon membranes prior to analysis. Chromatographic separation and mass spectrometric detection were performed using a Vanquish UPLC system coupled to a Q Exactive Hybrid Quadrupole-Orbitrap mass spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific) equipped with a heated ESI source. High-resolution mass spectrometry data were acquired in selected ion monitoring (SIM) mode. 

# Mapping of short reads and SNP calling

Paired-end resequencing reads were mapped to the pomegranate ‘Moshiliu’ reference genome with BWA (version 0.7.17) using the default parameters (Li and Durbin, 2009). The mapping results in SAM format were converted to BAM format using SAMtools (version: 1.9), and the unmapped and nonunique reads were also filtered. The Picard package (Version: 1.119) was used to filter duplicated reads. The filtered results in BAM format were used for SNP calling. SNP detection was performed using the Genome Analysis Toolkit (GATK, version 3.4–46) (McKenna et al., 2010) and SAMtools. The same variants in GATK and SAMtools were selected. SNPs were filtered using the criteria of an MAF ${ \ge } 0 . 0 5$ and a missing data rate of no more than $30 \%$ by using PLINK for GWAS (Purcell et al., 2007). 

# Genome-wide association studies

Single-nucleotide polymorphisms were filtered using the criteria of an MAF ${ \ge } 0 . 0 1$ and a missing data rate of no more than $10 \%$ by using PLINK for phylogenetic and population structure analysis. The obtained phylogenetic tree (neighbour-joining method) was analysed with TASSEL (Zhang et al., 2010) with default parameters. Admixture was used for the pomegranate population structure calculation (with the simulated K value ranging from 2 to 7) and PCA with default parameters (Alexander et al., 2009). The TBtools (Chen et al., 2023a) and R (version 4.2.2) packages of ggplot2 were used for population structure and PCA result visualization. GWASs were performed using the EMMAX and TASSEL programs (Kang et al., 2010), and the threshold was set as 0.05/total SNPs. LDBlockShow was used to visualize linkage disequilibrium (LD) (Dong et al., 2021). The percentage of phenotypic variation explained (PVE) was calculated using TASSEL5 via the GLM and MLM (Li et al., 2020). 

# Transcriptome sequencing (RNA-Seq)

Inner seed coats of ‘M0128’ (soft-seeded) and ‘Moshiliu’ (hardseeded) were collected at 20, 30, 50, 90 and 120 DAF. Peels of ‘Sanbai’ (white peel), ‘M0109’ (red peel) and ‘Moshiliu’ (black peel) were collected at 30, 50 and 120 DAF. Total RNA was extracted via the CTAB method. RNA purity was evaluated with a Nanodrop NC2000 instrument (Thermo Scientific). An RNA Nano 6000 Assay Kit was used for RNA concentration and integrity measurements. Libraries were constructed using the Hieff NGS Ultima Dual-mode mRNA Library Prep Kit (Yeasen). The Illumina NovaSeq 6000 system was used to sequence the mRNA library. After the adaptors and low-quality bases were filtered, the clean data were used for 

transcript-level expression analysis. HISAT, StringTie and Ballgown were used for mapping, transcript assembly and detection of differentially expressed genes and transcripts, respectively with default parameters (Pertea et al., 2016).

For PgNST3, the inner seed coats of three hard-seeded accessions (‘Kai Feng Si Ji Hong’, ‘Sanbai’ and ‘Moshiliu’) and three soft-seeded accessions (‘M1028’, ‘M0109’ and ‘Yi-3’) at 20, 30, 50, 60 and 90 DAF were obtained for RNA extraction.

Gene expression analysis was conducted via the use of Taq Pro Universal SYBR qPCR Master Mix (Vazyme, China) on a LightCycler 480 system (Roche). PgActin and SlActin served as internal controls. The data were analysed via internal controls and the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C t } }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen, 2001). Each experiment included at least three biological replicates. All primer sequences are listed in Table S16.

The thermal cycling conditions were as follows: initial denaturation at $9 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 2 min, followed by 35 cycles of denaturation at $9 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for ${ 1 0 \ 5 } ,$ annealing at $5 4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 5 s and extension at $6 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for ${ 1 5 5 , }$ with a final extension at $6 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for 5 min. The PCR products were sequenced by Youkang Biotech Co., Ltd., to identify DNA polymorphisms. 

# CRISPR/Cas9-engineered mutations in and pomegranate transformation

The leaf segments of dwarf pomegranate (Punica granatum L. var. nana) were used for transformation. To generate the CRISPR/Cas9 vector for PgANS, two 19-bp sequences (Yan 

et al., 2015) targeting exon 1 of PgANS were assembled into the binary CRISPR-Cas9 vector pZHA702 using the BsaI enzyme and T4 ligase. Agrobacterium tumefaciens-mediated transformation was used to transform the leaves. A freeze–thaw approach (Holsters et al., 1978) was used to introduce the plasmid into Agrobacterium tumefaciens LBA4404. The explants were immersed in medium supplemented with A. tumefaciens $( \mathsf { O D } = 0 . 6 \substack { - 1 . 0 } )$ for 15 min. After inoculation, the explants were placed on regeneration medium (MS medium supplemented with 2 mg/L TDZ, $0 . 1 2 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ NAA, 1 g/L PVP, $3 0 ~ { \mathfrak { g } } / { \mathsf { L } }$ sucrose and $7 ~ { \mathfrak { g } } / { \mathfrak { L } }$ agar at pH 5.8). After cocultivation for 3 days at $2 3 \pm 2 ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { C } }$ in the dark, the inoculated explants were placed on regeneration medium supplemented with $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ kanamycin and $1 0 \mathrm { \ m g / L }$ meropenem at $2 5 \pm 2 ^ { \circ } C$ in the dark for 2 months. Leaves were collected from different transgenic plants for DNA extraction. The primers designed referencing the coding region at the target sites in the PgANS gene were used for further sequencing analysis to detect the mutated sites. 

# Overexpression of $P g N S T 3 ^ { G }$ and $P g N S T 3 ^ { A }$ in tomato

Tomato plants (Solanum lycopersicum var. ‘Micro-Tom’) were utilized for functional analysis. The CDS of $P g N S T 3 ^ { G }$ and $P g N S T 3 ^ { A }$ were subsequently cloned into the pCAMBIA 1301-GFP vector to construct the 35S::PgNST3G and $3 5 S { : : } P g N S T 3 ^ { A }$ expression vectors. Both wild-type (WT) and T2 generation overexpression (OE) lines were cultured in an illuminated incubator with a $1 6 { : } 8 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ light/dark photoperiod, $70 \%$ relative humidity and a temperature of $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . 

A total of 100 seeds were collected from five different tomato fruits, each originating from distinct overexpression lines and WT, which represented separate biological replicates. Briefly, the hardness of the seeds was measured using a TA-XT plus texture analyzer (Stable Micro Systems, UK) with a $2 \mathsf { m m }$ diameter probe. 

# LUC/REN assay

The PgMYB46 promoter (3000-bp upstream of the ATG start codon) was introduced into the pGreen II 0800-Luc vector to construct the proPgMYB46:LUC reporter plasmid. The CDS of $P g N S T 3 ^ { G }$ and $P g N S T 3 ^ { A }$ were inserted into the pGreenII 62-SK vector. The resulting constructs were then transiently coexpressed in N. benthamiana leaves via Agrobacterium (Agrobacterium tumefaciens, strain GV3101-psoup)-mediated infiltration, with the agrobacteria resuspended (final $\mathsf { O D 6 0 0 } = 0 . 7 5$ ) in infiltration buffer (10 mM MgCl2, 10 mM MES and $2 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ acetosyringone, pH 5.6). The ratio of the pGreenII 62-SK vector:pGreenII 0800-LUC vector was 10:1.
