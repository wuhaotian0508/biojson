# Summary

Soybean cyst nematode (SCN, Heterodera glycines) is a major pathogen harmful to soybean all over the world, causing huge yield loss every year. Soybean resistance to SCN is a complex quantitative trait controlled by a small number of major genes (rhg1 and Rhg4) and multiple micro-effect genes. Therefore, the continuous identification of new resistant lines and genes is needed for the sustainable development of global soybean production. Here, a novel disease-resistance quantitative trait locus Rscn-16 was identified and fine mapped to an $8 . 4 \ – \ k \ b$ interval on chromosome 16 using an $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population. According to transcriptome and metabolome analysis, a UDP-glucosyltransferase encoding gene, GmUGT88A1, was identified as the most likely gene of Rscn-16. Soybean lines overexpressing GmUGT88A1 exhibited increased resistance to SCN, higher isoflavone glycosides and larger seed size while the phenotype of RNA-interference and knockout soybean lines showed sensitivity to SCN and decreased in seed size compared to wild-type plants. GmMYB29 gene could bind to the promoter of GmUGT88A1 and coordinate with GmUGT88A1 to regulate soybean resistance to SCN and isoflavone accumulation. Under SCN infection, GmUGT88A1 participated in the reorientation of isoflavone biosynthetic metabolic flow and the accumulation of isoflavone glycosides, thus protecting soybean from SCN stress. GmUGT88A1 was found to control soybean seed size by affecting transcription abundance of GmSWEET10b and GmFAD3C, which are known to control soybean seed weight. Our findings provide insights into the regulation of SCN resistance, isoflavone content and seed size through metabolic flux redirection, and offer a potential means for soybean improvement. 

# Introduction

Soybean (Glycine max (Linn.) Merr.), which is widely cultivated in China, is rich in protein and oil (Kofsky et al., 2021). However, soybeans are susceptible to different pathogens, including fungi, bacteria, viruses, and parasitic nematodes (Ko et al., 2019). Soybean cyst nematode (SCN, Heterodera glycines) is a severe soil-borne pathogen and causes serious soybean yield losses. SCN has a serious effect in the United States, Brazil, Argentina and northeast China (Dong and Hudson, 2022). Therefore, the development of technology to research how to resist SCN and increase soybean yield has been promoted (Lian et al., 2023). SCN is a soybean disease that is extremely difficult to control. It is estimated that soybean yield loss annually caused by SCN is up to 9 million tons worldwide, and the direct or indirect economic loss caused by SCN is more than $\$ 1.62$ billion (Bradley et al., 2021; Koenning and Wrather, 2010). The long-term damage from SCN will harm soybean throughout its whole growth period. When SCN invades soybean roots, it leads to the rupture of taproot bulge, the reduction of root nodules, and dysplasia of lateral roots in soybean (Ste-Croix et al., 2023). Due to root dysplasia, soybean plants are dwarfed, leaves are 

aged and yellowed, plants have fewer flowers and pods, and in severe cases, seedlings die at the seedling stage, leading to production reduction or even extinction (Li, 2022). At present, the best means of control is to find a genetic resistance locus in soybean germplasm resources (Dong and Hudson, 2022). To date, rhg1 and Rhg4 have been the main loci of SCN resistance. The rhg1 locus of ‘(PI) 88788’, which is the main locus of SCN resistance and has been used in commercial varieties, greatly reduces the harm of SCN to soybean yield (Patil et al., 2019; Shaibu et al., 2020). In recent years, although resistance sites can effectively control SCN, some population have gradually overcome rhg1-type resistance of ‘(PI) 88788’ (Han et al., 2023). Therefore, it is becoming increasingly important to explore new SCN resistance loci, understand the molecular mechanisms of SCN and develop more effective strategies to defend against SCN. 

Glycosylation is the most extensive modification reaction in plants. Glycosylation is the process of transferring a sugar group from the donor molecule to the receptor molecule under the catalysis of glucosyl-transferase (GT) (Lairson et al., 2008). Glycosyl-transferase, as an enzyme that catalyses glycosylation, plays an important role in all stages of plant growth (Chen 

et al., 2016). In the process of glycosylation in plant, glycosyltransferase mainly uses uridine diphosphate (UDP)-glucose, UDP-galactose and UDP-rhamnose as glycosyl donors, and uses small molecule compounds such as flavonoids, terpenes and plant hormones as receptors to modify and produce sugars or glycosides (Chen et al., 2019). Studies have shown that plant glycosyltransferase can glycosylate six kinds of plant flavonoids into corresponding glycosides, including flavonols, flavanols, flavanones, flavonoids, anthocyanins and isoflavones (Xie et al., 2019). It has been reported that isoflavones have certain effects on the viability of SCN (Wang et al., 2020b). Related studies have shown that daidzein, daidzin, genistein, genistin, glycitin and glycitein (Ma, 2020), which are soybean isoflavones, have certain inhibitory effects on egg hatching and J2 activity of SCN. Plant glycosyltransferase not only participates in plant secondary metabolism and glycosylation of plant hormones but also participates in various biological and abiotic stresses by synthesizing or modifying stress response factors and yield (Rehman et al., 2018). Dong et al. (2020) studied the rice uridine diphosphate-glucuronosyltransferase (UGT) gene GSA1 and found that overexpression of GSA1 gene could increase the grain size of rice and improve the resistance to salt, high temperature and drought stress. He et al. (2018) showed that UGT gene may be involved in response to Fusarium stress in wheat. Mohnike et al. (2021) found that Arabidopsis ugt76b1 mutant had an enhanced defence response to Pseudomonas syringae (Derksen et al., 2013). At present, although there are many reports on the biological stress of plant glycosyltransferase, there are no studies on SCN resistance of plant glycosyltransferase. 

In the present study, a population of 111 recombinant inbred lines (RILs) derived from a cross between ‘Dongnong ${ \mathsf { L } } - 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ (resistant to four SCN HG types) and ‘Heinong 37’ (susceptible to all SCN HG types), along with 309 germplasm resources, was employed to map the resistance gene to SCN HG type 0 using genome-wide association analysis (GWAS) and linkage analysis strategies. GmUGT88A1, as a candidate gene for broad-spectrum SCN resistance, was fine mapped and characterized. The findings of this study provide a theoretical foundation and technical support for elucidating the molecular mechanism of broad-spectrum resistance of soybean to SCN.

# Rscn-16 is a major and broad-spectrum quantitative trait locus that regulates the SCN resistance

The SCN resistance of ‘Dongnong L-10’ differed from that of ‘Peking’ and ‘PI88788’ (Han et al., 2017). In a previous study, we found that ‘Dongong $\mathsf { L } \mathsf { - } 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ carried the resistance to two HG types of SCN, and the resistance locus was initially localized in Chr.16 of soybean genome (Chang et al., 2011). To study the resistance mechanism of ‘Dongnong ${ \mathsf { L } } - 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ to SCN, we examined whether the known SCN resistance loci rhg1 and Rhg4 were present in ‘Dongnong $\mathsf { L } \mathsf { - } 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ . The results revealed that ‘Dongnong ${ \mathsf { L } } - 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ simultaneously carried beneficial alleles of both rhg1 and Rhg4, simultaneously (Table S1). In order to prove the existence of the resistance locus located in Chr.16 and to distinguish the effects of this locus from the known loci, rhg1 and Rhg4, carried by ‘Dongnong $\mathsf { L } \mathsf { - } 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ , three populations of recombinant inbred lines (RIL, $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 8 : 1 9 } )$ derived from crosses between the disease-resistant parent ‘Dongnong ${ \mathsf { L } } - 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ and three susceptible parents, ‘Suinong 10’, ‘Suinong $1 4 ^ { \prime }$ and ‘Heinong 37’ were used. We identified the 

resistance to SCN of the three mapping populations (Figure S1) and constructed linkage map of Chr.16 (Figure S2). The results showed that three quantitative trait loci (QTLs), qSCN16-3-1, qSCN16-3-2 and qSCN16-3-3 were detected underlying soybean resistance to SCN HG type 0 in all three RIL populations. The three QTLs shared the same linked molecular marker BACK-SOYSSR_16_1056 (Table 1). 

The offspring of three RIL populations carrying major SCN resistance loci rhg1 and/or Rhg4 were identified through genotyping. A total of 120 RILs carrying rhg1 and/or Rhg4 alleles were removed from the populations and the remaining 232 lines without rhg1 and Rhg4 loci were used for QTL analysis (Figure S3). The results showed that in the absence of known major loci rhg1 and Rhg4, the genetic distance between QTL and marker BACKSOYSSR_16_1056 was shortened, and the QTL contributions to the phenotype were significantly increased compared with the mapping results using all lines (Table 1). qSCN16-3-1, qSCN16-3-2 and qSCN16-3-3 shared the same linked molecular marker BACKSOYSSR_16_1056; therefore, they might be the same locus. The locus was stably detected in different genetic backgrounds, indicating that this locus was a major QTL of soybean resistance to SCN HG type 0. We named it Rscn-16. Meanwhile, the results of this study showed that the detection efficiency of a single major QTL was weakened when multiple major loci existed at the same time, and QTL mining by eliminating known sites was of great significance for the detection of novel QTLs. 

To confirm that Rscn-16 locus conferred soybean resistance to multiple SCN HG types, we conducted QTL analysis on soybean resistance to SCN HG types 257, 1347 and 12 357 in the ‘Heionong $3 7 ^ { \prime } \times ^ { \prime }$ ‘Dongnong $\mathsf { L } \mathsf { - } 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ population. It is concluded that Rscn-16 is a major disease-resistant locus against four SCN HG types.

# Fine mapping of Rscn-16

To narrow the interval of Rscn-16, a genetic map with a high marker density was constructed using a set of recombinant inbred lines (RILs) derived from the cross between ‘Heinong 37’ and ‘Dongnong L-10’. The genetic map was $2 6 4 3 . 2 \mathsf { c m }$ and included 6608 bin markers, with an average genetic distance of $0 . 4 \mathsf { c M }$ (Table S3). Based on the female index $( \mathsf { F } | \% )$ of the above mapping population (Figure S1a), eight QTLs associated with SCN HG type 0 were identified on chromosomes 1, 7, 8, 13, 16, 18 and 20 (Figure 1b, Table S3). Notably, QTLs located on chromosomes 8 and 18 overlapped with those previously reported for rhg1 and Rhg4 loci (Cook et al., 2012; Liu et al., 2012), indicating the accuracy of our localization. Furthermore, the QTL redefined as Rscn-16 was located on Chr. 16 covering the genomic region 33.58–33.78 Mbp and including the peak SNP from the mapping result of GWAS, was considered the redefined Rscn-16. This QTL explained $1 8 . 7 6 \%$ of phenotypic variation. 

By combining the interval of Rscn-16 located by linkage analysis and GWAS, we identified 17 genes were distributed in the genomic region 33.58–33.78 Mbp of Chr.16 (Figure 1c). To clone the resistance gene of Rscn-16, we screened resistant progenies in the RIL population and selected a resistant progeny ‘RI-Line $1 2 ^ { \prime }$ (average $\mathsf { I F } = 7 . 5 0 \mathrm { \Omega }$ , which carries the beneficial allele of Rscn-16 without carrying the two main loci (rhg1 and Rhg4) (Table S1). We then performed high-resolution mapping using a genotype selected $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population derived from the cross between 

‘Heinong 37’ and ‘RI-Line 12’. In the genomic region 33.58–33.78 Mbp on Chr.16, 89 potential markers were screened and 55 markers were found to be polymorphic between parents. Linkage analysis was conducted using these 55 markers and one binary phenotypic dataset representing SCN resistance for the $1 1 0 4 \mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ plants. # Phenylpropanoid metabolic pathway involved in SCN resistance

To study the metabolic pathway of the novel locus Rscn-16 in response to SCN from SCN-resistant cultivar ‘Dongnong L-10’, two ${ \mathsf { B C } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { F } } _ { 4 }$ lines, $' \mathsf { B C } _ { 2 } \mathsf { F } _ { 4 } – 1 1 ^ { \prime }$ and $' B C _ { 2 } F _ { 4 } – 7 5 ^ { \prime }$ , were generated by back-crossing ‘Heinong 37’ with ‘RI-Line $1 2 ^ { \prime }$ . $' \mathsf { B C } _ { 2 } \mathsf { F } _ { 4 } – 1 1 ^ { \prime }$ carries the beneficial allele of Rscn-16 and showed resistant to SCN HG type 0 (average $| \mathsf { F } = 9 . 0 0 \rangle$ ). The susceptible line $' B C _ { 2 } F _ { 4 } – 7 5 ^ { \prime }$ showed susceptibility to SCN HG type 0 (average $\mathsf { F } = 1 3 8 . 0 0 \mathrm { \Omega }$ ) without carrying the beneficial allele of Rscn-16. The two ${ \mathsf { B C } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { F } } _ { 4 }$ lines and their parents were selected to conduct transcriptome and metabolome data establishment and analysis before and after SCN stress. As shown in Figure S6, transcriptome sequencing revealed a total of 725 up-regulated differentially expressed genes (DEGs) and 456 down-regulated DEGs (Figure S6a,b, Table S4). The DEGs were significantly enriched in isoflavone biosynthesis, flavonoid biosynthesis and phenylpropanoid biosynthesis pathways in SCN-resistant cultivars ‘Dongnong $\mathsf { L } \mathsf { - } 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ and SCN-resistant line ‘BC2F4-11’ derived from ‘Dongong $\mathsf { L } \mathsf { - } 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ (Figure S6c). Metabolomics analysis revealed a total of 238 positively regulated metabolites and 113 negatively regulated metabolites (Figure S7a,b, Table S5). The most differentially regulated metabolites were enriched in $' B C _ { 2 } F _ { 4 } – 1 1 ^ { \prime }$ . We then analysed the metabolic pathways involved in differential metabolites among four soybean lines before and after SCN stress. The levels of DEMs related to isoflavonoid biosynthesis and flavonoid biosynthesis in the resistant lines, $' \mathsf { B C } _ { 2 } \mathsf { F } _ { 4 } – 1 1 ^ { \prime }$ and ‘Dongong L-10’, were significantly enriched compared to the susceptible lines, $' B C _ { 2 } F _ { 4 } – 7 5 ^ { \prime }$ and ‘Heinong 37’ after SCN stress (Figure S7c). The results of the combined analysis of two omics suggested that isoflavone biosynthesis, flavonoid biosynthesis, and phenylpropanoid biosynthesis metabolic pathways might play an important role in SCN resistance controlled by Rscn-16. We also validated the DEGs in these pathways through RT-qPCR, and the results were consistent with the gene expression trends obtained from the transcriptome (Figure S8, Table S6). 

Based on transcriptome and metabolome studies, we mapped the SCN resistance gene–metabolite network diagram of the phenylpropanoid metabolic pathway in $' B C _ { 2 } F _ { 4 } – 1 1 ^ { \prime }$ (Figure 2). After subjecting $' B C _ { 2 } F _ { 4 } – 1 1 ^ { \prime }$ to SCN HG type 0 stress, the increased expression of phenylalanine ammonia lyase (PAL) and cinnamate-4-hydroxylase (C4H) was increased, resulting in elevated levels of $\beta$ -coumaric acid and cinnamic acid, This, in turn, led to an increase in $\beta \cdot$ -coumarate-CoA content in flavonoid metabolism. Within the metabolic pathway of flavonoids, $\beta$ -coumarate-CoA facilitated the production of isoliquiritigenin and naringenin chalcone through chalcone synthase 

(CHS), as well as liquiritigenin and naringenin through chalcone isomerase (CHI). Among them, liquiritigenin and naringenin are not only metabolites in the flavonoid metabolic pathway but also precursor metabolites in the isoflavone metabolic pathway (Bolanos-V˜ asquez and Werner,´ 1997). In the isoflavone metabolic pathway, the contents of 2,6,7,4-tetrahydroxyisoflavanone, 2,7,4-trihydroxyisoflavone and 2-hydroxy-2,3-dihydrogenistei significantly increased through glycosylation of glycosyltransferase after SCN stress, resulting in the contents of genistein 7-Oglucoside, daidzein 7-O-glucoside and glycitein 7-O-glucoside. In conclusion, the isoflavone metabolic pathway plays a significant role in soybean resistance to SCN. The levels of various metabolites, including 2,7,4-trihydroxyisoflavone, 2-hydroxy-2,3- dihydrogenistei, genistein, daidzein, genistein 7-O-glucoside, glycitein 7-O-glucoside, coumarol and $6 ^ { \prime \prime }$ -O-malonyl daidzin metabolites were found to increase significantly after exposure to SCN HG type 0 stress. In the transcriptome data, the main gene, GmUGT88A1, which we had previously located, was differentially expressed in the isoflavone pathway. The GmUGT88A1 gene encodes a Uridine diphosphate-glucuronosyltransferase (UGT) containing a UDPGT box domain. UGTS catalyses the transfer of active sugars linked to UDP to receptors, such as lipids, proteins and secondary metabolites, and regulates their biological activity, solubility and stability (Le Roy et al., 2016). GmUGT88A1 can promote the isoflavone metabolism pathway through glycosylation. This further proves the reliability of the candidate gene GmUGT88A1. 

# GmUGT88A1 enhances soybean resistance to multiple SCN HG types

To verify our hypothesis, we cloned the GmUGT88A1 gene with a total length of 1416 bp. Six homozygous $\mathsf { T } _ { 2 }$ transgenic lines, including three GmUGT88A1 overexpression lines (GmUGT88A1- ox) and three GmUGT88A1-RNAi lines (GmUGT88A1-i) were obtained using the SCN-sensitive soybean variety ‘Dongnong $5 0 ^ { \prime }$ as a transformation receptor. PCR, RT-qPCR, bar test strips and SCN disease resistance identification were performed on the transgenic lines (Figures S9 and S10). There was a significant difference in the number of cysts between the transgenic lines, ranging from 18 to 180, and the average number of cysts in the ‘Dongnong $5 0 ^ { \prime }$ line was about 120. Compared with ‘Dongnong $5 0 ^ { \prime }$ , the number of female cysts per plant was significantly decreased in GmUGT88A1-ox lines and was significantly increased in GmUGT88A1-i lines (Figure 3a–d). The up-regulated expression of GmUGT88A1 gene improved soybean resistance to four kinds of SCN HG types (Figure S11). These results indicate that the expression level of GmUGT88A1 was associated with SCN resistance and exhibited a resistance to multiple HG types of SCN. 

# GmUGT88A1 participates in soybean resistance to SCN by regulating isoflavone content

Isoflavone 7-O-glucosyltransferase (IF7GT) catalyses the glycosylation of daidzein, genistein and glycitein to produce genistein 7- O-glucoside, daidzein 7-O-glucoside and glycitein 7-O-glucoside (Noguchi et al., 2007). # Discussion

In the study of SCN resistance loci, over 300 SCN resistance QTLs have been identified distributed on 20 soybean chromosomes. However, as the SCN population continues to evolve, SCN resistance conferred by most major QTLs is gradually lessened. Therefore, it is important to explore and utilize new QTLs to resist SCN (Boucher St-Amour et al., 2020). In the process of studying the SCN-resistant germplasm ‘Dongnong $\mathsf { L } \mathsf { - } 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ , we found that ‘Dongnong $\mathsf { L } \mathsf { - } 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ carried the two known SCN resistance loci rhg1 and Rhg4. However, there were SCN-resistant lines in the progenies of ‘Dongnong $\mathsf { L } \mathsf { - } 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ that did not contain rhg1 and Rhg4. It is speculated that there may be other resistant loci in ‘Dongnong ${ \mathsf { L } } - 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ apart from rhg1 and Rhg4. Consistent with the speculation, the present study identified a novel major diseaseresistant QTL Rscn-16 in ‘Dongnong $\mathsf { L } \mathsf { - } 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ by linkage analysis. This QTL explained $1 8 . 7 6 \%$ of the phenotypic variation and contributed to soybean resistance against four SCN HG types. The locus was also be detected by GWAS indicating that Rscn-16 exists in the soybean natural population and has universality in molecular marker-assisted selection breeding. We further targeted the candidate gene GmUGT88A1 encoding a UGT for Rscn-16 through fine mapping. UGT is involved in isoflavone glycosylation, which plays a crucial role in plant defence mechanisms. As a broad-spectrum phytoprotegerin, soybean isoflavone has antipathogen, anti-virus, and antioxidation functions in plants and plays an important role in defending against biotic and abiotic stress in plants (Dakora and Phillips, 1996). However, our understanding of the synergistic regulation mechanism between SCN resistance and other soybean agronomic traits is still unclear. Our study of the GmUGT88A1 gene involved in SCN resistance of soybean will help to deepen our understanding of how soybean isoflavones affect resistance to SCN, as well as seed size, weight and isoflavone content in soybean.

The up-regulated expression of GmMYB29 increases the content of genistein, daidzein, and glycitein in soybean by regulating the expression of CHS8 and IFS2. Meanwhile, overexpression of GmUGT88A1 results in the accumulation of glycosides in the process of promoting glycosylation of isoflavones. Therefore, regulating the expressions of GmMYB29 and SCN resistance haplotype of GmUGT88A1 simultaneously can synergistically increase soybean isoflavone content and SCN resistance. This discovery established the regulatory mechanism of soybean SCN resistance and revealed the strategy of synergistically improving soybean SCN resistance and isoflavone content. 

Studies have shown that the PP2C-1 (Putative Phosphatase 2C) gene regulates the soybean BR signalling pathway and controls seed size, and this gene interacts with the core transcription factor GmBZR1 in BR pathway (Lu et al., 2017). This results in dephosphorylation and activation of downstream transcription factors, which increase seed size and weight by regulating the expression of related genes. In the present study, we found that GmUGT88A1 overexpression increased the expression level of Glyma.18G061000 (protein phosphatase 2C, PP2C ) and Glyma.13G266600 (BES1/BZR1 homologue protein 4, BZR1). We speculated that GmUGT88A1 might affect soybean seed size through BR signalling. In addition, GmSWEET10b (Glyma.08G183500) was reported to positively regulate soybean grain size (Wang et al., 2020a), while GmFAD3C (Glyma.11G174100) was reported to negatively regulate soybean grain size (Singh et al., 2010). Our results showed the seed size enlargement of the overexpressed GmUGT88A1 gene plant might also be related to the up-regulated and down-regulated expression of the above two genes. 

Studies have shown that increasing the expression of UGT genes in rice can promote flavonoid metabolism, increase grain size and affect yield (Dong et al., 2020). Similar to this study, changes in the expression of GmUGT88A1 can adjust seed size and affect the isoflavonoid content, thus affecting soybean yield. In summary, GmUGT88A1 is a valuable genetic resource with the potential to coordinate yield, quality and enhance SCN resistance in soybean. 

# Methods

# Cultivation and phenotypic identification of plant materials

A total of 309 cultivated soybean germplasm accessions from the Chinese soybean collection were obtained for the genome-wide association study (GWAS) of soybean resistance to SCN. Among 

these accessions, one parental line ‘Dongnong ${ \mathsf { L } } - 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ (resistant to SCN HG types 0, 257, 1347 and 12 357) and three parental lines: ‘Heinong 37’, ‘Suinong14’ and ‘Suinong $1 0 ^ { \prime }$ (susceptible to all SCN HG types) were used to develop RILs through the single-seed descent method. Three mapping populations consisting of 111 RILs, 127 RILs and 114 RILs were generated from the crosses between ‘Dongnong ${ \mathsf { L } } - 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ and ‘Heinong 37’, ‘Suinong $1 4 ^ { \prime }$ , and ‘Suinong $1 0 ^ { \prime }$ . Fine mapping was conducted using 1500 $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ individuals obtained by crossing ‘Heinong 37’ and ‘Dongnong L-$1 0 ^ { \prime }$ . The SCN-susceptible soybean ‘Dongnong $5 0 ^ { \prime }$ and the SCNresistant soybean ‘RI-Line $1 2 ^ { \prime }$ were used as the recipients of genetic transformation. 

For SCN resistance evaluation, all soybean materials including lines and individuals were grown in a greenhouse at $2 5 \mathrm { - } 2 8 ^ { \circ } C$ with a photoperiod of 16 h light/8 h darkness and $7 5 \%$ relative humidity. The soybean plants were evaluated for SCN resistance using a completely randomized design with three replicates under glasshouse conditions, according to the protocol described by Chang et al. (2011). The female index $( \mathsf { F } \mathsf { I } \% )$ per line or individual was calculated as follows: $\mathsf { F l } =$ (number of cysts and females of the detected plant) / (average number of cysts and females of ‘Lee $6 8 ^ { \prime } ) \times 1 0 0$ . The plants were rated as resistant $( \mathsf { F l } = 0 - 9 )$ ), moderately resistant $( \mathsf { F l } = 1 0 - 3 0 )$ ), moderately susceptible $( \mathsf { F l } = 3 1 - 6 0 )$ or susceptible $\left( \mathsf { F l } > 6 0 \right)$ to classify the response to SCN inoculation (Schmitt and Shannon, 1992). 

For RNA-seq, RT-qPCR and metabolomic sampling, tested soybeans were planted individually in pots with a diameter of $7 . 5 \mathsf { c m }$ . The cultivation substrate was a 1:1 mixture of sand and soil. Upon emergence of the soybean cotyledons, each plant was inoculated near its root with an SCN egg suspension containing 2000 eggs, while the control group received an equivalent volume of distilled water. Roots were harvested and rapidly frozen in liquid nitrogen for subsequent analysis at 5 dpi. 

Genetic transformation materials (GmUGT88A1-ox, GmUGT88A1-i and wild-type ‘Dongnong50’) were planted at Northeast Agricultural University in Harbin $( 4 5 ^ { \circ } 7 5 ^ { \prime } \mathsf { N }$ , $1 2 6 ^ { \circ } 6 3 ^ { \prime } \mathsf { E } )$ , Heilongjiang, China during the 2022 growing seasons. Each line was planted in three replicate plots (row length of $_ { 2 \mathsf { m } }$ and width of $6 5 \mathsf { c m } _ { \iota } ^ { \prime }$ ) in a randomized block design, and plants were maintained with typical field management practices. Soybean plants were harvested, and agronomic traits were analysed after drying to a moisture content $< 1 3 \%$ . 

# Genotyping of soybean germplasms and bi-parental populations

For each tested soybean line or individual, genomic DNA was isolated from the leaf tissues using the cetyltrimethyl ammonium bromide (CTAB) method (Sun et al., 2013). 

Genome re-sequencing was performed on a natural population of 309 soybean accessions, with a depth of $1 0 \times$ was conducted for each accession. The quality of the extracted DNA was assessed using NanoDrop™ One (Thermo Fisher Scientific (China) Co., Ltd., Shanghai, China), and the whole genome of DNA samples with OD260/OD280 in the range of 1.8–2.0 was re-sequenced. Sequencing was completed by Beijing Genomics Institute Company (Beijing, China), and the sequencing process was carried out according to sample DNA quality testing, sequencing library construction, library quality testing and library sequencing. The sequenced data were filtered and compared with the soybean reference genome version Glycine max Wm82.a2.v1 (https://phytozome-next.jgi.doe.gov/info/Gmax_Wm82_a2_v1), 

and the variant SNPs in the data were detected using GATK4 software. The minor allele frequency (MAF) threshold was set to 0.05. The genotype matrix was ultimately imputed using the nearestneighbour algorithm (Huang et al., 2010). 

For three RI populations, we constructed partial genetic maps covering the initial localization interval of Rscn-16 using SSR markers. The PCR products of the SSR markers were denatured and separated on $6 \%$ (wt/vol) denaturing polyacrylamide gel by electrophoresis. IciMapping $\lor 4 . 2$ software was used to construct a high-density genetic map. 

For the RI population derived from the cross between ‘Heinong 37’ and ‘Dongnong $\mathsf { L } \mathsf { - } 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ , we conducted genome resequencing with a depth of 20 folds for parents and five folds for offspring. The sequenced data were filtered and compared with the reference genome, and the variant SNPs in the data were detected using GATK4 software. SNPs were annotated using SnpEff software, and polymorphic SNP markers among parents were selected to construct a genetic map. The SNPs in most subgroups were merged by the sliding window method to form a bin marker, and QTL IciMapping v4.2 software was used to construct a high-density genetic map of bin markers. 

For $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ generation, we screened 55 potential SSR and SNP markers for polymorphisms between the two parental lines in the interval and flanking regions of Rscn-16. The identified polymorphic SSR markers were used to genotype the $1 5 0 0 ~ \mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ individuals to identify the markers that co-separated with resistance to SCN HG type 0 via a linkage analysis approach. SSR marker genotyping was mentioned above. The SNP PCR products were scanned by Light Scanner 96 (Idaho, USA) based on high resolution melting (HRM) (Wittwer et al., 2003). 

# Quantitative trait loci analysis

Inclusive composite interval mapping (ICIM) was performed using QTL IciMapping v4.2 software to detect the QTL related to resistance to SCN HG type 0. The threshold of screening QTL was an $\mathsf { L O D } > 2 . 5$ . The candidate interval of QTL was selected as the physical location interval of the bin corresponding to two LOD values on both sides of the highest peak of the QTL. 

# Genome-wide association analysis mapping of soybean resistance to SCN

A total of 135 million SNPs from 309 soybean accessions were used for association analysis in conjunction with the general linear model (GLM) in TASSEL 5.0 (Bradbury et al., 2007). The Bonferroni method $\alpha \leq 0 . 0 5$ (corresponding to $P$ value $\leq 7 . 4 3 \times 1 0 ^ { - 7 } )$ was used as the threshold to determine whether a significant association existed (Holm, 1979). 

# RNA extraction and RT-qPCR

Total RNA was extracted from various soybean tissues using the Trizol method (Rio et al., 2010). Reverse transcription and cDNA synthesis were performed using the ReverTra Ace qPCR RT Master Mix with gDNA Remover (Toyobo) from 500 ng of total RNA. The ABI 7300 Real-Time PCR System and Fast Start Universal SYBR Green Master Mix with ROX (Roche (China) Holding Ltd., Shanghai, China) were used to perform quantitative RT-qPCR analysis. The 7300 system software (Version 1.4.0) was used for data collection, and the expression data were analysed using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta C _ { \intercal } }$ method and Microsoft Excel 2013. Actin genes were used for normalization. There were three biological replicates for all analyses. PCR primer sets for gene amplification are shown in the Table S8. 

# Genotyping of the recombinant inbred lines population

KASP-labelled primers were designed in Premier 5.0 software using the resistance QTL information of rhg1 and Rhg4 used by Shi et al. (2015) (Table S8). Fluorescent connectors were added to the $5 ^ { \prime }$ end of the primers, and PCR amplification was completed using the ABI7500 Fast fluorescence quantitative PCR instrument (Agilent Technologies, Palo Alto, CA). 

# RNA sequencing

RNA sequencing was performed by Hanyu Bio-Tech (Shanghai, China). Total RNA from all samples was isolated using Trizol (Invitrogen, Co., Ltd., Carlsbad, CA, USA). The quality and integrity of RNA were examined by agarose gel electrophoresis and a 2100 Bioanalyzer. The qualified RNA was treated with DNase $( 5 \mathsf { U } \mu \mathsf { L } ^ { - 1 } )$ (Takara Biomedical Technology (Beijing) Co., Ltd., Beijing, China) at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $3 0 \mathsf { m i n }$ . The DNase-treated RNA was purified using Dynabeads Oligo (dT) 25 (Life). The library was prepared using the NEBNext®UltraTM RNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina following the manufacturer’s instructions. The quality of the cDNA library was evaluated by performing $2 \%$ agarose gel electrophoresis, qubit quantification and high-sensitivity DNA chip analysis. The sequencing library (10 ng) was sequenced on the Illumina NOVA platform to obtain $2 \times 1 5 0$ bp data. 

# Metabolomic analysis

Metabolic group analysis was carried out using biocame (Wuhan, China). Agilent high-performance liquid chromatography–triple quadrupole liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (Agilent LC–MS/MS6495) was used for LC–MS analysis. Agilent Profinder software was used to correct the retention time, peak recognition, peak extraction, peak integration, peak alignment and other work of the original MS data, and finally output the CEF file. Then, Agilent Massive Parallel Processor software was used for statistical processing and combined with the Metlin database for substance identification. The differential metabolites of different samples were screened according to the three criteria of intra-group $\mathsf { R S D } < 0 . 5$ , multiple of difference between groups (|log2 (Foldchange) $> 1$ ), and significance level (P-value $< 0 . 0 5$ ). The differential metabolites were mapped to the KEGG database, and the results were visualized by TBtools software. 

# Plasmid construction and soybean transformation

To construct the recombinant plasmids of GmUGT88A1-ox and GmMYB29-ox, 1416 bp and 816 bp CDSs were amplified from the cDNA of ‘Dongnong L-10’ by PCR. Then, the two PCR products were ligated to the linear vector pCAMBIA3300 using a homologous recombination system (ClonExpress II One Step Cloning Kit, Vazyme, China). To construct RNAi plasmids of GmUGT88A1-i, the specific sequences of 400 bp sense and antisense fragments of the GmUGT88A1 gene were cloned into the RNA interference plant expression vector pFGC5941 through homologous recombination. The recombinant plasmids were transformed into Agrobacterium rhizogenes EHA105 according to a previously described method (Zhao et al., 2018), and transgenic soybean was obtained. 

For gene editing, targeting sequences were selected within GmUGT88A1 and GmMYB29 genes and their targeting specificity was confirmed using CCTop (Stemmer et al., 2015). Then, a cassette for GmU6-driven crRNA expression that includes DRs and target sequences was synthesized and inserted into the plasmid through HindIII/XmaI digestion to construct the CRISPR-LbCpf1 

binary vector (Duan et al., 2021). The constructed CRISPR-LbCpf1 vectors were introduced into Agrobacterium rhizogenes strain K599 and then transformed into soybean line ‘RI-Line12’ according to a previously described method (Cheng et al., 2021). 

All the primers and target sequences for vector construction were listed in Table S8. 

# In vitro treatment of SCN with isoflavone glycosides and aglycones

According to the content of daidzein, genistein, glycitein, genistein 7-O-glucoside, daidzein 7-O-glucoside and glycitein 7- O-glucoside in soybeans measured by Dong et al. (2020), three isoflavone glycosides/aglycones were configured into $1 / 1 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathsf { m } \mathsf { L } ^ { - 1 }$ solutions to treat fresh SCN J2s in vitro. Using sterile water as a control, each treatment had 3 replicates. The oscillation frequency of SCN J2s was measured in 1 min under a stereomicroscope at 1, 3, 6, 12 and $2 4 \mathsf { h }$ . The determination of oscillation frequency mainly refers to the method of Tsalik and Hobert (2003). At the same time, the activity of glutathione mercaptotransferase in SCN J2s was determined after treatment. The OD value/reaction time was measured. The change value of absorbance per minute was recorded as A340/min, and the specific activity of enzyme was calculated. 

# Cloning and activity analysis of the truncated promoter region of GmUGT88A1

The sequences of GmUGT88A1 initiation codon upstream of 201, 482, 589, 768, 840, 1124, 1486, 1601, and 2000 bp were cloned into the pGreenII-0800-LUC vector. All clones were sequenced to ensure that the sequences were accurate. The new fusion plasmid was introduced into Agrobacterium GV3101 (pSoup-p19) and transformed into the leaves of N. benthamiana according to a previously described method (Chen et al., 2021). Enzyme activity was detected using a fluorescein detection kit (E1910, Promega Dual-Luciferase® Reporter Assay System). 

# Yeast one-hybrid assay

We constructed the GmUGT88A1 promoter sequence 589 bp upstream of the ATG on the yeast expression vector pBait using the yeast one-hybrid test. All procedures were performed according to the manufacturer’s protocol (Clontech Laboratories, Inc., Mountain View, CA, USA). Using yeast strain Y1HGold (pBait AbAi) for transformation, one transformed report plasmid was introduced into Y1HGold (pBait AbAi). Interactions were then tested on selection plates (SD/–Ura–Trp + ABA). 

# Dual-luciferase assay and ChIP-seq analysis

Full-length CDS of GmMYB29 was cloned into pCAM-BIA3300::35S to act as the effector. The upstream 533 bp fragment of the GmUGT88A1 cloned from ‘Heinong $3 7 ^ { \prime }$ and ‘Dongnong $\mathsf { L } \mathsf { - } 1 0 ^ { \prime }$ was fused with pGreenII0800-LUC to obtain two reporters. Transient co-expression of the effector and reporter constructs was transfected into the leaves of N. benthamiana for $2 0 \mathsf { h }$ . Briefly, fireflyluciferase (LUC) and Renilla luciferase (REN) activities were surveyed with a dual-luciferase reporter assay kit (Promega), and the LUC activity, normalized to REN activity, was determined. The REN gene driven by the 35S promoter was used as an internal control. Transient transactivation with the reporters and the empty vector pCAMBIA3300::35S were used as controls. All primers used for these constructs are listed in the Supporting Information. Full-length CDS of 

GmMYB29 was cloned into 1300-3xflag to act as the effector. The roots of wild-type and transgenic soybean (GmMYB29-1300-3xflag) were fixed and quenched with glycine. ChIP-seq was carried out as described in Wang et al. (2021). 

# EMSA

EMSAs were performed using the GmMYB29 protein that was expressed in the Escherichia coli strain BL21 and purified according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The primers for the GmUGT88A1 gene promoter region, covering the MYB binding site sequence and the 533 bp SNP along with its adjacent base pairs, were synthesized by Sangon Company and labelled with biotin as probes. The relevant primers are listed in Table S8. The binding reaction was performed in the dark for 25 min. Electrophoresis was carried out at 95 V until the bromophenol blue migrated to two-thirds of the gel length, at which point it was stopped. Membrane washing and signal detection were conducted according to the instructions provided by the LightShift® Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit.
