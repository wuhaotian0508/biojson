# Variations in carbon flux allocation among cassava (Manihot esculenta) cultivars arise from balanced competition between starch accumulation and structural component development

Check for updates 

Min Li1,2, Juanjuan Xu1,2, Zheng Cai1,2, Pingchuan Zhu1 , Kunhang Liu1 , Shiting Deng1 , Youzhi Li1 & Xianwei Fan 1 

Cassava (Manihot esculenta Crantz) storage roots exhibit significant variation in starch content among cultivars, yet the metabolic and molecular mechanisms governing carbon allocation between storage and structural components remain poorly understood. Here, we investigated carbon partitioning in two cassava cultivars with distinct starch phenotypes: high-starch FX01 and low-starch SC16. Using $^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ isotope labeling coupled with metabolomic analysis, we traced the pathway of carbohydrates through primary and secondary metabolism. The results revealed that SC16 exhibits enhanced photosynthetic capacity and elevated soluble sugar content in storage roots, whereas FX01 demonstrates superior starch synthesis due to its efficient glucose and fructose phosphorylation. Conversely, SC16 exhibits a faster conversion of $^ { 1 3 } \mathsf { C }$ -labeled ferulic acid, directing carbon flow towards lignin biosynthesis via the phenylpropanoid pathway. Further, by silencing the MeCOMT8 gene, encoding a key enzyme in ferulic acid biosynthesis, we observed a reduction in lignin content and an increase in ADP-glucose levels in the MeCOMT8-silenced cassava plants, suggesting a regulatory link between these competing pathways. Our research elucidated that the variations in carbon allocation between starch and lignin biosynthesis among different cultivars are finely orchestrated through the specific-step alteration of metabolic flux. These findings provide potential candidate targeted points and valuable insights for high-starch breeding in cassava. 

Cassava (Manihot esculenta Crantz) is a major tropical crop with abundant starch stored in its roots. It is used as a raw material for starch production, animal feed, and industrial production in tropical and subtropical regions, providing a staple food to nearly one billion people worldwide1–3 . Breeding cultivars with high starch production is a primary strategy for meeting the huge demand for cassava starch. The starch content in the storage roots of cassava cultivars varies greatly, commonly ranging from 63 to $8 7 \%$ of their dry weight4 . Our previous studies showed a dynamic balance between starch accumulation and cellulose and lignin deposition during the development of cassava storage roots4,5 . A decrease in lignification of storage roots contributes to enhanced starch formation in cassava and sweet potatoes5,6 . 

However, the metabolic partitioning of carbon flux during starch synthesis and lignin deposition in root organs remains largely unknown. 

Starch synthesis is a systemic physiological process involving photosynthesis and rapid sugar transport, and it is an expansive sugar sink in various tissues7 . Previous studies have shown that improving photosynthetic efficiency is associated with increasing yield8 , and Rubisco activity, an important index for carbon assimilation, reaches $4 3 \%$ of the total limitation for photosynthesis across some cultivars9 . Thus, high photosynthetic efficiency serves as a critical driver of rapid root growth and starch accumulation in cassava10. The synthesized sucrose is delivered to the phloem loading zone, where it is deposited into the phloem, transferred to the sieve

tubes of the phloem, and transported into storage roots over long distances11,12. Cassava predominantly transported photosynthates via symplasmic phloem loading and unloading pathway with plasmodesmatal connections7,13,14. Apoplasmic pathways provide auxiliary support as well as invertases, particularly during the developmental transition from fibrous to tuberous roots of cassava. Within storage root cells, sucrose is metabolized by sucrose synthase (SuSy), cell wall-bound acid invertase (CWI), and soluble acid invertase (SAI) to drive efficient starch synthesis15. Starchmetabolic enzymes involved in starch formation and degradation, such as ADP-glucose pyrophosphorylase (AGPase), starch synthase (SS), starch branching enzyme (SBE), and amylase, have been reported16. Additionally, the upregulation of expression of several genes encoding enzymes such as AGPL3, AGPS1a, 1SA1, SBE, and SS2b aligns with the observed starch accumulation17. 

Lignin is a primary structural component of cell walls and vascular tissues, and it plays an indispensable role in water transport18. Rapid formation of vascular tissue can induce the expansion of cassava roots and thus increase the sink of carbohydrates in the roots at an early stage of cassava development5 . Gibberellin promotes root lignin deposition and reduces starch formation in sweet potatoes19. For cassava storage roots, higher lignin content impairs starch accumulation and composition while reducing starch pasting properties4 . Phenylalanine (Phe), synthesized via the shikimate pathway, is a crucial precursor for a diverse array of phenolic compounds in plants, including lignin, lignans, and flavonoids. These Phe-derived compounds account for a significant portion of the total carbon flux in some plants, up to $3 0 \% ^ { 2 0 }$ . Lignin biosynthesis initiates when Phe enters the phenylpropanoid pathway. Upstream of this, hexokinase catalyzes the phosphorylation of glucose to glucose-6-phosphate (G6P), a step that provides carbon sources for the shikimate pathway, and ultimately enables Phe production21. A previous study showed that heterologous expression of the leaf color gene (LC) in sweet potato can increase anthocyanin pigment accumulation with the enhancement of lignin content and reduce starch formation in early storage roots22. This indicates that the modification of phenylpropanoid metabolism could alter carbon allocation between lignin and starch biosynthesis. However, mapping out the precise flow of carbon from photosynthesis to the storage root is more complex, especially when trying to understand the detailed connections between the transport tissues and how starch is made and stored in different cassava types. $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ metabolic flux analysis (MFA) is a powerful tool for developing metabolic maps of plant tissues based on 13Carbon-labeling feeding experiments23. Recent metabolomic studies have illuminated the metabolic network of carbon flux in photosynthetic cells using stable $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ isotope labeling24 and depicted alterations in leaf carbon flow in response to high light intensity and xylem vessel cell differentiation in Arabidopsis25. Depiction of a metabolic flux in the storage root is important for improving storage root quality; however, little information is available for cassava. 

Previously, we showed a negative relationship between starch accumulation and lignin deposition, as well as a decrease in starch pasting properties with high lignin content in cassava starch4,5 . Metabolic flux analysis contributed to the exploration of metabolite divergence between starch accumulation and lignin deposition in cassava storage roots. In this study, isotope $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ labeling was employed to map the metabolic divergence between starch and lignin formation pathways in cassava storage roots. We also examined variations in photosynthetic phenotypes, crucial gene expression or silencing, and enzyme activities between two cassava cultivars. Our findings revealed that the phosphorylation efficiency of glucose-6- phosphate (G-6-P) is the key determinant of starch metabolic flux during storage root development. In contrast, the rate of lignin deposition was dependent on the active conversion of caffeic acid to ferulic acid. We demonstrated that silencing the MeCOMT8 gene, which encodes a transferase catalyzing the conversion of caffeic acid to ferulic acid, led to a decrease in the precursor availability for the monolignol pathway. This, in turn, resulted in reduced lignin deposition and increased starch content in vivo. The catalytic activity of MeCOMT8 was also confirmed in vitro, 

further supporting its critical role in lignin biosynthesis. We proposed a metabolic flux model for storage roots to provide previously unidentified insights into metabolism that can improve the quality of storage roots through metabolic modulation. 

# Results

# Metabolic differences in photosynthesis, sugar transport and its conversion in cassava cultivars

Improved photosynthesis leads to increased plant productivity and yield26,27. Leaf photosynthetic products are transported to the roots, primarily forming starch and vascular tissues during the development of the cassava storage root system. Metabolic competition for carbon flux between the structural components (such as lignin and cellulose) and starch accumulation is common in storage crops (Fig. 1a). Our previous study demonstrated a negative correlation between starch content and lignin deposition in cassava storage roots, highlighting a trade-off in carbon allocation5 . To dissect the divergence in carbon flux, FX01 and SC16 cultivars identified as high starch and low starch were used in this study. The results showed that the net leaf photosynthetic rate in SC16 was significantly higher than that for FX01 (Fig. 1b), which was consistent with the higher ribulose-1, 5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase (RuBisCO) activity in SC16 (Fig. 1c). This is supported by the fact that a higher net leaf photosynthetic rate positively correlates with higher PEP carboxylase activity28. Stomatal conductance and transpiration rate were also observed to be significantly higher in SC16 compared to FX01 (Fig. 1d, e). Nevertheless, an intriguing observation was that the enhanced photosynthesis in SC16 failed to result in an increased starch content within its storage roots (Fig. 1f), whereas lignin content exhibited a higher trend in SC16 (Fig. 1g). Sugar transport can affect carbon flux allocation through transporters such as SWEETs, sucrose transporters (SUTs), and sugar transport protein (STP)11,29–31. Therefore, we also quantified soluble sugar and sucrose content in different cassava tissues to investigate the potential differences in sucrose transport between the two cassava cultivars (Figure. S1). Soluble sugar content in roots was higher in SC16 than that of FX01. Despite the higher sucrose content in leaves for cultivar FX01, it was similar in root between two cultivars, suggesting that sucrose availability in storage roots was not the primary determinant underlying the divergent starch accumulation phenotypes (Fig. S1d). When combined with the higher sucrose synthase activity observed in roots of FX01 (Fig. 1h), these results indicated that the metabolism efficiency of sucrose following unloading, rather than sucrose transport or availability, served as the key driver of the contrasting starch accumulation patterns. Sucrose hydrolysis catalyzed by cell wall invertase (CWI) varied in storage roots between cultivars. CWI activity was significantly higher in SC16 storage roots compared to FX01 (Fig. 1i). This enzyme is likely to play a minor role in cassava sucrose metabolism due to the less enzymatic activity than other enzymes. Notably, SuSy activity was significantly higher in FX01 storage roots compared to SC16 (Fig. 1h), suggesting more efficient sucrose catabolism inducing starch accumulation in the high-starch cultivar. SuSy catalyzes the reversible conversion of sucrose and UDP to UDP-glucose and fructose, serving as the primary route for sucrose metabolism in the symplastic pathway. The resulting UDP-glucose is converted to glucose-1-phosphate (G1P) by UDP-glucose pyrophosphorylase, and G1P is subsequently converted to G6P by phosphoglucomutase. G6P is then transported into plastids, where it is converted back to G1P by plastidial phosphoglucomutase. G1P is subsequently used to produce ADP-glucose via ADP-glucose pyrophosphorylase, which serves as the substrate for starch biosynthesis32. To evaluate the capacity of sucrose synthesis, sucrose-phosphate synthase (SPS) activity was determined under both substrate-limiting and saturating conditions. No significant difference of SPS activity was observed between two cultivars under substrate-limiting conditions. Whereas, SC16 showed significantly higher SPS activity under saturating conditions, suggesting the greater sucrose synthesis capacity dependent on substrate supply (Fig. 1j).

# Metabolic profiles in lignin-synthesized and starch-synthesized pathway in the storage root of cassava cultivars

Non-targeted metabolomics is based on peak deconvolution and exclusion of ion features. The differential variables were screened based on orthogonal partial least squares discriminant analysis (OPLS-DA) combined with orthogonal signal correction (OSC) and PLS-DA methods. Hierarchical clustering analysis revealed distinct metabolic profiles between FX01 and SC16 storage roots, with clear separation of the two cultivars based on their metabolite abundances (Fig. 2a). In total, 772 metabolites were preliminarily identified from the chromatograms using broad fragmentation patterns available from a mass spectrometry databank (https://www.metaboanalyst. ca/). There were 60 metabolites with higher levels and 373 metabolites with lower levels in storage root of SC16 than in FX01, and 339 metabolites with no significant difference between them (Fig. 2b). The detected metabolites were primarily amino acids, terpenoids, phenylpropanoids, alcohols, aromatics, phenols, nucleosides, flavonoids, alkaloids, and sugars, which is consistent with the major metabolites found in cassava storage roots. To understand the biological significance of these changes, KEGG pathway enrichment analysis was performed on the differentially accumulated metabolites. This analysis revealed that upregulated metabolites in SC16 were enriched in 20 metabolic pathways (Fig. 2c), with phenylpropanoid biosynthesis showing the strongest enrichment, followed by pathways for secondary metabolite production and amino acid metabolism. Conversely, downregulated metabolites were enriched in primary metabolic pathways (Fig. 2d), particularly flavonoid biosynthesis and purine metabolism. Most compounds involved in lignin synthesis and flavor synthesis showed higher levels in SC16 including ferulic acid 4-sulfate, L-phenylalanine, etc. (Fig. 2e). 

# Average $^ { 1 3 } \pmb { \complement }$ enrichment of key starch metabolites at different time points post-labeling

Sucrose originating from leaf photosynthetic products is mainly transported into cassava roots, and used for the formation of starch, structural 

components, and energy metabolism, which are mainly involved in starch synthesis and shikimate and phenylpropane metabolism (Fig. 3a). Two healthy cassava cultivars were grown until the visible bulking stage of storage roots (140 days after planting) (Fig. S2), and the photosynthetic products of the cassava leaves were tracked by injecting $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ into a closed device (Fig. S3). Cassava plants did not suffer from oxidation damages in response to the labeling treatment (Fig. S4). To capture the progressive metabolic changes during storage root development, we monitored $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ incorporation dynamics at multiple developmental stages. The isotopic enrichment of critical metabolic intermediates (ADP-glucose, UDP-glucose, and hexose phosphates) was tracked at four intervals (3, 6, 9, and 12 days) following pulse-labeling in both high starch (FX01) and low starch (SC16) cultivars (Fig. 3b-h). The carbon allocation patterns along the extension of time showed pronounced changes between starch accumulation and lignin deposition pathways in two cultivars likely due to the divergence of carbon flux competition. The progressive enrichment patterns observed across time points suggested that high-starch cultivar showed the more rapid metabolic flux in starch synthesis pathway than that of low-starch cultivar. FX01 exhibited progressive $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ enrichment in metabolites leading to starch synthesis (including UDP-glucose, G6P, G1P, and ADP-glucose; Fig. 3b, fh), consistently surpassing SC16 levels. While hexokinase (HXK) phosphorylates free glucose to G6P in a secondary pathway, we measured its activity as an indicator of overall hexose phosphorylation capacity. Enzyme assays confirmed that both AGPase and HXK activities were significantly higher in FX01 than those in SC16 (Fig. 7d, e). These findings align with the observed starch accumulation rates, which consistently favored FX01 over SC16 (Fig. 7f). We hypothesize that the differences in starch content between these cultivars may also influence the composition of structural components, such as lignin. To further investigate this, we assessed the expression levels of genes associated with lignin biosynthesis. Specifically, genes involved in lignin synthesis, including MeCAD15, MeCOMT1, MeCOMT8, and MeCCR2 were found to be significantly upregulated in low-starch SC16 cultivar (Fig. 7g, h, i, j). We further examined the activity of cinnamyl alcohol dehydrogenase (CAD) across 

multiple time points. CAD plays a pivotal role in catalyzing the final step of monolignol conversion to lignin, thus influencing lignin deposition. CAD enzyme activity assays conducted on SC16 cultivars at various time points consistently revealed higher CAD activity compared to FX01 (Fig. 7k), which corresponds directly with the faster lignin accumulation rate observed in SC16 (Fig. 7l). Additionally, we observed that the accumulation of structural carbohydrate (cellulose) was much greater in SC16 than in FX01 (Figure. S6). These findings indicate a contrasting relationship between starch synthesis and lignin deposition in two cultivars, highlighting the complexities of carbohydrate metabolism in cassava.

# Discussion

Generally, a higher photosynthetic capacity in crops is associated with increased plant biomass production and yield26,27. Consequently, the selection for high photosynthetic efficiency emerges as a pivotal long-term strategy in crop breeding programs. In this study, we investigated the intricate changes in metabolic flux, and observed that a higher net photosynthetic rate in cassava cultivars with more RuBisCO enzyme activity is not necessarily accompanied by higher starch content in storage roots (Fig. 1). Cultivar SC16 exhibited higher soluble sugar content in storage roots than FX01, particularly elevated sucrose levels (Fig. S1). However, this sucrose accumulation does not reflect superior unloading capacity, as cassava relies on symplasmic transport via plasmodesmata, which is an apoplasticindependent process7 . Instead, the higher sucrose content in SC16 storage roots stems from metabolic backlogging caused by inefficient sucrose catabolism, as evidenced by significantly lower sucrose synthase activity in SC16 compared to FX01 (Fig. 1h). This interpretation is further supported by $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -labeling data that despite sufficient sucrose availability, SC16 showed slower incorporation of labeled carbon into starch precursors. Cell wall invertase (CWI) catalyzes apoplastic sucrose hydrolysis, regulating sugar availability and allocation in plant storage organs34. Given cassava’s symplastic unloading strategy, the higher CWI activity observed in SC16, rather than a primary unloading mechanism, now appears to be a compensatory response to metabolic backlogging. While CWI plays a minor role in overall sucrose metabolism in cassava, its upregulation in SC16 likely reflects an attempt to compensate for suboptimal intracellular sucrose processing by enhancing apoplastic hydrolysis. However, this alternative pathway is insufficient to match the efficiency of the symplastic sucrose catabolism that FX01 achieves through higher sucrose synthase activity, ultimately driving differential starch accumulation between cultivars. Sucrose synthase conserves the energy of the glycosidic bond by directly producing UDP-glucose, requiring only one ATP for subsequent starch synthesis, whereas the invertase pathway requires two ATPs to phosphorylate the resulting hexoses. This energetic advantage is particularly critical in storage organs, where massive carbon flux toward starch requires metabolic efficiency. The combination of enhanced SPS activity (under substrate-saturating conditions), and elevated CWI activity in SC16 indicates an active futile cycle of sucrose synthesis and degradation. This metabolic cycling imposes a substantial energetic burden, reducing starch accumulation in SC16. Additionally, continuous sucrose turnover dilutes the $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ isotopic signal via repeated substrate mixing, explaining the delayed metabolite enrichment in SC16. Collectively, these metabolic features suggest that SC16 prioritizes vegetative growth and structural development over carbohydrate storage, representing a fundamental difference in resource allocation strategy between cultivars. This growth-oriented metabolism contrasts sharply with the storage-focused carbon partitioning in 

FX01, highlighting divergent breeding outcomes for yield versus biomass production. 

Sucrose, as a primary energy source, is transported to roots, where it plays a crucial role in conversion of structural components and starch during the development of storage roots. Sucrose catabolism in cassava storage roots is predominantly catalyzed by $\operatorname { S u S y } ^ { 3 8 }$ , which cleaves sucrose into UDP-glucose and fructose. UDPglucose is converted to starch precursors. Fructose is usually phosphorylated and then re-enters the hexose-phosphate equilibrium primarily mediated by phosphoglucomutase (PGM) and phosphoglucose isomerase $( \mathrm { P G I } ) ^ { 3 9 }$ . Hexokinase (HXK) can phosphorylate both glucose and fructose, though its affinity for glucose is far higher, as evidenced by a Km one to three orders of magnitude lower than that for fructose40. In contrast, fructokinase (FRK) exclusively phosphorylates fructose to fructose-6-phosphate with high substrate affinity41. Although the low-starch cultivar SC16 exhibited elevated D-fructose transfer rates, the high-starch cultivar FX01 accumulated significantly more D-fructose-6-phosphate (Fig. 4a, c). This divergence was particularly pronounced when analyzing M6 isotopomer patterns at 12 days post-labeling, where FX01 converted a substantially larger proportion of substrate into M6-fructose-6-phosphate (Fig. 4b, d). These findings suggest that the high starch accumulation in FX01 is not solely driven by substrate availability but relies on an enhanced capacity to recycle the fructose generated by SuSy. This efficient recycling mechanism prevents metabolic inhibition and ensures that the full carbon flux initiated by SuSy is effectively captured and directed into the amyloplasts for starch biosynthesis. 

Although the $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ accumulation rate of sucrose transported from leaves was lower in the FX01 cultivar compared to SC16, the levels of key carbohydrate intermediates, such as glucose-6-phosphate (G6P), UDPglucose, and ADP-glucose, were higher in FX01 than in SC16 (Fig. 4b). This finding implies that FX01 has a robust capacity for starch synthesis by supplying ample precursor substances. Previous studies have demonstrated that the upregulation of starch-synthesis genes, including AGPase, SSs, GBSS, and SBEs, can enhance starch accumulation in potato somaclones42. Furthermore, antisense inhibition of AGPase has been shown to limit starch synthesis in V. narbonensis43 and influence starch synthesis in tomatoes44. The high-starch cultivar FX01 showed significantly higher transcription of MeAGPase1.3, MeSSⅢ, and MeGBSS-1 compared to low-starch cultivar SC16 (Fig. 7a-c). This upregulation corresponded with enhanced AGPase enzymatic activity (Fig. 7d), indicating more sucrose to starch efficient metabolic flux in FX01 storage roots. Direct assays of starch accumulation rates confirmed this accelerated biosynthesis (Fig. 7f). These findings align with recent evidence that AGPase activity, particularly via the MeAPL3 isoform, controls starch content variation across cassava genotypes45. Collectively, our results indicate that high AGPase activity as a key driver of starch synthesis, enables a greater carbon source toward starch and redirects less to structural components in storage roots.

In the high-starch cultivar, rapid glucose and fructose phosphorylation is reinforced, promoting starch synthesis, while lignin deposition is limited, likely due to slower ferulic acid production from caffeic acid. Conversely, in the low-starch cultivar, reduced hexose phosphorylation and faster ferulic acid production likely contribute to lower starch content and higher lignin deposition. However, it is plausible that rapid lignin and cellulose synthesis itself leads to lower starch accumulation. Gene expression and enzyme activities were consistent with metabolic flux changes between cultivars, suggesting a trade-off between structural components and starch accumulation in storage roots. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials

Plant growth experiments were conducted at the Life Science and Technology experimental farm station of Guangxi University, situated in Nanning $( 2 2 ^ { \circ } 4 9 ^ { \prime } 1 2 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { N }$ , $1 0 8 ^ { \circ } 1 9 ^ { \prime } 1 1 ^ { \prime \prime } \mathrm { W } )$ ), China. Two cassava cultivars were used: Fuxuan01 (FX01), obtained from the university, and Huanan16 (SC16), sourced from the Institute of Tropical Bioscience and Biotechnology, Chinese Academy of Tropical Agricultural Sciences. FX01, a high-starch, low-lignin cultivar, is a Cobalt 60 radiation-induced mutant of SC124 and is widely cultivated for its favorable characteristics. Conversely, SC16 has recently been released for its high yield, but it features a higher lignin and lower starch content in the storage roots. High- and low- starch (FX01/SC16) cassava cultivars were planted in pots $4 0 \mathrm { c m }$ diameter $\times ~ 2 0 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ depth) and grown in a greenhouse at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ $5 0 \%$ relative humidity, and a $1 6 { : } 8 \mathrm { h }$ day: night cycles, with a light density of $3 0 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } ~ \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ .

The net photosynthetic rate, transpiration rate, and stomatal conductance were measured in the upmost sixth fully expanded leaf using an LI-6400 portable photosynthetic system (LI-COR Biosciences, Lincoln, USA) between 9:00 and11:00 a.m. on a clear morning prior to $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ labeling. # $\scriptscriptstyle \mathfrak { s } _ { \pmb { \complement } }$ pulse labeling

$^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ pulse labeling was carried out in a controlled chamber in April 2023. To ensure that the storage roots were at a uniform developmental stage, $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ labeling experiments were performed 140 days post-planting, aligning with the period when both storage roots had attained a similar stage. Twenty-four cassava plants (soil surface covered with black mulch) were labeled through leaf photosynthesis for further carbon flux analysis in FX01 and SC16. The chamber $1 . 6 \mathrm { m }$ width $\times 1 . 6 \mathrm { m }$ breadth $\times 1 . 8 \mathrm { m }$ height) was made with a wooden frame (Fig. S3). A transparent polyethylene film with more than $9 0 \%$ light transmittance covered the surface, making it airtight50. An antifogging agent was applied to the inside walls of the labeling chamber to reduce water vapor condensation during $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ pulse labeling, increase light intensity, and decrease $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ dissolved in water drops. To reduce $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ in the labeling chamber, a vacuum pump and 1 M NaOH solution were used to absorb the initial $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ before starting the labeling experiment, and then $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ was released by injecting 1 M HCl into $1 0 \mathrm { g }$ of $\mathrm { B a } ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C O } _ { 3 }$ $9 8 \ \mathrm { a t o m \% }$ $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C } )$ ). The amount of supplied $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C }$ -bicarbonate $( 2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { C O } _ { 2 } \mathrm { h o u r } ^ { - 1 } \mathrm { c m } ^ { - 2 } )$ was varied to match the duration of the experiment and calculated on the basis of net assimilation of cassava51. The control chamber was treated with unlabeled $\mathrm { B a C O } _ { 3 }$ . The 1 M HCl solution was added five times $2 0 0 \mathrm { m L }$ each), with the $2 0 0 \mathrm { m L }$ slowly added with a syringe from outside the chamber into the solid $\mathrm { B a } ^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C O } _ { 3 }$ to maintain the chamber $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ at approximately 400 parts per million (ppm) across the labeling duration of 10 hours labeling period, which spanned from $9 { : } 0 0 \mathrm { a . m }$ . to $6 { : } 0 0 \mathrm { p . m }$ . Two electric fans (5 V, 8 mm in diameter) were used for headspace gas mixing inside the chamber. The $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ concentration in the chamber was monitored using an infrared gas analyzer (CIRAS III, PP-systems, Amesbury, MA, USA), which detects both $^ { 1 2 } \mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ and $^ { 1 3 } \mathrm { C O } _ { 2 } ^ { 5 0 }$ .

# Metabolic assay

One hundred milligrams of fresh tissues (storage roots) were finely ground into powder using a mortar and pestle, subsequently dissolved in $1 0 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of a cold 75:20:5 (v/v/v) methanol: $\mathrm { d d H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ : formic acid solution containing $3 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ of DDTC, and allowed to soak at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 6 \mathrm { h }$ . The mixture was then vortexed and centrifuged at $\mathbf { 1 0 , 0 0 0 \times g }$ at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The supernatant was carefully aspirated and subsequently dried using a vacuum concentrator (Eppendorf concentrator 5301, Eppendorf AG 22331, Hamburg, Germany) at a temperature of $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Four hundred microliters of methanol were added to re-suspend the dried aspirate, which was then filtered through a $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter (Costar, Corning Inc.). The filtered solution was transferred to glass vials (Agilent, Xpertek) for analysis by liquid chromatography-quadrupole Exactive Orbitrap tandem mass spectrometry (UHPLC-Q Exactive Orbitrap MS/MS; Thermo Fisher Scientific), equipped with a heated electrospray ionization (HESI) source, operating in both positive and negative ionization modes. Chromatographic separation was achieved using a binary mobile phase system consisting of solvent A (water containing $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid) and solvent B (methanol). A linear gradient elution program was employed as follows: 0-2 min, $9 5 \%$ A; 2-13 min, $9 5 \mathrm { - } 0 \%$ A; 13-16 min, $0 \%$ A; 16-16.1 min, $0 . 9 5 \%$ A, and 16.1-19 min, $9 5 \%$ A. The injection volume was set to $2 \mu \mathrm { L } ,$ with a flow rate of $0 . 3 \mathrm { m L } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ . The column compartment and autosampler tray were maintained at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $1 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ respectively, to ensure optimal chromatographic performance and sample stability. The mass spectrometer was operated under optimized conditions, with the following key parameters: spray voltage, 3 kV; capillary temperature, $3 2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ;$ sheath gas flow rate, 35 arbitrary units; and auxiliary gas flow rate, 10 arbitrary units. Nitrogen was used as both the sheath and auxiliary gas. The auxiliary gas was heated to $3 5 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to aid in solvent evaporation and improve ionization efficiency. Data acquisition was performed in Full MS and Full MS/data-dependent MS2 (Full MS/DD-MS2) scanning modes, covering a mass range of $2 0 { - } 2 0 0 0 \mathrm { m / z }$ . The resolution settings for the primary (Full MS) and secondary (MS2) scans were 70,000 and 17,500, respectively, ensuring high mass accuracy and resolving power for confident metabolite identification. 12 reference standards were prepared and analyzed under the same LC-MS/MS conditions used for the sample analysis. These standards were specifically chosen to represent the broad spectrum of metabolites anticipated in this study. The reference standards were diluted across a concentration range of 0.1 to $1 0 0 0 \mathrm { n g } \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ . Raw data files were processed using Compound Discoverer 3.2 software (Thermo Fisher Scientific) for metabolite profiling. Individual metabolite peaks were identified by comparing their parent ion/daughter ion pairs and retention times with those of authentic standards analyzed under identical conditions (Figure. S9). 

# Differential metabolite analysis

Root samples were collected from cassava plants prior to isotopic labeling and analyzed by LC-MS/MS. Raw peak intensities were normalized using Z-score transformation to enable sample comparisons. Hierarchical clustering analysis was performed on normalized metabolite abundances, with results visualized as heatmaps to reveal metabolic patterns between cultivars SC16 and FX01. Differential metabolites were defined as |log2 fold change $| \geq 1$ and adjusted $P < 0 . 0 5$ after false discovery rate correction. Results were visualized using volcano plots, with upregulated metabolites shown in red and downregulated metabolites in blue. Pathway enrichment analysis was performed by mapping differentially accumulated metabolites to the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) database. Enriched pathways were identified using hypergeometric tests with a significance threshold of $P < 0 . 0 5$ . Pathway enrichment results were presented as bar plots, where color intensity represents statistical significance and bar length indicates the metabolic impact score, calculated based on the proportion of affected metabolites within each pathway.

# Physiological and biochemical assays

Root samples were collected from cassava plants at four distinct time points: 3, 6, 9, and 12 days following labeling. At each time point, three plants were destructively sampled. To preserve metabolite integrity, the samples were quickly flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen. For starch, lignin, and cellulose quantification, frozen samples were lyophilized and ground to a fine powder. Total starch, lignin, and cellulose contents were determined on a dry weight basis following previously established methods4 . Soluble sugar content was measured in fresh tissue using the anthrone method29. 

Starch content was quantified using a commercial detection kit (BC0700; Solarbio, Beijing, China). Fresh tuber tissue $\mathrm { 3 0 m g ) }$ ) was extracted with $0 . 6 \mathrm { m L }$ Reagent I at $8 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 min, centrifuged at ${ 3 , 0 0 0 \times \bf { g } }$ for 5 min, and the precipitate was resuspended in 0.3 mL distilled water. After boiling for 15 min and treatment with $0 . 6 \mathrm { m L }$ Reagent II, samples were centrifuged at ${ 8 , 0 0 0 \times \mathrm { ~ g ~ } }$ for 15 min. Starch concentration was determined spectrophotometrically at $6 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . Lignin content was measured using the acetyl bromide method (BC4205; Solarbio). Dried tuber powder (3 mg) underwent sequential extraction with multiple reagents at specified temperatures. Following extraction with Reagent IV and V at $8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , samples were mixed with ice-cold acetic acid and absorbance was measured at 280 nm. For cellulose determination, fresh tuber tissue $( 0 . 3 \ : \mathrm { g } )$ was extracted at $9 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 20 min, washed with acetone, and treated with extraction solution II for $1 5 \mathrm { h }$ . The resulting cell wall material was dried at $6 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and weighed. Cellulose content in 5 mg of dried material was quantified using the anthrone method, with absorbance measured at $6 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ . Soluble sugar content was analyzed by extracting 100 mg fresh tissue in boiling water for 30 min. After centrifugation at $6 { , } 0 0 0 \times { } { \bf g }$ for $1 0 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ , the supernatant was diluted and reacted with anthrone in concentrated sulfuric acid. Absorbance was measured at $6 2 0 \mathrm { n m }$ using an Epoch Microplate Spectrophotometer (BioTek, USA). 

Seven key enzymes were analyzed using commercial kits. ADP-glucose pyrophosphorylase (AGPase) (MM-4719402, Meimian, China) and RuBisCO (MM-060302, Meimian, China) activities were determined by ELISA, with absorbance measured at $4 5 0 \mathrm { n m }$ against standard curves. Both enzyme activities were expressed as units per gram fresh weight. Sucrose synthase (BC4310, Solarbio, China) activity was measured in the degradative direction. Tissue homogenates were incubated with UDP and sucrose at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ and fructose release was quantified colorimetrically at $5 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ following reaction with 3,5-dinitrosalicylic acid. Cell wall-bound acid invertase (BC4320, Solarbio, China) was assayed similarly, measuring reducing sugar 

formation from sucrose hydrolysis. Hexokinase activity (A077-3, Nanjing Jiancheng, China) was determined through a coupled enzyme assay monitoring NADPH generation at $3 4 0 \mathrm { n m }$ , as glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase oxidized the HXK product glucose-6-phosphate. Cinnamylalcohol dehydrogenase (BC4170, Solarbio, China) was measured following Sun et al. (2023)5 . SPS activity was measured in storage root extracts under both substrate-limiting and saturating conditions according to Huber and Israel (1982) with modifications52. For substrate-limiting conditions, the assay contained $5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ HEPES-NaOH (pH 7.5), 15 mM $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ , 1 mM EDTA, 5 mM fructose-6-phosphate, and 3 mM UDP-glucose. For saturating conditions, concentrations were increased to $2 0 \mathrm { m M }$ fructose-6- phosphate and $1 5 \mathrm { m M }$ UDP-glucose. Reactions were initiated by adding enzyme extract and incubated at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 30 min. Sucrose-phosphate formation was determined after phosphatase treatment by measuring sucrose content. All enzyme activities were calculated from differential absorbance values and expressed as specific activity units per gram fresh weight or per milligram protein.

# Virus-induced gene silencing

The MeCOMT8 gene was cloned from the SC16 genotype, with the silenced fragment specifically designed based on the MeCOMT8 gene sequence using specialized software (https://vigs.solgenomics.net/). A segment of the MeCOMT8 gene was subsequently incorporated into the pTRV2 vector, enabling the production of silenced MeCOMT8 cassava plants via a virusinduced gene silencing system previously described by Fan et al. $( 2 0 2 3 ) ^ { 2 9 }$ . Cassava plants (SC16) were grown for approximately six weeks prior to initiating gene silencing. Lignin and starch content were then measured 14 days after silencing. Further analyses were conducted in line with the method described53, and the primer sequences are provided in Table S1 for reference.

Root segments $( 0 . 5 \mathrm { c m } )$ were collected $5 c \mathrm { { m } }$ from the root tip of silenced and non-silenced cassava plants. The prepared sections were mounted in $5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ distilled water and examined for lignin autofluorescence using an Olympus SZ61 microscope (Japan) at 407 nm excitation wavelength. 

# Heterologous expression and purification of recombinant MeCOMT8 in E. coli

The pGEX-4T-1 vector was digested with BamHI (NEB, Beijing, China) to allow integration of the MeCOMT8 open reading frames, which were subsequently cloned using the ClonExpress Kit (Vazyme, Nanjing, China). 

These constructs were then introduced into E. coli BL21 (DE3) cells for protein production. Protein induction was carried out at $1 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 4 \mathrm { h }$ with $0 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ isopropyl- $\cdot \beta$ -D-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG). The recombinant MeCOMT8 protein was purified using a GST-tag affinity kit (Beyotime, Shanghai, China), its purity was assessed via SDS-PAGE, and protein concentration was determined by Bradford assay (Solarbio, Beijing, China).

# Statistics and reproducibility

All experiments were independently carried out and replicated a total of three times. Each replicate was analyzed individually to determine the maximum biological variation. Biological replicates represent independent plants grown under identical conditions. All experiments were conducted with at least three biologically independent replicates $\left( n \geq 3 \right)$ unless otherwise stated in figure legends, with each replicate analyzed individually to determine the maximum biological variation. Specific sample sizes: $n = 3$ for metabolomics and isotope labeling experiments; $n = 4 – 6$ for enzyme assays and photosynthesis measurements. 

The data are presented as mean values derived from the three independent replicates. Statistical analyses were performed using Student’s ttests, and multiple comparisons of means were subjected to Tukey’s test. A $\boldsymbol { p }$ -value of less than 0.05 $\left. ^ { \prime } P < 0 . 0 5 \right)$ was suggestive of a statistically significant difference. Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism 9.0. All experiments showing representative data were independently repeated at least three times with similar results.
