# Abstract

Glucosinolates (GSLs) are plant secondary metabolites comprising sulfur and nitrogen mainly found in plants from the order of Brassicales, such as broccoli, cabbage, and Arabidopsis thaliana. The activated forms of GSL play important roles in fighting against pathogens and have health benefits to humans. The increasing amount of data on A. thaliana generated from various omics technologies can be dug deeper in search for new genes and/or compounds involved in the GSL biosynthesis and metabolism. This review describes a comprehensive inventory of A. thaliana GSLs identified from published literature and databases such as KNApSAcK, KEGG, and AraCyc. A total of 113 GSL genes encoding for 23 transcription components, 85 enzymes, and five protein transporters were experimentally characterized in the past two decades. Continuous efforts are still on going to identify all molecules related to

the production of GSLs. A manually curated database known as SuCCombase (http://plantscc.org) was developed to serve as a comprehensive GSL inventory. Realizing lack of information on the regulation of GSL biosynthesis and degradation mechanism, this review also includes relevant information and their connections with crosstalks between various factors, such as light, sulfur metabolism, and nitrogen metabolism, not only in A. thaliana but also in other crucifers.

Keywords: aliphatic glucosinolates; indolic glucosinolates; nitrogen-containing compounds; secondary metabolites

# Introduction

Plants are sessile, hence they require a systemic defense system that enables them to effectively recognize invading molecules in cells damaged by herbivores or pathogens and immediately activate immune responses1,2. Several molecules such as proteins and specialized secondary metabolites with anti-nutritional effects that are toxic and repellent to pests are produced to overcome pest invasion3,4. Plant secondary metabolites can be grouped into three chemically distinctive main classes, i.e., terpenes, phenolics, and nitrogen-containing compounds5. There are several classes of nitrogen-containing compounds such as alkaloids, cyanogenic glycosides, nonprotein amino acids (NPAAs), and glucosinolates. Glucosinolates (GSLs) are sulfur- and nitrogen-containing compounds involved in plant defense against herbivores and pathogens. They are commonly found in the cruciferous vegetables of the Brassicaceae family, which includes the model plant, Arabidopsis thaliana, broccoli, cabbage, cauliflower, cress, kale, and mustard6.

Plant glucosinolates (GSLs) attract increased interest for their property as a cancer preventive agent7–9. Glucoraphanin (or 4-methylsulphinylbutyl GSL) is a derivative from aliphatic GSL,

also known as a precursor for bioactive isothiocyanates, also called sulforaphane10. Several animal studies were conducted on the ability of sulforaphane to inhibit the carcinogenic cells of malignant rodent tissues at early or late stages, including liver, lung11, colon12, breast13, prostate14, and pancreas15.

Another type of GSL known as indolic is associated with phytochemicals, such as auxins16,17 and phytoalexins18,19. Previous studies on the modified indolic GSLs and their hydrolysis products showed their roles in plant-pathogen interactions. For example, isothiocyanates (ITCs), 4-hydroxy-indole-3-yl-methyl GSL, and 4-methoxy-indole-3-yl-methyl GSL are involved in plant resistance against pests20–23 as well as plant innate immunity24–26. Indole-3- carbinol (I3C) is an indolic GSL that has a role in guiding the oviposition of specific insects in crucifers27,28. I3C is produced from the breakdown of glucobrassicin (3-indolylmethyl GSL) and was recognized as a dietary supplement in managing cancer, especially for women recuperating from breast cancer29. Previous studies on human cells showed that I3C inhibits cell-cycle progression, tumor invasion, and metastasis30,31. More recent studies have suggested that I3C can induce apoptosis in several human cells, such as colorectal cancer cell lines32 and osteosarcoma cells33.

Benzyl isothiocyanate (BITC) is another type of active GSL produced from benzyl GSL. Several studies have reported the capability of BITC to suppress the progression of a variety of cancers, which include breast34–36, colon37, and pancreatic38 tumors. It was also found to inhibit the growth and toxin production of Escherichia coli and Staphylococcus aureus, therefore suggesting its potential as a therapeutic agent against bacterial infections39.

In GSL-producing plants, the conversion of inactive GSL precursors to isothiocyanates is facilitated by a myrosinase enzyme known as $\beta$ -thioglucoside. This enzyme and the inactive GSL are shown to co-exist and located in different plant organelles40,41. The bioactive

isothiocyanates and other GSL end-products such as nitriles, thiocyanates, epithionitriles, and oxazolidine-2-thiones are produced in damaged cells during microbial and herbivore attacks as well as physical processes, such as chewing and food preparation6.

To date, there are more than 130 scientifically documented GSL structures found in A. thaliana and related plants42. Table 1 shows a list of GSLs identified in A. thaliana from several sources, including the KNApSAcK database and previously published literature. In recent years, many studies have successfully identified GSL biosynthetic genes using various approaches in systems biology, including mutant studies, quantitative trait loci (QTL) mapping, and genomewide association studies (GWAS)8,43. The importance of uncovering the components involved in GSL biosynthesis is to increase the production of beneficial GSLs, such as the anticarcinogenic glucoraphanin through classical breeding44, transgenics or synthetic biology approaches45. Despite the health-beneficial effects of the GSL-containing vegetables, GSLs such as sinigrin and progoitrin contribute to sharp and bitter flavors of these crucifer plants44,46. A high concentration of both GSLs (sinigrin and progoitrin) might contribute to the poor taste linked to human rejection of the consumption of Brussels sprouts47. Based on a comprehensive review on the taste and flavor perceptions of GSLs and ITCs48, there are multiple factors to be considered in crop improvement. These include the individual GSLs synthesized and cooking methods as well as the composition of GSL end-products (ITCs and nitriles)48.

For plant scientists, the information about the biosynthesis and regulation of GSLs function in Brassicales crops is based on studies with the reference pathway of the model plant A. thaliana 45,49,50. A comparative genomics study between Arabidopsis and $B$ . rapa identified the core GSL biosynthetic genes that share a sequence homology of up to $8 4 . 3 \%$ in a genome-wide level. Another study investigated the expression of GSL biosynthetic genes in twelve $B$ . oleracea edible organ genotypes covering subspecies of cabbage, kale, kohlrabi, and

cauliflower. The study identified 84 GSL genes associated with aliphatic, indolic, and aromatic as well as those related to the transcription and degradation pathways. However, only eight genes were expressed in kohlrabi, while the majority were expressed in cabbage, kale, and cauliflower51. Thus, a detailed inventory of GSL genes is crucial in providing an up-to-date list of components involved in the production of GSLs.

A comprehensive review on the elucidation of A. thaliana GSL biosynthetic pathway components was published a decade ago describing the three main phases (side-chain elongation, core structure synthesis, and side-chain modification) of GSL biosynthetic processes8. However, genes encoding proteins that regulate the corresponding GSL biosynthetic pathways and transporters involved are lacking. Thus, this review highlights new GSL genes including biosynthetic enzymes, degradation enzymes, regulators, and transporters with supporting experimental evidences. A comprehensive GSL biosynthetic pathway is manually constructed using information from the literature and pathway databases (KEGG and AraCyc). Transcriptional components that are involved in the GSL biosynthesis are described with discussion on the effects of nitrogen and sulfur supply on the production of GSLs52–54. All information on the identified GSLs and GSL genes in A. thaliana can be found in a continuously updated public database, SuCComBase55.

# Identification of genes involved in the glucosinolate pathway

GSLs are divided into three main categories: (1) aliphatic GSLs derived from alanine, leucine, isoleucine, valine or predominantly methionine; (2) indolic GSLs derived from tryptophan; (3) aromatic GSLs derived from phenylalanine or tyrosine. All information related to GSL biosynthesis was thoroughly searched from published literature and two pathway databases, i.e., AraCyc version 17.1 and KEGG version 94.1. From the identified 113 GSL genes, eighty-

five are biosynthetic genes with supporting experimental evidences (Figure 1), in which thirtytwo genes are newly described in this review as annotated in Table 2.

All the identified A. thaliana GSL genes are listed in Table 2 with supporting references. A total of fifty-three genes are related to the GSL pathway in either KEGG or AraCyc databases, and thirty-one genes exist in either KEGG or AraCyc but are not annotated in the GSL biosynthetic pathway. Only one gene (UGT74C1) with supporting literature evidence but not found in both pathway databases56. Most of the thirty-one GSL genes are involved in the production of various essential co-substrates in GSL biosynthesis, such as glutathione (GSH), 3’-phosphoadenosine-5’-phosphosulfate (PAPS), benzoyl-CoA, and sinapoyl-CoA (Figure 2).

The production of GSL requires the cooperation between GSL groups which have specific roles, such as TFs, transporters, and enzymes that are involved in the side-chain elongation, core structure synthesis, and the side-chain modification (Figure 2). GSL genes that are involved in the biosynthesis of aliphatic, indolic, and aromatic GSLs will be described in different sections of this review.

# Aliphatic GSL Biosynthesis

Aliphatic GSL derivatives are the majority of GSLs, constituting about $5 7 . 9 7 \%$ of the total GSL content in Brassica species57–59. Their concentrations are relatively higher in the seeds, sprouts, and shoots compared to other indolic and aromatic GSLs57. Production of aliphatic GSLs involves a cascade of genes that are regulated by plant hormones, such as the jasmonates60,61.

# a. Transcription regulation

Three TFs, MYB28, MYB29, and MYB76, which are known as High Aliphatic GSL 1 (HAG1), HAG3, and HAG2, respectively are key players in the modulation of aliphatic GSL

genes in Arabidopsis and Brassica spp. 8,62,63. MYB29 has been shown to respond to jasmonate treatment49,63. TFs in the basic helix-loop-helix (bHLH) family, namely MYC2 (bHLH006), MYC3 (bHLH005), and MYC4 (bHLH004) were identified as positive regulators which directly interact with the MYB TFs in regulating aliphatic GSL biosynthesis64–66.

MYB115 and MYB118 are two TFs usually expressed in the seeds and co-expressed with the known aliphatic GSL enzymes, i.e., BZO1, SCPL17, and AOP3, suggesting their role in the benzoylated GSL (BzGSL) pathway67. GSL accumulation was altered in the seeds, leaves, and flowers of myb115 and myb118 knockout lines67.

# b. Aliphatic GSL side-chain elongation

The chain elongation machinery of aliphatic GSL biosynthesis is initiated by methionine that undergoes deamination by a branched-chain amino acid aminotransferase 4 (BCAT4) producing 2-oxo acid68 followed by the three-step transformation. The process starts with the condensation of acetyl-CoA producing a methylthioalkylmalate (MAM) intermediate, which is facilitated by methylthioalkylmalate synthase enzymes (MAM1 and MAM3). MAM1 and MAM3 were found in the GSL-ELONG locus and control diverse aliphatic GSLs69,70. MAM1 participates in the biosynthesis of GSLs via two elongation cycles69,71,72 while MAM3 is involved in the production of all aliphatic GSLs that are derived from one, five, and six elongation cycles72,73.

The next step involves isomerization and oxidative decarboxylation catalyzed by isopropylmate isomerases (IIL1, IPM1, IPM2) and isopropylmalate dehydrogenases (IMDH1, IMDH2, IMDH3), respectively in producing chain-elongated 2-oxo acid. It can either undergo more cycles of elongation processes or enter the core structure of GSL synthesis. Thus, this whole cycle not only produces homomethionine but also creates several chain-elongated derivatives of methionine (met), such as dihomomethionine, trihomomethionine,

tetrahomomethionine, pentahomomethionine, and hexahomomethionine8,72. Direct coexpression of IPM1, IPM2, IIL1, and IMDH1 with the known aliphatic GSL transcription factor (MYB28)63,74,75 further suggests their involvement in the met-derived GSL biosynthesis63. The next side-chain elongation process is the transamination process that converts chain-elongated 2-oxo acids into corresponding amino acids with the production of homomethionine from methionine. These reactions are catalyzed by either cytosolic BCAT4, BCAT6 or the chloroplastic BCAT376–78.

# c. Aliphatic GSL core structure synthesis

There are five main biochemical processes involved in GSL core synthesis, i.e., oxidation, oxidation with conjugation, C-S cleavage, glucosylation, and sulfation79,80. In the aliphatic GSL biosynthesis, CYP79F1 converts all chain-elongated methionine derivatives whereas CYP79F2 is responsible for converting long-chained methionine derivatives into aldoximes81– 83. Next, cytochrome P450 from the CYP83 family oxidizes the aldoximes into activated compounds whereby CYP83A1 converts aliphatic aldoximes into activated aci-nitro compounds8,84.

Another P450 enzyme, which is cytochrome ${ \sf b } _ { 5 }$ (CB5), also plays an important role in the GSL biosynthesis through its co-expression with GSL biosynthetic genes (SOT17, SUR1, GGP1, IPMI1, and UGT74B1)85,86. Further studies conducted on the mutants of CB5C found that the production of long-chained aliphatic GSLs was reduced significantly in one of the mutant lines supporting the contribution of CB5C in the aliphatic GSL biosynthesis pathway86.

The aci-nitro compound will then be converted into a GSH conjugate by glutathione Stransferases (GSTF11 and GSTU20)63,87,88. Gamma-glutamyl peptidase 1 (GGP1) is hypothesized to metabolize the GSH conjugate to produce Cys-Gly conjugates in Arabidopsis88,89. C-S cleavage is the next step involving C-S lyase (SUR1) that forms

thiohydroximates from S-alkyl-thiohydroximate. A study conducted on a sur1 mutant revealed reduced levels of aliphatic and aromatic GSLs in Arabidopsis which supports SUR1 involvement in GSL core synthesis90,91.

During the glucosylation process, the glucotransferases of the UDP-glycosyltransferase 74 (UGT74) family convert thiohydroximates into desulfoglucosinolates. UGT74B1 is the first enzyme determined to glucosylate the thiohydroximate group via biochemical analysis92. Another UGT74 family member, UGT74C1, was proposed to be involved in the biosynthesis of aliphatic GSL evidence of co-expression with other known met-derived GSLs93. Further analysis corroborated those findings through phylogenetic, gene expression, and knockout study on ugt74c1-2 mutant56. The final step in GSL core structure synthesis is the sulfation of desulfoGSLs to form intact GSLs involving the cytosolic sulfotransferase group. Biochemical characterization of the sulfotransferases suggests SOT17 and SOT18 favor the long-chained aliphatic GSLs, such as desulfo-7-methylthioheptyl GSL and desulfo-8-methylthiooctyl GSL94,95.

# d. Aliphatic GSL side-chain modification

Another essential step in GSL biosynthesis is the side-chain modification that is currently unavailable in the KEGG database. Side-chain modification is of interest because the structures of side chains determine the biological functions of GSL hydrolysis products (isothiocyanates and nitriles)96. It was discovered that five flavin monooxygenases, FMOGS-OX1-5, initiated the steps of side-chain modification via S-oxygenation from methylthioalkyl to methylsulfinylalkyl GSL in aliphatic GSL biosynthesis96–98.

Subsequently, it was found that FMOGS-OX enzymes (FMOGS-OX6 and FMOGS-OX7) were involved in the construction of methylsulfinylalkyl GSL99. These two genes were identified to be in monophyletic clade with the known monooxygenases (FMOGS-OX1-5). Further expression

analysis revealed similar spatial expression of both genes that partially overlapped with the known monooxygenase enzymes which were mostly expressed in vascular tissue. Besides, it was discovered that fraction of methylthioalkyl GSLs (mixtures of methylthioalkyl and methylsulfinylalkyl GSL) would decrease in $F M O _ { G S - O X 6 }$ and $F M O _ { G S - O X 7 }$ Arabidopsis overexpressors suggesting their role in converting methylthioalkyl GSLs into methylsulfinylalkyl GSLs99.

The next step is the side-chain modification oxygenation involving 2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenases (AOP2 and AOP3). AOP2 is located within the GSL-ALK locus, and AOP3 is localized to the GSL-OHP locus. These loci, GSL-ALK and GSL-OHP, are collective known as GSL-AOP100. Specifically, AOP2 and AOP3 can convert methylsulfinylalkyl GSL into alkenyl GSL and hydroxyalkyl GSL, respectively7,101. The next process is the conversion of alkenyl GSL into hydroxylated alkenyl GSL by GSL-OH that also requires the involvement of other aliphatic GSL enzymes, including AOP2 and MAM18,102. The hydroxyalkyl GSL would then be converted into benzoylated GSLs (BzGSLs) and sinapoylated GSLs (SnGSLs) catalyzed by serine carboxypeptidase-like acyltransferases 17 (SCPL17)103. BzGSL is mainly found in A. thaliana and cannot be found in other closely related plant species67. It is another form of GSL where benzoylation occurs at the glucose ring of the GSL core structure104. In A. thaliana, BzGSL produced during seed development is synthesized by several side-chain enzymes, including AOP3, BZO1, and SCPL17103,105.

# Indolic GSL Biosynthesis

Indolic GSL biosynthesis is metabolically linked to several biological processes such as phytoalexin metabolism24,41,106 and auxin homeostasis107,108. Indole GSL concentration in Brassica species was the highest in the root or shoot tissues. Hence, the compositions and

concentrations of specific GSLs differ across crops, tissues, and developmental stages57. For example, glucobrassicin (3-indolylmethyl GSL), which is one of the indolic GSLs, is highly expressed in Arabidopsis leaves, whereas the methoxyindol GSL derivatives, 4-methoxyindole-3-yl-methyl GSL and 1-methoxy-indole-3-yl-methyl GSL are more abundant in Arabidopsis roots109,110.

# a. Transcription regulation

MYB34, MYB51, and MYB122 belong to the R2R3 domain MYB family which are characterized as essential regulators of indole GSL biosynthesis106,111 that is interconnected with indolic phytoalexin. MYB34, MYB51, and MYB122 are responsible in regulating the biosynthesis of indolic GSLs in shoot and root in the presence of different plant hormones. MYB34 is known as important regulator of indolic GSLs through abscisic acid (ABA) and jasmonic acid (JA) signaling, meanwhile MYB51 and MYB122 are involved in GSL biosynthesis induced by salicylic acid/ethylene (SA/ET) and JA/ET, respectively106,112,113. Another group of TFs, MYC2 (bHLH006), MYC3 (bHLH005), and MYC4 (bHLH004) which interact with R2R3-MYB TFs directly regulate indolic GSL biosynthesis65,114. The myc2 myc3 myc4 triple mutants show significantly reduced levels of indolic GSLs in Arabidopsis64. Further studies suggest that the bHLHs most probably act by tethering mediator complex and chromatin-modifying factor of DNA to unwind chromatin and make it accessible to MYBs and transcription machinery65,114.

Another type of regulator that controls indolic GSL production in A. thaliana is the CIRCADIAN CLOCK-ASSOCIATED1 (CCA1)115. The aphids feeding on CCA1 overexpression plants (CCA1-OX) showed lower reproduction with reduced body size and weight compared to aphids feeding on wildtype (Col-0) plants. Furthermore, basal indolic

GSLs were higher in CCA1-OX compared to Col-0, suggesting the importance of CCA1 in plant defense against aphids115.

# b. Indolic GSL core structure synthesis

Tryptophan synthase $\beta$ -subunit (TSB1) is capable of producing tryptophan from indole. A previous study showed increased TSB1 expression in indolic MYB TF overexpression lines suggesting its role in indolic GSL biosynthesis116. The indolic cytochrome P450 enzymes, CYP79B2 and CYP79B3 convert tryptophan derivatives into aldoximes 16,117,118. Next, the cytochrome P450 of the CYP83 family, CYP83B oxidizes the aldoximes into nitrile oxide compounds84,119. The nitrile oxide compound can then be converted into a GSH conjugate by GSTF9 and GSTF1063,87. GSTU13 is another enzyme recently found in the indolic biosynthetic pathway, which was postulated to conjugate GSH based on co-expression analysis and mutant studies of the Arabidopsis gstu13 plants120. Next, the GSH conjugate will be metabolized to Cys-Gly conjugates by the $\gamma \mathrm { . }$ -glutamyl peptidases, GGP1 and GGP389,107. It is suggested that SUR1 enzyme is responsible in producing thiohydroximates via C-S lyase reaction in indolic GSL biosynthesis91,107,121. The next two processes in producing GSL core structure are glycosylation by S-glucosyltransferase (UGT74B1)92,107 followed with sulfonation by the sulfotransferases (SOT16, SOT17)8,94.

# c. Indolic GSL side-chain modification

In the indolic GSL side-chain modification, the cytochrome P450 monooxygenases of the CYP81F subfamily are responsible for converting 3-indolylmethyl GSL (I3M) to hydroxyl GSLs, such as 1-hydroxy-3-indolylmethyl GSL (1OH13M) and 4-hydroxy-3-indolylmethyl GSL (4OHI3M). CYP81Fs, are capable of carrying out hydroxylation reactions at the position of the I3M indole ring22,122. Most of CYP81F genes, CYP81F1, CYP81F3, and CYP81F4, are grouped at chromosome 4, except CYP81F2 at chromosome 5 in the Arabidopsis genome. A

previous study that engineered an indole GSL pathway into the Nicotiana benthaminana expression system found that CYP81F1, CYP81F2, and CYP81F3 can perform hydroxylation reactions at positions 1 and 4 of the indole ring, producing 1OH13M and 4OH13M, whereas CYP81F4 can only catalyze hydroxylation at position 1, resulting in 1OH13M122.

Another step in indolic GSL biosynthesis is the conversion of 1OH13M or 4OHI3M to 1- methoxy-3-indolylmethyl GSL (1MOI3M) or 4-methoxy-3-indolylmethyl GSL (4MOI3M), respectively. The key enzymes capable of converting hydroxyl to methoxy groups are the indole glucosinolate $O$ -methyltrasferases (IGMTs) encoded by IGMT1 and IGMT2 genes22,122. IGMT5 is $70 \%$ similar to both IGMT1 and IGMT2 genes and located at the right arm of Arabidopsis chromosome 1123. The expression of IGMT5 strongly correlates with CYP81F4 in various Arabidopsis tissues and treated samples122. A study was conducted to validate IGMT5 as the critical enzyme in the indolic GSL pathway biosynthesis using a metabolomics approach on the two Arabidopsis lines containing T-DNA inserted into the IGMT5 promoter. The disruption of the IGMT5 function led to a significant reduction in the amount of 1MO13M as well as an accumulation of 1OH13M in those Arabidopsis lines123.

# d. Aromatic GSL biosynthesis

The concentrations of aromatic GSLs are the highest in Brassica roots, in which variations of total GSLs can also be observed57. This may be due to the existence of numerous pathogens that can invade roots in the soil environment124. Aromatic GSL biosynthesis is less studied compared to the aliphatic and indolic GSLs in terms of the biological components involved and its molecular mechanism as a whole. Thus, transcription factors that control the production of aromatic compounds remain unclear. Recently, MYB28 was proposed as a potential regulator in aromatic GSL biosynthesis, where the aromatic compounds are highly abundant in

the GSL-containing plants, such as Sinapis alba 49,125. High expression of MYB28 in the stem and leaf of S. alba may support the possibility of MYB28 as one of the critical regulatory candidates in the aromatic GSL biosynthesis49,125.

Aromatic GSLs are generally derived from phenylalanine or tyrosine. Similar to aliphatic GSL biosynthesis, both amino acids are elongated during the chain elongation process44,126. A previous study found several amino acids such as phenylalanine and methionine from the characterization of MAM3 through in vitro assays72, suggesting the involvement of MAM3 in chain elongation of phenylalanine 127.

In core structure synthesis, CYP79A2 and CYP83B1 catalyze the phenylalanine substrate in benzyl GSL production via an engineered biosynthetic pathway in Nicotiana benthamiana79,128–130. CYP79C1 and CYP79C2 are another two cytochrome P450 members of the CYP79 family that were recently postulated to take part in the aromatic core structure GSL pathway by co-expressing those genes with known GSL biosynthetic genes131. The next Arabidopsis core structure synthesis enzymes are GGP1 and SUR1 that are known to catalyze the production of benzyl GSLs from the study on engineered N. benthamiana lines88,89,130,132. The next step is glycosylation catalyzed by UGT74B1, which is suggested to metabolize phederived thiohydroximates based on in vitro and in vivo analyses92. Finally, sulfonation of desulfobenzyl GSL catalyzed by SOT16 was discovered from the engineering of benzyl GSL pathway in N. benthamiana45,130.

# GSL transport and degradation

Five experimentally verified transporters are involved in GSL biosynthesis. One of them is bile acid: sodium symporter family protein 5 (BAT5), which plays an important role in the sidechain elongation process. It was shown as a chloroplastic transporter that directs 2-oxo acids into the chloroplast via feeding experiments of the 2-oxo-acids and amino acids of different

chain lengths. The bat5 mutant with a defective function of BAT5 led to a reduction of the aliphatic GSL level, suggesting its direct role in the aliphatic GSL biosynthesis78,133.

Sulfate transporters of the first group, SULTR1;1 and SULTR1;2, which belong to the sulfate permease (Su1P) family, were identified as GSL transporters involved in transporting sulfate by Arabidopsis roots. Based on transcriptomic data, the expression of SULTR1;1 and SULTR1;2 increased in sulfur-deficient transgenic Arabidopsis plants134,135. In Brassica, GSL is produced in maternal tissues and then transported to seeds. The maternal tissues suggested as sources for GSLs are the siliques136 and rosettes137,138. These findings corroborated another discovery in 2012 on the over-accumulation of GSLs in both rosettes and siliques of gtr1 gtr2 Arabidopsis mutants139. GTR1 and GTR2 encode for GSL transporters that mediate the translocation of both aliphatic and indolic GSLs to Arabidopsis seeds139,140. Additionally, roots were identified as a source of long-chained aliphatic GSLs in Arabidopsis where GTR1 and GTR2 control the GSL distribution between roots and shoots140,141. Figure 3 shows the translocation of GSLs and the GSL transporters (GTR1 and GTR2) in the GSL biosynthesis mechanism in Arabidopsis.

In the event of herbivory, the GSL stored in vacuoles are hydrolyzed by the myrosinases into GSL active compounds such as isothiocyanates and nitriles142,143. In the Arabidopsis genome, six genes encode myrosinase enzymes: TGG1, TGG2, and TGG3 are located in chromosome 5 and the remaining three (TGG4, TGG5, and TGG6) are located in chromosome 1144. However, TGG3 and TGG6 are known as pseudogenes in several Arabidopsis ecotypes including Col-0, even though they were found to be expressed in specific Arabidopsis tissues145. Nonsense mutations, i.e., insertions, deletions, or premature translation termination, will lead to nonfunctional proteins in these pseudogenes144,145. It was discovered that TGG1 and TGG2 found in vacuoles of A. thaliana contributed to the hydrolysis of GSL disintegration146,147.

Another set of myrosinases, TGG4 and TGG5, were less characterized compared to TGG1 and TGG2. These two enzymes were not involved in the breakdown of aliphatic GSL in Arabidopsis leaves142. A recent study revealed that both genes were expressed specifically in Arabidopsis roots and involved in auxin biosynthesis and root growth regulation through the study of tgg4 tgg5 double mutant line148. The authors also found that the indolic GSLs may be transported to Arabidopsis root-tips and hydrolyzed into unstable aglycones by TGG4 and TGG5, which reacts instantly to form indole-3-acetonitrile (IAN). IAN will then be transformed into indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) by the specifier protein, nitrilases, in the roottips148. In GSL degradation, all GSLs (aliphatic GSL, indolic GSL, and aromatic GSL) can form isothiocyanates and nitriles, but only aliphatic GSLs are degraded into epithionitriles42.

# Trancriptional components in GSL biosynthesis

GSL biosynthesis is important for plant defense and affected by both biotic48 and abiotic150,151 stresses in Brassicacea plants49. Among the regulatory molecules that are involved in the initiation of GSL biosynthesis are methyl jasmonate (MeJA)152–154, ethylene155, and salicylic acid154. These molecules target the downstream transcription factors (TFs) which alter the production of GSLs49. MYBs and bHLHs are two groups of TF families that are proposed to regulate the expression of GSL biosynthetic genes. MYB TFs belong to the R2R3 domain MYB family involving 125 R2R3-MYB genes in A. thaliana156. The genes in this family are described as essential regulators in plant stress responses as well as plant development and specialized metabolism157,158. Another group of TFs that regulates GSL biosynthesis is the basic helix-loop-helix (bHLH) family comprising MYC2 (bHLH006), MYC3 (bHLH005), and MYC4 (bHLH004), which directly interact with R2R3-MYB TFs65,114.

During an unelicited condition, the MYC TFs are repressed by a protein complex that is formed by co-repressors, such as Jasmonate ZIM domain (JAZ) proteins, Novel Interactor of JAZ (NINJA), and TOPLESS proteins159–161. In contrast, once the JA is activated, the ubiquitinligase complexes (SCFCOI1) will regulate signal transduction leading to the degradation of JAZ proteins. As a result, the MYC TFs can activate the defense response against the biotic stresses160,161. The protein-protein interaction (PPI) between MYC-MYB and the corresponding co-repressor complexes are shown in Figure 4. The PPI was constructed using the STRING database and visualized using Cytoscape 3.8.0.

Several studies have recorded the effects of light and the involvement of GSL genes in A. thaliana that took place in changing environments162,163. The JAZ7 protein was identified as a regulator in dark-induced leaf senescence where it binds to MYC2/MYC3/MYC4 which subsequently prevents the activation of indolic GSL biosynthesis in the dark environment162. Another study that was conducted recently found Far1 Related Sequence 7 (FRS7) and Far1 Related Sequence 12 (FRS12) as two repressor proteins that regulate GSL biosynthesis in short-day conditions. A combinatorial analysis with transcriptome data revealed that FRS7 can trancriptionally affect IPM1, IPMI2, BAT5, and GSTF11, while FRS12 affected MAM1163. Further protein interaction analysis revealed the physical interaction between FRS7 and FRS12 with the co-repressor proteins (JAZ4, JAZ6, JAZ8, and NINJA), which leads to the repression of MYC TFs in GSL biosynthesis163,164.

Another member of the GSL transcriptional co-regulator is the mediator complex that is known as a multisubunit protein and conserved across various plant species165. For example, Mediator Subunit 5 (MED5) that involved in the crosstalk between phenylpropanoid and GSL biosynthesis. The accumulation of GSL derivatives (aldoximes) would limit phenylpropanoid biosynthesis that requires MED5109,165,166. MED25 is another mediator complex related to GSL biosynthesis in A. thaliana. The expression of PFT1 that encodes MED25 affects several GSL

biosynthetic and regulatory genes, such as MAM1 and MYB28 in the presence of glucose167. Table 3 contains information on other identified A. thaliana transcriptional components and transporters that are involved in GSL biosynthesis. IQD1 and CAMTA3 are the key regulators of GSL metabolism that are involved in the $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ /calmodulin (CaM)-dependent signaling in response to insect herbivory in A. thaliana168–170. Other TFs that are related to sulfur metabolism will be described in the next section.

# Crosstalk between sulfur and nitrogen metabolism with GSL biosynthesis

Since the core structure of GSL usually contains at least two sulfur atoms whereby aliphatic GSLs may have additional sulfur in their side chains, sulfur uptake would be expected to greatly influence GSL biosynthesis171. The effects of sulfur and nitrogen availability on GSL abundance and composition have been investigated in various Brassica species including Arabidopsis54. SLIM1 is shown to be a central transcription regulator that mediates the activation of sulfate acquisition and GSL degradation under low-sulfur conditions172. Another type of protein that was identified from the sulfur-deficient environment was the repressor proteins, encoded by sulfur deficiency induced 1 (SD1), and sulfur deficiency induced 2 (SD2) genes. The expression of both SD1 and SD2 showed negative correlations in the GSL biosynthetic pathway from the observation of transcript and metabolite levels. These findings suggest the capability of both SD1 and SD2 as major repressors that control GSL biosynthesis during sulfur deficiency52.

A recent study found the involvement of atypical myrosinases, $\beta$ -glucosidase 28 (BGLU28) and $\beta$ -glucosidase 30 (BGLU30), in GSL catabolism under sulfur deficiency environment by activating primary sulfur metabolism. This information would be beneficial in understanding plant adaptation strategies during sulfur depletion, and the identification of both $\beta$ -glucosidases

could contribute to new strategies in manipulating the production of beneficial GSLs in crucifers173.

The sulfur assimilation process is essential in GSL biosynthesis regulated by the GSL MYB $\mathrm { T F s } ^ { 1 7 4 , 1 7 5 }$ . A previous study observed increased GSL biosynthesis with sulfate assimilation under light-induced condition involving LONG HYPOCOTYL5 (HY5) as a transcription regulator. Specifically, all three aliphatic MYB TFs were strongly induced in hy5 while the indolic MYB TFs (MYB34 and MYB122) were significantly repressed176. Thus, HY5 was suggested as a transcriptional repressor of aliphatic MYB TFs and acted as an activator for MYB34 and MYB122176.

In sulfur assimilation process, the $3 ^ { \circ }$ -phosphoadenosine- $. 5 '$ -phosphosulfate (PAPS), a sulfate donor for the sulfotransferase reaction, is produced from adenosine- $. 5 '$ -phosphosulfate (APS). Adenylyl-sulfate kinases (APK1 and APK2) are the responsible enzymes in catalyzing the conversion of APS to PAPS8. In a double knockout study conducted on apk1 apk2, the GSL content was significantly reduced $( \sim 8 0 \% )$ with high levels of desulfoGSLs in A. thaliana177. This supports the contribution of APK1 and APK2 in the GSL biosynthesis pathway. Both APK1 and APK2 are localized in the plastid based on the green fluorescent protein (GFP) tagging177 while the sulfotransferases were found in the cytosol178. This indicates the involvement of a transporter that is responsible for channeling PAPS from chloroplast to cytosol8. Figure 2 shows the sulfur assimilation process in the chloroplast, producing PAPS that contributes to the production of GSLs by the sulfotransferases.

The existence of nitrogen in the GSL chemical structure suggests the role of nitrogen metabolism in GSL biosynthesis54. Several studies found a positive correlation between nitrogen availability and the level of indolic GSL biosynthesis in various GSL-containing plants54,179,180. One of the studies aimed to observe the impact of nitrogen fertilization on GSL

biosynthesis in various parts of broccoli. The study confirmed the relation of GSL composition and concentration with nitrogen supply where the broccoli plants that were treated with high nitrogen fertilization show higher indolic GSLs concentration compared to aliphatic GSLs181.

In summary, a comprehensive GSL inventory of molecular components involved in the $A$ . thaliana GSL biosynthetic pathway has been described. However, the information on the GSL biosynthesis in the existing pathway databases (AraCyc and KEGG) remains incomplete. In KEGG, the GSL pathway contains only the side-chain elongation and core structure synthesis part of the pathway. In contrast, both are available in AraCyc with additional of the GSL sidechain modification. To date, there are a total of 113 GSL genes that were experimentally characterized between years 2000 and 2020. Fifty-three GSL biosynthetic genes can be found in either KEGG or AraCyc databases, and thirty-two experimentally verified GSL biosynthetic genes were identified from an exhaustive bibliomic search. The majority of the GSL biosynthetic genes found from bibliomic searches are found to be involved in producing cosubstrates, such as GSH, PAPS, benzoyl-CoA, and sinapoyl-CoA, which play essential roles in GSL biosynthesis. Furthermore, twenty-eight GSL genes comprising twenty-three transcriptional components and five protein transporters are included in this review article. Ten of the transcriptional components are newly described in this article which is the benzoyloxy GSL TFs (MYB115 and MYB118)67, the sulfur deficiency-induced repressors (SD1 and SD2)52, CIRCADIAN CLOCK-ASSOCIATED1 (CCA1)115, LONG HYPOCOTYL5 (HY5), Far1 Related Sequence repressors (FRS7 and FRS12)163 as well as the GSL transcriptional mediators, MED5166 and MED25167. The latest set of TFs discovered since 2010 shows a significant increase that is linked with several factors, such as light and sulfur metabolism. Using the extensively acquired information, we manually constructed a GSL biosynthetic pathway. A reconstructed GSL biosynthetic pathway (Figure 2) illustrates a comprehensive overview of the biosynthesis and degradation of aliphatic GSLs and indolic GSLs in A. thaliana

with the regulation by transcription factors. We also discussed the crosstalks between the metabolism of nitrogen and sulfur involved in the GSL biosynthesis. Despite that the GSL core biosynthetic pathway in A. thaliana is relatively well elucidated, more information on the regulation and transportation of GSL biosynthesis are still required. For example, it is unclear on how PAPS from the sulfur assimilation in the chloroplast is shuttled to the cytosol for GSL biosynthesis. Given the complexity of plant defense mechanism, the regulation of GSL biosynthesis under different stresses and environmental factors remains to be uncovered in different species.

MYC2, MYC3, and MYC4 interact directly with the R2R3-MYB TFs (MYB28, MYB29, MYB76, MYB34, MYB51, and MYB122) to regulate GSL biosynthesis. This pathway contains four major steps starting from the side-chain elongation. This is followed by the core structure synthesis, which includes sulfate assimilatory pathway that produces co-substrates for GSL

biosynthesis: GSH (ϒ-Glu-Cys-Gly) and PAPS $3 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphoadenosine- $\cdot 5 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphosulfate). The next process is the side-chain modification that produces either aliphatic GSL or indolic GSL. The last step is the degradation of GSL by the myrosinases.
