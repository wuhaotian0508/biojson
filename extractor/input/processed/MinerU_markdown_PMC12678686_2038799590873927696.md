# ABSTRACT

Symbiotic nitrogen fixation in legumes requires the exquisite regulation of the environment within the infected region of the nodule. The microaerobic environment critical for nitrogenase activity is maintained through the physical oxygen diffusion barrier of the cortex and locally the oxygen‐binding protein leghemoglobin (Lb). Leghemoglobin binds and releases oxygen with heme moiety to maintain oxygen gradients inside the infected cell (IC) during nitrogen fixation. Heme binds to diverse proteins and plays critical roles in different redox reactions. However, the role and regulation of host‐controlled heme production during symbiotic nitrogen fixation are not 

clear. Here, we identified coproporphyrinogen III oxidase plastid related 1 (CPOP1) as a key regulator of symbiotic heme biosynthesis in Lotus japonicus. CPOP1 is specifically highly expressed in nitrogen‐fixing nodules, and knocking out CPOP1 alone causes leaf etiolation and dwarfism which could be recovered by the exogenous application of nitrogen source, indicating nitrogen fixation defect. The IC‐specific expression of CPOP1 was directed by the −881 to −740 bp promoter region. The cpop1 mutant shows significantly increased nodule oxygen level and decreased nitrogen fixation activity compared to the wild‐type. Intriguingly, bacteria proliferation is inhibited due to the down‐regulation of cell division‐ related gene expression upon CPOP1 knockout. Our data showed that CPOP1 is essential for the microaerobic environment control of ICs and the activation of rhizobial nitrogenase required for symbiotic nitrogen fixation, through host‐ regulated nodule heme synthesis.

# INTRODUCTION

egume nodules are symbiotic nitrogen‐fixing organs induced through the legume‐rhizobia interaction (Ferguson et al., 2010; Roy et al., 2020). Within the infected cells (ICs) of nodules, rhizobia differentiated into the nitrogen‐fixing bacteroids, which are enclosed in the plant‐derived membrane structure‐‐symbiosome membrane (SM), together with the peribacteroid space (PBS), forming the basic nitrogen‐fixing unit‐‐ symbiosome (Emerich and Krishnan, 2014; Yang et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023). Symbiotic nitrogen fixation of legumes relies on the elaborately controlled microenvironment of the nodule, especially the oxygen level within the ICs. There is conflict of oxygen requirement for symbiotic rhizobia: oxygen is toxic to nitrogenase but required for bacteroid respiration and metabolism. Excess oxygen could inactivate the expression of some Nif or Fix genes and inactivate nitrogenase by oxidation of the FeMo‐cofactor (Rubio and Ludden, 2008; Masson‐Boivin et al., 2009; Oldroyd and Dixon, 2014). Nodules contain two types of hemoglobin: leghemoglobin (Lb) and phytoglobin (Glb). The host‐ produced Lbs could act as an oxygen buffer to reversibly bind to oxygen and facilitate oxygen delivery to bacteroids (Rutten and Poole, 2019), while the heme‐containing Glbs could modulate the nitric oxide concentration to regulate nodulation and nitrogen fixation (Berger et al., 2020). In addition to redox regulatory proteins, the nodule cortex participates in symbiotic microaerobic condition regulation by acting as an oxygen diffusion barrier (Venado et al., 2022). 

As the cofactor of hemoglobins, heme is also deployed as the prosthetic group by diverse metalloproteins, such as cytochromes, catalases, peroxidases, and heme‐dependent transcription factors (Tanaka et al., 2011; Germani et al., 2013; Chiabrando et al., 2014; Kuhl and Imhof, 2014; Kobayashi and Masuda, 2016). In higher plants, heme synthesis takes place in the plastid or chloroplast via a series of conserved steps that convert δ‐aminolevulinic acid to protoporphyrin IX, followed by the insertion of $\mathsf { F e } ^ { 2 + }$ for heme production, and insertion of ${ \mathsf { M } } { \mathsf { g } } ^ { 2 + }$ for chlorophyll production (Tanaka and Tanaka, 2007; Layer et al., 2010; Mochizuki et al., 2010; Larkin, 2016). Heme is then transferred to other subcellular compartments and widely distributed within cells (Mochizuki et al., 2010). On the other hand, hemoproteins of bacteroids are essential for nitrogen fixation (O'Brian, 1996; Huo et al., 2022); for example, the heme‐ containing FixL could induce the expression of nitrogenase genes under microaerobic conditions (Poole et al., 2018; Wright et al., 2018). Moreover, the cbb3‐type cytochrome oxidase with a high affinity for oxygen, facilitates the microaerophilic respiration of bacteroids (Preisig et al., 1996; Pitcher and Watmough, 2004). 

Although the heme anabolism pathway has been well characterized, the biological function of host‐produced heme during legume‐rhizobium symbiosis has not been fully revealed (Sangwan and O'Brian, 1992, 1999; Balestrasse et al., 2008; Wang et al., 2022). Here we analyzed Lotus japonicus symbiotic heme through the functional study of the nodule‐ specific CPOP1 gene, which encodes a member of coproporphyrinogen III oxidase (CPO). CPO is a tetrapyrrole 

biosynthesis pathway enzyme that catalyzes coproporphyrinogen III to protoporphyrinogen IX through a two‐step oxidative decarboxylation (Layer et al., 2010). Lotus japonicus contains two types of CPOs, one is putative plastid localized oxygen‐ dependent CPO (CPOP), or Heme Synthesis F (HEMF), and the other is putative mitochondria‐localized oxygen‐independent CPO, termed coproporphyrinogen III oxidase mitochondrion related (CPOM) or Heme Synthesis N (HEMN). CPOP utilizes oxygen while CPOM utilizes S‐adenosylmethionine as the electron acceptor (Layer et al., 2010; Tanaka et al., 2011). Studies in Arabidopsis and soybean suggest that by maintaining the homeostasis of cellular reactive oxygen species (ROS), CPOP participates in light‐dependent defense responses, while CPOM is involved in gametophyte development (Ishikawa et al., 2001; Santana et al., 2002; Guo et al., 2013; Pratibha et al., 2017; Ma et al., 2020). 

We show here that a decrease in heme content caused by host heme synthesis gene CPOP1 mutation could increase free oxygen levels at the nitrogen‐fixing region of nodules, and subsequently impair symbiotic nitrogen fixation. Unlike other enzymes in the heme biosynthesis pathway and its homologs, due to its unique promoter region, CPOP1 is specifically highly expressed in the ICs of mature nodules where efficient nitrogen fixation takes place, making CPOP1 an ideal target for microaerobic environment manipulation during symbiotic nitrogen fixation. 

# RESULTS

# CPOP1 encodes a nodule‐specifically expressed heme synthase

To better understand the functions of heme during symbiotic nitrogen fixation, we first analyzed the heme synthesis enzymes for their expression patterns and evolutionary relations. There are two copies of CPOP and one CPOM gene in the L. japonicus genome. The CPOP1 gene showed specifically high expression in nodules among the heme synthesis enzymes, according to the online gene expression atlas (Figure S1A; Table S1) (Mun et al., 2016). Intriguingly, the palaeopolyploid soybean (Glycine max) contains a single copy of the CPOP gene, which is broadly expressed and strongly induced in nitrogen‐fixing nodules (Madsen et al., 1993; Smith et al., 1993; Schmutz et al., 2010). Phylogenetic analysis showed that CPOP and CPOM are widely distributed in legumes and non‐legumes, with the gene copy numbers of CPOP and CPOM varying among species (Figure S1B). As the functional homolog of CPOP, CPOM is present in plants and bacteria only and catalyzes the oxygen‐ independent coproporphyrinogen III oxidation process (Pratibha et al., 2017) (Figure S1C). The CPOP family originated early during evolution and is highly conserved for the catalytic region in diverse species including animals, plants, and prokaryotes (Figure S1D, E). The CPOPs in plants and animals mainly differ in the N‐terminal transit peptides, with plants’ CPOPs containing plastid transit peptides, and animals’ CPOPs possessing mitochondrial transit peptides (Figure S1D).

# CPOP2 and CPOM mainly participate in vegetative and reproductive development

To investigate the biological function of CPOP2 and CPOM, we analyzed the phenotype of single‐knockout mutants. Unlike cpop1, cpop2, cpom, and cpop2 cpom double mutant seedlings produced red nodules and normal green leaves under the nitrogen‐limiting growth condition, suggesting the nodule heme synthesis and nitrogen fixation were not affected (Figure S4C–E). However, the cpop2, cpom single or double mutants showed growth inhibition, demonstrated as dwarf seedlings and smaller organs (Figure S4C, D). The cpop2 had reduced aboveground and underground biomass, and reduced nodule number compared to WT (Figure S4F–H). Although the heme content of cpop2 was equivalent to WT (Figure S4I), the nitrogen fixation rate per gram nodule was reduced in cpop2 (Figure S4J). The nitrogen fixation rate per plant was significantly reduced in cpop2 (Figure S4K), mainly due to the reduced nodule number in cpop2 (Figure S4H). The overall nodule development and structure, as well as the infection region size and IC size, appeared normal for cpop2 compared to WT of the same developmental stage upon exogenous nitrogen depletion (Figure S5A, B), although cpop2 showed abnormally enlarged mitochondria and plastids in ICs (Figure S5C, D). 

On the other hand, cpom had shorter siliques (Figure S4F) and a significantly reduced seed setting rate (Figure S4L), suggesting the mitochondria‐localized CPOM is involved in reproductive development. We were unable to obtain cpop1 cpop2 double mutant and cpop1 cpop2 cpom triple mutant which were probably lethal. 

Taking all the evidence, we propose that CPOP1, but not CPOP2 or CPOM, is the key component for heme synthesis during symbiotic nitrogen fixation. 

Heme biosynthesis regulates symbiotic nitrogen fixation 

# CPOP1 gene knockout increases free oxygen levels within nodules and reduces the expression of rhizobial nitrogen‐fixing related genes

Since the heme‐containing proteins such as Lb, Glb, and cytochrome participate in cellular redox regulation, we managed to detect the free oxygen levels of both WT and cpop1 nodules employing the oxygen microsensor (Pedersen et al., 2006). Oxygen microprofiling of the WT nodule dropped dramatically in the cortex region and fell below the detection threshold in the infection region (Figure 3A, B). In contrast, the curve of the free oxygen level demonstrated a slower drop in cpop1 compared to WT, and the oxygen level of cpop1 remained positive in the outer region of the infection zone (Figure 3A, B). Statistical analysis suggested that the microaerobic region of the cpop1 nodule was significantly smaller than that of the WT (Figure 3C). 

To better elucidate how the nodule oxygen level affects nitrogen fixation in cpop1, we analyzed the expression of oxygen‐sensitive nitrogenase genes. NifA2 (Nif‐specific transcriptional activator 2) in Mesorhizobium loti acts as the transcriptional activator during symbiotic nitrogen fixation, and its expression is regulated by oxygen (Agron et al., 1993; Sullivan et al., 2013). The expression levels of NifA2 gene and its downstream NifA1 (Nif‐specific transcriptional activator 1) were both downregulated in the cpop1 nodule, probably due to the increased oxygen level (Figure 3D). Consequently, the nitrogen fixation rate of cpop1 was significantly lower than that of WT (Figure 3E–G). To assess whether the CPOP1 mutation alters key nitrogen‑fixation proteins, we conducted Western blot analyses of Lb, the activity of which depends on its heme cofactor, and rhizobial NifH, the dinitrogenase‑reductase subunit of nitrogenase. Results showed that nodule‐specific Lb levels were markedly lower in cpop1 than in WT (Figure 3H), whereas NifH abundance was similar in both genotypes (Figure 3I), indicating that heme deficiency in cpop1 might destabilize Lb, leading to its rapid turnover, whereas NifH, being heme‑independent, is unaffected. 

In summary, the impaired heme production of cpop1 nodules could interfere with the strictly controlled microaerobic environment of the nodule infection zone, and reduce the expression of oxygen‐labile NifA genes in rhizobia. The significantly reduced nitrogenase activity upon CPOP1 mutation demonstrates it as the key regulator during symbiotic nitrogen fixation.

# Transcriptome analysis reveals core pathways in CPOP1‐regulated symbiotic nitrogen fixation

To better understand the inhibitory mechanism of rhizobium division in cpop1 symbiosomes, we performed quantitative polymerase chain reaction (PCR) to analyze the expression levels of bacterial cell division‐related genes in WT and cpop1 nodules at 10 and 21 DPI. The rhizobial cell cycle regulator gene cell cycle transcriptional regulator A (CtrA) (Hallez et al., 2004), cell division protein‐coding genes filamentation temperature‐ sensitive Z (FtsZ), and filamentation temperature‐sensitive H (FtsH) (Srinivasan et al., 2008; Alunni and Gourion, 2016) were significantly downregulated in cpop1 nodules compared to WT at 10 DPI, while showing no different expression in cpop1 nodules at 21 DPI (Figure S7). Other genes, such as the transcriptional repressor Replication protein A (RepA), the cell division regulator Minicell locus C (MinC), the morphogenetic gene Flagellin C (FliC) (Hallez et al., 2004), and the FtsH protease activity modulator High Frequency of lysogenization K (HflK) (Bandyopadhyay et al., 2010) were also significantly downregulated in the symbiotic rhizobia of cpop1 compared to WT at 10 DPI (Figure S7). 

Data suggest that the number of either differentially or specifically expressed host genes between WT and cpop1 at 21 DPI, was much higher than those at 10 DPI (Figure S8A, B). To further address the potential functions of CPOP1 during symbiotic nitrogen fixation, we performed Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) and Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analyses with the differentially expressed genes between WT (21 DPI) and cpop1 (21 DPI). Results highlighted the KEGG‐enriched pathways including the biosynthesis of amino acids, citrate cycle, purine metabolism, synthesis and degradation of ketone bodies, and so forth (Figure S8C), while GO enrichment analysis demonstrated processes such as organonitrogen compound metabolic process, small molecule metabolic process, and purine ribonucleoside triphosphate binding (Figure S8D). Altogether, the enrichment analyses imply that CPOP1 is probably 

involved in organonitrogen metabolism and amino acid biosynthesis processes, implying the impaired nitrogen assimilation in cpop1. Quantitative PCR suggested that the heme biosynthesis pathway components CPOP1, GluTR1, and the heme degradation enzyme HO1 (Zhou et al., 2023) were downregulated in the nodules of cpop1 compared to WT at either 10 or 21 DPI (Figure S9A–C). We further analyzed the expression level of the heme‐binding proteins in nodules including Lb, Glb, cytochrome P450, catalase, peroxidase, and sulfite reductase (SUOX) (Table S1). Transcriptome data showed that the nodule expression of Lb and Glb genes were significantly induced rather than inhibited in cpop1 which had reduced heme content (Figure S9D, E). On the other hand, the expression levels of SUOX and catalase were downregulated in cpop1 nodules (Figure S9F), suggesting the possible roles of CPOP1 in nitric oxide (NO) and ROS signaling regulation during symbiotic nitrogen fixation. 

# The specialized nitrogen fixation function of CPOP1 in L. japonicus relies on its promoter sequence

To better understand the functional specificity of L. japonicus CPOP1, we further analyzed the CPOP gene in Glycine max which has only a single homolog of L. japonicus CPOP1. GmCPOP demonstrated higher expression levels in mature nodules and nodule primordia, compared to leaves and roots (Figure S10A). When expressed under its native promoter, GmCPOP1‐GFP (green fluorescent protein) accumulated chiefly in the nodule infection zone (Figure S10B) and was confined to plastids of ICs, with no detectable signal in plastids of uninfected cortical cells (Figure S10C). Down‐ regulation of GmCPOP expression through RNA interference (RNAi) (Figure S10D, F) resulted in more vacuoles in ICs and a lower ICs/UCs ratio (Figure S10G), a cytological pattern indicative of sub‐optimal bacteroid proliferation, reminiscent of the L. japonicus cpop1 phenotype. Because complete loss of GmCPOP1 function is seedling‐lethal (Ma et al., 2020), we analyzed the ethyl methanesulfonate (EMS)‐derived hypomorphic allele Gmlmm2‐1, which carries a Tyr‐192 to Cys substitution in a conserved catalytic residue (hereafter gmcpop, GmCPOPY192C; Figure S10E). gmcpop plants are viable, although smaller than WT (Figure S10H), and they produce nodules that remain competent for nitrogen fixation (Figure S10I). In summary, in addition to its function in rhizobia loading, the single copy GmCPOP displays more versatile functions compared to CPOP1 from L. japonicus.

To locate the core regulatory element for symbiosis in the CPOP1 promoter, we performed promoter truncation analysis in transgenic nodules. Results demonstrated that promoter constructs containing the −881 bp region or the (−881 to −740 bp) $^ +$ mini‐35S fusion drove GFP fluorescence exclusively in ICs (Figure 5A), indicating that the −881 to −740 bp region of the CPOP1 promoter is essential for IC‐specific transcriptional activity. We evaluated the functional relevance of the differential regulation of expression for CPOPs through promoter‐swap experiments. The L. japonicus CPOP1 promoter‐driven CPOP1, CPOP2, or AtCPOP was sufficient to recover the symbiotic phenotype of cpop1, suggesting the evolutionary divergence of CPOP promoters, and the functional conservation among CPOP proteins (Figure 5B). As expected, GFP‐tagged CPOP2 and AtCPOP, expressed under the CPOP1 promoter, accumulated in plastid‐like structures within ICs—mirroring the localization pattern of native CPOP2 (Figure 5C). 

As CPOP proteins are conserved among species, it is reasonable to speculate that the biological functional divergence of CPOPs mainly relies on their differential expression patterns and localizations. We aligned the promoter of L. japonicus CPOP1 with those from its closely related species. Intriguingly, the −881 to −740 bp promoter region with IC‐specific cis‐element exists only in the LjCPOP1 promoter but not the promoters from any other 18 species, suggesting a novel evolutionary event might be involved (Figure 5D). We screened this promoter region against the repetitive sequence database‐‐Repbase (https://www.girinst. org/), and identified a highly similar transposon sequence from Amblyraja radiata (length $= 2 5 6$ bp; similarity $= 0 . 6 2 9 6 )$ , indicating a possible horizontal sequence transfer. Our results demonstrate the evolutionary acquisition of the symbiotic nitrogen fixation function of the L. japonicus CPOP1 promoter. 

In summary, L. japonicus CPOP1 specifically localizes at the ICs and plays essential roles during host‐controlled symbiotic heme biosynthesis and microaerobic environment maintenance in the infected region of nodules. However, its functional homologs CPOP2 and CPOM are not directly involved in the symbiotic nitrogen fixation regulation. Cis‐ element mapping and promoter‐swap experiments suggest that CPOP1's functional divergence is associated with acquisition of the −881 to −740 bp sequence in its promoter. 

# DISCUSSION

To accommodate the rhizobia and facilitate nitrogen fixation, legume nodules adapt to maintain high levels of the oxygen‐ binding Lb, for oxygen buffering within ICs. A lack of Lbs in L. japonicus could induce the formation of white fix‐nodules, a significant reduction of nodule heme content, and early nodule senescence (Wang et al., 2019). As the oxygen‐binding moiety of Lbs and other heme‐binding proteins, the nodule requires a large amount of heme for exquisite redox homeostasis regulation. Inside nodules, host heme could bind to Lbs for oxygen storage and transportation, Glbs for NO level regulation 

(Dordas et al., 2003; Fukudome et al., 2016), and very likely other heme‐binding proteins for undetermined functions. CPOPs of higher plants are involved in the synthesis of heme, as well as other tetrapyrroles, such as chlorophyll, and phytochromobilin (Tanaka and Tanaka, 2007). Mutation of CPOP1 in L. japonicus resulted in a severe nitrogen deficiency phenotype that could be recovered by exogenous nitrogen supply. Supported by the low free oxygen level detected in the cpop1 nodule, we conclude that CPOP1 is mainly involved in microaerobic environment control during symbiotic nitrogen fixation (Figure S11). On the other hand, similar to the Lb‐null triple mutant lb123 (Wang et al., 2019), the IC mitochondria of cpop2 but not cpop1 showed disrupted mitochondrial cristae and considerably increased sizes, suggesting the heme produced by CPOP1 and CPOP2 participate in different downstream regulatory pathways. Because heme production is also greatly reduced in lb123, there is the possibility that the mitochondria phenotype in lb123 is partly due to heme reduction in addition to the microenvironment change caused by hemoglobin depletion. Interestingly, although Lb transcripts were up‑regulated in cpop1 (Figure S9D), Lb protein levels were markedly diminished (Figure 3H), indicating an “messenger RNA–protein decoupling” likely caused by post‑transcriptional regulation tied to limited heme availability. 

Both the symbiotic rhizobia and the host ICs could produce heme from either glycine or glutamate, respectively (Santana et al., 1998). The two symbiotic partners share similar terminal steps of heme biosynthesis, including CPO‐catalyzed oxidative decarboxylation of coproporphyrinogen III to protoporphyrinogen IX (Dailey, 2002). Early on, it was believed that rhizobia are mainly responsible for the production of Lb heme (Nadler and Avissar, 1977; Jacobs et al., 1989). However, as more evidence showed up, it was proposed that the heme moiety of Lb is derived from plant cells (O'Brian, 1996; Santana et al., 1998). Our results demonstrate that the LjCPOP1 mutation could cause a significant loss of nodule heme synthesis capacity and strongly impair the symbiotic nitrogen fixation rate, which goes along with the idea of host‐supported Lb heme production. On the other hand, bacteroids require heme for essential processes such as respiratory electron transport, and could utilize plant‐derived precursors for heme biosynthesis (Sangwan and O'Brian, 1991). Recent research showed that the controlled heme level in Sinorhizobium meliloti is essential for it to establish effective nitrogen‐ fixing symbiosis with Medicago (Sankari et al., 2022). Compared to WT, L. japonicus CPOP1 mutation could cause a significant transcriptome shift of symbiotic rhizobia, including changes in cell division regulatory genes (Figure S7). It remains to be elucidated whether the dramatic change in bacteroids is due to a disturbed microaerobic environment or heme supply from plant sources. 

There are 10 steps and more than 20 genes involved in the heme synthesis pathway of L. japonicus. Although three enzymes are involved in protoporphyrinogen IX production, our results showed that unlike CPOP1, CPOP2, and CPOM are not involved in the Lb‐mediated redox status regulation within nodules. Promoter function analysis and pairwise promoter alignments revealed a unique sequence of the

CPOP1 promoter in L. japonicus which is responsible for the expression specificity of the CPOP1 gene. Among heme‐ biosynthetic genes, CPOP1 is the most highly expressed in nitrogen‐fixing nodules (Figure S1A). Loss of CPOP1 alone markedly impairs nitrogen fixation in L. japonicus and is accompanied by elevated free oxygen levels within the nodules. Our study demonstrates the functional specificity of CPOP1 in cellular microenvironment regulation and provides perspectives on its application in symbiotic nitrogen fixation regulation through bioengineering.

# Hairy root transformation

# Free oxygen measurement

The free oxygen levels of nodules were measured using a $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ oxygen microsensor (OX‐100; Unisense; Denmark) according to the manufacturer's instructions. The L. japonicus nodules were embedded in $3 \%$ low‐melting point agarose (V2111; Promega; WI, USA), and measurement was performed with the probe set to forward $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { m }$ vertically for each step. The data were processed using the MicroProfiling System (Unisense, SensorTrace Suite; Denmark).

# Total heme measurement

The measurement of total heme content was carried out as described (Howieson and Dilworth, 2016; Jiang et al., 2021). Briefly, 0.1 g fresh tissue from 21 DPI nodules, roots, or leaves was homogenized in liquid nitrogen and suspended in 500 mL 

pre‐cooled 100 mmol/L phosphate buffer (pH 6.8). Samples were centrifuged and the supernatants were further processed using the Heme Assay Kit (MAK316; Sigma), according to the manufacturer's protocol. The total heme contents were estimated by a colorimetric determination at 400 nm. 

# Freeze‐fracture SEM

The protocol for freeze‐fracture SEM was adopted from previous publications with some modifications (Webb and Sheehy, 1991; Kaneko et al., 2019). Lotus japonicus nodules of different development stages with 1 cm root attached were selected, and the nodules were fixed on the freezing transmission base through conductive glue. The nodules were then immediately frozen in liquid nitrogen and loaded into the vacuum cryotransfer system (EM VCT500; Leica; Austria). The frozen nodules were transferred to a high vacuum coater (EM ACE600; Leica; Austria) and fractured by a knife. Freeze‐fractured nodules were freeze etched by sublimating ice at $\mathtt { - 9 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ for 5 min to preserve the structure, before coating under an angle with metal $( - 1 4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , 15 mA, 2 nm/s, 2.5 nm platinum $( \mathsf { P t } ) )$ and a carbon backing up film (EM ACE600; Leica; Austria). The samples were cryo‐transferred using a vacuum cryotransfer system (EM VCT500; Leica) to a multifunctional field emission SEM (Crossbeam 340; Carl Zeiss; Germany) and were kept at $- 1 2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for SEM imaging. 

# Transmission electron microscopy

The TEM protocol was adopted from earlier research (Jia et al., 2017). The L. japonicus nodules were cut into $1 \mathsf { m m } ^ { 3 }$ cubes. Samples were then pre‐fixed using $4 \%$ (w/v) paraformaldehyde (158127; Sigma; MA, USA) and $2 . 5 \%$ (v/v) glutaraldehyde (G5882; Sigma; MA, USA) in 0.1 mol/L sodium cacodylate buffer (pH 7.2) for 2 h at room temperature, and stored overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Samples were post‐fixed using $1 \%$ (w/v) osmium tetroxide (O5500; Sigma; MA, USA) for 2 h at room temperature, and then stained with $1 \%$ (w/v) uranyl acetate solution (6159‐44‐0; SPI‐ Chem) for 1 h. After that, samples were dehydrated at series acetone solutions (179124; Sigma; MA, USA) and infiltrated using a series of resin‐acetone mixes, followed by resin embedding (Embed 812; EMS; PA, USA). Samples were sectioned into 60–70 nm slides and stained with $0 . 2 \%$ lead citrate solution. TEM images were captured using a transmission electron microscope (JEM‐1400; JEOL Ltd; Japan).
