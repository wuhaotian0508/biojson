# Summary

Dwarf plant architecture facilitates dense planting, and increased planting densities boost the maize yield. However, breeding applications of dwarfing materials for maize are currently limited. There is an urgent need remove the obstacles to applying dwarf resources. Here, we innovated a new method to add a novel maize dwarf germplasm through the distant hybridization of Maize–Tripsacum–Teosinte allopolyploid (MTP) with maize. We identified ten independent dwarf families with unique characteristics. Five germplasms in our library were controlled by their respective dwarf genes. However, no allele was controlled by Br2. Subsequently, d024 in the library was successfully fine mapped, revealing its linkage to indel-4 in ZmCYP90D1. The indel-4 polymorphism regulates the expression of ZmCYP90D1 and is controlled by an upstream transcription factor (ZmBES1/BZR1-5). The indel-4 of ZmCYP90D1 allele, which reduces plant height, originated from Tripsacum, a wild variety of maize. However, d024 exhibits sensitivity to brassinosteroids (BRs), with lower castasterone levels in the internodes than that in the wild type. Furthermore, ZmCYP90D1 interacted with ZmFDXs and ZmNAD(P)H to positively regulate the downstream BR synthesis pathway. Additionally, we showed that introgressing the indel-4 of the Tripsacum allele into modern hybrids ensures yield potential and improves the harvest index under high-density conditions. Overall, as we begin to manufacture highly engineered dwarf materials using the MTP, this approach will solve the problems faced by corn dwarfs. 

# Introduction

Dwarf crop resources serve as the foundation for crop dwarf breeding, and their identification constitutes the cornerstone of the 20th-century Green Revolution. Dwarfism imparts various advantages to crop varieties, including improved lodging resistance, denser growth, and increased harvest index, offering significant benefits for crop production (Mansfield and Mumm, 2014). Dense planting could increase yields per unit area, solving the challenges posed by the growing world population amid limited arable land (Tilman et al., 2011). In response to this imperative, breeders of the cereal crop maize (Zea mays) have strategically tailored plant height traits to suit intensive cultivation. Currently, the focal point of maize dwarf gene breeding revolves around the Br2 gene (Multani et al., 2003). However, the concentrated application of a single dwarfing gene (Br2) leads to limited inheritance, highlighting the need to expand genetic resources. There is an urgent need to create, identify, evaluate, and preserve additional dwarf resources while comprehensively analysing their underlying mechanisms. 

Natural mutations with low frequency, artificial mutagenesis without rules and multiple disadvantages, and genetic engineering involve complex and challenging operations, making it difficult to achieve significant breakthroughs in creating maize dwarf germplasms (Best et al., 2016; Best and Dilkes, 2022). For example, Br2-type natural mutants showed shortening of the lower stalk internodes (Zhang et al., 2019). However, other plant parts, including the mesocotyl, coleoptile, leaves, ear, and tassel, were not affected by the size or growth of Br2-type mutants (Xing et al., 2015). In contrast, maize dwarf1 and na2-1 were approximately $10 \% { - } 2 0 \%$ of the height of wild-type siblings at maturity, which makes it difficult to breed with shortened internodes, feminized tassels, and broad and dark-green leaves (Best et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2014). The na2-1 is defective in reducing 24-methylenecholesterol to campesterol during the initial stages of brassinosteroid (BR) biosynthesis (Best et al., 2016). The maize dwarf gene ZmDWF4 is a C22 hydroxylase involved in the early steps of the brassinolide synthesis pathway (Liu et al., 2020). The maize dwarfing gene Brd1, which encodes a C-6 oxidase and catalyses the final step of brassinosteroid synthesis, was previously identified (Makarevitch 

et al., 2012). The ZmBRI1 gene encodes a brassinolide signal transduction receptor in maize (Kir et al., 2015). The ZmBZR1 was found to bind to the promoters of brassinolide-related genes that participate in the brassinolide signal transduction pathway, as verified using molecular experiments (Zhang et al., 2020b). Indeed, only the maize-dominant D11 (Mo17) heterozygote lines were crossed as pollen donors to maize inbred lines, which are characterized by compact internodes, slightly rolled leaves, and severely degenerated ears (Wang et al., 2013, 2016). Additionally, using ethyl methanesulfonic acid (EMS), maize d5, which features dwarfism and short internodes, was obtained by screening approximately 50 000 M2 mutant seeds using the maize inbred line B73 (Li et al., 2023). The maize-dominant dwarf D9 mutant shows delayed flowering, increased tillering, and varying degrees of male sterility (Best and Dilkes, 2022). Despite efforts to edit GA biosynthetic pathway genes using genetic engineering technology and generate semi-dwarf maize plants, practical applications are yet to be realized (Paciorek et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2020a). 

Distant hybridization has been a source of alleles for overcoming various challenges in maize through selective breeding, particularly for creating superior germplasm resources. An excellent material, dlf1, controls leaf number and flowering time and was identified from a cross between maize and its wild ancestor, teosinte (Sun et al., 2020). Upright Plant Architecture 2 reduced the leaf angle of maize, enhancing high-density yields originating from teosinte (Tian et al., 2019). The cold tolerance exhibited by bZIP68-NILteosinte originates from a cross between maize (W22) and its wild ancestor, teosinte (8759) (Li et al., 2022). THP9-teosinte, derived from the ${ \mathsf { B C } } _ { 6 } { \mathsf { F } } _ { 4 }$ population between B73 and teosinte, enhances maize seed protein content and increases nitrogen use efficiency (Huang et al., 2022). Furthermore, wild relatives of maize, such as Zea. perennis and Tripsacum dactyloides. Natural gene swapping of Zea. perennis or Tripsacum into the maize subspecies is infrequent, primarily because of the barriers posed by hybridization bottlenecks (Liu et al., 2019). Nevertheless, establishing genetic bridges between crops and wild materials provides a straightforward way to overcome strong hybridization barriers and facilitate the introgression of wild genes into crops (Hickey et al., 2019). In previous studies, hybridization and $2 \mathsf { n } + \mathsf { n }$ special sexual reproduction have been used to construct a tri-species intergeneric hybrid (MTP, $2 \mathsf { n } = 7 4$ ) involving maize, Z. perennis, and T. dactyloides, which was successfully backcrossed it with maize (Iqbal et al., 2019; Yan et al., 2020). The latter research led to the questions of whether the Green Revolution alleles that can be used for artificial selection in maize will appear in wild biological species, and if so, how many different dwarf alleles exist, how they are distributed, and how they function. 

In this study, we established a dwarf material library using distant hybridization facilitated by the genetic bridge MTP. Subsequently, we identified ten genetically stable and excellent dwarf germplasms from the introgression lines of the MTP-distant hybrid maize. Five germplasms in our library exhibited semi-dwarfing characteristics and unique features. Moreover, these semi-dwarf materials were controlled by their respective dwarf genes but not the alleles with Br2. The dwarf mutant d024 in the library was positively regulated by ZmCYP90D1. Moreover, variants of a 191-bp deletion (Indel-4) polymorphism in the ZmCYP90D1 promoter led to 

down-regulating transcription levels, affecting the expression of genes involved in both BR synthesis and signalling pathways. The 191-bp deletion of indel-4 originated from the tetraploid T. dactyloides. Additionally, ZmCYP90D1 is regulated by upstream ZmBES1/BZR1-5 and interacts with ZmFDXs and ZmNAD(P)H to influence the endogenous brassinosteroids (BRs) content. Furthermore, introducing indel-4 from the Tripsacum allele into modern hybrids can ensure yield potential and improve harvest index under dense planting. Consequently, this study generated outstanding dwarf resources through distant hybridization, thereby providing valuable genetic reservoirs for maize dwarf breeding. 

# Results

# MTP-induced maize to produce dwarf library by distant hybridization

We conducted mutagenesis on maize lines (B73, Mo17, zheng58, chang7-2, and PH6WC) using the distant hybridization method with MTP to facilitate fundamental research and identify novel alleles for plant height in maize. Following early identification and purification steps, we obtained ten independent dwarf families (Figures 1 and S1). Library Screening resulted in the discovery of numerous dwarf materials across different maize inbred lines. Notably, the dwarf materials d056, d114, and d417 demonstrated the novel characteristic of initially reducing ear height, thereby enhancing the potential for lodging resistance. Concurrently, with the reduction in overall plant height, we observed that most dwarf materials were approximately half the height of tall wild plants, as exemplified by d024, d056, d114, d259, d260, and d417 (Figure 1b; Table S1). The uniformity of the plant structure and the coordination of male and female characteristics in these materials provide an excellent dwarf germplasm for maize dwarf breeding. Furthermore, five stable and excellent new dwarf materials were obtained from dwarf families (Figure 1c). In addition to uniform dwarfism, these mutants exhibited slightly shorter leaves and tassels, accompanied by marginal decreases in seed size and weight per plant (Table S2). Allele tests demonstrated that all $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ plants exhibited distinct phenotypes compared to the parents, indicating that plant height genes d024, d056, d114, d328, and $d 4 1 7$ were not allelic to Br2, and these dwarf materials were controlled by their respective dwarf genes (Figure S2). 

$\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ and BC segregation populations using five dwarf materials and six backbone inbred lines were constructed for genetic analysis. The $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ and BC generations revealed segregation ratios of 3:1 and 1:1 (tall/dwarf) in plant height, respectively (Table S3), proving that a single recessive gene governs the dwarfing phenotypes of d024, d056, d328, and d417. Additionally, all $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ plants in which d114 was crossed with B73 displayed normal phenotypes resembling those of the tall-type parent (Table S4). Furthermore, the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population exhibited a single peak and normal distribution of plant height (Figure S3). These data suggested that d114 behaves in a polygenic recessive manner. Regarding hormones responses in the dwarf materials, the results showed that d114 did not respond to any of the three hormones $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ , IAA, and BR), while d056, d328, and d417 exhibited responsiveness to exogenous $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ at high concentrations. However, d024, increased with increasing BR concentrations, indicating a specific sensitivity to high concentrations of BR (Figure S4).

# Allelic variation from Tripsacum (parent of MTP) leads to d024 dwarfism

BR-related dwarfing materials are extreme dwarfs and are difficult to apply in breeding (Best et al., 2016; Kir et al., 2015). Nevertheless, we identified a BR-related dwarf mutant, designated d024, which displayed semi-dwarfing and androgynous coordination in the dwarf mutant library. In addition to the semi-dwarfing trait, other characteristics of d024 included shrunken and curved leaves, exhibiting the distinctive feature of deep green leaves (Figure S5). Furthermore, traits such as tassel length, tassel branch number, ear length, ear width, ear row number, hundred-grain weight, and total node number showed slight alterations compared to those of the wild type (H024) (Figure 2a; Table S2). We compared the 

number and length of internodes between d024 and the H024 (WT) and found that the number of internodes of d024 was similar to that of the WT, whereas the internodes at the ear position and all internodes above the ear position were significantly shortened (Figure 2b). This observation indicated that the reduced height of the higher internodes mainly contributed to the dwarf phenotype of d024. The cytological examination further revealed that the length of longitudinally cut cells in the ear stem nodes of d024 was significantly reduced by $4 3 . 3 \%$ compared with that of the WT (Figure 2c–e). In conclusion, these findings suggested that dwarfism in d024 can be attributed to a decrease in the cell length of the higher internodes.

# ZmCYP90D1 positively regulates plant crop height

We generated Zm00001d039453 knockout mutants in the maize inbred line B73 background using the CRISPR/Cas9 technology (Figure S15) to assess whether the mutation in Zm00001d039453 caused the dwarf phenotype. The KO#1 (zmcyp90d1–1) and KO#2 (zmcyp90d1–2) mutants displayed similar semi-dwarf or more severely defective phenotypes (much more dwarf) than the d024 mutant (Figure 3a, b) and exhibited significantly lower expression levels than B73 in the stem (Figure 3c). Together, these results verified that the plant height phenotype of d024 was caused by the loss of ZmCYP90D1. Furthermore, ZmCYP90D1 was constitutively expressed in all the examined tissues, with high expression in the ear internodes (Figure S16). Subcellular localization analysis of ZmCYP90D1 revealed that it was expressed in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) (Figure S17). 

We generated transgenic Arabidopsis AtOE#1 and AtOE#2 plants harbouring ZmCYP90D1. The transformed Arabidopsis plants were cultivated on $1 / 2 \times { \mathsf { M S } }$ media plates supplemented with $5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g / m L }$ kanamycin (Figure S18). Subsequently, the heights of the AtOE#1 and AtOE#2 lines were approximately $2 1 . 9 \%$ and $3 0 . 7 \%$ greater than those of Col-0, respectively (Figure 3d, e). The expression levels of ZmCYP90D1 were higher in the stems of AtOE#1 and AtOE#2 plants than in those of Col-0 plants (Figure 3f). Furthermore, we screened the T-DNA insertion mutant SALK-036476 (AT3G13730) to validate the homologous gene function of ZmCYP90D1 in Arabidopsis thaliana. At the termination of flowering, the height of the SALK-036476 lines was approximately $3 4 . 8 \%$ lower than that of Col-0, and the expression levels of AT3G13730 were lower in the stems of SALK-036476 than in Col-0 (Figure S19). These results suggested that ZmCYP90D1 significantly influenced crop height.

# Mechanism of a novel dwarf mutant inducer tool

Typically, the pathway for creating dwarf crop resources involves natural, physical, chemical (EMS), and biological mutations. Since the Br2 gene of natural mutation was identified and cloned (Multani et al., 2003) and subsequently used in large numbers around the world, which has created a narrow genetic basis for dwarf breeding parents, it has been difficult to make a great breakthrough at the breeding level (Balzan et al., 2018; Pilu et al., 2007). Therefore, it is crucial to develop new dwarf resources to improve crop dwarfing (Liu et al., 2019). EMS-induced maize dwarfs, such as na2, Brd1, and gad5/dwarf5, exhibit extreme dwarfing and tortile leaves (Best et al., 2016; Li et al., 2023; Makarevitch et al., 2012). Mutator2 (Mu2)-tagged dwarf maize an1 reduced internode lengths, foreshortened broad leaves, and unbranched tassels (Bensen et al., 1995). From the perspective of maize dwarf phenotypes, all other dwarf materials, except for Br2 plants, which display better coordination, exhibited extreme dwarfism or were associated with unfavourable traits. Although genetic engineering technology can generate semi-dwarf or extremely dwarfing maize, there have been no reports on its utility in dwarf breeding (Li et al., 2020; Paciorek et al., 2022). 

In this study, we used the genetic bridge MTP (derived from maize, T. dactyloides, and Z. perennis) to establish a novel approach for distant hybridization. Using this method, we created a library of genetically stable and excellent dwarf germplasms from introgression lines of MTP-distant hybrid maize. We searched ten genetically stable and excellent dwarf germplasms from the library, yet we also found many more extremely dwarfing materials that were not selected. Each semi-dwarf material exhibited unique characteristics (Figures 1 and S1). In general, the dwarfing library exhibited semi-dwarfing traits with coordinated characteristics, providing favourable conditions for maize dwarf breeding. Each semi-dwarf material in our library was controlled by its respective dwarf genes, but neither allele was controlled by Br2 (Figure S2). Our studies further showed that both the specific characterization of internodes and the differential hormonal responses emphasize the all-encompassing nature of our dwarf library. Furthermore, the vast majority of identified dwarf genes behave in a recessive manner, and only D8, D9, and D11 are dominant genes (Wang et al., 2016; Winkler and Freeling, 1994). # ZmBES1/BZR1-5-ZmCYP90D1-ZmFDXs/ZmNAD(P)H associate regulatory model for dwarf

The key regulatory genes involved in the BR synthesis pathway were identified. DWARF4 encodes a C-22 hydroxylase in the intermediate steps of the BR biosynthesis pathway and is classified into the CYP90B subfamily (Fujita et al., 2006), and the CPD gene catalyses C-23 hydroxylation and belongs to the CYP90A1 subfamily (Ohnishi et al., 2012). The ROT3 gene encodes a CYP90C with steroid hydroxylases, which are necessary for the regulated middle steps of the BR synthesis pathway (Kim et al., 1998). Several major BR regulatory pathway genes have been identified in maize. ZmDWF4 reactions in the BR biosynthetic pathway belong to the cytochrome P450 superfamily (Liu et al., 2007). The Zmbrd1/lil1 gene controls the final steps of biosynthesis and encodes a BR C-6 oxidase belonging to the CYP85A family (Castorina et al., 2018; Makarevitch et al., 2012). Coincidentally, our findings revealed that ZmCYP90D1 is the dwarf gene of d024. In addition, d024 and ZmCYP90D1-knockout plants showed lower levels of CS and TY than WT (Figure S24a). # The promoter variation as potential targets for distant hybridization

During the process of distant hybridization, excellent alleles of wild parents are always exchanged with maize, with the target 

of this introgression likely being the promoter region, resulting in new types of superior characteristics. For example, Upright Plant Architecture 2 is controlled by a two-base sequence variation that regulates the expression of downstream genes, which reduces the leaf angle originating from teosintes (Tian et al., 2019). Similarly, the bZIP68 promoter has a 358-bp insertion/deletion (Indel-972) polymorphism in maize and its wild ancestor, teosinte, which influences the transcriptional activation of bZIP68 (Li et al., 2022). Here, we demonstrated that the 191-bp (indel-4) promoter variation from tetraploid T. dactyloides regulates the expression of ZmCYP90D1 (Figures S9 and S10). The indel-4 variation in the promoter region directly governs the dwarfing phenotype of d024. This finding underscored the importance of introgression in the promoter region during distant hybridization, 

which influences gene expression. Therefore, introgression at the promoter can affect maize characteristics by altering the binding affinity of transcription factors, thereby modulating their functionality. We introduced this variation conferring semi-dwarfism through repeated backcrossing and molecular marker-assisted selection into two parents of elite maize hybrids widely cultivated in China to assess the potential of indel-4. 

# The indel-4 of Tripsacum represents great potential for breeding

Given the escalating global demand for corn owing to the expanding world population and rapid growth in feedstock needs, there is a pressing need to enhance crop yields on limited arable lands to achieve higher productivity (Shiferaw et al., 2011; Tilman et al., 2011). Increasing planting density and enhancing lodging resistance in maize are key factors, and achieving short and thick characteristics is crucial for achieving these objectives (Duvick and Cassman, 1999; Gou et al., 2007). Semi-dwarf mutants in rice (Sd1) and wheat (Rht) have been widely used in breeding programs to address these challenges (Angira et al., 2019; Wurschum€ et al., 2017). Moreover, efforts have been made to overexpress ZmDWF4 in maize to enhance yield; however, the impact has been limited to individual yield increases and has proven challenging for high-density production (Liu et al., 2020). Nevertheless, the ZmSPL12 inbred line could reduce plant height, increase lodging resistance under 135 000 plants/ha, and have a higher grain yield than CK (Zhao et al., 2022). The limitation is that dwarf hybrid combinations were not tested in the high-density experiments. Nevertheless, integrating shortstature maize into commercial maize production can significantly enhance global maize production on limited arable land. The indel-4 allele in Tripsacum induces semi-dwarfism and stem thickening, improves lodging resistance, and ensures high yield under high-density planting conditions. Restricting BR production in the nutrient tissues of dwarf corn results in a decrease in straw biomass. However, the yield per plant at 127500 plants/ha remained unchanged, increasing the harvest index by approximately 0.14–0.17. The increase in the harvest index observed in short-stature maize represents a significant improvement with the potential to enhance commercial maize productivity. In addition, the improved $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ $( { \mathsf { B } } 7 3 ^ { \mathsf { d } 0 2 4 } \times { \mathsf { M } } 0 1 7 ^ { \mathsf { d } 0 2 4 } )$ and original $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ $( { \mathsf { B } } 7 3 \times { \mathsf { M o } } 1 7 )$ exhibited similar grain yields at planting densities of 127 500 plants/ha. This underscored the potential of the indel-4 allele of Tripsacum to enhance yield potential and improve the harvest index under dense planting conditions. Higher yields and improved lodging resistance can be anticipated with further optimization of the density and traits. The Tripsacum indel-4 allele is a valuable resource for enhancing maize yield per unit area. In summary, the results obtained from the dwarf material (d024) in this library emphasize the potential for the continued identification of dwarfs and optimization of known breeding genes, representing a significant breakthrough in maize dwarf breeding. 

# Experimental procedures

# Plant materials and phenotype analysis

The genetic bridge MTP corresponding to tetraploid Z. mays (V182), tetraploid T. dactyloides (TZ07), and tetraploid Z. perennis (9475) was established (Yan et al., 2020), Therefore, F1 plants were obtained by crossing MTP as the donor parent and with maize inbred lines (B73, Mo17, Zheng58, Chang7-2, and 

PH6WC) as the recurrent parent. Subsequently, the ${ \mathsf { B C } } _ { \mathsf { x } } { \mathsf { S } } _ { \mathsf { y } }$ maize introgression lines (MILs) were synthesized through repeated backcrossing and self-crossing (Figure 1a). The ${ \mathsf { B C } } _ { 3 } { \mathsf { S } } _ { 3 - } { \mathsf { B C } } _ { 3 } { \mathsf { S } } _ { 4 }$ dwarf mutant materials were identified in Wenjiang in Sichuan Province, China (17WJ, $3 0 ^ { \circ } 4 0 ^ { \prime }$ N, $1 0 4 ^ { \circ } 0 4 ^ { \prime }$ E, altitude $7 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m }$ ) and Jinghong in Yunnan Province, China (17JH, $2 2 ^ { \circ } 0 1 ^ { \prime } \mathsf { N } _ { \cdot }$ , $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } 5 8 ^ { \prime } ~ \mathsf { E } ,$ altitude $5 5 1 \mathrm { ~ m } )$ in 2016. Then, we continued to purify and preserve the dwarf materials in 2017 and 2018. Detailed analyses of the phenotypes were conducted, recording parameters such as plant height, the number and length of nodes, tassel length, node diameter, and the size and weight of seeds. Arabidopsis (Columbia ecotype, Col-0) and SALK_036476 T-DNA insertion mutants (Col-0) were supplied by the Arabidopsis Biological Resource Center (https://www.arashare.cn/index/). 

For the skotomorphogenesis analyses of the dwarf materials, sterilized seeds were grown hydroponically in complete darkness at $2 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ and $80 \%$ relative humidity for 2 weeks. To measure cell size, mature stem at the ear position of three individuals were sampled from dwarf material d024 and high material H024 (WT). The longitudinal cells of the stem were observed using an Olympus BX53 microscope. Three fields were observed for each stem, and twenty cells per field were measured. The average length of measured cells from three stems was used to represent cell length for each genotype, and cell size was calculated using ImageJ software. 

# Response of plant height growth to exogenous IAA, $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 } ,$ and epiBL treatment

WT and dwarf plants were sprayed exogenous indole-3-acetic acid (IAA), gibberellic acid $( \mathsf { G A } _ { 3 } )$ , and epiBL after 40 days in field growth. The exogenous IAA containing 0, 50, 100, and $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ , $\mathsf { G A } _ { 3 }$ containing 0, 30, 40, and $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / L }$ , and epiBL containing 0, 1, 10, and $1 0 0 \ \mathrm { \ u g / L }$ . The exogenous hormone treatment was applied every five days. Treated plants were cultivated until the pollination period, and subsequently, plant heights were measured. 

WT and dwarf seeds were surface-sterilized with $10 \%$ (W/V) NaClO for 10 min and washed with sterile water. After five days of germination, seedlings exhibiting consistent growth were selected and transferred to a Hoagland liquid solution containing 0, 1, 10, and $1 0 0 { \mathrm { ~ u g } } / { \mathrm { L } }$ of epiBL, respectively. The seedlings were grown hydroponically in a $2 8 { \ ^ \circ \mathsf { C } } / 2 5 { \ ^ \circ \mathsf { C } }$ (day/night) chamber at $80 \%$ relative humidity under a 16 h-light/8 h-dark photoperiod. The Hoagland liquid solution was renewed every three days. The plant height was measured on the 9th day of treatment, with at least 10 seedlings for each material. 

# Mapping, isolation, and sequencing of the ZmCYP90D1

The different tall maize inbred lines (Jing724, PH6WC, B73, Mo17, chang7-2, SW1611, and H024) were selected to construct $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ and ${ \mathsf { B C } } _ { 1 }$ populations with d024, d056, d114, d328, and d417. Subsequently, the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ populations derived from the cross between d024 and H024 were utilized for bulked segregant analysis (BSAseq). In this approach, the DNA pool was generated by bulking 25 dwarfs or 21 wild-type individuals, and their DNA was used to construct the dwarf bulk (DB) and the high bulk (HB), respectively. High-throughput sequencing of DNA libraries was conducted using Illumina HiSeqTM2000/MiSeqTM at novogene Technology Co. Ltd. (Beijing, China), with the B73 reference genome (RefGen_v4) used as the reference (Li and Durbin, 2009). The BSA-seq data were deposited in Beijing Institute of Genomic Data Center (GSA: CRA014241) (Chen et al., 2021). 

A more detailed fine-mapping analysis was constructed based on 2762 individuals in $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ populations by d024 and SW1611. Indeed, simple sequence repeat (SSR) makers obtained from the MaizeGDB Database (https://maizegdb.org/) and indel molecular makers provided by the Jian Liu research group at Sichuan Agricultural University were employed for this analysis (Table S11) (Liu et al., 2015). To identify the mutation site of the ZmCYP90D1 gene, according to B73 RefGen_v4, we amplified the full-length coding sequence (CDS) and the ${ \sim } 2 . 3 ~ \mathsf { k b }$ promoter region of the ZmCYP90D1_T002 between d024 and WT. 

# Transgenic functional validation

Targeted gene editing CRISPR/Cas9 technology was employed to generate ZmCYP90D1 knockout plants. A target site located from 14 to 36 bp after the ATG codon was synthesized and cloned into pCPB-UBi::hspCas9 vector. The maize inbred line (B73) was utilized for mutant generation, and the ZmCYP90D1 knockout plants were created by Wuhan Biorun Biotechnology Co., Ltd (Wuhan, China). 

Wild-type Arabidopsis thaliana (L.) at the flowering stage were subjected for Agrobacterium-mediated transformation of harbouring 35S-ZmCYP90D1_T002-eGFP according to the floral-dip method with slight modifications (Clough and Bent, 2008). The ${ \sf T } _ { 1 }$ seeds were plated onto 1/2x MS medium plates with $5 0 ~ \mathrm { { u g / m } | }$ kanamycin for screening of the transformants. The $\mathsf { T } _ { 2 }$ plants were evaluated and self-pollinated to generate ${ \sf T } _ { 3 }$ . The homozygous lines without segregation in ${ \sf T } _ { 3 }$ were selected, and two independent transformants, including the AtOE#1 and AtOE#2 lines, were used for further study (Table S12). 

# Phylogenetic analysis

The full-length amino acid sequence of ZmCYP90D1_P002 was utilized to perform BLAST search in protein databases of GRAMENE (http://www.gramene.org/) and TAIR (http://www. arabidopsis.org) to identify the homologous genes in Oryza sativa, Zea mays, Arabidopsis, and Sorghum. The amino acid sequences were aligned using the clustalw-2.0.10 software. A phylogenetic tree was constructed using a neighbour-joining method with bootstrapping (1000 replicates) in MEGA 7.0 software (Kumar et al., 2016). 

# RNA isolation and RT-qPCR analysis

Total RNA was extracted using the HiPure Plant RNA Mini Kit (Magen, Guangzhou, China) kit from five-day-old primary roots, mesocotyl, and sixty-day-old primary roots, ear node, ear leaf, silk, and tassel. The RNAs were reverse transcribed using RevertAid First Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (TaKaRa, Beijing, China) with random primers or $\mathsf { \Lambda } ) | { \mathsf { i } } _ { 9 } \mathsf { o } ( { \mathsf { d } } \mathsf { T } ) _ { 1 2 - 1 8 }$ primers. The sixty-day-old ear node of mutant and WT were sampled for expression analysis. RT-qPCR was performed with the SYBR- Premix Ex TaqTM II (TaKaRa, Beijing, China) on an ABI 7500 real-time detection system. Three independent biological replicates were collected for each tissue and four technical replicates. The relative expression level was determined using the $2 ^ { - \Delta C t }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen, 2001) with ZmGAPDH genes as the reference controls (Table S12). 

# Quantification of endogenous Brassinolide

Endogenous BRs levels in the stem internodes from d024, KO#1, and H024 (WT) plants were determined using gas chromatography–mass spectrometry (GC–MS). Ear node from d024, KO#1, and WT were harvested during the sixty days after 

germination and immediately lyophilized at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . Ear nodes were extracted with $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of $9 5 \%$ methanol, purification and quantification were performed according to the method described by Nanjing Ruiyuan Biotechnology Co., Ltd (Nanjing, China). 

# Subcellular localization

The full-length CDS of ZmCYP90D1_T002 without the stop codon was amplified and cloned into xba1 enzyme sites of the pCAMBIA2300-GFP vector to generate the 35S::ZmCYP90D1- GFP fusion plasmids (Table S13). The resulting constructs were transformed into GV3101, with GV3101 containing nucleus and endoplasmic reticulum (ER) serving as localization markers. Different combinations of GV3101 were transiently co-expressed in tobacco (Nicotiana benthamiana) leaf epidermal cells and the fluorescence signals were assessed using the confocal microscope LSM880 (Carl Zeiss, Germany). 

# Promoter activity assays

The CYP90D1 promoter sequences (indel-4) of Tripsacum (329 bp) and B73 (520 bp) were amplified and cloned into the P1300-GUS-T vector (Table S13). The plasmids were introduced into the Agrobacterium strain GV3101, which was injected into four-week-old N. benthamiana leaves with MMA medium (50 mM MES, 10 mM MgCl , $2 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M }$ acetosyringone, pH 5.6) for transient protein expression. The GUS activity assays using a GUS staining kit (Coolaber, SL7160). 

# Yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) validate protein interaction

Total RNA was extracted from sixty-day-old B73 seedlings and genomic DNA was removed after DNaseI treatment. cDNA was synthesized using RevertAid First Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (TaKaRa). The Y2H assay was conducted according to the manufacturer’s manual (PEG/LiAC Transformation Solution, YT0006, Coolaber) and the Matchmaker GAL4 two-hybrid system. The CDS of ZmCYP90D1_T002 were cloned into the bait plasmid pGBKT7 (BD). To confirm the identified protein–protein interactions, the CDS of candidate genes were cloned into the pGADT7 (AD). BD-ZmCYP90D1 and the AD-candidate fusions vector (empty vector was used as negative control) were transferred together into yeast Y2HGold using the PEG/LiAc method. After culturing on synthetic medium plates (SD medium) without Trp and Leu ( LT) for two days, the transformants were transferred onto SD-Trp/ Leu/-His/ Ade (-LWAH) for an additional three or four days. 

# Bimolecular fluorescence complementation and split firefly luciferase complementation assay

To generate the constructs for the bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) assays, the full-length CDS of ZmFDX2, ZmFDX5, ZmNAD(P)H, and ZmCYP90D1 were amplified and cloned into the p106YN and p104YC vector for fusion with the N-terminus and C-terminus of YFP by linearizing with xba1, respectively (Table S13). The plasmids were introduced into the Agrobacterium strain GV3101, which was injected into fourweek-old N. benthamiana leaves with MMA medium for transient protein expression. The tobacco epidermal cells were then imaged by confocal laser scanning microscopy LSM880 (Zeiss, Germany), and the combination with empty vector was used as the negative control. 

The split firefly luciferase complementation assays (FLCA) were performed to examine the interactions between ZmFDX2, 

ZmFDX5, ZmNAD(P)H, and ZmCYP90D1 using the constructs nLUC and cLUC. The full-length CDS of ZmFDX2/ ZmFDX5/ZmNAD(P)H without the stop codon and ZmCYP90D1 were amplified and cloned into nLUC and cLUC, respectively, forming the ZmFDX2/ZmFDX5/ZmNAD(P)H-n and c-ZmCYP90D1 constructs (Table S13). The two fusion constructs were cotransformed to tobacco (N. benthamiana) leaves. After culturing for two days, luciferin (1 mM) was injected to activate luciferase, and the fluorescence signals were observed by the chemiluminescent imaging system (Tanon-5200), and the combination with empty vector was used as the negative control.
