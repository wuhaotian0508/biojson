# ABSTRACT

Global consumer acceptance of gene-edited food crops is increasing with new breeding technologies that can modify the genome without foreign DNA integration. Here, we report an Agrobacterium-based system for transgene-free, gene editing of the banana cultivar, Cavendish. The protocol uses a three-tiered approach whereby cells containing T-DNA are enriched by positive antibiotic selection over a 48-h period, CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene editing occurs over a short transient window and negative selection of cells containing T-DNA is achieved by the addition of 5-FC, which is converted to cytotoxic 5-FU by the co-expressed CODA enzyme. Two key enzymes in the carotenoid biosynthesis pathway were targeted as visual markers of editing: phytoene desaturase (pds) and lycopene $\beta$ -cyclase (LCYb). Disruptive edits of the LCYb gene were easily identifiable at the calli stage of the regeneration process with cells appearing pink due to lycopene accumulation. Eight of 32 plants for pds and 34 of 125 plants for LCYb contained edits and were likely free of integrated vector DNA as determined by targeted genome sequencing and T-DNAspecific PCR, respectively. Plants containing disruptive tri-allelic edits in either gene displayed an albino phenotype. A subset of potentially transgene-free, edited plants was verified by whole genome sequencing. The transient editing protocol has an estimated $1 7 . 6 \%$ to $2 1 . 9 \%$ efficiency in generating plants containing disruptive tri-allelic modifications that are free of novel DNA. The protocol overcomes the regulatory limitations associated with the release of gene-edited, vegetatively propagated crops and provides an effective means of creating new disease-resistant and agronomically superior Cavendish cultivars. 

# 1 | Introduction

The vast majority of cultivated bananas are selections of naturally occurring hybrids. They are parthenocarpic with seedless fruit and are vegetatively propagated from suckers that develop laterally from the rhizome. Near sterility has hampered the introgression of new agronomic traits into these cultivars through conventional breeding, with genetic improvements mainly limited to the selection of spontaneous or induced 

mutation variants. For instance, the Cavendish subgroup (Musa acuminata, AAA), which constitutes more than half the bananas grown worldwide, is represented by many variants/cultivars including Grand Nain, Williams, Valery, Dwarf Cavendish and Baxijiao. More recently, genetic modification has been used to generate new traits including fungal disease resistance (Dale et  al.  2017; Harding et  al.  2025), enhanced micronutrient content in the fruit (Paul et  al.  2017; Buah et al. 2025), and extended fruit shelf-life (Elitzur et al. 2016). 

The emergence of plant gene editing technologies through CRISPR/Cas9 and similar site-directed nuclease technologies, has provided new approaches to re-model the banana genome (Tripathi et al. 2024; Miao et al. 2025). Gene editing offers the capacity to generate gene knockouts, to change nucleotides or to make epigenetic modifications. The new ‘search-andreplace’ genome editing platforms offer even greater flexibility to insert, replace or delete sequences (Anzalone et  al. 2019). These advances have provided new opportunities to genetically improve Cavendish and create new variants that are subject to a reduced regulatory burden. 

To date, CRISPR/Cas9-mediated editing has had limited applications in Cavendish. In early studies, we used Agrobacterium to stably deliver Cas9 and a polycistronic gRNA targeting the phytoene desaturase (pds) carotenoid biosynthesis gene resulting in photobleaching in cv. Williams (Naim et al. 2018). More recently, CRISPR/Cas9 editing has been used to generate useful agronomic traits in Cavendish such as fruit with reduced ethylene synthesis and extended shelf life by targeting the MaACO1 gene in cv. Brazilian (Hu et al. 2021) and the production of fruit enriched with $\beta$ -carotene by targeting the lycopene epsilon cyclase gene in cv. Grand Nain (Kaur et  al.  2020). In non-Cavendish cultivars, CRISPR/Cas9 editing has been used to engineer resistance to Xanthomonas wilt in cv. Sukali Ndizi (Tripathi et  al.  2021, 2024), semi-dwarfism in cv. Gros Michel (Shao et al. 2020) and elevated $\beta$ -carotene accumulation in the fruit of cv. Rasthali (Kaur et  al.  2020). In plantains, a type of cooking banana, gene editing has been used to confer resistance to Xanthomonas wilt (Ntui et al. 2023) and to disable integrated banana streak pararetrovirus sequences that can self-activate to form viral particles and initiate infection under conditions of stress (Tripathi, Ntui, et al. 2019). In all these examples, the gene editing machinery was delivered on T-DNA by Agrobacterium, the T-DNA was integrated into the host genome and the predominant edit type was a disruptive deletion. This gene editing approach is commonly used in sexually propagated plants, where the T-DNA can be eliminated through self-fertilisation or backcrossing, allowing the desired edit to be retained in the segregating, transgene-free progeny. This cannot be achieved with bananas and most other vegetatively propagated crops. 

The development of a gene editing platform for Cavendish without DNA integration would be a major step towards further trait development and, from a regulatory perspective, provide a simplified path to product release. Very recently, Van den Broeck et al. (2025) have described the use of an Agrobacteriummediated base editing system to generate transgene-free edited banana plants by co-editing the endogenous acetolactate synthase (MaALS) gene, enabling herbicide-based selection of edited, non-genetically modified (GM) individuals. This study marks a major advance in achieving transgene-free genome editing in banana. However, the efficiency of editing a single MaALS locus was low $( 3 . 2 \% )$ , and co-editing of a second target was reduced further to $1 . 0 \%$ . 

Here, we describe a CRISPR/Cas9 transient editing system in Cavendish cv. Williams, using Agrobacterium as the delivery vehicle, that incorporates both a short antibiotic selection regimen and a lethal negative selection agent to generate transgenefree, gene-edited plants. As convenient visual indicators, we 

independently targeted two endogenous carotenoid biosynthesis genes, pds and lycopene $\beta$ -cyclase (LCYb), for dropout editing using a polycistronic tRNA-gRNA system. This approach achieved tri-allelic editing efficiencies exceeding $1 7 \%$ . Phenotypic and genotypic analysis of regenerated events suggested this transient editing platform is an efficient means of creating tri-allelic gene knockouts without DNA integration. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | Vector Design

The strategy adopted here was reliant on the use of a positive selection regime to enrich for those cells containing extrachromosomal T-DNA and a negative selection regime to kill those cells containing integrated T-DNA (Figure 1). To achieve this, a codA:nptII gene fusion expression cassette was included in the T-DNA of CRISPR/Cas9 vectors targeting the banana pds and LCYb genes. Cells expressing the codA:nptII gene fusion would be resistant to the antibiotic kanamycin but capable of converting the non-toxic 5-fluorocytosine (5-FC) substrate to the cytotoxic 5-fluorouracil (Figure  2a). The codA:nptII gene fusion had previously been used to generate selectable marker gene-free banana plants using a recombinase-based excision platform, with 5-FC at $1 5 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ shown to be lethal to banana cells expressing the CODA:NPTII fusion protein (Kleidon et  al.  2020). NPTII-mediated selection using kanamycin (at a range of $1 0 0 { - } 2 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L ) }$ has been consistently used to isolate transformed banana cells by us and others (Khanna et al. 2004; Tripathi et al. 2021). To assist in the visual detection and tracking of banana cells containing T-DNA, a gfp expression cassette was included in one vector, pCOD:NPT-PDS-GFP (Figure 2a). The other vectors targeting pds (pCOD:NPT-PDS.V2) and LCYb (pCOD:NPT-LCYb), did not contain a gfp expression cassette (Figure 2a). In pCOD:NPT-PDS.V2, the pds target 1 site in the polycistronic tRNA-gRNA was replaced with a new target sequence located immediately downstream in pds exon 1. This new target 3 sequence had a higher potency score compared to the original target 1 sequence, as predicted by the WU-CRISPR gRNA design software (98 versus 69 rating, respectively) and, therefore, we assumed it would be more efficient at generating dropout deletions when used in conjunction with the original target 2 gRNA. pCOD:NPT-LCYb was essentially the same as pCOD:NPT-PDS.V2 except the two gRNA targets were specific for the LCYb gene. 

# 2.2 | Transient Editing Experiments

Up to three independent transient editing experiments were performed using embryogenic cells derived from the triploid Cavendish banana cv. Williams and recombinant Agrobacterium harbouring either pCOD:NPT-PDS-GFP, pCOD:NPT-PDS.V2 or pCOD:NPT-LCYb. Each experiment utilised two flasks containing approximately 3 mL of packed cell volume in a $5 0 \mathrm { m L }$ suspension. In parallel, stably transformed banana cells were also generated with these vectors using the standard protocol described by Khanna et al. (2004) with long-term selection using the antibiotic kanamycin $\mathrm { ( 1 0 0 m g / L ) }$ and no 5-FC treatment. # 2.3 | Targeted Genome Sequencing

A 444 base pair (bp) sequence of the pds gene was amplified by PCR, spanning the predicted gRNA target sites, using genomic DNA extracted from all 32 plants regenerated through the pds transient editing process (representative PCRs are shown in Figure S1). All lines, except 125–4 and 125–25, yielded a single ~450 bp amplicon suggesting the plants were either not edited or contained small indels. Line 125–4 produced three distinct PCR amplicons of ~750, ~450 and ~300 bp, the latter potentially representing a precise dropout. Line 125–25 produced two distinct PCR amplicons of ${ \sim } 6 5 0 $ and ~450 bp. 

For the 12 LCYb plants, two allele-specific primer sets were used to PCR amplify an 823 bp sequence (primers LCYb-F1/ LCYb-R1 for alleles 1 and 2) and an 822-bp sequence (primers LCYb-F2/LCYb-R2 for allele 3) of the LCYb gene, spanning the predicted 783-bp dropout region between the gRNA target sites (representative PCRs are shown in Figure S2). Only three lines (1326, 1426 and 1504) yielded a single ~820 bp amplicon. PCRs on the remaining nine plants generated at least two amplicons ranging in size from 630 to 820 bp suggesting these plants contained a combination of no edits, indels or dropout deletions. The one exception to this was line 1282, for which no amplicon was obtained using primers LCYb-F2/LCYb-R2, suggesting a larger deletion edit beyond one or both primer binding sites in allele 3. 

PCR products from each line were cloned and up to 10 colonies, representing each cloned PCR product, were selected. Plasmid DNA extracted from each colony was Sanger sequenced and aligned to the native banana pds and LCYb gene sequences. The resulting edit types identified for each pds and LCYb line are provided in Tables 1 and 2, respectively. Sanger sequencing identified potential edits in 9 of the 32 pds plants regenerated (Table 1). The modifications included predominantly small indels $( < 1 0 \mathrm { n t } )$ at one or both gRNA target site(s) but also larger insertions ranging from $+ 3 1$ to $+ 3 0 1$ nt and larger deletions ranging from $^ { - 1 2 }$ to −301 nt. One large dropout deletion (−138 nt) was identified 

in line 125–4. At least one disruptive edit (causing a frame shift and early termination of the pds coding region) was identified in all albino plants. Only a single form of pds modification was identified in the clones sequenced for albino lines 95–9, 95–10, 95–24 and 125–7. Importantly, Cavendish has a triploid (AAA) genome and, therefore, the clones sequenced may not fully capture the nature of the edit in all three pds alleles. Two distinct modifications were observed in albino lines 125–5 and 125–18 and three modifications were identified in albino line 125–25 (Figure 2c1). 

Line 125–21 was a green plant and, of the clones sequenced, only two contained a −2-nt indel at the gRNA target 2 site. The remainder of the clones matched wild-type pds sequence. This would suggest this plant contains a single disruptive monoallelic edit, the effect of which is compensated for by the remaining two intact alleles. Interestingly, all three alleles of the green plant 125–4 were edited (Figure 2c1). Three different modifications were identified in this plant, (1) a 138 nt dropout causing a 46 amino acid deletion towards the N-terminus of the PDS protein, (2) a short frame switch between the gRNA sites resulting in a PDS protein with 46 different amino acids in this region, and (3) a large $+ 3 0 1$ -nt insertion (with sequence similarity to the maize polyubiquitin promoter) that when combined with a −1- nt indel created an in-frame addition of 100 novel amino acids. Considering this plant was dark green, it is plausible that none of these protein modifications negatively affected PDS activity. Also, the addition of T-DNA-derived maize polyubiquitin promoter sequence at the edit site precluded this event from being considered transgene-free. There was no evidence of edits in any of the remaining green plants suggesting these were likely all escapes. 

In the case of the 12 LCYb plants, two primer sets were used to independently amplify gene target regions in alleles 1 and 2 (primers LCYb-F1/LCYb-R1) and allele 3 (primers LCYb-F2/ LCYb-R2). In doing so, we were able to assign edits more accurately to individual LCYb alleles and accordingly the data from this is presented differently in Table  2. In contrast to pds, all lines contained an edit of some form and large dropout deletions, ranging in size from 101 to $2 0 9 \mathrm { n t }$ , were detected in one or two of the LCYb loci of 8 of the 12 $( 6 7 \% )$ events. Of these, four plants (1291, 1311, 1325, 1489) contained dropout edits of or near to the predicted size (193–195 bp). Small indels ranging in size from $^ { - 4 }$ to $+ 1$ bp were also evident at one or both gRNA sites in 10 of 12 $( 8 3 \% )$ plants. # 2.4 | PCR Screening to Detect Integrated T-DNA

Six primer pairs were designed to amplify different gene sequences or genetic features interspersed throughout the T-DNA and common to all three CRISPR/Cas9 vectors. The PCR targets included the gfp-coding region (in pCOD:NPT-PDS-GFP only), Cas9 gene, codA-coding region, the right T-DNA border, the polycistronic tRNA-gRNA cassette, and the nptII-coding region. Plants that were PCR negative for these features were deemed potentially free of integrated T-DNA whereas those that were PCR positive were likely GM. Three controls were included for quality control and PCR validity. First, a primer pair designed to amplify a segment of the banana actin housekeeping gene was included for every plant to confirm the purity and integrity of the isolated gDNA template. Second, gDNA from a wild-type (WT) banana (Musa acuminata cv. Williams) was included as a template for all primer sets to confirm their specificity for the T-DNA targets only. Third, gDNAs isolated from two independent banana plants stably transformed with the vector pCOD:NPT-PDS-GFP (Lines S1 and S2) were included as templates for all primer sets to confirm their ability to amplify products from plant-derived gDNA. 

All 32 pds plants and 125 LCYb plants generated through the transient editing pipeline were subjected to PCR screening for integrated T-DNA (representative PCRs are supplied in Figures S3 and S4). PCRs using the actin primer set generated a 200 bp product for all plant gDNA extracts, confirming the integrity of all the nucleic acid templates. PCRs using the T-DNA-specific primers failed to amplify products from wild-type gDNA, confirming these primers were indeed highly specific to the T-DNA only. PCRs using the T-DNA-specific primers amplified products of the expected size from both stably transformed positive control lines, S1 and S2, confirming these primer sets were effective with plant-derived gDNA templates. PCRs using the T-DNA-specific primers failed to amplify products from 30 of the 32 pds plants $( 9 3 . 8 \% )$ with only lines 95–2 and 95–15 testing positive. For the LCYb plants, 111 of the 125 plants $( 8 8 . 8 \% )$ tested negative with the T-DNA-specific primer sets. Importantly, the 12 lines selected for further genetic characterisation all tested negative for integrated T-DNA. 

# 2.5 | Whole Genome Sequencing

The two albino lines, 95–9 and 95–10, generated through the transient editing experiments with vector pCOD:NPT-PDS-GFP and two lines 1291 and 1465 generated with vector pCOD:NPT-LCYb were subjected to whole genome sequencing (WGS) to definitively confirm the absence of integrated vector DNA and fully characterise their pds and LCYb modifications. In addition, a wild-type plant and the positive control line S2 (stably transformed with pCOD:NPT-PDS-GFP) were also sequenced. 

A minimum of 18 Gb of WGS data, representing a calculated $1 0 \times$ genome coverage, was requested; however, the amount received varied, ranging from 9.6 to 28.3 Gb, equivalent to approximately 5 to $1 6 \times$ coverage. Using stringent alignment settings, the short 150 bp reads representing about $1 0 \times$ genome coverage for the pds-edited plants were mapped to the complete sequence of vector pCOD:NPT-PDS-GFP (Figure 4A). For the S2 positive control plant for which $1 6 \times$ genome coverage of data was received, 13 040 reads (150 bp each) aligned to the entire vector sequence, corresponding to $1 . 9 \mathrm { M b }$ of data and an estimated plasmid coverage of ${ \sim } 1 0 2 \times$ . This not only confirmed the GM status of this plant but suggested it may contain as many as 6 or 7 copies of the pCOD:NPT-PDS-GFP T-DNA in addition to the integrated vector backbone, the latter likely due to border skipping by the VirD2 endonuclease during T-DNA transfer. In contrast, reads from the wild-type plant only mapped to two regions in the vector T-DNA, those being the duplicated 77 bp tRNA sequence in the polycistronic tRNA-gRNA cassette. This was anticipated as the tRNA system is a ubiquitously conserved RNA cleavage mechanism that uses RNaseP and RNaseZ to precisely cleave endogenous RNAs. For our purposes, the tRNA served to process the polycistronic gRNA into two independent target: scaffold segments. Like all plant species, the banana genome encodes an endogenous tRNA processing system, with copies of the sequence interspersed over a predicted 8 chromosomes (https:// banana-genome-hub.southgreen.fr/data_search/organism). The banana tRNA sequence is $9 8 \%$ homologous to the tRNA sequence utilised in our CRISPR/Cas9 vectors; hence, short reads derived from the wild-type plant containing this sequence will closely align to the tRNA vector sequence. Sequencing reads from pds lines 95–9 and 95–10 (Figure 3a) and LCYb lines 1291 and 1465 (Figure  3b) mapped similarly to those derived from the wild-type plant, aligning only to the tRNA sequences. When the mapping parameters were relaxed, reads from the wild-type and the four transiently edited plants did align to other short sequences (equal to or less than 20 bp) in the vector, including the two 20 bp gRNA pds and LCYb target sequences. 

WGS reads for pds-edited lines 95–9 and 95–10 and LCYbedited lines 1291 and 1465 were also mapped to the endogenous Cavendish pds exon 1 and LCYb coding sequences, respectively (Figure  S5). Reads aligning to the two pds gRNA target sites identified two additional modifications in both lines 95–9 and 95–10, not previously detected by targeted genome Sanger sequencing (Figure 4a,c). These three different edit types likely reflect modifications to each of the three pds alleles. Together this information confirmed both albino pds-edited plants 95–9 and 95–10, regenerated through the transient editing process, were indeed tri-allelic pds knockouts free of integrated vector DNA. For the LCYb-edited lines, mapping of short reads to the gRNA target sites validated the edits previously predicted by targeted genome sequencing (Figures 4b,c and S6). Together this information verifies that these plants are indeed edited and free of novel or foreign DNA. 

# 2.6 | Transient Editing Enables High Frequency of T-DNA-Free Edits

This study demonstrates the successful application of a transient gene editing protocol in banana, with a high number of 

lines generated and analysed for both pds (32 lines) and LCYb (125 lines). A large proportion of plants were confirmed to be T-DNA free, with $9 0 . 6 \%$ of pds and $8 8 . 8 \%$ of LCYb lines showing no detectable T-DNA integration (Table 3). Despite the majority of these T-DNA-free plants not containing edits (20 out of 29 for pds, or $6 9 \%$ , and 80 out of 111 for LCYb, or $7 2 \%$ ), editing efficiency (defined as the proportion of lines with edits in one, two, or all three alleles) for both genes was relatively high: $2 8 . 1 \%$ for pds and $2 4 . 8 \%$ for $L C Y b$ (Table 3). Importantly, $2 1 . 9 \%$ of pds lines and $1 7 . 6 \%$ of LCYb lines were both T-DNA free and carried triallelic knockouts, representing the most desirable outcome for transgene-free functional knockout applications. 

# 3 | Discussion

Many different varieties of the Cavendish subgroup are grown around the world; each likely selections or somatic variants that were propagated for their superior agronomic performance in different agro-ecological zones. As they are seedless and largely sterile, their further genetic improvement has been greatly limited particularly for disease resistance and nutritional value. The first deliberate genetic improvements to Cavendish were developed using genetic modifications for (1) resistance to Fusarium oxysporum f. sp. cubense (Panama Disease Tropical Race 4) by over-expression of a resistance gene derived from a wild banana relative (Musa acuminata spp. malaccensis) (Dale et  al.  2017; Harding et al. 2025), (2) elevated fruit pro-vitamin A (pVA) by over-expression of a Fe'i banana-derived phytoene synthase 2a (MtPsy2a) gene (Paul et al. 2017), and (3) delayed fruit ripening and extended shelf-life by suppression of two MADS box genes, MaMADS1 and MaMADS2, using RNA interference (Elitzur et  al.  2016). In other non-Cavendish cultivars, traditional GM strategies have also been used to elevate fruit pVA (Buah et  al.  2025); to generate resistance to biotic stresses including banana bunchy top disease (Shekhawat et al. 2012; Elayabalan et al. 2013), Fusarium wilt (Paul et al. 2011; Sunisha et al. 2020) and Xanthomonas wilt (Tripathi et al. 2014) and abiotic stresses including salinity and drought (Rustagi et al. 2015). 

With expanding banana genomic resources and rapidly evolving platforms for precision gene editing, it is possible that many of these transgene-derived traits could be generated by modifying the Cavendish genome using gene editing. Indeed, banana improvements in the form of $\beta$ -carotene-enriched fruit (Kaur et al. 2020), semi-dwarfism (Shao et al. 2020) and extended fruit shelf-life (Hu et al. 2021) via targeted CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene knockout have recently demonstrated this. In other plant systems, CRISPR/Cas9 genome editing has been used to engineer resistance to multiple pathogens including bacteria, fungi and viruses. In most of these cases, editing was used to attenuate the expression of a host disease ‘susceptibility’ (S) gene, that is, a gene that negatively regulates immunity or supports pathogen growth and survival. Early examples of S gene targets for disease resistance included dmr6 (Thomazella et al. 2021), the SWEET sugar transporters (Oliva et  al.  2019) and SEC3A (Ma et al. 2018). In recent years, the number of S gene candidates has expanded significantly (Bishnoi et al. 2023; Koseoglou et al. 2022). These modes of editing do, however, have great practical value for generating gene knockouts, knockdowns or potentially upregulating a host gene's expression by manipulating its transcriptional regulatory sequence(s). Considering these restrictions, we sought to develop a gene editing platform for Cavendish that could modify the genome without extraneous DNA integration. We adopted a strategy similar to that described by Bánfalvi et al. (2020) in potato, whereby the CRISPR/Cas9 editing machinery was delivered on T-DNA via Agrobacterium-mediated transformation and a positive/negative selection scheme was incorporated in the banana regeneration process. The method was dependent on a codA:nptII gene 

fusion, the product of which confers both resistance to the antibiotic kanamycin for positive selection and catalyses the conversion of 5-FC to the toxic product 5-FU for negative selection of cells containing T-DNA. A short kanamycin selection window of 2 days post Agrobacterium co-culture was sufficient to enrich for transformed cells and suppress the proliferation of those not containing T-DNA. Omission of the antibiotic for the remainder of the banana regeneration process likely reduced the potential for T-DNA integration. This result is similar to Bánfalvi et al. (2020) who showed transient editing in potato was effective and DNA integration was reduced using a short 3-day antibiotic selection window, compared to selection over a 14-day period. In the cases where T-DNA integration had occurred, the addition of 5-FC at the pre-embryogenic stage of banana development 

was sufficiently lethal as only 3 of 32 plants for pds and 16 of 125 plants for LCYb contained some form of integrated T-DNA. The time from Agrobacterium-mediated T-DNA delivery to plant regeneration using this process was quicker than that of standard stable transformation, taking between 6 and 7 months. We assume stable transformation is lengthier as the cells are continuously exposed to kanamycin which may slow cell growth rates even when nptII is expressed and/or constitutive expression of the selection gene may be a metabolic burden. The number of plants regenerated through transient editing, however, was about 10-fold lower compared to stable transformation, and the number of escapes regenerated was much higher, up to $7 0 \%$ for pds transient editing versus $0 \%$ for pds stable editing (Naim et al. 2018). The latter characteristic likely was due to the short kanamycin selection phase and the absence of prolonged antibiotic selection pressure. 

An important additional outcome of the results reported here is that the complete attenuation of LCYb activity resulted in an early visual indicator of editing success in the form of pinkcoloured calli. LCYb plays a central role at the lower branch point of the carotenoid biosynthesis pathway. Down-regulation of LCYb gene expression prevents the conversion of the red pigmented tetraterpene, lycopene, into downstream carotenoids such as $\propto$ -carotene, $\beta$ -carotene, and lutein and therefore stimulates its accumulation (Ma et al. 2011). Hence, the pink colouration is directly associated with lycopene accumulation in these cells. Plants regenerated from such calli, however, all developed albinism suggesting the enzyme is essential to maintain homeostasis in this and other interrelated photosystem pathways. For our purposes, this early visual marker has been particularly useful for rapidly assessing modifications to our editing platform and the pCOD:NPT-LCYb vector has served as a convenient internal control when editing new gene targets. 

In this study, polycistronic tRNA-gRNAs were delivered with the aim of generating precise dropout deletions in either the pds or LCYb genes to render them non-functional. For pds, the majority of edits identified were small (1–48 nt) indels at one or both gRNA sites, although a single accurate dropout edit was identified in one plant generated using COD:NPT-PDS.V2; a vector in which the gRNA cassette was re-engineered to contain more potent target sequences. In contrast, large deletions were obtained in $6 7 \%$ of LCYb-edited plants suggesting the effectiveness of creating dropouts using transient editing in banana was dependent on both the gene and gRNA design. Mono-, bi- and tri-allelic edits were identified and in one LCYb-edited plant, a complete sequence inversion between the target sites was detected. Overall, the generation of T-DNA-free Cavendish plants containing tri-allelic edits was approximately $1 7 \% - 2 2 \%$ . Together, these findings indicate the transient editing protocol is suitable for complete gene knockouts; an attribute that may be particularly useful for abolishing the expression of disease susceptibility genes. In addition, it could be utilised to knock down endogenous gene expression in an allele-specific and dose-dependent manner or perhaps create targeted sequence inversions with the aim of restructuring the genomic landscape. 

Only three other transgene-free editing strategies have been reported in banana. In the first example, Hu et al. (2023) used CRE/loxP and Saccharomyces cerevisiae FLP/FRT site-specific 

recombinases in combination with embryo-specific autoexcision to remove integrated sequences following CRISPR/ Cas9-mediated editing of the pds gene in Musa acuminata ‘Sucrier’ (AA group). Forty-eight percent of the resulting plants displayed an albino phenotype, contained disruptive pds edits and were likely transgene free, based on PCR and genome sequencing. While these plants were technically transgene-free, 138 bp of vector-derived sequence was retained in the banana genome following excision, including the right and left T-DNA borders and the LoxP/FRT recombinase scar sequences. Despite being a major step towards the development of transgene-free edited bananas, legislature in many jurisdictions would categorise these plants as GMOs. In the second example, Awasthi et  al.  (2022) used biolistics to deliver a CRISPR/Cas9 vector, targeting the carotenoid cleavage dioxygenase 4 gene, into embryogenic banana (Musa spp. cv. Rasthali) cells and regenerated plants without any antibiotic selection pressure. Over 300 plants were screened, and approximately $1 0 \%$ were reported to carry the same point mutation and be free of the Cas9 gene, although no direct evidence of the latter was provided. Moreover, the authors acknowledged that these plants were likely clones propagated from a single edited event via tissue culture, rendering any estimation of editing efficiency from their dataset highly speculative. The actual editing efficiency is therefore likely to be very low, as comparable strategies in other monocot model species rarely exceed $9 \%$ effectiveness (Liang et al. 2017; Poddar et al. 2023). While the ‘shot-gun’ selection-free strategy may be a viable approach to produce transgene-free, edited plants in a small number of species, it is well documented that this approach has proven relatively inefficient in most (generally $< 3 \%$ efficiency), requiring high-throughput screening to identify edited events and with an increased risk of generating chimeras that is, plants derived from a conglomerate of edited and unedited cells (Impens et  al.  2022; Kocsisova and Coneva  2023). Lastly, a 2025 publication by Van den Broeck and colleagues used an Agrobacterium-mediated base editing approach to produce transgene-free banana plants. Their method leveraged the endogenous MaALS gene to enable herbicide-based selection, allowing for the recovery of edited but non-GM individuals. While this represents important progress towards non-GM precision breeding in banana, editing efficiency at a single MaALS locus was low at $3 . 2 \%$ . Furthermore, when both MaALS alleles were co-edited to approximate multiplex editing efficiency for an agronomically relevant gene target, the success rate dropped further to only $1 . 0 \%$ . Although this system provides a valuable proof-of-concept, its practical utility for iterative or serial editing is fundamentally constrained. Once the MaALS gene has been modified to confer herbicide resistance, it can no longer be used as a selectable marker in subsequent rounds of editing, limiting its use in multistep trait stacking strategies. 

In comparison, the dual-selection, transient editing methodology presented here can produce plants containing deletion edits that are devoid of novel DNA, with a reduced risk of chimerism and without the need for mass screening. An additional advantage of this approach is its capacity to facilitate serial improvements through iterative rounds of transient and targeted gene modifications. 

The transient editing process detailed here is an efficient means of modifying the Cavendish genome that avoids the regulatory 

hurdles associated with GM plants and increases the prospects for stakeholder and public acceptance of new gene-edited banana varieties. It could be used to manipulate fruit quality traits, post-harvest attributes, metabolic pathways and potentially create disease resistance by simple knockout editing of critical gene targets that have been previously described or that are yet to be discovered. The ability to create large deletions in the Cavendish genome would greatly expand the capabilities of the system to include applications such as chromosome remodelling (Schindele et al. 2020; Rönspies et al. 2021) and attaining gain-of-function phenotypes (Bhunia et  al.  2022). In addition to Cavendish, this technology may be a useful platform for the improvement of other culturally and commercially relevant banana cultivars grown today or the modernisation of historical varieties that are not grown to the scale they once were; for instance the previously dominant export cultivar, Gros Michel, which was rendered commercially extinct in the mid-20th century due to its susceptibility to Fusarium wilt race 1. 

# 4 | Experimental Procedures

# 4.1 | Vectors

Two CRISPR/Cas9 vectors targeting the Cavendish pds gene were assembled based on the vector pUbi-Cas9-PDS described by Naim et al. (2018). First, the hygromycin resistance cassette (2X35SP-hph-35st) was replaced with the codA:nptII fusion gene expression cassette (35SP-int-AMV-codA:nptII-nosT) derived from pMF1 (Schaart et  al.  2004) using standard cloning techniques. This intermediate vector was called pCOD:NPT-PDS. Next, a green fluorescent protein (gfp) expression cassette (35SP-gfp-nosT) was inserted into the unique EcoRI site located between the codA:nptII selection cassette and polycistronic tRNA-gRNA cassette. The resulting vector was called pCOD:NPT-PDS-GFP. The second vector was a variation on pCOD:NPT-PDS in which the first PDS target sequence (Target 1: $5 ^ { \prime }$ -GTCTCTCCCATGAAGTTAAGTGG- $\cdot 3 ^ { \prime }$ ) in the polycistronic tRNA-gRNA cassette was replaced by a new target sequence (Target 3: ${ 5 ^ { \prime } }$ -CAATTCAGAGAAGATACTGGTGG-3′) with a higher potency score, as predicted by WU-CRISPR (http://crispr.wustl.edu), while Target 2 ( $5 ^ { \prime }$ -AAGGACAAGAAGCCAAGACATGG-3′) was common to both vectors. The target sequence was replaced by conventional overlapping PCR and the resulting vector was called pCOD:NPT-PDS.V2. Editing by gRNA1 and gRNA2 (pCOD:NPT-PDS-GFP) should create a 165 bp dropout in the pds gene, whereas editing with gRNA3 and gRNA2 (pCOD:NPT-PDS.V2) should create a 140 bp dropout. For editing the Cavendish LCYb gene, the 1803 bp Musa acuminata DH Pahang Lycopene beta cyclase, chloroplastic/chromoplastic gene (Macma4_09_g21100.1; chr09:31168431 to 31 170 233) was extracted from the Banana Genome Hub (Droc et al. 2013) and its 1515 bp coding region interrogated for potential gRNA target sites using WU-CRISPR. A third vector was assembled by replacing the two PDS gRNA target sequences in pCOD:NPT-PDS.V2 with target sequences specific to the $L C Y b$ gene (Target 1: $5 ^ { \prime }$ -AGAATTGAGTTGCTCCACCGAGG- $3 ^ { \prime }$ and Target 2: 5′-CCTTTAGATGAGTCATACAAGGG-3′) using overlapping PCR. Editing using this vector should create a 193 bp 

deletion in the LCYb coding region. Vectors pCOD:NPT-PDS-GFP, pCOD:NPT-PDS.V2 and pCOD:NPT-LCYb (Figure  2a) were Sanger sequence verified and mobilised into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain AGL1 by electroporation. 

# 4.2 | Banana Transient Editing and Positive/ Negative Selection

Transient editing of Cavendish cv. Williams embryogenic cells was essentially as described for stable transformation by Khanna et  al.  (2004), with the following modifications. For co-culturing with Agrobacteria, banana cells were not centrifuged with the bacteria but were dispersed on co-culture media that was solidified with agarose rather than agar. Cells were then incubated at $2 3 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 days in the dark. Residual Agrobacteria were removed by washing the cells three times in liquid BL-C media (BL containing $4 0 0 \mathrm { { m g / L } }$ cefotaxime). Cells were then diluted in liquid BL-AC media (BL containing $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ ascorbic acid, $4 0 0 \mathrm { { m g / L } }$ cefotaxime) to a ratio of 1:30 and $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of this cell suspension was transferred to a 50-mL conical flask. Flasks were incubated at $2 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ in the dark with shaking $( 9 0 \mathrm { r p m } )$ . After this, the media was replaced with BL-AC containing $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ kanamycin and again incubated at $2 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ in the dark with shaking (90 rpm). Residual kanamycin was removed by washing the cells three times in liquid BL-AC media. Cells were harvested and resuspended in $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of liquid BL-AC then aliquoted onto agar solidified BL-AC media overlayed with a glass fibre filter disk. Plates were incubated at $2 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark for 1 month, after which the cells were transferred to solid MA3 media containing $2 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ cefotaxime and $1 5 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ 5-fluorocytosine. Cells were maintained on this media for 3 months, with monthly subculturing, during which distinct proliferating embryos developed and were segregated. Plants were regenerated from embryos on MA4 media and rooted on MS media (both containing $2 0 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ cefotaxime and $1 5 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ 5-fluorocytosine). All tissue culture media constituents are described in Khanna et al. (2004). 

# 4.3 | PCR Screening to Detect Integrated T-DNA

Genomic DNA was purified from in  vitro banana leaf material using a GenElute Plant Genomic DNA Miniprep (Sigma-Aldrich; Merck Life Science Pty Ltd., Bayswater, VIC, Australia) according to the manufacturer's specifications. PCR was used to confirm the presence or absence of integrated vector DNA by designing primer pairs to amplify different genetic features present within the T-DNA (Table  S1). As an internal control, primers designed to amplify the banana actin housekeeping gene were used. Approximately $4 0 \mathrm { n g }$ of gDNA was used as the template in a PCR containing GoTaq Green PCR Master Mix system (Promega; Alexandria, NSW, Australia) and 10 ρmol of each primer pair (Table S1). PCRs were cycled using the following conditions: $9 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 min, followed by 35 cycles of $9 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 0 \mathrm { s } / 5 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } - 6 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $3 0 \mathrm { s } / 7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 min and a final extension of $7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 min. PCR amplicons were electrophoresed through a $1 . 5 \%$ agarose gel. 

# 4.4 | Targeted Genome Sequencing to Confirm pds and LCYb Edits

Approximately $4 0 \mathrm { n g }$ of plant gDNA, isolated as described above, was used as the template in a PCR with 2 units of Phusion High Fidelity DNA Polymerase (ThermoFisher Scientific; Seventeen Mile Rocks QLD, Australia) and primer pairs PDS-F2 $5 ^ { \prime }$ -TTTAATTATCGATTGTAGATAGACC -3′) and PDS-R2 $5 ^ { \prime }$ -GAGGGCTGGCACCATGTCTTGGCT -3′), LCYb-F1 $5 ^ { \prime }$ -TCTCTTTGGAATGTCTTCACCT-3′) and LCYb-R1 $5 ^ { \prime }$ -GGGCTTCTCAAGGTGCCT- $. 3 ^ { \prime }$ ), LCYb-F2 ( $5 ^ { \prime }$ -CTCTTTGGAATGTCTTCACCG-3′) and LCYb-R2 ( $5 ^ { \prime }$ -AGGCTTCTCAAGGTGCCT-3′). The PCR cycle conditions were as follows: an initial denaturation step at $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 min, 35 cycles of $9 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 15 s, $5 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 15 s, and $7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 15 s, followed by a final extension at $7 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 min. The resulting PCR products were electrophoresed through agarose and gel purified using a High Pure PCR Product Purification Kit (Merck). Purified amplicons were ligated into pJET1.2 vector using a CloneJET PCR Cloning Kit (ThermoFisher Scientific) according to the manufacturer's instructions and transformed into E. coli (XL1 Blue) using heat shock. Ten representative colonies were selected for each GM line and plasmid DNA isolated using alkaline lysis. Plasmids were sequenced using primers JET-F $5 ^ { \prime }$ -CCCAGTCACGACGTTGTAAAACG- $\cdot 3 ^ { \prime }$ ) and JET-R ( $5 ^ { \prime }$ -AGCGGATAACAATTTCACACAGG-3′) at Macrogen (Seoul, South Korea) and analysed using Geneious Prime 2024.0.7 (http://www.geneious.com). 

# 4.5 | Whole Genome Sequencing

Genomic DNA was purified from $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of in  vitro banana leaf using a GenElute Plant Genomic DNA Miniprep (Sigma-Aldrich). Approximately $4 0 0 \mathrm { n g }$ of gDNA from each plant was sent to Novogene (China) for Whole Genome Sequencing using NovaSeq PE150 with a minimum of 18 Gb per sample requested (representing an estimated 3n genome coverage of $1 0 \times$ ). Geneious Prime 2024.0.7 (http://www.geneious.com) was used to map reads to (1) vector sequences to detect the presence or absence of integrated vector DNA in the banana genome and (2) the banana pds and LCYb genes to confirm the nature of the edit in each allele. Mapping parameters were set at ‘Medium’ sensitivity/Fast with ‘Maximum’, gap size: 20 bp.
