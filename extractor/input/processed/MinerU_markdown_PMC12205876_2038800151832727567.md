# Summary

Carotenoid-rich orange-fleshed sweet potato (OFSP) is an important staple diet and source of nutrition in developing countries, including Africa and Asia. However, the regulation of carotenoid biosynthesis remains to be better understood. A natural allelic variation closely linked to carotenoid biosynthesis was identified in the promoter region of the IbbHLH25 gene that encodes a basic helix–loop–helix (bHLH) transcription factor, by transcriptome and haplotype analyses of different flesh colour sweet potato accessions. An 86-bp deletion reduced the transcription of the IbbHLH25 promoter in white- and yellow-fleshed sweet potatoes; however, the deletion was absent in OFSP. IbbHLH25 was highly expressed in the storage roots of carotenoid-rich sweet potato. The overexpression of IbbHLH25 significantly increased the carotenoid contents (by 2.5-fold–6.0-fold) and proportions, especially $\beta$ -carotene and bcryptoxanthin; their contents increased by 21.2-fold–55.7-fold and 4.6-fold–9.5-fold, respectively, and their proportions increased by $4 8 . 5 \%$ and $1 3 . 0 \%$ , respectively, and the silencing of IbbHLH25 had opposite effects. IbbHLH25 formed heterodimers with IbbHLH66 to directly and synergistically activate the transcription of carotenoid biosynthesis key genes IbGGPPS, IbLCYB and IbBCH. The overexpression of IbbHLH66 significantly increased the carotenoid contents (by 2.3-fold–3.8-fold) and proportions, especially $\beta$ -carotene and $\beta \cdot$ - cryptoxanthin; their contents increased by 15.2-fold–25.6-fold and 3.1-fold–5.1-fold, respectively, and their proportions increased by $3 1 . 1 \%$ and $9 . 6 \%$ , respectively. These findings expand our understanding of bHLHs in regulating carotenoid biosynthesis and suggest additional roles in affecting carotenoid component proportions, providing candidate genes for nutritional biofortification of agricultural products. 

# Introduction

Sweet potato, Ipomoea batatas (L.) Lam., is the eighth most important high-yield security crop worldwide, with an annual production of 88.87 million tons (Food and Agriculture Organization, 2021). It can not only adapt to marginal lands and barren soils, but also provide significant quantities of nutrients to subsistence farmers in developing countries, including Asia and Africa, where the population density is high (Wu et al., 2018). Orange-fleshed sweet potato (OFSP) is particularly rich in carotenoids, which are natural antioxidants that promote health and reduce the risk of certain cardiovascular diseases and cancers. OFSP contains $\beta$ -carotene and $\beta$ -cryptoxanthin, which are the primary precursors of vitamin A biosynthesis. OFSP can therefore lower the risks of premature death and various eye diseases, including macular degeneration, cataracts and blindness (Gao et al., 2021). The International Potato Center (CIP) has replaced white-fleshed sweet potato varieties, which lack $\beta$ -carotene and $\beta \cdot$ -cryptoxanthin, with varieties of OFSP that are rich in provitamin A. The consumption of OFSP can therefore effectively address the widespread concern about vitamin A deficiency, which affects 

over $40 \%$ of children under 5 years of age in sub-Saharan Africa (Wu et al., 2018). The 2016 World Food Prize was awarded to four scientists who revolutionized biofortification with OFSP and emphasized its significance in improving human health (World Food Prize Foundation, 2016). Therefore, further studies are necessary to obtain deeper insights into the mechanisms regulating carotenoid biosynthesis in sweet potato. 

Carotenoids are synthesized by plants, algae, fungi and bacteria (Xing et al., 2023), and nearly 100 carotenoids are present in detectable quantities in the human diet (Milani et al., 2017). Six carotenoids, namely, lycopene, $\pmb { \alpha }$ -carotene, bcarotene, $\beta$ -cryptoxanthin, lutein, and zeaxanthin, are most abundant in the blood plasma, comprising over $9 5 \%$ of the total carotenoid content. They can be categorized into two groups, namely, carotenoids without provitamin A activity (lycopene, lutein, and zeaxanthin) and carotenoids with provitamin A activity (a-carotene, $\beta \cdot$ -carotene and $\beta$ -cryptoxanthin) (Ines Elvira-Torales et al., 2019). GGPP is subsequently catalysed by phytoene synthase (PSY), phytoene desaturase (PDS), carotenoid isomerases (Z-ISO and CRTISO) and $\zeta \cdot$ -carotene desaturase (ZDS) to produce lycopene (Diepenbrock et al., 2021; Nisar et al., 2015). Lycopene is cyclized by lycopene e-cyclase (LCYE) and/or lycopene $\beta$ -cyclase (LCYB) to form symmetric $\nsim$ and $\beta$ -carotene, respectively (Sun and Li, 2020). The $\beta$ -carotene thus generated is converted to $\beta \cdot$ -cryptoxanthin by $\beta \cdot$ -carotene hydroxylase (BCH) (Ku et al., 2019). Although the carotenoid biosynthetic pathway is well understood in plants, the precise regulatory mechanisms require further investigation. 

Numerous studies have increased our understanding of carotenoid metabolism and genetic regulation in plants. In citrus, CrMYB68 directly targets CrBCH2 and CrNCED5 to repress the transformation of a- and $\beta$ -branch carotenoids in the flavedo layer (Zhu et al., 2017). The species-specific accumulation of carotenoids and apocarotenoids in the peel of citrus fruits is attributed to a natural variation in the promoter region of CrCCD4b, which encodes the CAROTENOID CLEAVAGE DIOXY-GENASE 4b CCD4 protein (Zheng et al., 2019). It has been reported that haplotype-1 of GmCCD4 is positively associated with higher carotenoid levels in soybean (Gao et al., 2021). The SlBBX20 protein of tomato modulates carotenoid biosynthesis by directly activating PSY1 and is subsequently targeted for 26S proteasome-mediated degradation (Xiong et al., 2019). Another study reported that the SlMBP15 protein interacts with RIN to alter carotenoid accumulation (Yin et al., 2018). The overexpression of the IbOr-R96H gene harbouring a single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) has been shown to significantly increase the carotenoid content of sweet potato compared to that of calli overexpressing the wild-type IbOr gene (Kim et al., 2021). By integrating metabolic and transcriptional analyses, a previous study identified that IbCCD4 plays a crucial role in reducing the carotenoid content of sweet potato (Zhang et al., 2023). Another study reported that the IbNAC29-IbMYB1R1-IbAITR5 module alters carotenoid biosynthesis by regulating the expression of IbPSY1 (Xing et al., 2023). The genetic basis and molecular mechanisms underlying natural carotenoid biosynthesis remain poorly investigated owing to the large and highly complex genome $( 4 . 4 ~ \mathrm { G b } )$ and high degree of self-incompatibility of hexaploid cultivated sweet potato $2 n = { \tt B } _ { 1 } { \tt B } _ { 1 } { \tt B } _ { 2 } { \tt B } _ { 2 } { \tt B } _ { 2 } =$ $6 x = 9 0 ^ { \cdot }$ ). 

The basic helix–loop–helix (bHLH) superfamily is the second largest transcription factor (TF) family that is widely present in 

eukaryotes and is named after the highly conserved bHLH domain (Li et al., 2006; Xue et al., 2022). The bHLH domain comprises 50–60 amino acids and two functionally distinct regions, including a basic region and an HLH region. The basic region, which binds to the E-box sequence $5 ^ { \prime }$ -CANNTG-30 ) to regulate the transcription of downstream genes, located at the N-terminus consists of approximately 15 amino acids, including six alkaline residues. The HLH region located at the C-terminus consists of two amphipathic $\propto$ -helices separated by a loop of variable length which interacts with other bHLH proteins to form homodimers or heterodimers (Pires and Dolan, 2010). The bHLH TF family has numerous functions in plants, including plant development (Fu et al., 2014; Lin et al., 2015), photomorphogenesis (Pham et al., 2018), anthocyanin biosynthesis (Tao et al., 2020) and abiotic stress responses (Geng and Liu, 2018; Liu et al., 2021; Xue et al., 2022). It has been reported that although several bHLH proteins are involved in carotenoid biosynthesis, their primary functions include the regulation of light and metabolic pathways in the chloroplasts of plant tissues (Bou-Torrent et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2023; Toledo-Ortiz et al., 2010) and fruit development (Zhou et al., 2019). The regulatory activities of bHLH proteins in plant roots have been scarcely investigated to date. 

In this study, the transcriptomes and haplotypes of sweet potato accessions with varying flesh colours were analysed. The findings revealed that a natural allelic variation in the promoter region of the IbbHLH25 gene, which encodes a bHLH TF, enhances carotenoid biosynthesis in sweet potato. The overexpression of IbbHLH25 significantly improved the contents and proportion of carotenoids, especially $\beta$ -carotene and $\beta$ -cryptoxanthin, in the storage roots of sweet potato. The results further demonstrated that IbbHLH25 TF interacts with IbbHLH66 to directly target the key genes involved in carotenoid biosynthesis, including IbGGPPS, IbLCYB and IbBCH. IbbHLH25 and IbbHLH66 synergistically activate the expression of these genes to promote carotenoid biosynthesis. Notably, the findings obtained herein provide novel insights into the genetic basis and molecular mechanisms underlying natural carotenoid biosynthesis in sweet potato.

# An 86-bp insertion in the promoter region of IbbHLH25 increases its expression

In order to establish whether IbbHLH25 is the candidate gene that regulates carotenoid biosynthesis, its expression patterns were examined in sweet potato varieties with varying flesh colours. Notably, the white/yellow/orange colour of the flesh was significantly positively correlated with the carotenoid content (Figure 1e). IbbHLH25 was highly expressed in the storage roots of carotenoid-rich varieties, but its expression was lower in varieties with low carotenoid contents (Figure 1e), suggesting that a variation in the regulatory region of IbbHLH25 could contribute to the differences in carotenoid levels. 

The promoter regions of IbbHLH25 in the orange-fleshed variety Pushu32 and the white-fleshed variety Xushu18 were subsequently sequenced. Notably, an 86-bp allelic variation located approximately 142-bp upstream of the initiation site was detected in the promoter region of the IbbHLH25 gene of the Pushu32 and Xushu18 varieties. The 86-bp deletion was not detected in Pushu32 but was present in Xushu18 (Figure 1f). In order to further determine whether this allelic variation was associated with the carotenoid content, the promoter regions of the IbbHLH25 genes of sweet potato varieties with varying flesh colours were amplified. The results revealed that the 86-bp deletion was present in the white- and yellow-fleshed varieties, but absent in orange-fleshed varieties with high carotenoid content (Figure 1g), suggesting that this allelic variation might have occurred during domestication. 

Additionally, in order to further investigate whether the 86-bp allelic variation alters the activation of IbbHLH25 in sweet potato varieties with varying flesh colours, three IbbHLH25pro:LUC reporters, namely, IbbHLH25PsIpro:LUC, IbbHLH25XsIpro:LUC and IbbHLH25XsDpro:LUC, were separately expressed in the protoplasts of sweet potato. The results demonstrated that the activities of the promoter region of IbbHLH25PsI and IbbHLH25XsI $1 b b H L H 2 5 ^ { P s I }$ were markedly higher than that of $1 b b H L H 2 5 ^ { X s D }$ ; however, there were no significant differences between the activities of the promoter regions of $1 b b H L H 2 5 ^ { P s I }$ and $1 b b H L H 2 5 ^ { X s I }$ (Figure 1h). Overall, our data suggested that the 86-bp insertion in the promoter region of IbbHLH25 led to the transcriptional activation of IbbHLH25 in OFSP.

# IbbHLH25 significantly increases the content and proportion of $\beta \mathrm { \cdot }$ -carotene and $\beta .$ -cryptoxanthin in storage roots

In order to investigate whether IbbHLH25 contributes to carotenoid biosynthesis in sweet potato, 13 overexpression lines (OE25-1 to OE25-13) and four lines generated using RNA interference (RNAi) technology (Ri25-1 to Ri25-4) were generated using the light-yellow sweet potato variety Lizixiang (Figure S1a– g). Three overexpression lines (OE25-2, OE25-4 and OE25-12) and three RNAi lines (Ri25-1, Ri25-2 and Ri25-4) were subsequently selected for further analysis (Figure S1h). Southern blotting analysis revealed that the transgenic plants displayed different integration patterns, and the copy number of the integrated hygromycin B-resistant gene (in both overexpression and RNAi plasmids) increased and varied from 1 to 2; however, there was no clear relationship between the expression levels of related genes and the copy number of the integrated fragments (Figure S2). 

The transgenic and wild-type (WT) plants were transplanted to the field and harvested at 120 DAP (Figure S1i–n). The IbbHLH25-OE lines exhibited a shift in flesh colour towards orange. However, the flesh colour of IbbHLH25-Ri lines was lighter than that of WT plants and tended to shift from the original light-yellow hue towards white (Figure 3a). Carotenoids are stored in the chromoplasts of plants (Xing et al., 2023). The results of transmission electron microscopy revealed that there was a significant increase in the number of carotenoid globules in the storage roots of IbbHLH25-OE lines compared to that of WT. Conversely, the number of carotenoid globules in the storage roots of IbbHLH25-Ri lines decreased significantly compared to that of the WT (Figure 3b). These results indicated that the overexpression of IbbHLH25 visibly increased the carotenoid levels in the storage roots of sweet potato. 

The carotenoid contents were quantified by ultra-performance liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry (UPLC-

MS/MS). Generally, the contents of carotenoid compounds were significantly higher in the storage roots of IbbHLH25-OE plants, compared to those of the WT. The total carotenoid content, primarily comprising $\beta$ -carotene, $\beta$ -cryptoxanthin, zeaxanthin, phytoene, phytofluene, antheraxanthin, violaxanthin, neoxanthin, and lutein, increased by $2 5 1 . 6 \% - 6 0 0 . 9 \%$ in IbbHLH25-OE lines and decreased by $1 3 . 1 \% - 1 6 . 4 \%$ in IbbHLH25-Ri lines compared to that of the WT (Figure 3c; Figure S3). Notably, the levels of $\beta \cdot$ - carotene, the primary precursor of vitamin A, increased by $2 1 2 4 . 0 \% - 5 5 7 0 . 7 \%$ in IbbHLH25-OE lines and decreased by $1 7 . 4 \% - 3 4 . 9 \%$ in IbbHLH25-Ri plants, compared to those of WT plants (Figure 3c). The contents of $\beta$ -cryptoxanthin, a potent antioxidant and immunomodulatory component, increased by $4 6 1 . 4 \% - 9 4 8 . 2 \%$ in IbbHLH25-OE lines and decreased by $2 . 2 \% -$ $1 3 . 1 \%$ in IbbHLH25-Ri plants, compared to those of the WT (Figure 3c). Moreover, the proportion of $\beta$ -carotene and $\beta \cdot$ - cryptoxanthin increased from $6 . 8 \%$ and $2 1 . 7 \%$ , respectively, in WT plants to $5 5 . 3 \%$ and $3 4 . 7 \%$ , respectively, in IbbHLH25-OE lines (Figure 3d). Additionally, the dry matter and starch content of IbbHLH25-OE plants decreased by $6 . 9 \% - 1 3 . 1 \%$ and $4 . 2 \% -$ $6 . 3 \%$ , respectively, whereas those of IbbHLH25-Ri plants increased by $0 . 4 \% - 4 . 7 \%$ and $0 . 6 \% - 1 . 0 \%$ , respectively, compared to those of the WT (Figure S4a,b). The soluble sugar content of IbbHLH25-OE plants increased significantly by $1 9 . 1 \% -$ $3 4 . 7 \%$ , whereas that of IbbHLH25-Ri plants decreased significantly by $5 . 7 \% - 1 9 . 8 \%$ , compared to that of the WT (Figure S4c). These findings indicated that IbbHLH25 affects the quality traits of sweet potato storage roots by significantly enhancing the content and proportion of carotenoids, especially $\beta$ -carotene and $\beta$ -cryptoxanthin, and thereby enhance the nutritional and medicinal values of sweet potato.

# IbbHLH25 interacts with IbbHLH66 to form heterodimers in sweet potato

The bHLH proteins of plants typically function by forming homodimers or heterodimers via their bHLH domains (Xue et al., 2022). In this study, a total of 14 putative IbbHLH25- interacting proteins, including two TFs, namely, IbMYB73 and the IbbHLH66 protein, were identified by yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) library screening (Table S2). As there were no visible changes between the flesh colours of the IbMYB73-OE plants obtained in our laboratory and WT plants (Figure S6), the potential IbbHLH25- interacting protein, IbbHLH66, was selected for further investigation (Figure 5a). 

Bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC), firefly LUC complementation imaging (LCI) and co-immunoprecipitation (co-IP) assays were further performed to verify the interaction between IbbHLH25 and IbbHLH66. The results demonstrated that IbbHLH25 interacted with IbbHLH66 to form heterodimers both in vitro and in vivo (Figure 5b–d). 

# IbbHLH66 significantly increases the content and proportion of $\beta \mathrm { \cdot }$ -carotene and $\beta .$ -cryptoxanthin in storage roots

In order to investigate the role of IbbHLH66 in carotenoid biosynthesis, three overexpression lines, namely, OE66-3, OE66-4 and OE66-5, were generated using the Lizixiang variety (Xue et al., 2022). The plants were transplanted to the field and harvested at 120 DAP. Similar to that of the IbbHLH25-OE plants, the flesh colour of the IbbHLH66-OE lines shifted towards orange, and there was a significant increase in the number of carotenoid globules compared to that of the WT (Figure 5e,f). The carotenoid 

contents in the storage roots of IbbHLH66-OE lines were significantly higher than those of the WT. The total carotenoid, $\beta$ -carotene, and $\beta$ -cryptoxanthin contents increased by $2 2 5 . 1 \% -$ $3 8 0 . 4 \%$ , $1 5 1 6 . 7 \% - 2 5 6 3 . 6 \%$ , and $3 1 1 . 2 \% - 5 1 2 . 7 \%$ , respectively, in IbbHLH66-OE lines, compared to those of the WT (Figure 5g). Additionally, the proportion of $\beta$ -carotene and $\beta \cdot$ - cryptoxanthin increased from $6 . 9 \%$ and $2 1 . 7 \%$ , respectively, in WT plants to $3 8 . 0 \%$ and $3 1 . 3 \%$ , respectively, in IbbHLH66-OE lines (Figure 5h). 

The dry matter and starch content of IbbHLH66-OE plants increased by $1 1 . 2 \% - 1 6 . 1 \%$ and $8 . 6 \% - 1 5 . 4 \%$ , respectively, compared to those of the WT (Figure S8a,b). Additionally, the soluble sugar content in IbbHLH66-OE plants increased significantly by $1 4 . 7 \% - 2 4 . 5 \%$ compared to that of the WT (Figure S8c). These findings indicated that IbbHLH66 affects the quality traits of the storage roots of sweet potato by significantly enhancing the content and proportion of carotenoids, especially $\beta$ -carotene and $\beta \cdot$ -cryptoxanthin. 

# IbbHLH66 directly targets carotenoid biosynthesis key genes IbGGPPS, IbLCYB and IbBCH

The expression of the key genes involved in the carotenoid biosynthetic pathway in the storage roots of IbbHLH66-OE and WT plants was detected by qRT-PCR in this study. The findings revealed that nearly all the key genes involved in carotenoid biosynthesis were significantly upregulated in IbbHLH66-OE plants (Figure 6a). As IbbHLH66 is also a bHLH protein, the direct regulatory effects of IbbHLH66 on the expression of key genes involved in carotenoid biosynthesis were explored. Interestingly, the results of ChIP-qPCR demonstrated that IbbHLH66 could directly target IbGGPPS, IbLCYB, and IbBCH in vivo by binding to the same binding sites as IbbHLH25 (Figures 4b–d and 6b–d); however, like IbbHLH25, IbbHLH66 did not directly target the IbPSY, IbZDS, IbCCS, IbNXS, IbLCYE, and IbCYP97A genes of sweet potato (Figure S9). Additionally, the results of EMSA and dual-LUC assays revealed that IbbHLH66 directly targeted IbGGPPS, IbLCYB, and IbBCH and activated their expression (Figure 6e,f). These findings indicated that IbbHLH66 regulated the transcription of key genes involved in the carotenoid biosynthetic pathway of sweet potato.

# Discussion

Sweet potato is a staple crop and important source of nutrition and food safety worldwide. The development of carotenoid-rich OFSP varieties has become a major goal in sweet potato breeding, which emphasizes the necessity of obtaining a better understanding of the genetic basis and molecular mechanisms underlying natural carotenoid accumulation in sweet potato. The present study identified that IbbHLH25 functions as a core regulatory factor in carotenoid biosynthesis in sweet potato. IbbHLH25 forms a heterodimer with IbbHLH66 to significantly activate the expression of key genes involved in carotenoid biosynthesis, which markedly improved the content and proportion of carotenoids, especially $\beta$ -carotene and $\beta$ -cryptoxanthin, in the storage roots of sweet potato (Figure 7c). 

Sweet potato breeding faces several challenges, including high costs, high labor requirements, and significant land use. Additionally, the identification of stable phenotypes is timeconsuming as the root system of sweet potato is susceptible to environmental influences. The development of molecular markers can aid the efficient and accurate prediction and screening of sweet potato with target traits at the seedling stage, prior to transplantation to the fields. Presently, there is a scarcity of reports on the carotenoid-related molecular markers in sweet potato. In this study, the 86-bp deletion was not detected in the promoter regions of IbbHLH25 of the orange-fleshed varieties with high-carotenoid contents (Figure 1g), suggesting that this variation could serve as a molecular marker for selecting high-carotenoid varieties in the early stages of breeding. Additionally, although several major varieties exhibit good adaptability and a high starch content, their carotenoid contents are typically low. The 86-bp allelic variation can serve as an excellent target of genetic editing for improving the carotenoid content of different varieties of sweet potato, thus making them more suitable for human health and nutrition. This strategy can therefore be used to enhance the efficiency of breeding sweet potatoes in future. 

Carotenoids are found in a variety of food products, and they act as biological antioxidants by aiding in reactive oxygen species depletion (Ines Elvira-Torales et al., 2019). $\beta$ -Carotene is abundant in carrots, spinach, mangoes and sweet potatoes, and can potentially prevent and protect against hepatic steatosis, fibrosis, oxidative stress, inflammation, and apoptosis (Rodriguez-Amaya et al., 2008; Yilmaz et al., 2015). $\beta$ -Cryptoxanthin is typically present in pumpkins, peppers, carrots, and oranges, and it has been reported that $\beta$ -cryptoxanthin regulates the immune response of macrophages and possesses anti-inflammatory effects (Katsuura et al., 2009). A previous study demonstrated that the rate of oxidative DNA damage and lipid peroxidation is inversely related to the human plasma concentrations of bcryptoxanthin (Haegele et al., 2000). The present study demonstrated that the overexpression of IbbHLH25 and IbbHLH66 in sweet potato significantly increased the total carotenoid content of the storage roots by 2.5-fold–6.0-fold and 2.3-fold–3.8-fold, respectively (Figures 3c and 5g). The overexpression of IbbHLH25 and IbbHLH66 substantially increased the content of $\beta \cdot$ -carotene by 21.2-fold–55.7-fold and 15.2-fold–25.6-fold, respectively, in the storage roots, and considerably increased the $\beta$ -cryptoxanthin content by 4.6-fold–9.5-fold and 3.1-fold–5.1-fold, respectively (Figures 3c and 5g). The proportion of $\beta$ -carotene and $\beta \cdot$ - cryptoxanthin increased by approximately $4 8 . 5 \%$ and $1 3 . 0 \%$ , respectively, in IbbHLH25-OE plants, and by $3 1 . 1 \%$ and $9 . 6 \%$ , respectively, in IbbHLH66-OE lines (Figures 3d and 5h). Therefore, the application of gene editing approaches for enhancing the expression of IbbHLH25 and IbbHLH66 could serve as a potential strategy for improving the carotenoid content and medicinal properties of sweet potato in future breeding practices. 

The traits that constitute the quality of sweet potato storage roots, including the contents of starch, sugars and carotenoids, play a key role in determining the taste and consumer preference. An increase in the carotenoid content of OFSP is usually accompanied by a decrease in dry matter during breeding. In this study, the increase in the carotenoid content induced by IbbHLH25 was accompanied by a reduction in the dry matter content and starch accumulation, and a significant increase in the soluble sugar content (Figure S4). In contrast, IbbHLH66 concurrently increased the carotenoid content, dry matter, starch accumulation, and soluble sugar content (Figure S8). These findings indicate that the regulatory mechanisms and phenotypes of TFs have diverse effects on the quality traits of sweet potato, and reveal that IbbHLH66 can potentially aid in balancing and enhancing the quality traits of sweet potato.

# Experimental procedures

# Plant materials and growth conditions

All the plant materials used in this study were stock materials conserved in the laboratory. Sweet potato variety Pushu32 with orange-coloured flesh and high-carotenoid content was used for cloning IbbHLH25, and sweet potato variety Lizixiang was used for the functional characterization of IbbHLH25. Transgenic sweet potato plants that were grown in vitro were cultured on Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium at $2 7 \pm 1 ^ { \circ } { \mathsf { C } }$ under a 13- h/11-h light/dark photoperiod (cool-white fluorescent light at $5 4 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ { \ m } ^ { - 2 } ~ { \varsigma } ^ { - 1 } ,$ ). The sweet potato plants were subsequently transplanted to a field, greenhouse or growth chamber at China Agricultural University, Beijing, China. 

# Transcriptome analysis

The raw data were obtained from previous studies (Jia et al., 2022; Qin et al., 2017; Suematsu et al., 2020). The Taizhong6 reference genome (Yang et al., 2017) was retrieved from https://sweetpotao.com/pasi3.fa. The adapter sequences and low-quality reads were removed using the Fastp software, v0.23.4 (Chen et al., 2018). The reads obtained by Illumina sequencing were mapped to the Taizhong6 reference genome using HISAT2, v2.2.1 (Kim et al., 2015). The transcriptome was assembled and quantified using the StringTie software, v2.1.7 (Pertea et al., 2015). The values of Fragments Per Kilobase of transcript per Million fragments mapped reads (FPKM) were calculated based on gene lengths. Differential gene expression between the samples was calculated using DEseq2, v1.22.1 (Varet et al., 2016), based on the original count data. Genes with $| | \mathsf { o g } _ { 2 }$ fold change| $\geq 1$ and $\mathsf { F D R } < 0 . 0 5$ were regarded as DEGs. 

# DNA sequencing and analysis

The resequencing and phenotypic data from the study by Xiao et al. (2023) were retrieved. The genotypes in the vcf file and the corresponding carotenoid contents in the phenotypic data file were extracted, with violin plots generated using python scripts. The genomic DNA (EasyPure Plant Genomic DNA Kit; Transgen Biotech) and total RNA (TRIzol Reagent; Transgen Biotech) were extracted from the fresh storage roots of sweet potato. The complementary DNA (cDNA) was synthesized using a HiFiScript 

gDNA Removal cDNA Synthesis Kit (CwBio, Jiangsu, China). The primers used for amplifying the genomic DNA and cDNA sequences are enlisted in Table S3. The conserved domains were identified using InterPro (https://www.ebi.ac.uk/interpro/), and multiple sequence alignment of the protein sequences was performed using the DNAMAN software (Lynnon-BioSoft, California). Phylogenetic analysis was performed using the neighbour-joining method in MEGA v6.0 (Mega Limited, Auckland, New Zealand) with 1000 bootstrap iterations (Gao et al., 2023). The exon–intron structures of the genes were analysed using the Splign program (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih. gov/sutils/splign). 

# Gene expression analysis

Total RNA was extracted from fresh storage roots or plants grown in vitro. A HiFiScript gDNA Removal cDNA Synthesis Kit (CwBio) was used to synthesize cDNA from the total RNA. RT-qPCR was performed using a QuantStudio 6 real-time PCR system (Applied Biosystems, Massachusetts) and TB Green- Premix Ex TaqTM II (RR82WR; Takara Biotechnology, Dalian, China), according to the manufacturer’s protocol. The $\beta$ -actin gene of sweet potato (GenBank AY905538) was used as the internal control. The expression levels were calculated using the comparative $C _ { \mathsf { T } }$ method (Schmittgen and Livak, 2008). The analysis was performed with three independent biological replicates. The primers used for gene expression analysis are enlisted in Table S3. 

# Promoter activity assay

The promoter sequences of IbbHLH25 from the Xushu18 and Pushu32 were separately cloned into the KpnI and PstI restriction sites of the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector. The Renilla luciferase (REN) reporter gene driven by the 35S promoter region in this vector served as the internal control. The petiolar protoplasts of sweet potato were prepared and Polyethylene glycol (PEG)-mediated transformation was performed as previously described (Zhang et al., 2020). Firefly LUC and REN activity levels were measured using a Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (YEASEN, Shanghai, China) with a Glomax- Discover luminescence detector (Promega, Wisconsin). The LUC activity was normalized to REN activity. Three independent biological replicates were performed for this analysis.

# Transcriptional activation assay in yeast

The full-length CDS of IbbHLH25 or fragments encoding amino acid residues 1–85 (IbbHLH25N85) and 86–301 (IbbHLH $2 5 ^ { \mathsf { C 2 1 6 } }$ ) of IbbHLH25 were inserted into the NdeI and PstI restriction sites of the pGBKT7 vector (Invitrogen). The pGBKT7-53 construct was used as the positive control, and the pGBKT7-Lam served as the negative control. These plasmids were separately transferred into the AH109 strain of yeast according to the Yeast Protocols Handbook (Clontech). The transformed strains were screened on synthetic defined (SD) plates without tryptophan (SD/ Trp) and subsequently transferred to SD medium lacking tryptophan/histidine/adenine but supplemented with X-a-gal (SD/ Trp/ His/ Ade/+X-a-gal). 

# Transcriptional activation assay in protoplasts

The sequences of IbbHLH25, IbbHLH25N85, and $1 b b H L H 2 5 ^ { C 2 1 6 }$ were individually cloned in frame with the GAL4 DNA-binding domain of the Pro35S:GAL4 BD vector for use as effectors. The LUC gene driven by five copies of GAL4 UAS served as the reporter. The REN gene driven by the 35S promoter was used as the internal control. The effector, reporter, and internal control plasmids were co-transfected into the protoplasts of sweet potato by PEGmediated transformation. Firefly LUC and REN activity levels were measured using a Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (YEASEN) with a Glomax $\textcircled{1}$ Discover luminescence detector (Promega). The LUC activity was normalized to REN activity. Three independent biological replicates were performed for this analysis. 

# Generation of transgenic plants

The 35S:IbbHLH25-GFP (pCAMBIA1300-GFP) construct was used to generate the overexpression lines. For generating the RNAi lines, a specific 200-bp fragment of IbbHLH25 was cloned into the pCAMBIA1300-35SI-X plant RNAi vector (digested with BamHI/- SalI for the forward fragment and with SacI/KpnI for the converse fragment, respectively). These constructs were transformed into the EHA105 strain of Ag. tumefaciens. Transformation and plant regeneration were performed using embryogenic suspension cultures of Lizixiang via Ag.-mediated transformation, and selection was performed using hygromycin B (Xue et al., 2022). 

# Southern blotting

Southern blotting was performed according to the method described by Zhai et al. (2016). Briefly, the genomic DNA was 

extracted from transgenic IbbHLH25 lines and WT plants using the cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) method. A total of $3 0 ~ \mu \ g$ of genomic DNA was digested with EcoRI, separated on a $0 . 8 \%$ agarose gel by electrophoresis under $2 5 ~ \mathrm { e V }$ for $1 8 ~ \mathrm { h }$ , and transferred to a Hybond- $\cdot \ N +$ nylon membrane with transfer buffer $2 0 \times$ SSC). The probe sequence (557 bp) was obtained by PCR using specific primers designed from the CDS of hygromycin Bresistant gene (Table S3). Probe labeling, prehybridization, hybridization, and detection were performed using a DIG High Prime DNA Labeling and Detection Starter Kit II (Roche, Basel, Switzerland). 

# Analysis of carotenoid content

The carotenoids in the storage roots of transgenic and WT plants were extracted and analysed as previously described by Jia et al. (2022). The samples were freeze-dried, ground into powder (30 Hz, 1.5 min), and stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ until further analysis. Following purification and saponification, the extract was dissolved in $1 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of a mixed solution of MeOH/MTBE (1:1, v/v). The saponified extracts were then analysed using a UPLC-APCI-MS/MS system (UPLC, ExionLCTM AD; MS, Applied Biosystems 6500 Triple Quadrupole). The specific protocols for extraction, saponification, identification, and quantification of the carotenoids, and the chromatographic conditions and parameters of the UPLC-APCI-MS/MS system are provided in Material S1 under Appendix S1. 

# Analysis of dry matter, starch, and soluble sugar contents

A total of 100 g of the freshly harvested peeled storage roots of transgenic and WT plants were dried for 10 days to a constant weight, and the dry matter content was calculated by dividing the dry weight by the fresh weight. The harvested storage roots of transgenic and WT plants were frozen and ground to a powder (30 Hz, 1.5 min), following which $0 . 1 \ 9$ of the powder was separately weighed for determining the contents of starch and soluble sugar. The contents of starch and soluble sugar were determined using a starch kit (Cominbio, Jiangsu, China) and a soluble sugar kit (Cominbio, Jiangsu, China), respectively, according to the manufacturer’s protocol. 

# ChIP assay

ChIP assay was performed according to the method described by Gao et al. (2023). Briefly, 2 g of the leaf tissues (OE25-12 and empty vector plants) were cross-linked with $1 \%$ (m/v) formaldehyde. The samples were ground to a powder in liquid nitrogen and subjected to nuclear isolation. The chromatin was sonicated, and an anti-GFP antibody (BE2002; EasyBio, Beijing, China) and 

Protein A/G magnetic beads (P2108; Beyotime, Shanghai, China) were used to specifically immunoprecipitate the chromatin fragments. An equal amount of chromatin sample without antibody precipitation was used as the input control. After reverse crosslinking and DNA purification, the fragments enriched with IbbHLH25-GFP were detected by qPCR, and the ChIP values were normalized against the values of the respective inputs. The primers used for ChIP-qPCR are enlisted in Table S3. The experiment was independently repeated three times and similar results were obtained. 

# EMSA

The CDSs of IbbHLH25 and IbbHLH66 were separately cloned into the pGEX6p-1 vector using the BamHI and EcoRI restriction enzymes. The IbbHLH25-GST and IbbHLH66-GST fusion proteins were induced with 0.5 mM IPTG by shaking at 180 rpm for $1 8 ~ \mathrm { h }$ at $1 6 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . GST resins (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Massachusetts) were used to purify the recombinant proteins. Probes labelled with or without biotin at their $5 ^ { \prime }$ ends were used as binding or competitive probes. A chemiluminescent EMSA kit was used in the assay, according to the manufacturer’s protocol (20148; Thermo Fisher Scientific, Massachusetts). 

# Dual-LUC assay

The CDSs of IbbHLH25 and IbbHLH66 were separately inserted into the pGreenII 62-SK vector and used as reporters. The promoter sequences of IbGGPPS, IbLCYB, and IbBCH were cloned into the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector and used as effectors. The effector and reporter plasmids were co-transfected into the protoplasts of sweet potato by PEG-mediated transformation. Firefly LUC and REN activity levels were measured using a Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (YEASEN) with a Glomax-Discover luminescence detector (Promega). The LUC activity was normalized to REN activity. Three independent biological replicates were performed for this analysis. 

# Y2H assay

Y2H assay was performed according to the method described in the Yeast Protocols Handbook (Clontech). The pGBKT7-IbbHLH25C216 construct was used as the bait, and the cDNA library inserted into the pGADT7 vector was established as previously described (Zhang et al., 2019). The constructs were co-transformed into the AH109 strain of yeast. The transformants were plated on SD/ Trp/ Leu medium, and the positive clones were selected using an SD/ Ade/ His/ Leu/ Trp/+3AT/+X-a-gal medium. 

# BiFC assay

The CDSs of IbbHLH25 and IbbHLH66 were separately cloned into pSPYNE-35S and pSPYCE-35S vectors, respectively, and fused to the N-terminus (nYFP) and C-terminus (cYFP) of yellow fluorescent protein, respectively (Walter et al., 2004). The constructs were then co-transfected into the leaves of N. benthamiana via Ag.-mediated transformation, and the yellow fluorescence signal was observed after $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ of infiltration under a confocal laser scanning microscope (LSM880; Zeiss) using argon lasers with an excitation wavelength of $5 1 4 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ . 

# LCI assay

The CDSs of IbbHLH25 and IbbHLH66 were cloned into the Cterminus- and N-terminus-encoding regions of the LUC reporter gene (Chen et al., 2008). The constructs were then co-transfected into the leaves of N. benthamiana via Ag.-mediated 

transformation, and the infiltrated leaves were analysed for determining LUC activity after $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ of infiltration using an in vivo imaging system (NightShade LB 985; Berthold). 

# Co-IP assay

IbbHLH25-GFP and HA-IbbHLH66-FLAG were transiently expressed in the leaves of N. benthamiana by Ag.-mediated infiltration. After 2 days of growth, the infiltrated leaves of N. benthamiana were collected for extracting the total proteins using an extraction buffer (Zhang et al., 2020). The total proteins were mixed with HA magnetic beads (EasyBio) and incubated at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ . The beads were then washed at least five times with the extraction buffer and boiled in $5 \times$ SDS loading buffer for 20 min for separating the proteins from the magnetic beads. The immunoprecipitates were further separated by SDS-PAGE and detected by immunoblotting with anti-GFP (BE2002; EasyBio) and anti-HA antibodies (BE2008; EasyBio). A horseradish peroxidaseconjugated goat anti-rabbit IgG (H + L) (BE0101; EasyBio) antibody was used as the secondary antibody.
