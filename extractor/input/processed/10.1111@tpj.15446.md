# SUMMARY

Flavonoids are specialized metabolites widely distributed across the plant kingdom. They are involved in the growth and survival of plants, conferring the ability to filter ultra-violet rays, conduct symbiotic partnerships, and respond to stress. While many branches of flavonoid biosynthesis have been resolved, recent discoveries suggest missing auxiliary components. These overlooked elements can guide metabolic flux, enhance production, mediate stereoselectivity, transport intermediates, and exert regulatory functions. This review describes several families of auxiliary proteins from across the plant kingdom, including examples from specialized metabolism. In flavonoid biosynthesis, we discuss the example of chalcone isomerase-like (CHIL) proteins and their non-catalytic role. CHILs mediate the cyclization of tetraketides, forming the chalcone scaffold by interacting with chalcone synthase (CHS). Loss of CHIL activity leads to derailment of the CHS-catalyzed reaction and a loss of pigmentation in fruits and flowers. Similarly, members of the pathogenesis-related 10 (PR10) protein family have been found to differentially bind flavonoid intermediates, guiding the composition of anthocyanins. This role comes within a larger body of PR10 involvement in specialized metabolism, from outright catalysis (e.g., (S)-noroclaurine synthesis) to controlling stereochemistry (e.g., enhancing cis-trans cyclization in catnip). Both CHILs and PR10s hail from larger families of ligand-binding proteins with a spectrum of activity, complicating the characterization of their enigmatic roles. Strategies for the discovery of auxiliary proteins are discussed, as well as mechanistic models for their function. Targeting such unanticipated components will be crucial in manipulating plants or engineering microbial systems for natural product synthesis.

Keywords: flavonoid, anthocyanin, metabolism, chalcone isomerase-like, pathogenesis-related 10, auxiliary protein, metabolic engineering.

# INTRODUCTION

Plants produce an incomparable diversity of specialized metabolites, often limited to a species, a space, or a time of development. They possess a great breadth of physiological and ecological roles that can be seen as evolutionary strategies to cultivate methods of defense, communication, or reproductive success. Flavonoids are a significant class of specialized metabolites, with over 9000 compounds and an uncommonly wide distribution throughout the plant kingdom (Williams and Grayer, 2004). Flavonoid biosynthesis likely arose in microalgae and bloomed into a diversity of branching pathways along with the colonization of land by plants 450 million years ago (Davies et al., 2020; Goiris et al., 2014; Stafford, 1991). In the course of terrestrialization, the photoprotective

qualities of flavonoids are suggested to have been indispensable (Yonekura-Sakakibara et al., 2019). Today, the rich array of pigmentation is particularly apparent in the realm of angiosperm fruit and flowers, where it plays a crucial role in the attraction of pollinators. In addition to visual cues, flavonoids are part of a chemical arsenal that can deter pathogens and attract mutualistic symbioses. The structural diversity of flavonoids enables the broad scope of functions we see in specific lineages.

The term flavonoid, sensu stricto, defines compounds with a $C_6 - C_3 - C_6$ carbon framework, including flavans, flavones, flavonols, and anthocyanidins. The first committed step in flavonoid biosynthesis is the condensation of $p$ -coumaroyl-CoA with three units of malonyl-CoA, catalyzed in a four-step reaction by chalcone synthase (CHS)

Both chalcones can spontaneously cyclize in solution to give enantiomeric mixtures, but the enzyme-catalyzed reaction is $10^{7}$ times faster than the spontaneous cyclization (Bednar and Hadcock, 1988). The resulting flavanones are substrates for many enzymes and diverging pathways, including flavonol, isoflavonoid, and anthocyanin biosynthesis. In one of the possible routes, naringenin is converted to dihydrokaempferol by flavanone 3-hydroxylase (F3H). Subsequently, flavonoid $3^{\prime}$ -hydroxylase $(\mathrm{F}3^{\prime}\mathrm{H})$ is hydroxylated at the $3^{\prime}$ position on the B-ring to form dihydroquercetin, which can be derivatized in several steps to flavan-3-ols, such as catechin, a potent antioxidant.

This review will not attempt to cover the many pathways emanating from naringenin and will focus instead on the activity of auxiliary proteins and their effect on core intermediates and end-state metabolites, such as wedding bells anthocyanin (WBA), a complex compound found in Japanese morning glory (Ipomoea nil). While we know the main biosynthetic components of flavonoid metabolism, the identification of regulatory and auxiliary elements guiding the output is still being pursued. We will delve into the roles of CHI-like (CHIL) and pathogenesis-related 10 (PR10) proteins, which have been implicated in guiding carbon flux through the flavonoid biosynthetic pathway. The dimeric $\alpha + \beta$ barrel (DABB) proteins, which exercise a similar role in product selectivity in phytocannabinoid biosynthesis, will also be discussed. Auxiliary proteins are potentially crucial features

in pathways producing high-value compounds. In this respect, flavonoids continue to capture the imagination of scientists, with their immense potential for human health as antioxidant, antidiabetic, and anti-inflammatory compounds.

# CHALCONE ISOMERASE FOLD; STRUCTURE AND EVOLUTION

The CHI family of proteins is classified into four subfamilies (type I to IV) based on biochemical activity and phylogenetic distribution (Figure 2) (Ngaki et al., 2012). Bona fide enzymatic activity is restricted to types I and II. Among the two, type I enzymes accept naringenin chalcone as a substrate, while the latter can additionally catalyze isoliquiritigenin. Type II CHIs were initially designated as being specific to legumes (Fabaceae), such as soybean (Glycine max); however, more recent reports have discovered type II activity in so-called 'basal land plants', i.e., in some, but not all, species of the bryophyte (e.g., liverwort [Marchantia spp.]) and lycophyte (e.g., Selaginella spp.) clades (Cheng et al., 2018; Ni et al., 2020). Phylogenetic analyses have shown that these 'bryophyte/lycophyte' type II CHIs form a separate group from type I and 'legume' type II CHIs (Figure 2) but share a similar biochemical activity with the latter (Cheng et al., 2018).

The bona fide CHIs are closely related to type IV CHIs, also known as CHIL proteins (Ngaki et al., 2012; Ralston et al., 2005). CHILs are conserved in all land plants but are entirely devoid of chalcone-flavanone isomerase activity due to several substitutions at the catalytic residues. Finally, we have type III CHIs, which form the most basal group in the phylogenetic tree and are present in both land plants and green algae. Type III CHIs in Arabidopsis thaliana are involved in fatty acid biosynthesis and were renamed fatty acid-binding proteins (FAPs) (Ngaki et al., 2012). In

The FAP family can be further divided into three subfamilies with differential binding affinities for their respective ligands. FAPs are likely homologous to CHI fold proteins from protists and fungi (Ngaki et al., 2012). Therefore, the FAPs and CHILs constitute the only homologous protein families to the bona fide CHIs; they are presumably more ancient but are devoid of CHI activity.

All four CHI fold subfamilies share a conserved three-dimensional structure (Ngaki et al., 2012). A study using a combination of ancestral sequence inference, directed evolution, and in-depth structural work raised the hypothesis that CHI activity, indeed, emerged from a non-catalytic ancestor (Kaltenbach et al., 2018). This finding sets the evolution of CHI apart, as most enzymes diverge from other enzymes by accruing mutations that expand inherent promiscuous activities. Generally, we see a trajectory of gene duplication, followed by loss-of-function, subfunctionalization, or neofunctionalization, transforming a single or a spectrum of activities into a new role. However, in the case of chalcone-flavanone isomerase activity, it appears to have evolved from the non-catalytic binding capacity of FAPs.

The structure of the CHI fold has been extensively studied and reveals five residues as part of the catalytic core: Arg36, Thr48, Asn113, Tyr106, and Thr190 (residue numbers according to the structure of Medicago sativa MsCHI) (Jez et al., 2000). Three residues, Arg36, Thr48, and Tyr106, are highly conserved in extant CHIs and crucial to activity (Ngaki et al., 2012). CHIL homologs have substitutions at all or many of these residues. However, the presence of these catalytic residues is not sufficient to confer activity. Moreover, the CHIL scaffold orients the ligand, naringenin, in a non-productive 'opposite mode' compared to CHI (Kaltenbach et al., 2018). The emergence of CHI activity requires a significant repositioning of the substrate and increased mobility of the Arg36 residue, allowing the latter to exert a catalytic effect. Using directed evolution, Kaltenbach et al. (2018) found that the initiation of CHI activity was facile, with the possibility of multiple starting points and trajectories.

As mentioned previously, chalcone-flavanone isomerization can proceed spontaneously, albeit at a slow rate (a half-life of approximately 3.6 h); however, it produces a racemic $(2S / R)$ mixture. CHIs are enantiospecific and produce only (2S)-naringenin. Using ancestral inference, Kaltenbach et al. (2018) predicted that the stereoselectivity is conserved even in the common ancestor of catalytic CHIs and non-catalytic CHILs. Therefore, selection of the (S)-enantiomer appears to be embedded in the inactive CHIL scaffold. This point can inform the study of CHIL as a noncatalytic binding protein for chalcone-flavanone

intermediates. Given the possibility of spontaneous isomerization, it is conceivable that evolutionary intermediates (i.e., isoforms) of CHIL were able to weakly catalyze or even exert stereochemical control by simply binding naringenin chalcone to produce (2S)-naringenin.

Despite exhibiting no enzymatic activity, the abundance of CHIL proteins correlates with flavonoid biosynthesis and the accumulation of downstream compounds in the plant (Jiang et al., 2015; Morita et al., 2014). CHIL appears to serve a non-catalytic function in coordination with CHS and CHI, guiding the flux of tetraketide intermediates into chalcone-flavanone production, dictating the composition of flavonoids across many land plants.

# Transparent testa (tt) mutants and the hurdle of CHIL characterization

The initial characterization of bona fide CHI activity was facilitated by the transparent testa 5 (tt5) mutant in A. thaliana (Shirley et al., 1992), part of an extensive collection of mutants with impaired flavonoid accumulation. The dark brown-colored seeds of wild-type Arabidopsis are replaced in the tt mutants with a transparent seed coat, revealing the yellow-colored cotyledons underneath. Chemically, this defect is due to the disruption of proanthocyanidin (PA) accumulation in the seed coat.

Several flavonoid biosynthetic enzymes have likewise been characterized in Arabidopsis mutants: in $tt4$ , CHS; in $tt6$ , F3H; in $tt7$ , F3'H; and in $tt11/17/18/tds4$ , anthocyanidin synthase. These have been comprehensively reviewed by Saito et al. (2013). This gene discovery process was simplified because the enzymes were encoded by single-copy genes, which is not the case in the general phenylpropanoid pathway (e.g., two genes for phenylalanine ammonia lyase [PAL]) or in species with larger genomes. For example, soybean carries four genes encoding bona fide CHI enzymes (GmCHI1A, GmCHI1B1, GmCHI1B2, and GmCHI2). The TT loci also account for regulatory factors controlling flavonoid biosynthesis, including $TT2/MYB123$ , a regulator of PA biosynthesis (Nesi et al., 2001), $TT8/BHLH042$ , a regulator of anthocyanin accumulation (Nesi et al., 2000), and $TT16/MADS-box$ protein, a determining factor in endothelial identity, crucial to seed coat pigmentation (Nesi et al., 2002; Xu et al., 2017). Finally, the $TT12$ locus encodes a multidrug and toxin extrusion (MATE) protein, which was found to transport epicatechin $3^{\prime}$ -O-glucoside (Debeaujon et al., 2001; Zhao and Dixon, 2009).

It is abundantly clear from the above examples that CHI and many flavonoid biosynthetic, regulatory, and transport proteins were readily characterized with the aid of the $tt$ mutants. However, no $TT$ locus has been associated with CHIL loss-of-function. Furthermore, CHIL cannot rescue the $tt5$ mutant phenotype because, as mentioned previously, it does not carry CHI activity. The function of CHIL was

deduced much later using a mixture of forward and reverse genetics, as described in the following section.

# Loss-of-function chil mutants in Japanese morning glory and Arabidopsis

Japanese morning glory (I. nil) bears funnel-shaped flowers with colors ranging from blue to reddish-purple, conferred by anthocyanin molecules decorated with glucosyl and caffeoyl moieties, such as the majestic WBA. A study of spontaneous mutants with pale-colored flowers identified a gene designated Enhancer of Flavonoid Production (EFP) (Morita et al., 2014). EFP encodes a CHIL protein, which, as expected, lacks bona fide CHI activity (Figure 1). Despite a perceived lack of enzymatic activity, the loss-of-function efp mutants had reduced levels of anthocyanins and flavonols, including kaempferol glycoside. Both metabolite classes contribute to pigmentation and emanate from the flavanone precursor naringenin. Morita et al. (2014) also confirmed that silencing EFP homologs in petunia (Petunia hybrida) and torenia (Torenia hybrida) yielded the same pale flowers, suggesting that the role is conserved across several angiosperms.

Interestingly, knockout or knockdown of EFP/CHIL resulted in a significant reduction of colorless flavonoids, flavonols, and flavones. Flavones, in particular, are produced early in the biosynthetic pathway, suggesting that EFP encodes a protein acting in collusion with CHS or CHI rather than later-stage enzymes involved in anthocyanin production. Indeed, a bottleneck appears to have formed at CHS activity, with substrates such as $p$ -coumaroyl-CoA potentially diverted to the formation of caffeic acid derivatives (chlorogenic acid and 1-O-caffeoyl glucoside), rather than forming the chalcone backbone (Figure 1). Morita et al. (2014) hypothesized that EFP works in concert with CHS as an enhancer of early flavonoid biosynthesis.

In Arabidopsis, chil mutants accumulate lower levels of flavonols (quercetin and kaempferol) and PAs (Jiang et al., 2015). However, unlike the $l.$ nil mutant, anthocyanin levels were unchanged. A caveat here is that the study from 2015 was conducted on total levels of anthocyanins and PAs, measured by spectrophotometry rather than quantification of specific metabolites. Thus, the chil mutant might have impacted levels of individual anthocyanins not perceived by relative spectrophotometric measurements of total extractions. Overexpression of AtCHIL in wild-type Arabidopsis enhanced levels of kaempferol and PAs.

The study of CHIL in Arabidopsis revealed that the gene is coregulated with the $TT2$ locus and genes involved in PA biosynthesis. The gene at $TT2$ encodes an R2-R3 MYB-type transcription factor. Overall, CHIL appears to encode a non-catalytic enhancer of flavonoid metabolism, which guides greater flux into downstream pathways. Loss-of-function mutants in I. nil and Arabidopsis had varying impacts on flavonoid metabolism; however, it is unclear if

the disparities are due to structural differences in the CHIL isoforms or other facets of biosynthetic control.

# Chalconoids in hops

The female cones of hops (Humulus lupulus) have glandular trichomes brimming with terpenophenolics, which lend the characteristic bitter taste. In particular, the phenylpropanoid-derived metabolites accumulating in these trichomes have been noted for their potential pharmaceutical applications (Colgate et al., 2007; Diller et al., 2005; Miranda et al., 1999). These bioactive compounds include trace amounts of prenylated chalcones, which diverge from the canonical chalcone-to-flavanone pathway discussed above after the CHS-catalyzed reaction (Figure 1).

The chalcones are prenylated by a membrane-bound prenyltransferase (PT), which in $H.$ lupulus has been identified as HIPT1L, forming demethylxanthohumol (DMX). Here, the prenylated chalconoid remains in the open-ring formation and exhibits greater bioactivity thanks to the $\alpha,\beta$ -unsaturated ketone functional group. DMX is further methylated by an O-methyltransferase (HLOMT1) to form xanthohumol (XN), which is a target compound for anticancer, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant use (Colgate et al., 2007).

Thereby, in hops, the ring closure catalyzed by CHI, which ushers metabolites into the flavonoid pathway, is eschewed for the decorated chalconoid form. A report by Ban et al. (2018) suggested that the biochemical mechanism underlying the efficient XN pathway is mediated, in part, by the work of non-catalytic CHILs. The two noncatalytic CHI fold genes in hops are HICHIL1 and HICHIL2, designated as type III and IV CHILs, respectively. They are expressed at high levels in the glandular trichomes of female hop flowers, where CHI fold expressed sequence tags are among the most abundant.

HICHIL1 regulates the metabolic composition of prenylated chalcones by binding the intermediates and stabilizing the open-ring conformation. This binding activity leads to a greater accumulation of chalconoids in glandular trichomes in place of closed-ring flavonoids. On the other hand, HICHIL2 (type IV) confers greater enzymatic efficiency to CHS and HIPT1L by interacting physically with each protein. In vitro, conversion rates of coumaroyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA by CHS were increased approximately 5.6-fold and 17.5-fold, respectively, upon the addition of HICHIL2. Similarly, the HIPT1L prenylation rate of naringenin chalcone or dimethylallyl diphosphate was improved by HICHIL2.

The overall output of reconstituted DMX (chalconoid) or naringenin (flavanone) pathways in engineered yeast was enhanced by adding HICHIL2 or CHILs from other sources, including from the bryophyte Physcomitrella patens. The ubiquity of CHIL regulation of CHS activity suggests that it

is a conserved function across land plants, associated with flavonoid production and land colonization. In addition, the binding of CHI fold proteins with CHS appears to be a conserved trait from type IV CHIs like HICHIL2 in hops to bona fide CHIs in the soybean isoflavonoid pathway (Ban et al., 2018; Burbulis and Winkel-Shirley, 1999; Dastmalchi et al., 2016).

Building on the study in hops, another research group analyzed CHIL activity in the related species cannabis (Cannabis sativa) with regard to cannabinoid production. Similar to hops, two non-catalytic homologs, CsaCHIL1 and CsaCHIL2, were identified in cannabis (Gulick et al., 2020). CsaCHIL1 was highly and specifically expressed in trichomes, while CsaCHIL2 was present broadly in aerial tissues. The authors paired CsaCHILs with 12 CsaPTs in engineered yeast and did not record any changes in prenyltransferase activity. In contrast with hops, it appears that cannabis CHILs do not improve the enzymatic efficiency of PTs. Further research must be conducted to explore CsaCHILs and their potential role in polyketide synthesis in interaction with tetraketide synthase or olivetolic acid cyclase (OAC) (See section on DABB proteins, Figure 1).

# Mechanism of CHIL activity; rectifying CHS promiscuity

As discussed in the introduction, CHS is a type III polyketide synthase (PKS), catalyzing the consecutive condensations of one $p$ -coumaroyl-CoA molecule and three malonyl-CoA molecules to form a linear tetraketide intermediate (Kreuzaler and Hahlbrock, 1972). In the canonical pathway, CHS catalyzes an intramolecular C6-C1 Claisen condensation reaction to cyclize and form naringenin chalcone, finishing the first step of flavonoid biosynthesis.

In vitro assays have shown that chalcone biosynthesis can be derailed by lactonization after the condensation of $p$ -coumaroyl-CoA with either two or three malonyl-CoA molecules to form bisnorygangonin (BNY) or $p$ -coumaroyl-triacetic acid lactone (CTAL), respectively (Figure 1) (Yamaguchi et al., 1999). These by-products do not accumulate

in the plant, but it can be expected that the promiscuity of CHS activity would likely decrease the overall efficiency of flavonoid biosynthesis. On the other hand, this malleability of function has been cited as a possible evolutionary springboard for the neofunctionalization of plant PKSs, including stilbene synthase (STS) (Tropf et al., 1994) and $p$ -coumaroyl-triacetic acid synthase (CTAS) (Akiyama et al., 2001). Well-known stilbenoids emanating from STS activity include resveratrol in grapes (Vitis vinifera) (Jang et al., 1997) and pinosylvin, an antifungal produced by pine (Pinusspp.) roots (Kodan et al., 2002; Seppänen et al., 2004). On the other hand, CTAS activity can lead to hydrangeic acid, which has purported antidiabetic function (Zhang et al., 2009).

The problem of CHS promiscuity might only be relevant when the catalyst is expressed in a heterologous host or in vitro, where the context of the plant cell has been stripped away. In plants, the chalcone product is swiftly incorporated into downstream flavonoid classes in a lineage-specific manner. Furthermore, CHS has been shown to interact physically with CHI (Burbulis and Winkel-Shirley, 1999) and other enzymatic partners in flavonoid and isoflavonoid biosynthesis forming a metabolon (multi-enzyme complex) (Figure 3) (Dastmalchi et al., 2016; Waki et al., 2016). Soluble proteins, including CHS, are tethered to the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) by association with cytochrome P450s (CYPs), which serve as the nucleating anchors of the complex. Thus, a metabolon involving CHS can initiate the channeling of intermediates between active sites, increasing local concentrations and precluding the formation of lactone by-products. However, given that the multi-step elongation and condensation are all catalyzed by CHS and do not involve any other enzymes, the metabolon theory does not explain how plants avoid lactonization by-products.

The factor rectifying CHS promiscuity appears to be CHIL. The work characterizing CHIL in I. nil (Morita et al., 2014) and Arabidopsis (Jiang et al., 2015) showed that the protein indeed enhances flavonoid production. This

The mode of CHIL activity is yet to be determined, with obvious credence lent to the concept of a CHS-CHIL metabolon by the conserved interaction of these two partners across the plant kingdom (Waki et al., 2020). A complex featuring both proteins might be involved in mediating product specificity. An alternative hypothesis is that CHIL can bind the intermediates and lactone derivatives, thereby controlling metabolic flux.

# DABB proteins have a similar function in phytocannabinoid biosynthesis

DABB proteins are another example of unanticipated metabolic components exerting a guiding role in the aromatization of tetraketide intermediates. They have been characterized with a similar function in phytocannabinoid biosynthesis. Phytocannabinoids are bioactive natural products found in a broad range of species, including fungi, liverworts, and angiosperms (Gülck and Møller, 2020). Of primary interest are the eponymous cannabis plant and the production of the euphoric drug tetrahydrocannabinol (THC) and other non-psychoactive drugs, such as cannabidiol (CBD). The site of phytocannabinoid and terpene accumulation are the glandular trichomes present on the aerial tissue and in the highest density on female flowers (reviewed by Blatt-Janmaat and Qu, 2021).

The biosynthesis of phytocannabinoids, similar to that of flavonoids, begins with a type III PKS. A molecule of hexanoyl-CoA is condensed with three units of malonyl-CoA (Figure 1). Researchers have identified a trichomespecific PKS capable of conducting this reaction, named

olivetol synthase (OLS) (Taura et al., 2009). However, in vitro assays with OLS do not produce the expected metabolite, olivetolic acid. Instead, OLS produces a decarboxylated form named olivetol (5-pentylresorcinol) and $\alpha$ -pyrone by-products. The by-products represent derailed lactonization of the tri- and tetraketide intermediates, forming pentyl diacetic acid lactone (PDAL) and hexanoyl triacetic acid lactone (HTAL), respectively. It appears that OLS suffers from similar enzymatic promiscuity issues as CHS, with the slight distinction that OLS is not sufficient to produce the final metabolite, olivetolic acid.

For many years, researchers tried to find the catalyst or auxiliary protein capable of producing olivetolic acid when combined with OLS. This metabolite is a necessary precursor for the alkylresorcinol moieties of cannabinoids and, therefore, a crucial bottleneck in resolving phytocannabinoid biosynthesis. In 2012, researchers noticed that total protein extract from glandular trichomes produced the correct C2-C7 aldol cyclization reaction when fed hexanoyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA, retaining the carboxylate moiety and forming olivetolic acid (Gagne et al., 2012). Therefore, they deduced that the trichomes held the answer to this biosynthetic puzzle. Exploration of a trichome-specific transcriptome led to a candidate list that included a CHIL, a Bet v 1-like, and a DABB protein. Biochemical characterization of the candidates in coupled assays with OLS revealed that the DABB protein, renamed OAC, can guide the intramolecular aldol cyclization to form olivetolic acid.

OAC does not exhibit intrinsic PKS activity and acts as an auxiliary protein binding the intermediates and catalyzing cyclization. In contrast to the need for protein-protein interaction in flavonoid biosynthesis, physical interaction is not required between OLS and OAC (Yang et al., 2016). Furthermore, the combined efforts of the two proteins still yielded a mixture of olivetolic acid and the $\alpha$ -pyrone byproducts in engineered yeast, suggesting that there is yet another missing element guiding metabolic flux in cannabis trichomes.

OAC is a diminutive protein (12 kDa, 101 amino acids) and is predicted to have the characteristic $\alpha +\beta$ barrel fold, with a hydrophobic cleft containing a polyketide binding site (Yang et al., 2016). Site-directed mutagenesis revealed that Tyr72 and His78 function as acid/base catalysts, performing the C2-C7 aldol condensation via an enolate intermediate. OAC possesses a unique active site architecture, which along with the predicted cyclization mechanism suggests that it has not evolved from bacterial cyclases (Ames et al., 2008; Yang et al., 2016). Furthermore, DABB proteins from bacteria and plants share low sequence similarity, leading to the conclusion that their shared functionality or structure is an example of convergent evolution. The current hypothesis is that both clades of DABB proteins learned to exploit the $\alpha +\beta$ barrel fold for polyketide cyclization (Cornilescu et al., 2004; Thompson et al., 2004).

Only a handful of DABBs have been characterized in plant species, with OAC being the sole homolog ascribed with a biochemical function. Plant DABBs are thermostable and induced by stress (Gu et al., 2004); however, they are not associated with other stress response proteins, such as heat shock proteins (HSPs). OAC shares $48\%$ identity with Arabidopsis heat-stable protein 1 (AtHS1); however, the latter does not possess tetraketide cyclase activity. A role for AtHS1 in metabolism or signaling has not been ruled out, especially when considering its stress-induced expression. DABB proteins in plants are very likely involved as auxiliary proteins in plant metabolism due to their conserved structure, their ubiquity in plant genomes, and the recurring need for cyclase activity in conjunction with type III PKSs.

# PATHOGENESIS-RELATED PROTEINS; FROM MOLECULAR CHAPERONES TO BONA FIDE CATALYSTS

Pathogenesis-related (PR) proteins are another intriguing superfamily, initially described as plant proteins synthesized in response to pathogen attack or other forms of stress (Gianinazzi et al., 1970; Van Loon and Van Kammen, 1970). Today, the definition has expanded to encompass proteins that share sequence or structural homology with diverse expression patterns. PRs are categorized into 17 subfamilies based on phylogeny, catalytic activity, and biological function. One of these subfamilies, PR10, is associated with several phylogenetically distant pathways in specialized metabolism, including flavonoid biosynthesis.

# Structure and phylogeny of PR10s

The discovery and characterization of PR10s in plants has led to independent but overlapping classification systems. The prototypical PR10, labeled PR1, was identified in parsley (Petroselinum crispum). The authors showed a fourfold increase in the transcription rate of PR1 within 5 min of exposure to a fungal elicitor (Somssich et al., 1986). A second label for the protein family came about with the discovery of the major pollen allergen Bet v 1 in white birch (Betula verrucosa) (Breiteneder et al., 1989). This class of proteins produces type 1 allergic reactions by inducing IgE antibodies in genetically predisposed individuals. Every year, the onset of spring and an air thick with Bet v 1-bearing pollen wreaks havoc on millions of people worldwide (Calderon et al., 2012). Major latex proteins (MLPs), first identified in opium poppy (Papaver somniferum) and named due to their laticifer-specific enrichment, comprise the third subclass of this family (Nessler et al., 1985). Researchers became aware of the shared lineage of these three classifications upon investigation of the structure and low molecular weight of the proteins (16-19 kDa).

The proteins form a helix-grip fold (also described as a 'baseball-glove grip'), which is usually characterized by three $\alpha$ -helices and a seven-stranded $\beta$ -sheet, enclosing a

large, mostly hydrophobic, cavity (Figure 4) (Sliwiak et al., 2016). The PR10 fold can accommodate a diverse range of hydrophobic ligands. The long C-terminal $\alpha^3$ helix, which forms a curved dome over the $\beta$ -sheet, is the most variable element of the PR10 structure (Pasternak et al., 2005), mediating substrate specificity by controlling entrance into the cavity. Despite the large hollow core, PR10s are robust proteins, resistant to stress and proteases (Chwastyk et al., 2014). This might be a prerequisite for their function as stress-inducible proteins. Indeed, they are often listed along with HSPs as genes upregulated in response to abiotic stress (e.g., extreme heat, salinity, and cold) (Rizhsky et al., 2002).

The PR10/Bet v 1/MLP fold is part of an ancient and even larger superfamily, known as the START/RHO alpha C/ PITP/Bet v 1/CoxG/CalC (SRPBCC) ligand-binding domain-containing protein superfamily, which spans all kingdoms of life. A deep hydrophobic ligand-binding pocket also characterizes the SRPBCC domain. They are known to bind lipids, including sterols (e.g., cholesterol, phosphatidylcholine, and ceramides) (Alpy and Tomasetto, 2005). The first steroidogenic acute regulatory (StAR) protein was identified in mouse tumor cells in response to luteinizing hormone stimulation (Clark et al., 1994). StAR is responsible for translocating cholesterol from cellular reserves to the mitochondria, where steroidogenesis occurs. Therefore, we can see a pattern in the mammalian counterparts of PR10s of (i) hormone-induced gene expression and (ii) activity in binding and shuttling hydrophobic cargo across membranes. Overall, despite low sequence identities, even amongst intra-specific homologs of PR10s or SRPBCC domain-containing proteins, they likely share an ancient common ancestor (Radauer et al., 2008). The biological function of the common ancestor is speculated to be lipid binding and trafficking of membrane components. The primordial gene has diverged into several subfamilies, some retaining the original fold, while others have added structural elements.

In plants, PR10s can be grouped into five major clades (Radauer et al., 2008). The classic PR10s (e.g., parsley PR1 and birch Bet v 1) group together in a 'dicot' clade. The next large clade includes three branches, 'monocot PR10 types I and II' and a 'conifer' branch. These branches are followed by a group of phytohormone-binding proteins (PhBPs), including several cytokinin-specific binding PR10s and norcoclaurine synthase (NCS). The latter was the first PR10 to be recognized with bona fide biosynthetic activity. Together, the PhBPs and the NCS-type proteins form two separate but closely related clades in hormone signaling and specialized metabolism (see PR10 functions). The final clade of plant PRs is the MLPs, which along with moss PR10-related proteins form the sister group to the rest of the phylogenetic tree. The biological functions of PR10s do not appear to be characteristic of any of the described clades or subfamilies. Indeed, the protein fold appears highly malleable and amenable to neofunctionalization, even within a genus. The study of PR10s in plants has found remarkable levels of gene duplication and the presence of gene copies within transposable element (TE)-rich regions. In fact, genomic clustering of PR10 genes has been reported in several plants, including 17 PR10 genes in V. vinifera on a single chromosome (Lebel et al., 2010), and clusters in apple (Malus domestica) (Gao et al., 2005), peach (Prunus persica) (Chen et al., 2008), poplar (Populus spp.) (Schenk et al., 2006), and opium poppy (Li et al., 2020). The tandem duplication of PR10s in plant genomes might also contribute to the evolution of new functions.

# PR10 functions

PR10 proteins have been assigned a wide array of biological and biochemical functions. In line with their discovery as pathogen-induced proteins, they are known to have protective roles against bacteria, fungi, nematodes, and viruses. However, the mode of antipathogen activity is not

well understood but can be broadly described as direct or indirect confrontation. In the direct role, certain PR10s are known to have protease inhibitory (Chadha and Das, 2006) or nuclease activity (Park et al., 2004), thereby degrading either the enzymatic arsenal or the nucleotides of the pathogen. In the indirect role, PR10s might deploy their phytohormone-binding and trafficking capacity to enact host defense pathways (Sliwiak et al., 2018).

In addition to biotic stress, PR10s are also implicated in abiotic stresses, such as tolerance to cold. A subfamily of PR10s in mulberry trees (Morus australis), labeled as winter accumulating 18 kDa proteins (WAP18s), were shown to accumulate in cortical parenchyma cells during cold acclimation (Ukaji et al., 2004). In vitro, WAP18s exhibited cryoprotective activity for the freeze-sensitive L-lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) enzymes. PR10s were also featured heavily in Arctic species of Oxytropis (Fabaceae) and were coexpressed along with other stress-related genes, such as cold dehydrin and defensins (Archambault and Strömvik, 2011). This role is likely rendered by the physical attributes of PR10s in creating high-density protein-metabolite mixtures, similar to natural deep eutectic solvent described in sorghum (Sorghum bicolor) (Laursen et al., 2016).

The formation of metabolons involving mixtures of proteins and metabolic intermediates has been reported in

many pathways of specialized metabolism (Møller and Laursen, 2021). However, the role of PR10s in such metabolons has not been investigated yet. Outside the plant kingdom, the START/PR10 homologs can sometimes occur as a unit within a multi-domain gene, which can be viewed as the closest degree of association. For example, the ceramide transfer protein (CERT) in humans carries a START domain in combination with other domains and motifs that are essential to its function (Figure 4c,d) (Prashek et al., 2017). CERT is involved in sphingolipid biosynthesis and transports the central building block, ceramide, from the ER to the Golgi. A pleckstrin homology domain at the N-terminus targets the protein to the Golgi by binding phosphatidylinositol 4-monophosphate. In turn, the START domain, with its large hydrophobic cavity, binds the ceramide cargo. There are additional motifs, including a serine-rich (SR) region, which act as additional levers to control the transport activity of the complex. While such a degree of multi-domain association has not been seen for PR10s in plants, the physical interactions of individual proteins can have a similar effect. Furthermore, the example of CERT illustrates how the binding capacity of START/PR10 proteins can influence specialized metabolism by acting as a molecular chaperone, shuttling metabolic intermediates.

Thus, a mechanistic understanding of PR10 function, as binding proteins for generally hydrophobic ligands, has replaced the initial categorization of being strictly linked to the pathogenesis response. PR10 proteins interact with biological ligands, including phytohormones, proteins, fatty acids, amino acids, phenolics, and several classes of alkaloids (Morris et al., 2020). PR10s are known to bind a wide range of phytohormones, including cytokinins (e.g., zeatin), brassinosteroids (e.g., brassinolide), auxins (e.g., melatonin), gibberellic acid, and abscisic acid (ABA). The general role of PR10s in their binding of phytohormones is unknown; however, certain cases shed light on a potential overall strategy. For example, the pyrabactin resistance1 (PYR1)-like proteins are PR10s known to bind ABA specifically. They function in the transduction of ABA signaling for stomatal closure under stress conditions (Lee and Luan, 2012). In birch, Bet v 1 proteins bind cytokinins and other hydrophobic ligands and translocate them across membranes (shown in complex with naringenin in Figure 4b). Therefore, we can deduce that by binding phytohormones, PR10s contribute to their storage, trafficking, and signal transduction. Finally, hormone binding might attenuate catalytic roles of certain PR10s (e.g., nuclease activity) (Fernandes et al., 2009).

# PR10s in specialized metabolism

The rare examples of bona fide catalytic function among PR10s presumably emanate from their prolific substrate range and binding affinities. They have chiefly been ascribed nuclease and detoxification activities, as part of

the pathogen response, across diverse plant lineages (Fernandes et al., 2013; Jain et al., 2016). However, a growing number of PR10s are associated with catalytic or auxiliary functions in specialized metabolism, including sporopollenin, iridoid, alkaloid, and flavonoid pathways. Most recently, a PR10-type protein was identified in the biosynthesis of cis-trans nepetalactol, an iridoid cat attractant in the Nepeta genera (Lichman et al., 2020). The gene labeled MLP-like (MLPL) occurs within an iridoid biosynthetic gene cluster. In coupled assays with iridoid synthase and 8-oxogeranial, MLPL enhances the formation of cis-trans nepetalactol at the expense of other diastereomers. Thus, the ability of PR10s to guide pathway stereochemistry and product selectivity appears to be a recurring theme in plant metabolism.

PR10s play a role in the biosynthesis of two alkaloid classes, the benzylisoquinoline alkaloids (BIAs) and the Amaryllidaceae alkaloids (AA). BIAs are a group of specialized metabolites found predominantly in the Ranunculales order, which includes opium poppy. Bioactive BIAs include the analgesic morphine, the antitussive and anticancer agent noscapine, and the antimicrobials sanguinarine and berberine (Singh et al., 2019). AAs are primarily limited to the subfamily Amaryllidioideae of the Amaryllidaceae, which includes the genera of Narcissus (daffodils), Galanthus (snowdrops), and Leucojum (snowflakes). AAs of note include galantamine, an acetylcholine esterase inhibitor used to treat mild to moderate Alzheimer's disease and other forms of cognitive decline (Ka et al., 2020).

Remarkably, PR10s are involved at the very inception of both clades of metabolites. For BIAs, two tyrosine derivatives (dopamine and 4-hydroxyphenylacetaldehyde [4-HPAA]) are condensed by NCS (Samanani and Facchini, 2002) in an enantioselective Pictet-Spengler reaction. The discovery of NCS sparked the hunt for PR10 homologs catalyzing similar condensation reactions. In AA biosynthesis, norbelladine synthase (NBS) was found to condense derivatives of tyrosine (tyramine) and phenylalanine (3,4-dihydroxybenzaldehyde [3,4-DHBA]) to form norbelladine (Singh et al., 2018). NBS was found via homology search for NCS-type sequences in wild daffodil (Narcissus pseudonarcissus) and shared $41\%$ amino acid identity with opium poppy NCS1. Both proteins are related to Hyp-1, a PR10 purportedly responsible for the complex biosynthesis of the antidepressant hypericin in St. John's wort (Hypericum perforatum) (Bais et al., 2003).

PR10s are critically involved further downstream in BIA metabolism within the morphinan alkaloid branch. The baine synthase (THS) and neopinone isomerase (NISO) are PR10 proteins involved in two steps previously assumed to be non-enzymatic and spontaneous (Gollwitzer et al., 1993; Lenz and Zenk, 1995). Indeed, the biosynthesis of morphine was declared complete from a gene discovery perspective in 2015; however, attempts at reconstituting the

pathway led to aberrant by-products (Galanie et al., 2015). The solution to this synthetic biology problem resides in the cytoplasm of laticifer cells in opium poppy, known as latex. Researchers purified THS from the latex exudate and found that it catalyzes the deacetylation and intramolecular allylic rearrangement of salutaridinol 7-O-acetate to thebaine (Chen et al., 2018). In the absence of THS, allylic elimination and hydroxylation lead to an erroneous product. Two steps down, NISO appears to reduce the activation energy for isomerization of neopinone to codeinone, providing the correct substrate for codeinone reductase, the penultimate enzyme of morphine biosynthesis (Dastmalchi et al., 2019). The inclusion of THS and NISO in engineered microbes drastically enhances the metabolic flux towards desired morphinan alkaloids by avoiding carbon loss to alternate pathways. Consequently, titers of codeine, morphine, and the semi-synthetic opioid hydrocodone have been significantly improved in microbe-based fermentation.

# PR10s in flavonoid metabolism

In flavonoid biosynthesis, we see PR10s playing a role akin to that of CHILs in potentially enhancing or guiding metabolic flux. The true nature of this activity evades direct assessment due to its necessity for coupled assays. Similar to MLPL in nepetalactol biosynthesis, flavonoid-associated PR10s do not appear to independently catalyze reactions but mediate or curtail the outcomes of enzymatic partners.

In asparagus (Asparagus officinalis), an intracellular PR gene (AoPR1), initially identified by wound or pathogen induction, showed a spatiotemporal expression pattern which correlated closely with that of anthocyanin accumulation and 'core phenylpropanoid pathway' genes (Warner et al., 1994). Similarly, in soybean, there were early reports of MLP homologs with high levels of expression in developing pods (Strömvik et al., 1999), potentially correlated with the accumulation of flavonoid metabolites, although the authors did not put forward this hypothesis at the time.

The most well-studied example of PR10s in flavonoid metabolism is the 'Fra a' family in strawberry (Fragaria × ananassa). They were initially discovered as IgE-binding proteins and presumptive allergens (Karlsson et al., 2004). The homology of Fra a proteins to potent allergens, Bet v 1 and Mal d 1, sparked an early interest in analyzing their structure and expression, with a view to generate or guide the development of hypoallergenic varieties. A comparison of strawberry cultivars bearing white (mutant) and red (wild-type) fruit revealed that the former was virtually free of a protein with homology to the birch allergen Bet v 1, which was named Fra a 1 (Hjernø et al., 2006). This work was crucial in emphasizing the role of Fra a 1 in producing allergenicity and potentially regulating anthocyanin pigmentation.

Transient RNA interference (RNAi)-mediated silencing of Fra a 1 and a homolog, Fra a 3, led to reduction of

anthocyanins (cyanidin 3-O-glucoside and pelargonidin 3-O-glucoside) and upstream metabolites (Muñoz et al., 2010) (Figure 1). The results demonstrated that the Fra a proteins play an essential role in pigment formation in strawberry fruits, albeit via an unknown mechanism. The authors speculated that Fra a may function as a carrier of pathway intermediates or as a (co)transporter of anthocyanins into the vacuole. However, overexpression of Fra a genes in the wild-type background does not increase metabolite levels. Further, Fra a expression was unchanged in naturally occurring white-fruited varieties, which have an upstream blockage at PAL and CHS. Indeed, genotype, rather than the color of mature fruits, appears to determine the quantity of Fra a proteins (Kurze et al., 2018). Therefore, Fra a expression or protein abundance, while impacting anthocyanin accumulation, is not sufficient for the red color of mature strawberries.

To understand the function of Fra a mechanistically, researchers have explored the binding affinities and structural aspects of this family of PR10s. Fra a paralogs from strawberry, Fra a 1E and Fra a 3, were crystallized in the presence of the flavan-3-ol (+)-catechin (Figure 4) (Casañal et al., 2013). Using isothermal titration calorimetry, researchers provided evidence for Fra a binding of the flavan nucleus, common to anthocyanins and flavonols. Fra a 1E binds quercetin-3-O-glucuronide $(\mathsf{K}_d5.3\mu \mathsf{M})$ whereas Fra a 2 binds myricetin $(\mathsf{K}_d19.5\mu \mathsf{M})$ two kaempferol-derived flavonols. Intriguingly, it appears that Fra a 3 has two binding sites, with the major site exhibiting a $K_{d}$ of $8.9~\mu \mathrm{M}$ for catechin (Figure 5a). In their apo (no substrate) conformation, the proteins show flexibility in specific loops extending over the internal cavity (L3, L5, and L7). These loops become more compact and rigid upon ligand binding. In the Fra a 3-catechin complex, the substrate is arranged linearly, parallel to the C-terminal $\alpha$ -helix (Figure 4a). Catechin is stabilized in the largely hydrophobic pocket by interacting with the polar side chains of amino acids. It is important to note that the Fra a paralogs in strawberry do not bind early phenylpropanoid intermediates (L-phenylalanine, coumaric acid, cinnamic acid, and caffeic acid). This distinction might shed light on the mechanisms of regulating anthocyanin or flavonol accumulation.

Three modes of Fra a function at the molecular level have been suggested in relation to flavonoid biosynthesis. In the first theory, Fra a functions as a member of the flavonoid metabolon (Figure 5b) (Fujino et al., 2018), interacting at the surface of the ER with biosynthetic enzymes. In this manner, they can bind intermediates, limiting diffusion and channeling potentially toxic or labile compounds between successive catalytic sites. In the second, and related, model, Fra a proteins act as molecular chaperones, facilitating the transportation of anthocyanins and flavonols into the vacuole, other organelles, or the apoplast

Thirdly, the capacity of Fra a proteins to differentially bind intermediates could control the direction and rate of metabolic flux (Casanal et al., 2013). The details of such a mechanism are opaque, as Fra a gene suppression leads to a scattershot compositional shift, increasing levels of some anthocyanins (pelargonidin 3-O-rutinoside) while reducing those of others (cyanidin 3-O-glucoside, pelargonidin 3-O-glucoside, and 3-malonylglucoside). A complete understanding of the role of Fra a proteins can, perhaps, be grasped by using (non-)isotopic labeling to track temporal changes in flavonoid intermediates alongside protein activity or gene expression.

There is also evidence implicating that Fra a proteins have a regulatory role, controlling expression of flavonoid biosynthetic genes. RNAi suppression of Fra a 1 and Fra a 3 led to off-target reductions in CHS and PAL transcript levels (Muñoz et al., 2010). Mechanistically, flavonol-dependent feedback inhibition of the biosynthetic pathway up to PAL has previously been reported and can explain a regulatory function of Fra a proteins (Yin et al., 2012). Furthermore, moonlighting proteins are a recurring motif in biosynthetic enzymes with a secondary role in regulation (Commichau et al., 2007; Schmid et al., 2019; Su et al., 2019). Indeed, a moonlighting activity has been suggested for CHS, potentially mediating epigenetic control over the flavonoid pathway via nuclear-localized interaction with

MOS9 (Watkinson et al., 2018). Proteins with the diminutive size of Fra a could conceivably diffuse through the nuclear pores, similar to some CHS isoforms, and become implicated in nuclear transcriptional regulation. Alternatively, this role can be played via binding messenger molecules, as seen with cytokinin-binding PR10s (Biesiadka et al., 2002), leading to indirect regulation of core phenylpropanoid genes (Figure 5d).

The activity of PR10s is likely to extend beyond anthocyanin and flavonol levels. The suite of Fra a 1 paralogs in the strawberry genome has been expanded based on recent studies to 39 genes encoding 30 proteins (Ishibashi et al., 2018). Therefore, facile development and tuning of binding affinities and substrate ranges are evolutionarily probable. A recent report found that priming strawberry leaves with the phytohormone salicylic acid (SA) leads to upregulation of PR genes, including PR10 genes, and concomitant increases in PA levels (Feng et al., 2020). In another study, silencing Fra a 1.02 led to downregulation of WRKY-type transcription factors and ABC and MATE transporters and to a diminished content of the hormones jasmonic acid, SA, and indole acetic acid, suggesting a role in stress responses (Orozco-Navarrete et al., 2021). This report and others indicate that stress-induced expression of PR10s, including Fra a proteins, can potentially orchestrate a metabolic shift via undiscovered roles as auxiliary proteins, chemical chaperones, or regulatory elements.

# PERSPECTIVE

CHI and PR10 families appear to represent two separate cases of a protein fold, adept at binding lipids or

hydrophobic ligands, having developed a variety of catalytic and non-catalytic functions independently across diverse lineages. The progression of CHIs from binding fatty acids to chalcones and finally catalyzing chalcones into flavanones is a narrower trajectory (Kaltenbach et al., 2018). CHILs are the predicted midpoint in that phylogeny, enhancing flavonoid production by reportedly interacting with CHS and mediating tetraketide cyclization. On the other hand, PR10s, with their low sequence homology, rapid expansion, and neofunctionalization, even within a genus, portray a more expansive form of divergence. As a result, a study of PR10s in plant species reveals members involved in disparate pathways from ABA signaling (Miyakawa et al., 2013) to morphine biosynthesis (Chen et al., 2018).

The sheer number of PR10 isoforms and the lack of shared biological activity, even within closely related clades, eludes easy characterization by functional genomics. Consequently, discoveries of PR10 involvement in metabolism have often materialized because the genes are associated with pathogen induction, found within significant biosynthetic clusters (e.g., MLPL in the nepetalactol cluster; Lichman et al., 2020) or heavily enriched within unique structures (e.g., NISO in opium poppy latex; Dastmalchi et al., 2019). The discovery of loss-of-function mutants helped characterize strawberry Fra a proteins (Munoz et al., 2010); however, such an occurrence is rare due to an inherent bias towards redundancy within the PR10 protein family.

The difficulty in discovery is only superseded by the effort of characterizing auxiliary proteins, such as PR10s, CHILs, or DABBs. For example, activity of the phytocannabinoid DABB homolog OAC can only be recorded in a coupled assay with OLS (Gagne et al., 2012). A great clue to this activity is when attempts at reconstituting plant pathways in heterologous systems go awry, resulting in aberrant side branches. Such derailment often occurs as a result of plant enzymes acting outside of their usual cellular context. Auxiliary proteins can serve as the 'guard rails' by mediating product selectivity, binding intermediates, and channeling them between contiguous enzymes. Indeed, the heterologous system has emerged as a great tool to complement functional genomics and decipher the impact of an auxiliary protein on a metabolic chassis (Morris et al., 2016). The use of such strategies can also help reconstitute and provide evidence for the existence of metabolons.

When we think of cellular processes, such as harvesting of light, transcription of genes, or translocation of proteins, we quickly realize that auxiliary proteins and chaperones are involved, forming large dynamic complexes. For example, the biogenesis and function of the most abundant protein on earth, ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase-oxygenase (RuBisCO), is reliant on auxiliary proteins (Hauser et al., 2015). The discovery

of such features in plant metabolism has been limited by the stepwise analysis of biosynthetic pathways and an overreliance on homology-based search. Fishing out auxiliary proteins will be difficult when they are buried in a high degree of noise, i.e., redundancy, the transience of interactions, and broad function. Fra a proteins in strawberry are a perfect example of a large family of homologs with differential binding capacities, which makes the task of pinpointing their role very complex. Nevertheless, PR10s and other auxiliary proteins can be crucial partners in plant metabolism, guiding pathway stereochemistry, product selectivity, and output as a whole. They have proven to be crucial components of plant biosynthesis of natural products and the ability of engineered microbes to replicate plant competencies.
