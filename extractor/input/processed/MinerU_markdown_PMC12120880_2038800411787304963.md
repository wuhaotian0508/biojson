# Summary

Rubber particles (RPs) are specialized organelles for the biosynthesis and storage of natural rubber in rubber-producing plants. However, the mechanisms underlying the biogenesis and development of RPs remain unclear. In this study, two latex-specific cis-prenyltransferases (CPTs), TkCPT1 and TkCPT2, were identified in Taraxacum kok-saghyz, with almost identical orthologues retained across other Taraxacum species. For the first time, Tkcpt1 single and Tkcpt1/2 double mutants were successfully generated using the CRISPR/Cas9 system. Rubber biosynthesis was significantly depressed in Tkcpt1 mutants and completely blocked in Tkcpt1/2 mutants. The absence of RPs in the Tkcpt1/2 was confirmed using oil red O and Nile red staining, high-speed centrifugal stratification, cryo-SEM and TEM on fresh latex or laticifer cells. Transcriptomic and proteomic analyses revealed that, in the latex of Tkcpt1/2, rubber biosynthesis was blocked at the protein level, while metabolomic profiling indicated an enrichment of lipids and terpenoids. Furthermore, knockout of TkCPTL1, a latex-specific CPT-like gene that encodes a rubber transferase activator, resulted in outright disruption of rubber biosynthesis and RP ontogeny, a phenotype similar to that of Tkcpt1/2 mutants. These findings indicate that rubber biosynthesis is a driving force for the biogenesis and development of RPs, providing new insights into rubber production mechanisms. 

# Introduction

Natural rubber (NR), a remarkably versatile and indispensable industrial raw material, is an essential cornerstone of contemporary industries. It is widely utilized across various sectors including agriculture, transportation, machinery manufacturing and medical appliance, with a boast of over 50 000 products (Cherian et al., 2019). The demand for NR has been steadily increasing, making it highly important to decode the mechanisms involved in the formation and regulation of NR production. Currently, Hevea tree (Hevea brasiliensis) is the exclusive commercial source of NR. Nevertheless, it is not an ideal plant for studying the mechanisms of rubber production due to its extended juvenile stage (typically 5–8 years from planting to flowering) and constraints in genetic transformation efficiency (Clement-Demange et al., 2007). In the long run, the global NR supply is insecure from the single tropical species—Hevea tree, due to various limitations, for example, climatic restrictions in planting regions, long growth cycles, narrow genetic diversity and vulnerability to fungal infections (Guyot and Le Guen, 2018). Several alternative rubber-producing plants such as guayule (Parthenium argentatum), rubber dandelion (or Kazakh dandelion; Taraxacum kok-saghyz) and lettuce (Lactuca sativa) have been explored (van Beilen and Poirier, 2007; Bushman et al., 2006). Of these plants, rubber dandelion stands out as a model plant for studying the biology of rubber 

production for the NR properties similar to that of Hevea tree, high root rubber content, the availability of high-quality reference genome and the establishment of efficient propagation and transgenic systems (Lin et al., 2017, 2021; Yang et al., 2024). 

In contrast to guayule, which synthesizes and sequesters NR within parenchyma cells, Hevea tree, rubber dandelion and lettuce exploit specialized vessels called laticifer systems to produce NR (Castelblanque et al., 2016). During the development of laticifers, individual cells fuse to form an articulated, anastomosing laticiferous network with interconnected cytoplasm (Teixeira et al., 2020). Latex, the cytoplasm of laticifers can be fractionated by ultracentrifugation into four principal subcellular components: rubber layer, Frey-Wyssling particles (plastids), cytosolic serum and the sediment bottom layer (lutoids) (Yamashita and Takahashi, 2020). The rubber layer comprises large rubber particles (LRP) in its upper region and small rubber particles (SRP) in its lower region. 

RPs are specialized organelles for rubber biosynthesis and storage, consisting of a hydrophobic rubber core surrounded by a lipid monolayer and membrane-bound proteins (Tan et al., 2023). The rubber transferase (RTase) complex on the surface of the RPs is responsible for NR biosynthesis across multiple rubber-producing plants (Cornish and Xie, 2012). The RTase complex has been extensively studied, particularly on its core enzyme, the cis-prenyltransferase (CPT) and the CPT-like (CPTL) 

proteins (Grabinska  et al., 2016; Yamashita et al., 2016). Although CPTL lacks the catalytic function of conventional CPT, it tethers CPT on the rubber particle through direct interaction (Brown et al., 2017; Harrison et al., 2011). In T. brevicorniculatum, knockdown of the laticifer-dominant TbCPT1/2/3 or TbCPTL led to a significant decrease in NR content (Epping et al., 2015; Post et al., 2012). Similarity, knocking down the latex-specific expressed CPTL orthologues in lettuce (LsCPTL2) and rubber dandelion (TkCPTL1) also resulted in significant reduction of NR biosynthesis (Niephaus et al., 2019; Qu et al., 2015). Moreover, co-expression of PaCPTL and PaCPT from guayule, or HRT1/HRT2 (HbCPT8/HbCPT6) from Hevea tree, could complement dolichol (a short cis-polyisoprene)-deficient yeast (Lakusta et al., 2019; Takahashi et al., 2012). Interestingly, heterologous expression of CPT from guayule or goldenrod in the lettuce CPT mutants yields significantly longer NR chains compared with their native plants, indicating that CPT alone does not determine NR length (Kwon et al., 2023). In addition to CPT and CPTL, rubber elongation factor (REF) and small rubber particle protein (SRPP) were identified in high abundance on rubber particles (Xie et al., 2022). RNAi silencing of SRPPs significantly decreased rubber content in both T. kok-saghyz and T. brevicorniculatum, whereas no NR reduction was found in SRPP-silenced lettuce (Chakrabarty et al., 2015; Collins-Silva et al., 2012; Hillebrand et al., 2012). To date, the exact roles of REF/SRPPs in regulating molecular weight, yield and quality of NR remain unclear. 

The ontogeny and development of RPs are crucial for rubber yield and biotic stress defence in rubber-producing plants (Ghaffar and Cornish, 2020; Ramos et al., 2019). Microscopic observations have revealed that the ontogeny of RPs occurs before the formation of laticifers, specifically within the secretory pathway of the endoplasmic reticulum–Golgi vesicular complex (Ghaffar and Cornish, 2020). The presence of the rubber layer, although diminished significantly, was observed in both CPT and CPTL knockdown lines, as well as in LsCPT3 knockout lines, indicating that rubber biosynthesis still occurs (Epping et al., 2015; Kwon et al., 2023; Niephaus et al., 2019). Therefore, these lines are not suitable for investigating the role of rubber biosynthesis in RP initiation and development. Here, two latexspecific CPT genes in T. kok-saghyz, TkCPT1 and TkCPT2, were knocked out using the CRISPR/Cas9 system. The resultant Tkcpt1 single mutant exhibited a significant reduction in the rubber content, whereas the Tkcpt1/2 double mutant was completely abolished for rubber biosynthesis. Meanwhile, the Tkcpt1/2 double mutant lacked RPs, as verified by imaging the laticifer cells and fresh latex. Multi-omics profiling revealed that the rubber biosynthesis pathway was completely blocked in the Tkcpt1/2 mutant, with a concurrent marked elevation in the lipid and terpenoid metabolites. Moreover, similar to the Tkcpt1/2 mutant, knockout of TkCPTL1 entirely disrupted rubber biosynthesis and RP biogenesis, further reinforcing the notion that rubber biosynthesis is essential to the ontogeny and development of RPs. 

# Results

# Latex-specific CPT proteins exhibit high conservation in the genus Taraxacum

TK20 is an elite rubber dandelion (T. kok-saghyz) germplasm sourced from Xinjiang, China. Previously, we established the propagation and transgenic system (Yang et al., 2024) and 

recently completed its genome assembly and annotation. Seven TkCPT and two TkCPTL genes were identified in TK20 (Figure 1a). Among them, TkCPT1 and TkCPT2 displayed high protein sequence identity with latex-specific CPTs reported in other T. kok-saghyz lines as well as different Taraxacum species (Lin et al., 2021; Schmidt et al., 2010), notwithstanding their divergent rubber-producing capabilities (Figure S1). Notably, TkCPT1 and TkCPT2 showed $9 6 . 1 0 \%$ sequence identity in TK20 (Figure 1b). Expression analysis across five tissues demonstrated that both TkCPT1 and TkCPT2 were specifically and highly expressed in latex (Figure 1c). Subcellular localization analysis revealed that both TkCPTs were colocalized with the endoplasmic reticulum marker protein, when transiently expressed in N. benthamiana leaves (Figure 1d). Intriguingly, it was observed that these proteins localized also in the nucleus of tobacco leaves (Figure 1d). The tertiary structures of TkCPT1 and TkCPT2 from the AlphaFold Protein Structure Database are highly similar, featuring five $\pmb { \alpha }$ -helices and six $\beta$ -sheets, with an RMSD (root mean square deviation) value of $0 . 2 2 1 \mathrm { ~ \AA ~ }$ (Figure 1e). Moreover, the seven extra amino acids present in TkCPT1 are positioned within an $\pmb { \alpha }$ -helix, similar in structure to that of TkCPT2 (Figure 1e). These results indicate that TkCPT1 and TkCPT2 are highly conserved in protein sequence, subcellular localization and 3D structure, implying potential functional redundancy. 

# Knockout of TkCPT1 or both TkCPT1 and TkCPT2 does not affect plant development

Due to the high sequence identity between the two latex-specific TkCPTs, an sgRNA could be designed to specifically target the longer one, TkCPT1, for the generation of single-knockout mutant (Figure 2a). In addition, two sgRNAs were employed to simultaneously target TkCPT1 and TkCPT2 for the generation of double-knockout mutant (Figure 2b). The target genomic regions were amplified via PCR and subsequently sequenced using the Hi-TOM 2.0 platform. Five Tkcpt1 single and four Tkcpt1/2 double mutants were obtained, with a $100 \%$ editing efficiency (Figure 2a,b, Figure S2). The knockout events were reconfirmed on plants vegetatively propagated from leaf explants, revealing the retention of multiple mutation types in a single individual (Figure 2a,b, Figure S2). Compared with the wild-type (WT) plants, Tkcpt1 and Tkcpt1/2 mutants exhibited no significant differences in above-ground phenotypes or root morphology after 3 months of soil cultivation (Figure 2c,d). Furthermore, no significant discrepancies in root dry weight were observed between WT and Tkcpt mutants (Figure 2e). These findings suggest that knockout of latex-specific CPTs does not result in visible differences in the growth and development of rubber dandelion. However, the ecological fitness might differ between the WT and Tkcpt mutants, as natural rubber biosynthesis has been reported to affect herbivory significantly in T. koksaghyz (Bottner € et al., 2023).

# Lipids and terpenoids are accumulated in the latex of Tkcpt1/2 mutants

To explore the impact of RP absence on latex metabolism, metabolome profiling was performed on fresh latex samples from Tkcpt1/2 and WT. Principal component analysis (PCA) displayed remarkable differences in the metabolic composition between the two sample groups (Figure 6a). Of the 1522 metabolites identified, 738 were identified as differentially accumulated metabolites (DAMs), of which 655 were upregulated in the Tkcpt1/2 mutant, while only 83 downregulated (Figure 6b). Based on the annotation, these DAMs were categorized into the primary and secondary metabolic groups (Table S4). Notably, terpenoids $( 1 0 . 5 6 \% )$ and lipids $( 1 1 . 3 8 \% )$ were identified as the major DAM contributors, alongside amino acids and derivatives, phenolic acids and alkaloids (Figure 6c). Consistently, as revealed by KEGG analysis, these DAMs were enriched in the pathways related to glycerophospholipid metabolism, monoterpenoid biosynthesis, sphingolipid metabolism (Figure 6d). Differential abundant lipids and terpenoids were further classified according to their secondary annotations, revealing that LPC (Lysophosphatidylcholine) and LPE (Lyso-phosphatidylethanolamine) were the most accumulated lipids in the Tkcpt1/2 mutant, while 

monoterpenoids and sesquiterpenoids were the largest amount gathered terpenoids (Figure 6e). The results indicated that, in the Tkcpt1/2 mutant, the primary metabolites typically allocated for RP formation were redirected towards the synthesis of lipids and terpenoids. NR chain consists of one allylic pyrophosphate initiator (GPP, FPP or GGPP) and ~ 20 000 IPPs, whereas the specific initiator for NR biosynthesis remains unclear (Figure 5g). Quantitative analysis indicated the contents of DMAPP, FPP and GGPP increased significantly in the Tkcpt1/2 mutant, accompanied by a notable decline in GPP (Figure 6f). In addition, there was no significant differences in the content of IPP (Figure 6f). This finding suggests that FPP and GGPP serve as the primarily initiator molecules for NR biosynthesis, while GPP is utilized mainly for the synthesis of short-chain terpenoids in laticifer cells.

# Discussion

Latex-producing plants with laticifer systems are widespread in the plant kingdom, while only approximately $20 \%$ of them produce rubber. RPs are specialized subcellular structures responsible for the biosynthesis and storage of NR. The lipid and protein compositions of RPs and latex have been extensively studied, suggesting that RPs may come from the ER or Golgi apparatus (Bae et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2018). However, the biogenesis and development process of RPs remain poorly understood. The subcellular initiation of RPs was first observed using TEM, confirming that their origination from the ER–Golgi 

vesicular complex (Ghaffar and Cornish, 2020). Based on both structure characteristics and protein composition of RPs, it is plausible to speculate that their formation process is similar to that of lipid droplets. The biogenesis of lipid droplets is tightly linked to triacylglycerols (TAG) biosynthesis. Briefly, diacylglycerol acyltransferase 1 (DGAT1) and phospholipid:diacylglycerol acyltransferase 1 (PDAT1) catalyse TAG biosynthesis, and the products are stored in the ER. As TAG accumulates, the ER develops an outward bulging structure and subsequently detaches, involving various regulatory and membrane proteins related to lipid droplets (Ischebeck et al., 2020). Notably, lipid droplet surface-associated proteins, such as LD-associated proteins (LDAPs), share significant similarities with RP surface proteins known as REF/SRPPs (Horn et al., 2013). Consequently, we hypothesized that, akin to lipid droplet formation, the catalytic synthesis of NR could be a pivotal factor in triggering the biogenesis of RPs. 

The rubber transferase complex, consisting of CPT, CPTL, REF and SRPP, plays a central role in the catalytic biosynthesis of NR. Knockdown of TbCPTs or TbCPTL in T. brevicorniculatum resulted in a marked decrease in rubber biosynthesis, accompanied by a notable increase in the contents of triterpenes and inulin (Epping et al., 2015; Post et al., 2012). Similar results were observed in the CPTL RNAi lines of T. kok-saghyz and Lactuca sativa (Niephaus et al., 2019; Qu et al., 2015). Recently, the first LsCPT3 null mutant was generated using the CRISPR/Cas9 system and resulted in the absence of high-molecular-weight NR in the latex (Kwon et al., 2023). However, it is noteworthy that the rubber phase remains present after latex centrifugation. These results indicate that, in these RNAi and knockout lines, rubber biosynthesis were depressed or produced only lower molecular weight rubber, but RP biogenesis and development were not completely inhibited. In this study, Tkcpt1 single and Tkcpt1/2 double mutants were first obtained by the CRISPR/Cas9 system in T. kok-saghyz, also the first report in dandelion species (Figure 2). TkCPT1 exhibited higher expression levels than TkCPT2, yet its knockout led to only an $\sim 3 0 \%$ reduction in rubber content (Figures 1c and 3b). This finding implies that the two latex-specific CPT genes exhibit considerable functional redundancy. Interestingly, the rubber layer is entirely absent in the Tkcpt1/2 double mutant. Detailed microscopic observation of fresh latex and laticifer cells showed that RPs formation and growth were completely vanished in the Tkcpt1/2 double mutant, while the Tkcpt1 single mutant exhibited a reduced number of RPs (Figure S4). Collectively, these results suggest that rubber biosynthesis is the main driving force for the biogenesis and development of RPs. 

For the first time, transcriptome, proteome and metabolome profiling were conducted on the latex samples of wild-type and Tkcpt1/2 mutant plants, corresponding to the presence and absence of rubber production, respectively. As expected, similar to other secondary metabolites, disrupting rubber biosynthesis had minimal effect on the transcription of genes in latex, including those involved in the NR biosynthesis pathways (Figure S5). In contrast, proteomic analysis revealed numerous DEPs. Notably, DEPs in the MVA pathway were downregulated in Tkcpt1/2 mutants, whereas those in the MEP pathway were upregulated (Figure 5f). Meanwhile, the contents of DMAPP, FPP and GGPP in latex were significantly enriched in the Tkcpt1/2 mutant, while the GPP levels were decreased dramatically (Figure 6f). These findings indicate that the MVA pathway provides the primary source of IPP for NR biosynthesis, with FPP 

and GGPP being the initiator molecules. These results are consistent with the depletion of NR and the increased levels of other lipids and terpenes in Tkcpt1/2 mutants, and reinforce the proposition that in the Hevea tree, the IPP monomers for rubber biosynthesis originate from the cytoplasmic MVA pathway, whereas the IPPs from the plastidial MEP pathway are used for synthesizing other isoprenoids (Sando et al., 2008; Tang et al., 2016). 

The rubber biosynthesis pathway is entirely blocked in the Tkcpt1/2 mutant due to the nearly undetectable levels of the latex-specific TkCPT proteins, alongside the complete absence of RPs. In addition, proteins involved in rubber biosynthesis, such as TkCPTL, TkREF and TkSRPPs, are also reduced significantly (Figure 5h). These results suggest that the absence of RPs significantly reduces protein levels associated with NR biosynthesis. Furthermore, we questioned whether the absence of RPs significantly altered other metabolic processes in latex. Metabolomic analysis identified 655 upregulated and 83 downregulated metabolites, with lipids and terpenoids comprising a significant portion (Figure 6b,c). Reasonably, the absence of RPs, which primarily consist of NR hydrocarbons and phospholipid membranes, enhances the biosynthesis of short-chain terpenoids and other lipids. 

To date, reconstructing in vitro long-chain rubber biosynthesis remains a significant challenge. One main reason for this difficulty is the unknown links between the components of the RTase complex. We discovered that TkCPTL1 establishes the catalytic complex on RPs by interacting with TkREF and TkCPTs, individually (Figure 7b,c, Figure S7). Moreover, knockout of TkCPTL1 also entirely disrupts the rubber biosynthesis and RP biogenesis. These results confirmed that CPTL and CPT are core components of the RTase complex. However, the REF knockdown lines showed a less significant decrease in rubber content compared with the knockdown of CPT or CPTL (Laibach et al., 2015), indicating that the localization of the RTase complex on RPs requires additional auxiliary proteins. Until now, our understanding of the roles of RP surface proteins or CPTL-interacting proteins remains limited. Therefore, we believe the next crucial step is to identify the key surface proteins on RPs that assist the RTase complex in synthesizing and storing NR. 

# Methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The rubber dandelion (TK20) utilized in this study was originally collected from Xinjiang, China. The methods of vegetative propagation and genetic transformation for TK20, as previously described (Yang et al., 2024), were employed in this study. Plants grown on Murashige and Skoog medium were under a 16-h light/8-h dark cycle at $2 4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , with a light intensity of $8 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ and $50 \%$ relative humidity. Transgenic plants used for phenotyping and the Nicotiana benthamiana for transient expression assays were cultivated in soil under the same temperature conditions, with a light intensity of $1 4 5 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ and $60 \%$ relative humidity. Three-month-old TK20 wild type (WT) and transgenic plants were analysed for rubber content, root section and latex staining, while 4-week-old leaves of N. benthamiana were used for transient expression studies. 

# Subcellular localization

The cDNA sequences of TkCPT1 or TkCPT2 were cloned and inserted to the pCAMBIA1300-GFP vector. The infusion vectors 

were transferred into Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101 and mixed equally with the strains containing either nuclear marker H2B-mCherry or the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) marker HDEL-mCherry vector. The mixture A. tumefaciens cells were coinjected into the lower epidermis of N. benthamiana leaves, followed by 1 day of darkness and 2 days of light cultivation. Leaf samples were excised and mounted on slides, with GFP or mCherry signals detected using a Nikon A1R HD25 confocal microscope. All primers used in this study are listed in Table S1. 

# CRISPR/Cas9-based knockout and transgenic plants screening

Small guide RNAs (sgRNAs) were obtained from the E-CRISP website (http://www.e-crisp.org/) using the coding sequences of TkCPT1 or TkCPT2 (Heigwer et al., 2014). One sgRNA specifically targeting TkCPT1 and two sgRNAs targeting both TkCPT1 and TkCPT2 were selected. The sgRNAs were ligated to the AtU6-26- sgRNA plasmid via Bsa I digestion and subsequently transferred to the pCAMBIA1300-pYAO:Cas9 vector using Spe I digestion (Yan et al., 2015). Transgenic TK20 plants were initially screened by PCR amplification of the vector sequence, followed by Hi-TOM sequencing of the sgRNA-targeted fragments (Sun et al., 2024). Only $100 \%$ edited plants were retained and propagated for further analysis. 

# Detection of the rubber content

Whole plants were dried to constant weight at $6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in an oven and the roots were ground into a fine powder. A 0.5-g sample of root powder was added to a 50-mL glass tube with 40 mL ${ \mathsf { d d H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ and incubated in a boiling water bath for $\uparrow \ \mathsf { h }$ . The mixture solution was filtered through a nylon membrane and the remains were boiled for an additional $^ { 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ with $4 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of $3 \%$ KOH. Then, the alkaline-boiled mixture was stirred with a magnetic stirrer for 30 min. After that, the rubber pieces were picked out, washed with ${ \mathsf { d o l } } { \mathsf { H } } { \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ to eliminate impurities and dried to constant weight (Ramirez-Cadavid et al., 2017). The rubber content was quantified as the ratio of dry rubber weight to dry root weight. Each measurement was performed with three biological replicates. 

# Iodine-bromide (I-Br) and oil red O staining

Roots with a diameter of $4 \mathsf { m m }$ were vacuum-infiltrated with $70 \%$ FAA solution for 30 min, followed by continuous fixation for $2 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ . For I-Br staining, fixed roots were dehydrated through a gradient of alcohol concentrations, treated with acetic acid and stained with I-Br solution $( 5 \%$ iodine and $0 . 0 4 \%$ bromide in acetic acid). Then, they were cleared with acetic acid, ethanol and n-butanol in sequence, embedded in paraffin and sectioned into $1 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ thick slices. The sections were dewaxed, re-stained with solid green and imaged using a SOPTOP EX20 microscope (Hao and Wu, 2000). For oil red O staining, fixed roots were dehydrated using sucrose solution and sectioned into $6 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } }$ thick slices. The slices were stained with saturated oil red O solution, differentiated in $60 \%$ isopropanol, washed with ddH2O water and then mounted with glycerin gelatin, as previously described (Liang et al., 2023). Images were captured using a 3DHistech Pannoramic 250 Flash slide scanner system (Hungary). 

# Nile red staining and latex centrifugation

Fresh latex was harvested by cutting the root base of rubber dandelion with a sharp knife. For Nile red staining, $4 0 ~ \mu \ L$ of latex was added to $2 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of TD buffer (0.1 M Tris–HCl, PH 7.5, 2 mM 

DTT), followed by $4 0 ~ \mu \ L$ of $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m g } / \mathsf { L }$ Nile red dye, that was dissolved in a 1:1 mixture of DMSO and glycerin, and mixed thoroughly with a pipette (Akin et al., 2004; Cooksey et al., 1987). Particle morphology in the latex was observed using a Nikon A1R HD25 confocal microscope, and particle diameters were measured with ImageJ software. For latex centrifugation, $4 0 ~ \mu \ L$ of fresh latex was transferred into a 0.5- mL centrifuge tube containing $3 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of TD buffer and mixed thoroughly. After centrifugation at room temperature at 12 000 rpm for 30 min, the latex stratification was photographed. 

# Cryo-scanning electron microscopy (Cryo-SEM)

Fresh latex samples were loaded onto a cryo-specimen holder, cryo-fixed in slush nitrogen $( - 2 1 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C )$ and rapidly transferred to the cryo-unit in the frozen state. Frozen latex samples were fractured by knife and sublimated at $- 9 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ in a preparation system (PP3010T, Quorum). After sublimation, the samples were platinum-sputtered, transferred to the SEM and viewed directly at $- 1 4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Samples were imaged using a cryo-scanning electron microscope (HITACHI SU8100). 

# Transmission electron microscopy (TEM)

Root segments of rubber dandelion, measuring 1 mm thick by 1 mm long, were fixed in $4 \%$ v/v glutaraldehyde and further fixed with $1 \%$ w/v osmium tetroxide at room temperature. Then, the segments were dehydrated through a gradient of alcohol concentrations, treated with acetone and embedded using SPI-Pon 812R resin. Following polymerization at $6 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ , the samples were sectioned into $6 0 { - } 8 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ slices using an ultramicrotome. The slices were double stained with a saturated solution of uranyl acetate and lead citrate, then dried overnight at room temperature. Images were captured under a transmission electron microscope (HITACHI, HT7700). 

# Yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) assay

Both split-ubiquitin-based and Gal4-based Y2H systems were employed in this study. TkCPTs were inserted into the pPR3N-NubG vector using EcoR I/BamH I digestion, while TkCPTL1 was cloned into the pBT-SUC-Cub-LexA-VP16 vector via Sfi I digestion. Furthermore, TkCPTL1 and TkREF were cloned to pGADT7-AD and pGBKT7-BD vectors, respectively, using EcoR I/BamH I digestion. Different combinations of constructs were transformed into the yeast strain NMY51 or AH109. The Y2H assays were conducted as described in the Yeast Protocols Handbook (Clontech). 

# Split luciferase assay

The cDNAs of TkCPTs, TkCPTL1 and TkREF were cloned into pCAMBIA-nLUC using Kpn I/Sal I digestion or pCAMBIA-cLUC via BamH I digestion. Then, constructs were transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101. Different combinations of transformed Agrobacterium cells were co-infiltrated into tobacco leaves. Luciferase activity was measured using D-luciferin potassium salt as a substrate, with the UVP ChemStudio imaging system (Analytik Jena, Germany). 

# Detection of IPP, DMAPP, GPP, FPP and GGPP contents

Standards of IPP (I0503), DMAPP (D4287), GPP (G6772), FPP (F6892), GGPP (G6025) were purchased from Sigma Aldrich Company. Fresh latex samples were frozen rapidly and ground into powder in liquid nitrogen. For each replicate, 0.1 g of the 

sample was weighted, homogenized with 2 mL of $70 \%$ methanol and 4 M ammonia solution, and extracted with an ultrasonic bath. After centrifugation, the supernatant was collected, dried under nitrogen and then re-dissolved in methanol. The concentration of extracts from latex was determined by ultra-performance liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry (UPLC-MSMS, Waters, USA). UPLC-MSMS was performed using an Acquity UPLC system coupled to an AB SCIEX 4000 with an electrospray ionization (ESI) source. Chromatographic separation was achieved using a BEH $1 . 8 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ , $2 . 1 \times 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m m }$ column. The mobile phases were (A) $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ ammonium acetate in water (pH 10.5) and (B) $0 . 1 \%$ ammonia in water $( 2 0 \%$ )/acetonitrile $( 8 0 \% )$ . The mobile phase eluded at 0.3 mL/min beginning at $9 5 \%$ solvent A and $5 \%$ solvent B, increasing to $9 5 \%$ solvent B from 0 to 3 min. From 3 to 7 min, $9 5 \%$ solvent A and $5 \%$ solvent B were applied. The column temperature was $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . All tests were performed with three biological replicates, and each sample was tested in triplicate. 

# RNA sequencing and quantitative RT-PCR

Total RNA was extracted from latex samples using TRNzol Universal Reagent (TIANGEN Biotech Co., Ltd., Beijing, China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. RNA quality was assessed with the INFINTE 200 PRO (TECAN, Switzerland) and Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies, CA, USA). mRNA libraries were prepared using the NEBNext Ultra RNA Library Prep Kit and sequenced on the Illumina NovaSeqTM 6000 platform. Sequencing data were analysed by HISAT2- Stringtie pipeline (Pertea et al., 2016), and gene expression levels were normalized as TPM (transcripts per kilobase million). The differentially expressed genes were identified by ‘DESeq2’ with an absolute value of $\log _ { 2 }$ (fold change) $\geq 1$ and padj $< 0 . 0 5$ . Gene expression was visualized using the R package ‘ggplot2’ (Wickham, 2016). The cDNA sample from different tissues was synthesized using the HiScript III 1st Strand cDNA Synthesis Kit (with gDNA wiper, R312, Vazyme, Nanjing, China). Then, qRT-PCR was conducted on the qTOWER3 G (Analytik Jena, Germany). 

# Proteome sequencing

The total protein was extracted according to the previously reported method with slight modifications (Xie et al., 2022). Briefly, latex samples were frozen and ground into powder in liquid nitrogen and then extracted with lysis buffer (0.7 M sucrose, 0.5 M Tris, 30 mM HCl, 50 mM DTT, 0.1 M KCl, 2 mM EDTA and $1 \%$ PMSF) and Tris-saturated phenol. The upper phenol phase was collected and mixed with five volumes of methanol containing 0.1 M ammonium acetate and precipitated overnight. After centrifugation, the supernatant was discarded, and the precipitated proteins were washed twice with 0.1 M ammonium acetate and ice-cold acetone solutions, then dissolved in 8 M urea solution. Proteins were digested with trypsin at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ overnight after reduction with DTT and alkylation with iodoacetamide. Peptide mixtures were detected with a nanoElute UHPLC (Bruker, USA) coupled to a timsTOF Pro2 mass spectrometer (Bruker, USA). The raw data were searched against the rubber dandelion protein database using DIA-NN v1.8.1 software (Demichev et al., 2020). The protein intensity was quantified and normalized by MaxLFQ (Cox et al., 2014). Differentially expressed proteins between WT and Tkcpt1/2 mutants were determined with $\log _ { 2 }$ (fold change) $\geq 1 . 5$ or ${ \le } 0 . 6 6 7$ , and $P _ { \mathrm { a d j } } < 0 . 0 5$ . 

# Metabolome profiling

For metabolomic analysis, 50 mg of lyophilized latex sample was mixed with $6 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of $70 \%$ methanol aqueous solution, then vortexed for 15 min and sonicated for additional 10 min. After centrifugation, the ethanol extracts were analysed by UPLC-MSMS system (ExionLCTM AD, SCIEX, USA). Mobile phase A was water with $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid, and mobile phase B was acetonitrile with $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid. Injections $( 2 ~ \mu \ L )$ were performed on a symmetry C18 trap column (Agilent SB-C18 $1 . 8 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ , $2 . 1 \ \mathrm { m m } \times 1 0 0 \ \mathrm { m m } )$ with a flow rate of 0.35 mL/min. Qualitative analysis of metabolites was conducted using the self-built Metware database (MWDB, Wuhan, China), based on retention time, ion pair information and secondary spectrum data. Quantification of metabolites was performed using multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) analysis with triple quadrupole mass spectrometry (Chen et al., 2013). Differentially accumulated metabolites between WT and the Tkcpt1/2 were determined by applying criteria: VIP (Variable importance in projection) $> 1$ , Pvalue ${ \le } 0 . 0 1$ and absolute value of log2(fold change) $\geq 1$ . 

# Protein tertiary structure and protein–protein docking

The three-dimensional (3D) structures of TkCPT1 and TkCPT2 were obtained from the AlphaFold Protein Structure Database (https://alphafold.ebi.ac.uk/), under UniProt IDs M9PNN2 and M9PNM7, respectively. The protein–protein docking was performed on the AlphaFold3 (Abramson et al., 2024) webserver (https://golgi.sandbox.google.com/). Hydrogen bond distances among TkCPT1 and TkCPTL1, as well as TkCPT2 and TkCPTL1, were visualized using the PyMOL v4.6 software.
