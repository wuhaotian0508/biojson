# Summary

Prunus mume, an important ornamental woody plant in the Rosaceae family, contains many interspecific hybridizations. Purple colour is a breeding trait of aesthetic value for P. mume, but little is known about the origin and genetic architecture of this trait. Here we address these issues by producing a haplotype-resolved genome from an interspecific hybrid cultivar of P. mume (M) and P. cerasifera ‘Pissardii’ (C), named P. mume ‘Meiren’, followed by a detailed molecular characterization. The final length of the diploid genome is 499.47 Mb, with 250.66 Mb of haplotype M (HM) and 248.79 Mb of haplotype C (HC). Approximately $9 5 . 4 2 \%$ (476.61 Mb) of the phased assembly is further anchored to 16 homologous chromosomes. Based on the genomic variation, we identify a 1.8 Mb large-fragmented inversion (INV) on chromosome 1b of HC, which co-segregates with purple colour traits of ‘Meiren’ inherited from its purple C parent ‘Pissardii’. We find that a MYB transcription factor, PmmMYB10.5b, resides at the distal breakpoint of the INV, which displays consistent allele-specific expression (ASE). By directly binding to the promoter of anthocyanin synthetic alleles, PmmMYB10.5b serves as a co-activator to promote anthocyanin accumulation in ‘Meiren’ organs. Notably, the INV identified in ‘Meiren’ is generated from ‘Pissardii’ rather than P. cerasifera, which alters the promoter sequence of PmmMYB10.5b, activates its expression and results in the purple colour trait. Results from this study shed light on the evolutionary origin of purple colour in ‘Meiren’ and could potentially provide guidance on the genetic improvement of colour traits in ornamental woody plants. 

# Introduction

In nature, the majority of plants appear green in colour via their chlorophyll. However, there is also a group of plants that appear a different colour because of the green pigment covered up by some other pigments. A unique colour of great evolutionary and ornamental significance for plants, purple has increasingly attracted a considerable interest of plant biologists and evolutionary biologists (Dalrymple et al., 2020; Hempel et al., 2015; Hughes and Lev-Yadun, 2023; Sapir et al., 2021; Surmacz, 2023). The main component of purple-red pigments is water-soluble anthocyanin (KAY et al., 1981), whose accumulation is closely related to flavonoid-anthocyanin secondary metabolites (Jaakola, 2013; Lepiniec et al., 2006). Previous studies show that anthocyanin biosynthesis is regulated by transcription factors (TFs) R2R3-MYB, bHLH, and WD40 repeat protein complexes, known as MYB -bHLH- WDR (MBW) (Dubos et al., 2010; Jaakola, 2013; Koes et al., 2005; Xu et al., 2014). The Rosaceae family include a diverse range of horticultural and ornamental species, in which substantial variations in colour are observed for flowers, fruits and leaves. In Prunus genus, numerous gene loci have been characterized for the colours of leaves, fruits and flowers, such as leaf colour (Gr), fruit skin colour (Sc), and flower colour (Fc) (Shimada et al., 2000; Toshiya Yamamoto et al., 2001). Locating 

in the Gr interval, R2R3-MYB PpMYB10.4 was identified to regulate anthocyanin pigmentation in peach leaf (Zhou et al., 2014). A total of 6 MYB activators, that is, PpMYB10.1- PpMYB10.6, homologues of AtMYB114 (Heppel et al., 2013), were found to mediate anthocyanin accumulation in various organs, such as leaf, flower, peel, and fruit flesh in peach cultivars (Hui et al., 2016; Rahim et al., 2014; Ravaglia et al., 2013; Tuan et al., 2015; Zhou et al., 2014, 2018a,b). Also, PsMYB10s were identified to regulate the anthocyanin accumulation of red fruit peel and flesh (Fang et al., 2021; Fiol et al., 2021) in Prunus salicina. However, researches on colour trait mostly focus on the excavation and role of key genes in Prunus, with few studies revealing the origin of purple from the perspective of genetic evolution. 

Interspecific hybridization is a common breeding approach that drives the fusion of allelic gene information in species and accelerates evolution (Huang et al., 2023; Shin et al., 2022). Heterosis is primarily attributed to the complementary effects between the genomes of the parental lines, exhibiting stronger performance in some relative traits. With the presence of high-throughput sequencing, research on the molecular mechanisms of heterosis is gradually involved, and allele-specific expression (ASE) is considered one of the important mechanisms for explaining heterosis (Wang et al., 2022). On the contrary, 

chromosomal inversion (INV) is an important structural variation (SV) that can affect the phenotypic variation of plants (Dai et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2021). Introgressions from other species and SV in the genome are effective ways to increase genetic diversity and underlie important trait improvement and domestication (Przewieslik-Allen et al., 2021), yet the contribution of the large-fragmented INV and ASE to the purple trait is still unclear. 

Prunus cerasifera, is a woody plant of Prunus species, native to Southeast Europe and Western Asia (Zhebentyayeva et al., 2019), which displays great diversity in organ colour. From this species, a variety, called ‘Pissardii’ (purple-leaf plum), was domesticated with purple leaves and branches. Among interspecific hybrids between P. cerasifera ‘Pissardii’ and P. mume Pink Double Group, a purple-leaved cultivar P. mume ‘Meiren’ was produced, whose stems, leaves, flowers, and fruits all appear purple (Figure 1a). Because of its morphological uniqueness, plus its strong adaptability, ‘Meiren’ has been widely used as an ornamental tree in the gardens. However, the genetic mechanisms and genomic origin research of purple formation, and molecular breeding on coloured leaves of P. mume is limited. As one of its parents, P. mume is an important ornamental woody plants and was sequenced (Zhang et al., 2012), whose molecular genetics has been accumulated over the years (Zhang et al., 2018; Zhuo et al., 2021). Based on wild types and a tortuous cultivar, only the complete haploid reference genomes of P. mume have been assembled (Zhang et al., 2012; Zheng et al., 2022). All these enable ‘Meiren’ to serve as an excellent model system to study the genetic mechanisms for purple formation and its genomic origin in Prunus. 

Here, we assembled a haplotype-resolved genome of ‘Meiren’ to confirm the genomic collinearity between the two haplotypes. We identified a 1.8 Mb INV in the ‘Meiren’ haplotype C (HC) from ‘Pissardii’, which is co-segregated with the purple colour trait. An allelic MYB activator PmmMYB10.5b was detected at the breakpoint of the INV to promote anthocyanin synthesis. This INV occurred in ‘Pissardii’ rather than the P. cerasifera activates the expression of MYB10.5b. We combine genomics, transcriptome, and ASE multi-aspect analyses to reveal the genetic mechanisms underlying the purple formation and its genomic origin in ornamental woody plants P. mume. 

# Results

# The genome assembly and haplotype-chromosomes construction

We first generated a total of 24.22 Gb high-throughput sequencing reads, 96.17 Gb Hi-C reads and 24.07 Gb $( \sim 9 5 \times )$ PacBio Sequel II HiFi reads for ‘Meiren’ by DNBSEQ T1, BGISEQ-500, and PacBio Sequel II accurate circular consensus sequencing (CCS) platform (Table 1, Tables S1–S3). The estimated genome size of ‘Meiren’ was about 311.56 Mb per 1 C with a heterozygosity of $3 . 6 9 \%$ by the k-mer 17 spectrum analysis (Table 1, Table S4, Figure S1a). A high-quality haplotype-resolved genome was produced by the Hifiasm software combining Hi-C data (Figure 1b). The initial phased contigs assembly length was 549.67, 289.36 Mb of haplotype M (HM) and 260.31 Mb of haplotype C (HC) with contig N50 of 21.65 and 22.50 Mb (Table S5, Table 1). Filtering redundant sequences from the initial assembly, the final haplotype-resolved genome size was 499.47 Mb, with 250.66 Mb of HM and 248.79 Mb of HC 

(Table 1). Approximately $9 5 . 4 2 \%$ (476.61 Mb) of the phased contigs were further anchored to 16 homologous chromosomes using chromatin contact patterns in 3D-DNA (Table 1, Figure 1c, Table S6). Subsequently, a heatmap of chromosomal interactions exhibits good intra-chromosomal interaction patterns (Figure S1b). The BUSCO completeness assessment of genome assembly was $9 8 . 7 \%$ (Figure S1c). 

Transposable elements (TEs), protein-coding genes and no-coding RNAs of the ‘Meiren’ genome were annotated by integrating with multiple approaches (Tables S7–S10). ‘Meiren’ genome contains a total of 276.47 Mb $( 5 0 . 1 9 \% )$ ) TEs, among which $2 6 . 4 5 \%$ (145.66 Mb) are long terminal repeat (LTR) retrotransposons representing the predominant repeat sequences (Table S7). ‘Meiren’ exhibits a higher percentage of repeat sequences as compared to the reference genomes of P. mume (Zhang et al., 2012; Zheng et al., 2022). Thus, we conducted a comparative analysis of TE quantity between ‘Meiren’ and its parental line reference genome and examined the TE density in the flanking region of genes. The TE number in the overall mother line reference genome (P. salicina) was much more than the father line reference genome P. mume, especially in LTRs, confirming previous findings (Liu et al., 2020, Figure S2a). Similarly, ‘Meiren’ HC had more TEs than HM, but the difference was reduced due to the increase in the TEs number of HM. However, ‘Meiren’ HM showed a significantly higher number of TEs compared to the wild P. mume, particularly in the LTRs (Figure S2a). The overall TE densities near genes did not exhibit significant differences between HM and HC, while both of them were notably higher compared to the wild $P .$ . mume and P. salicina (Figure S2b). This suggests that the increase in repeat sequences in ‘Meiren’ may primarily be inherited from its female progenitor and accumulate during the process of cultivation and domestication. 

We then identified a total of 59 980 protein-coding genes (30 248 for HM and 29 732 for HC) with completeness estimated to be $9 8 . 4 \%$ by BUSCO (Figure S1c), only $5 . 1 1 \%$ has no function annotation (Table 1, Table S9). The quality of diploid genome assembly and annotation was confirmed by using multiple genomic evaluation approaches, including BUSCO, LAI (LTR assembly index), NGS and Iso-seq reads mapping and Merqury assessment (Tables S11–S13, Figure S1c). Collectively, these results indicate that our haplotype genome assembly is of good quality and provides a high-quality reference genomic resource for the Prunus genus. 

To investigate the evolutionary position and chromosomal rearrangement of ‘Meiren’, we constructed a phylogenetic tree of 15 species from 225 conserved single-copy genes (Table S14, Figure S2c,d). The ‘Meiren’ HM had a cluster with wild P. mume and its cultivars, while the HC was clustered with the P. salicina, suggesting that ‘Meiren’ HM originates from P. mume (named ‘Meiren’ M) and ‘Meiren’ HC from plum (named ‘Meiren’ C). No evidence for exchange between HM and HC was found in any of the chromosomes by a general alignment and collinearity analysis among 16 chromosomes of ‘Meiren’ and reference genomes of P. armeniaca, P. mume, and P. salicina (Figure S3a). Moreover, by analysing the $K _ { S }$ ratio of the shared orthologous genes between ‘Meiren’ and its parental line reference genomes, we found that the recombination rate of ‘Meiren’ was only $0 . 6 5 \%$ (Figure S3b, Table S15), indicating little rearrangement in the genome of Mei. Taken together, ‘Meiren’ is confirmed to be an $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ hybrid without any rearrangements, primarily propagated through asexual reproduction.

# A 1.8 Mb chromosomal inversion (INV) is identified in ‘Meiren’ HC

Using McscanX, we detected a total of 20 070 syntenic gene pairs from syntenic blocks in HM and HC, accounting for $6 8 \%$ of 59 980 genes (Figure 1c, Figure S4). This shows a moderate and consistent syntenic relationship between the two haplotypes. We also found an ordered and tight synteny of ‘Meiren’ and its parental lines genome (Figure S5). Notably, a prominent 

chromosomal INV was observed at the central region of chromosome1 (Chr1) between the two haplotypes (Figure 2a). To confirm whether INV occurred and where it was located, we further examined the synteny between ‘Meiren’ and its parental line reference genomes, respectively. The INV presented in the Chr1b between ‘Meiren’ HC and P. mume var. tortuosa, but no INV was detected in ‘Meiren’ HM and P. mume var. tortuosa (Figure 2b). This conclusion was supported by the collinearity relationship between ‘Meiren’ and P. salicina (Figure 2c). These results suggest that the INV present in the Chr1b is postulated to occur in the ‘Meiren’ HC. 

Then, structural variations (SVs) and sequence variations between two haplotypes of genome were investigated (Table S16). A total of 155 INVs were identified, with the largest fragment (INV1023) located on Chr1b, approximately 1.8 Mb in length (Figure 2d, Table S17), which was consistent with the collinearity analysis (Figure 2a). Furthermore, we performed a micro-synteny analysis of ${ \sim } 3 . 0$ Mb sequences on the INV1023 and its flanking regions to display the INV and distribution of genes in the INV (Figure 2e). To further confirm the accuracy and location of the INV, we re-mapped the ‘Meiren’ HiFi reads and ‘Pissardii’ resequencing reads to the ‘Meiren’ HC. The fractional HiFi reads can span the proximal and distal breakpoint, while the resequencing reads from ‘Pissardii’ can completely map to HC across the breakpoints (Figure 2f,g). This observation indicates that the INV present in the ‘Pissardii’ is inherited and maintained in ‘Meiren’ through hybridization. However, the reads from green-leaved P. mume and P. salicina cultivars are unable to span the breakpoints, confirming the absence of INV (Figure 2f,g). Ultimately, the interval of INV1023 was estimated as 19 029 244–20 808 689 bp (Table S17). We also detected 3 232 487 SNPs, 274 045 insertions and 256 166 deletions in two haplotypes (Table S16), which may potentially contribute to trait differences in P. mume. In summary, we identified a large-fragmented INV on Chr1b of the ‘Meiren’ HC and its maternal parent ‘Pissardii’, which may have a significant impact on the growth and development of ‘Meiren’.

# Consistent allele-specific expression gene PmmMYB10.5b is the core activator for anthocyanin biosynthesis

Allele-specific expression (ASE), or imbalance between the expression levels of two parental alleles in the hybrid, has been regarded as being the mechanism of phenotypic transmission (Gaur et al., 2013; Shao et al., 2019). Based on the haplotype genome, we focused on the allelic characteristics of ‘Meiren’, and first identified 21 656 pairs of 42 985 alleles (21 571 of HM and 21 414 of HC) of all 59 980 annotated genes (Table 1). For genetic sequence variations, ‘Meiern’ genome has a high sequence identity between the two haplotypes $( 9 8 . 0 \% )$ and coding sequence of most alleles $( 9 3 . 4 \% )$ (Figure S9a,b). We further estimated the selection pressure on allelic gene pairs by calculating the Ka/Ks ratio. The majority $( 9 4 . 4 \% )$ of allelic genes had a Ka/Ks ratio less than 1, indicating stronger purification selection and the removal of deleterious mutations (Figure S9c). At least one nonsynonymous substitution sites were observed in $9 5 . 7 \%$ of allelic genes, respectively (Figure S9d), which may reflect the structural and functional differences of alleles. 

Then, we investigated allelic imbalance stemming from ASE with no specific parents by transcriptome analysis of 5 leaf and 8 fruit development stages (Figure S10, Tables S21 and S22). Principal component analysis (PCA) showed that the leaf or fruit transcriptomes were mainly separated by gene expressions at different developmental stages and secondarily by genome-wide alleles (Figure 4a, Figure S11). The number of HC-biased ASE genes (ASEGs) was more than HM-biased ASEGs across all leaf and fruit developmental stages, showing a slight imbalance in ASE (Figure S12). We primarily analysed the consistent ASEGs that are expressed to be biased towards one allele at all stages and inconsistent ASEGs expressed to be biased towards some but not all stages in the leaf and fruit (Figure 4b,c, Figure S13). A total of 1616 consistent ASEGs were expressed biased towards one allele towards all stages, of which contained 861 HC-biased ASEGs and 755 HM-biased ASEGs in leaf (Figure 4d). These consistent ASEGs are enriched to multiple metabolism and biosynthesis processes, including flavonoids and anthocyanin biosynthesis pathways (Figure S14). Except for consistent ASEs, we only found 48 and 5 inconsistent ASEGs that expressed bias towards some but not all stages in the leaf and fruit development, respectively (Figure 4c, Figure S13b). These results suggest that no strong imbalance occurs in leaf and fruit at the transcriptome level. 

We tested whether anthocyanin biosynthetic genes (ABGs) in ‘Meiren’ displayed ASE. Among ASE genes, little ABGs exhibited ASE in the leaves and fruits, and only the PmmCHS2 and PmmFLS3 showed a consistent ASE (Figure S15). However, both allelic ABGs from two haplotypes exhibited varying and high expression levels in response to the change of leaves and fruits flesh colour (Figures S16a and S17). They, especially PmmANS and PmmUF3GT, were also expressed differentially in green and purple hybrid individuals (Figure S16b). All this suggests that anthocyanin biosynthesis in ‘Meiren’ is a result of expression accumulation of ABGs from the two haplotypes, rather than a single ASEG from mother ‘Pissardii’. 

We further investigated the expression pattern and evolutionary relationship of the 6 MYBs annotated in the INV region (Figure 2e). A total of 438 PmmMYB genes were identified in ‘Meiren’ genome (Figure S18), containing 74 genes of flavonoid biosynthesis clades S4, S5, S6, and S15 (Dubos et al., 2010). Further phylogenetic analysis showed that the MYB cluster from the INV was located in the S6 clade, along with AtMYB113 and MYB10s in Prunus (Figure 4e). In addition, we found a total of 17 PmmMYB10s located in Chr1 and chromosome 4 (Chr4), named PmmMYB10.1-10.6 according to sequence similarity (Figure 4f). MRMv1.1_01bG02178 located at the distal breakpoint of INV1023 was named as PmmMYB10.5b. Expression analysis showed that only the allelic PmmMYB10.5b at the distal breakpoint was highly expressed during leaf and fruit development, showing consistent ASE (Figure 4g–i). PmmMYB10.5b also showed consistent ASE in the purple and green individuals of population and their parents ‘Meiren’ and ‘Xiangxue Gongfen’ (Figure 4j). We postulate that PmmMYB10.5b might activate the anthocyanin biosynthesis in ‘Meiren’ organs, and cause dominance effects on the purple colour traits it controls.

# PmmMYB10.5b physically interacts with PmbHLH3 to active anthocyanin biosynthesis

In order to further explore the biological function of PmmMYB10.5b located in the breakpoint of INV, we acquired the coding sequences of the allelic gene PmmMYB10.5. PmmMYB10.5 alleles both encode a 732 bp coding sequence and translate a R2R3-MYB protein with 244 amino acid, maintaining a high sequence identity (Figure S22). Except for the conservative R2 and R3 domain, PmmMYB10.5 contains the M1 motif (Figure S22) that is responsible for anthocyanin biosynthesis in plants (Gao et al., 2021; LaFountain and Yuan, 2021). Moreover, PmmMYB10.5 is confirmed to be a nuclear localization protein (Figure 5a). In order to clarify the allelic functional difference, we performed transient overexpression for two PmmMYB10.5 alleles alone or co-infiltration with PmmbHLH3 in tobacco leaves. The results indicated that both PmmMYB10.5a and PmmMYB10.5b overexpressed with PmmbHLH3 induced substantial anthocyanins accumulation in leaves compared to the empty vector (Figure 5b,c, Figure S23b,c). This suggests that there is no functional mutation in two alleles. Then, we investigated the regulatory role of PmmMYB10.5b by heterologous expression in Arabidopsis. Overexpressed PmmMYB10.5b (OE-35S: PmmMYB10.5b) transgenic Arabidopsis synthesized many anthocyanins, displaying stronger purple colours compared with wild-type (WT) in seed coat, seedings, leaves and inflorescence (Figure S23d,e). 

In addition, to further validate the role of PmmMYB10.5b in purple colour in P. mume, we have for the first time constructed an efficient genetic transformation system of hairy roots in P. mume. Compared with the vector control and wild-type roots of P. mume, the overexpressed PmmMYB10.5b (OE-PmmMYB10.5b) lines accumulated extensive red pigments in callus and hairy roots, with a pronounced increase in anthocyanin content leading to a red colour trait (Figure 5d,e). The expressions of PmmMYB10.5b evidently increased across all the six overexpressed lines (Figure 5f). Overexpression of PmmMYB10.5b further led to the upregulation of the mRNA levels of ABGs and PmmbHLH3 (Figure 5g). To further investigate whether PmmMYB10.5b participates in the regulation of anthocyanin synthesis as the protein complexes, we conducted 

yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) assays. The Y2H results show that PmmMYB10.5b physically interacts with PmmbHLH3 and itself (Figure 6a). These results suggest that PmmMYB10.5b, as a core activator, composes the PmmMYB10.5b-PmmbHLH3 protein complex and promotes the increase of expression of ABGs, particularly the downstream genes (PmmANS and PmmUF3GT ) that facilitates the conversion of leucoanthocyanins to stable anthocyanins. 

The alleles of ABGs from HM and HC were all expressed in the leaf and fruit (Figures S16 and S17). It was inferred that PmmMYB10.5b may simultaneously activate the expression of two alleles. To confirm whether PmmMYB10.5b actives allelic ABGs expression, the promoter around 2 kb of ABGs was isolated and analysed using the New PLACE. Plenty of MYB-binding motifs were predicted in ABGs promoter, including MYBCORE(CNGTTR), MYB1AT(WAACCA) and MYBPZM(MACCWAMC) (Figure 6b). Then, we performed a dual-luciferase assay by transient coinfiltration PmmMYB10.5b and promoters in tobacco leaves (Figure 6c). The dual luciferase activities were visibly stronger in co-infiltrating PmmMYB10.5b with the promoter of ABGs alleles likely PmmUF3GT, PmmF30 H, and PmmANS compared with the empty (Figure 6d, Figure S24). The EMSA results also confirmed that PmmMYB10.5b can bind to the motifs like MYBCORE and MYB1AT of the promoter of allelic ABGs in vitro (Figure 6e, Figure S25). These results indicate that PmmMYB10.5b is able to directly bind to promoters of ABGs alleles, like PmmUF3GT and PmmF30 H, which, by positively regulating gene expression, causes anthocyanin accumulations. 

In this study, other two R2R3-MYB repressors clustered at the S4 clade of flavonoid biosynthesis probably participate in anthocyanin biosynthesis (Figure 4e). Sequence analysis indicates that PmmMYBR1/2 contain motifs C1 and C2 (Figure S23) that are the common features of MYB repressors responsible for regulating anthocyanin biosynthesis in plants (Gao et al., 2021; LaFountain and Yuan, 2021). These two proteins are all confirmed to be nuclear localization proteins (Figure S23a). To test their functions, we transiently overexpressed PmmMYBR1 or PmmMYBR2 along with PmmMYB10.5b and PmmbHLH3 in tobacco and also overexpressed both PmmMYBR1 and PmmMYBR2 with PmmMYB10.5b and PmmbHLH3. The results indicated that both two proteins can decrease anthocyanin accumulation (Figure 5b,c). The dual luciferase analysis further confirms their significant inhibitory activity (Figure 6f). We also found that both PmmMYBR1 and PmmMYBR2 can interact with PmmbHLH3, and PmmMYBR1 also interacts with PmmMYB10.5b (Figure 6a). These results show that PmmMYBR1 and PmmMYBR2 as repressors may compete the PmmbHLH3 with PmmMYB10.5b to negatively regulate anthocyanin biosynthesis.

# The INV, originating from ‘Pissardii’ rather than P. cerasifera, changes the promoter of PmmMYB10.5b and leads to its expression activation

Eventually, to further confirm the origin and evolutionary relationship of the INV, we selected 7 species (P. mume ‘Dan Fenghou’, P. persica, P. armeniaca, P. sibirica, P. dulcis, P. cerasifera, and P. simonii) with green leaves of Prunus plants and aligned their resequencing data with the ‘Meiren’ HC (Table S23). The results showed that reads from all 7 species could not span the proximal and distal breakpoints of the INV, confirming no INV present in them (Figure S26a,b). Notably, we found that the $P .$ . cerasifera with green leaves also does not exhibit the INV, from which we postulate that the INV may occur during the formation of ‘Pissardii’. Therefore, we performed a simple de novo assembly of the resequencing data of P. cerasifera and obtained the long contigs near the INV. Next, we aligned the P. cerasifera contigs with ‘Meiren’ HC subgenome to analyse the position of INV and how it changed the promoter of MYB10.5 gene from P. cerasifera (PC-g) to P. cerasifera ‘Pissardii’ and P. mume ‘Meiren’ (PMM-p, Figure 7a,b). A diagram of the INV model showed the inverted fragment beginning at promoter region of PcMYB10.5 (Figure 7a, b1/b2), and ended with b3/4 of PC-g before the inversion. In PMM-p, the positions of b2 and b3 have reversed, and the inverted segment extended from the point b1/b3 to b2/b4 (Figure 7a). In the inverted allele, the corresponding PmmMYB10.5b promoter sequence is located after the b4 point. Through precise alignment of the contigs of PC-g with PMM-p, we confirmed that the changed sequence of MYB10.5 promoter was located at 137 bp upstream of the ATG initiation codon (Figure 7a,b). 

In addition, we examined the transcription levels of PcMYB10s in leaves of P. cerasifera in the permanently green cultivar and purple-leaved ‘Pissardii’. We find that only the PcMYB10.5 is highly expressed in purple-leaved ‘Pissardii’ but not expressed in green-leaved P. cerasifera (Figure S26c,d). These results indicated that the INV does not exist in P. cerasifera, but it is generated in purple-leaved ‘Pissardii’, which activates the MYB10.5 gene expression in ‘Pissardii’ and ‘Meiren’ (Figure S26d), due to a strong promoter activity (Figure 6g). 

To further investigate how the INV activates gene expression, we collected leaf samples of the P. ceasifera with green leaves to extract genomic DNA, and cloned $\sim 2 0 0 0$ bp fragment of the PcMYB10.5 promoter. The promoter sequence alignment showed that the non-inverted fragments were highly similar, whereas the inverted regions exhibited significant differences (Figure 7c). Next, we conducted a detailed comparison of the activity differences between the two promoters. Full-length and truncated different lengths of fragments were used to activate LUC reporter gene (Figure 7d). The full-length(P1) activity of 

proPmmMYB10.5b was greatly stronger than that of the proPcMYB10.5 (Figure 7e,f). As the fragment length decreased, the activity differences between proPmmMYB10.5b and proPc-MYB10.5 gradually declined. In proPmmMYB10.5b, the levels of LUC activity of P1 and P2 were higher than the basal promoter fragments P3 and P4, especially the P1 (Figure 7e,f), suggesting that ▵P1-P2 fragment in proPmmMYB10.5b is important for promoter with a strong activation. There may be enhancers in the ▵P1-P2 region that are involved in PmmMYB10.5b regulation. These results indicate that the significant increase in promoter activity following the INV directly resulted in the activation of PmmMYB10.5b expression. 

In summary, the INV occurred and changed the promoter of MYB10.5 gene, resulting in its expression activation in ‘Pissardii’. The genetic information of ‘Pissardii’ is flowed into ‘Meiren’ due to artificial hybridization. The INV and its associated expression of PmmMYB10.5b have a lot to do with the purple colour preserved in ‘Meiren’. PmmMYB10.5b can simultaneously bind to the promoter of ABGs alleles both from ‘Meiren’ HM and HC, promoting the extensive accumulation of anthocyanins in organs (Figure 8). The INV most likely has specificity in ‘Meiren’ and ‘Pissardii’, it could be applied as a specific molecular marker of purple colour in the genus Prunus in general. 

# Discussion

ASE, or imbalance between the expression levels of two parental alleles in the hybrid, has been suggested as a mechanism of heterosis and phenotypic transmission (Shao et al., 2019). The P. mume ‘Meiren’, belonging to the Meiren Group of P. mume (Li et al., 2022), was hybridized with P. mume Pink Double Group and P. cerasifera ‘Pissardii’, and a large number of sequences variations and heterosis have maintained during the clonal propagation. However, research on the heterosis of P. mume plants is still poorly understood. In this study, we primarily analyse the ASE patterns by the haplotype-resolved genome assembled with the Hifisam (Cheng et al., 2021, 2022). We categorize ASE into two types: consistent ASE and inconsistent ASE (Shao et al., 2019). The ASEGs in ‘Meiren’ mainly belong to the consistent ASE pattern, which participates in multiple biological process regulation, especially the anthocyanin synthesis. The key regulatory allele PmmMYB10.5, located in the INV, is specifically expressed in ‘Meiren’ organs and maintains a consistent ASEG, which may exert dominance effects on the purple colour traits that it control (Shao et al., 2019). 

The regulatory mechanisms and genes involved in anthocyanin synthesis are relatively conserved among plants (Koes et al., 2005; Xu et al., 2014). In the Rosaceae family, many S6 R2R3-MYB TFs have served as regulators of anthocyanin synthesis, directly binding to the promoters of ABGs to activate their expression 

(Chagne et al., 2007; Shimada et al., 2000; Toshiya Yamamoto et al., 2001). According to the homologous evolution, ancient MYB10 genes have been duplicated multiple copies in the Rosaceae family before species divergence. These duplications are classified into two major branches: MYB I and MYB II (Zhou 

et al., 2014). With continuous hybridization and domestication, the number of MYB10 genes varies in different species. In peach, different MYB10 homologous genes regulate anthocyanin accumulation in different organs (Hui et al., 2016; Rahim et al., 2014; Ravaglia et al., 2013; Zhou et al., 2014). However, in ‘Meiren’, only the allelic PmmMYB10.5b is highly expressed and promotes anthocyanin biosynthesis. In our work, we first time confirmed the function of R2R3-MYB PmmMYB10.5b in P. mume by hairy roots transformation. Notably, the leaf colour of ‘Meiren’ displays distinct seasonal variations and increased instability compared to its mother ‘Pissardii’. After hybridization, alterations have occurred in the response of the anthocyanin pathway to environmental stimuli. Transcriptome analysis has identified several genes associated with light and temperature, such as PmmHY5 and the PmmBBX family (An et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2024), which may play a role in regulating leaf colour changes in response to environmental cues. Moreover, extreme environmental factors like high temperatures or low light not only impact anthocyanin synthesis but can also trigger anthocyanin degradation (Rowan et al., 2009). During the development of plant organs, anthocyanins, acting as antioxidants, are degraded by some enzymes like Class III peroxidase, polyphenol oxidases, and $\beta \cdot$ -glucosidases for reducing the escape of hydrogen peroxide into vacuoles (Zhao et al., 2021; Zipor et al., 2015). Further exploration and research are needed to uncover the underlying causes for the changes in leaf colouration in ‘Meiren’. 

As a type of SVs associated with plant traits and fitness (Alonge et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2012; Shen et al., 2017), INV is thought to increase genetic diversity as a fuel for cultivar improvement and domestication (Dai et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2021). In horticultural plants, purple colour is a key agronomic quality and ornamental trait. Previous studies have revealed that variations occurring in MYB genes lead to differences in anthocyanin biosynthesis and the emergence of novel mutant varieties (Chen et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2019). Numerous SNPs and Indels have been identified in the MdMYB1, which has been shown to be responsible for the red phenotype in apples (Zhang et al., 2019). CaMYB114 in tea plants has also been found to exhibit a similar insertion, affecting leaf colouration (Chen et al., 2023). However, in this study, we discovered a novel chromosomal SV, the 1.8 Mb large-fragmented INV, affecting the purple colour in the P. mume. The distal breakpoint of the INV fell at a distance of 137 bp upstream of the ATG initiation codon of allele PmmMYB10.5b, which completely altered the promoter sequence of PmmMYB10.5b. The LUC activity indicated that the promoter activation of the inverted PmmMYB10.5b is significantly enhanced, leading to its expression. 

In our work, we further identified the potential cis-regulatory elements present in two promoters to understand the differences in promoter activity (Figure S27). The number of cis-elements varied across different fragments, of which the P4 and P3 

fragments, containing substantial TATA box and CAAT-box, are the basal promoter fragments. Our focus was on the ▵P1-P2 region of PmmMYB10.5b, where we observed the most significant difference in activity. Interestingly, this region coincides with a TIR transposon containing numerous hormoneresponsive elements and MYB binding site (Figure S27, Figure 7c). This suggests that the differences in transcriptional regulatory of the promoter may be induced by the presence of enhancers at the distal to the PmmMYB10.5b. Similar events of gene expression activation caused by enhancer elements have also been reported in maize (Sidorenko et al., 2000). The allelic $P$ genes in maize involved in flavonoid biosynthesis were activated or silenced by the enhancer repeats from transposable elements Ac/Ds (Sharma et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2011). In maize, alternative transposition can induce chromosomal rearrangement events, including inversions, deletions, inverted duplications, and translocations, which directly impact gene transcription regulation (Sharma et al., 2021; Sharma and Peterson, 2023; Yu et al., 2011). In our study, we also identified an 89 bp LTR insertion in the proximal breakpoint of INV (Figure S26a). This similar transposition event, inducing genome rearrangements and gene expression, may also exist in ‘Pissardii’ and its offspring ‘Meiren’. Besides, a strong gene expression activation motif CTRMCAMV35S (16 CT repeats) was discovered at ▵P2-P3 (Figure 7c). Previous studies have confirmed that the CT-rich motif identified in the S1 region of the cauliflower mosaic virus 35SRNA can enhance gene translation (Pauli et al., 2004). However, further research will be required to determine the reasons for the promoter activity and gene expression induced by inversion. 

# Conclusion

In summary, we first generated a high-quality haplotype-resolved genome of the ornamental woody plants P. mume ‘Meiren’. A novel large-scale genomic INV variation, occurred in the haplotype C from female parent P. cerasifera ‘Pissardii’, activating MYB10.5 gene expression, resulting in the formation of purple trait. With the new genomic resources, population genetics and ASE analyses, we explored the genomic origin of the purple colour and its underlying genetic mechanisms. Our study also provides a high-quality reference genomic resource and analytical tool for investigating the mechanisms of heterosis in the Prunus genus and beyond.

# Genome and transcriptome sequencing

The genomic DNA was extracted from the leaves using the cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) method. To assist in genome assembly, the qualified young leaves DNA was used to construct DNBSEQ T1 short reads libraries and Hi-C libraries and sequencing as Method S1 described. Pacbio-CCS sequencing libraries were prepared from young leaves DNA using 

SMRTbell Template Prep Kit v.1.0 and sequenced on the PacBio Sequel II System as described in Method S2. For thirdgeneration full-length transcriptome sequencing (Iso-seq), cDNA SMRTbell libraries were prepared with mixed leaves, stems and flowers RNA and sequenced on a PacBio Sequel system as Method S2 described. For RNA-seq, 1 lg of total RNA was prepared for library construction following TruSeqTM RNA sample preparation Kit from Illumina (San Diego, CA). Then, paired-end libraries were sequenced by Illumina NovaSeq 6000 with an insert size of 200–300 bp and ~6 Gb length of 150 bp paired-end data for each sample were generated (Shanghai BIOZERON Co., Ltd, No. 1180 Xingxian Road, Shanghai, China). 

# Genome assembly and annotation

Before assembling the haplotype-resolved genome, we estimated the genome size and heterozygosity using K-mer analysis and assembled a preliminary haploid genome with the short reads using SOAPdenovo (Xie et al., 2014). For haplotype-resolved assembly, we first used CCS to integrate and convert multiple subreads from the PacBio Sequel II platform and obtained 23.27 Gb of HiFi reads. Next, we selected the Hi-C integration mode of Hifiasm (0.16.1- r375) (Cheng et al., 2021, 2022) to assemble phased contigs as Method S3 described (Figure 1b). For chromosomal-level assembly, we employed 3D-DNA and mapped Hi-C short reads to assembly graphs to assist with the haplotype assembly process. Finally, the haplotype-resolved genome was resolved after manual correction with Juicebox (Robinson et al., 2018). 

The homologous search and the ab initio prediction strategies (as Method S3 described) were used to annotate transposable elements (TEs) by EDTA v1.9.4 (Ou et al., 2019) and RepeatMasker v.4.0.7 (Tarailo-Graovac and Chen, 2009). TEs were then combined from these two runs to obtain the final TE annotation. For the protein-coding genes annotation, we first screened the TEs and used Exonerate (Slater and Birney, 2005), Augustus (Stanke et al., 2006), GlimmerHMM (Majoros et al., 2004), GeneMark_ES (Borodovsky and Lomsadze, 2011) and minimap2 (Li, 2018) to predict protein-coding genes by homology-based prediction, ab initio gene prediction and Iso-seq transcriptomebased prediction. We aligned the gene set to SwissProt (http://www.UniProt.org/), TrEMBL (http://www.UniProt.org/), KEGG (http://www.genome.jp/kegg/), InterPro (https://www.ebi. ac.uk/interpro/), Nr (Non-Redundant Protein Sequence Database, https://ftp.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/blast/db/FASTA/) databases to perform functional annotation. Finally, the following methods were used to assess the quality and completeness of genome: genome comparation by BUSCO v5 (Simao et al., 2015) with the embryophyta_odb10 database, LTR Assembly Index (LAI) (Ou et al., 2018), Mergury (Rhie et al., 2020) and the mapping rates of the clean short reads and Iso-seq long reads with genome using BWA-MEM (Li, 2013) and minimap2, respectively. 

# Chromosome rearrangement analysis

‘Meiren’ haplotype M (HM, P. mume ‘Meiren’ M), haplotype C (HC, P. mume ‘Meiren’ C), and 14 other plant species (Table S14) were selected for gene families clustering using OrthoFinder (Emms and Kelly, 2015) based on diamond (Buchfink et al., 2015) alignment. The protein sequences of single-copy gene were aligned using MUSCLE v3.8.31 (Edgar, 2004) and used for phylogenetic analysis by RaxML (Stamatakis, 2014) with the nucleotide substitution model set as ‘GTR $^ +$ gamma’. 

In order to confirm the origin of the two haplotypes, we first used MUMmer (Marcais et al., 2018) to perform collinearity analysis 

between the 16 chromosomes of ‘Meiren’ and the reference genomes of P. mume (Zhang et al., 2012), as well as closely related species such as P. salicna (Liu et al., 2020) and P. armeniaca (Jiang et al., 2019), determining if there were any rearrangement on chromosome segments during the hybridization process. Then, we referred to the Populus trichocarpa genome (An et al., 2022) to detect whether rearrangements occurred between the two haplotypes of genome by estimating the Ks value. 

# Collinearity analysis and variation detection

We searched for collinear blocks between ‘Meiren’ HM and HC, as well as collinear blocks between P. mume ‘Meiren’ and P. mume var tortuosa (Zheng et al., 2022), or P. salicina ‘Sanyueli’ (Liu et al., 2020) genomes using the MCScanX- JCVI package (Wang et al., 2012). Genome-wide structural (inversions, INV; translocations, TRANS; duplications, DUP) and sequences variations (insertions, INS; deletions, DEL; SNPs) between ‘Meiren’ HM and HC as well as between P. mume ‘Meiren’ and P. mume var tortuosa or P. salicina ‘Sanyueli’ were aligned and detected using Nucmer (Marcais et al., 2018) and SyRI (Goel et al., 2019) software, respectively. For the biggest INV at Chr1b of ‘Meiren’ HC, we further conducted a total of ~3 Mb collinearity search for the variant region as well as up and downstream of breakpoints, demonstrating the collinearity relationship of genes in INV and its flanking regions. 

# The INV association analysis

To verify the association between INV and purple colour trait, we constructed an overall dataset involving our resequencing data of 100 hybrids from P. mume ‘Meiren’ $\times \ P .$ . mume ‘Xiangxue Gongfen’ $\mathsf { F } _ { 1 }$ population, as well as individuals of ‘Pissardii’ and recently published re-sequenced Prunus accessions (Table S23). The raw short reads of Prunus species including P. mume (Zhang et al., 2018), P. salicina, P. cerasifera (Huang et al., 2021), P. persica, P. armeniaca, P. sibirica, P. simonii, P. dulcis (Groppi et al., 2021) were download from the SRA database of NCBI (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/). Then, we used sequence-based alignment, QTL analysis and PCR detection methods described as Method S4 to search for the precise location of the INV and the association between INV and purple trait. 

# Allelic variations and ASE analysis

First, we identified the allelic genes between two haplotypes of ‘Meiren’ as Method S5. For allelic variations analysis, we applied the same method as described previously for the tea genome (Zhang et al., 2021). Briefly, the allelic sequence variations were analysed at the genome level and gene level based on the pairwise comparison of two haplotypes and allelic genes. 

For ASE analysis, the method in this study was referenced with the tea genome (Zhang et al., 2021). RNA-seq short reads from leaves and fruits were trimmed using the fastp program (Chen et al., 2018) and mapped against allele-aware annotated gene models using Bowtie (Langmead et al., 2009) with only the best alignment retained for each read. FPKM values were calculated using the RSEM program (Li and Dewey, 2011), which was implemented in the Trinity package (Haas et al., 2013). ASE was defined if the log fold change (FC) of FPKM values between two alleles was $^ { > 2 }$ with $\mathsf { F D R } < 0 . 0 5$ using DEseq2 (Love et al., 2014). Two ASE patterns were investigated in this study, including consistent ASE and inconsistent ASE according to the previous study (Shao et al., 2019). Other transcriptome data analysis was shown in Method S6. 

# Genetics transformation of hairy root and GUS activity in P. mume

The vector construction and genetic transformation of Arabidopsis and tobacco were described in Method S7. For hairy root transformation, the recombinant pSuper1300-PmmMYB10.5b-GFP plasmids were transferred into Agrobacterium rhizogenes strain K599 (WeiDi, Shanghai, China). Around 10 mL LB medium supplemented with $5 0 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ kanamycin (Kan) and $3 0 \mathrm { \ m g \ L } ^ { - 1 }$ streptomycin (Strep) was utilized to nurture the single colony until ${ \mathsf { O D } } _ { 6 0 0 }$ was 0.8–0.9. Following this, the overnight cultures were centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 10 min and resuspended in infiltration buffer composed of $1 0 \mathrm { \ m u \ M g C l _ { 2 } }$ , $1 5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { { M } }$ acetosyringone (AS), and 10 mM 2-(N-morpholino) ethanesulfonic acid (MES) with Ph 5.7. The bacterial suspension was then placed at room temperature in the dark for $3 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ . Then, a 1-mL syringe was used to inject the bacterial cultures into the stem round cotyledon node of 10-day P. mume seedings with 7–8 leaves. Hairy roots were observed after 20 days and validated by anthocyanins content and RT–qPCR (shown in Method S8 and S9, Table S24). 

The recombinant pBI121-proPmmMYB10.5b-GUS plasmids were transferred into EHA105 (WeiDi, Shanghai, China) for GUS activity analysis. 35S-GUS empty vector was used as the control. The same P. mume materials and methods above hairy root transformation were used, and the GUS activity was detected with cotyledon by GUS Stain Solution Kit (Biorigin, Beijing, China). 

# Yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) assay

For the Y2H assay, the recombinant plasmids of pGADT7-gene and pGBKT7-gene were transformed into yeast receptive states $\yen 1270$ (bait strain) and Y2H (prey strain, WeiDi, Shanghai, China), respectively. The $\yen 1270$ and Y2H cells were re-suspended in 50 ul ${ \mathsf { d d H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ and grown on SD-Leu and SD-Trp mediums at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 3 days. Then, the mating method was used for the yeast hybrid. The mating cells were grown on the SD-Trp-Leu and SD-Trp-Leu-His-Ade selective medium supplemented with AbA and X-a-Gal at $3 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ after 3 days for photography observation. 

# Luciferase complementation assay

All recombinant pGreenII 62-SK and pGreenII 0800-LUC plasmids were individually transformed into A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 (WeiDi, Shanghai, China) contained the pSoup helper plasmid, and incubated at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for 3 days. A single colony was cultured in the LB medium with $5 0 \mathrm { ~ m g ~ L } ^ { - 1 }$ Kan and rifampicin (Rif) overnight. Transformed agrobacteria cultures were centrifuged, and resuspended with equal volume in infiltration buffer $\cdot 1 0 \mathsf { m m }$ ${ \mathsf { M g C l } } _ { 2 } ,$ $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { { M } }$ AS and $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ MES; $\mathsf { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } = 0 . 8 )$ . The agrobacteria suspension was incubated at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ without shaking for $^ { 2 - 3 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ before infiltration. For transcription factor interaction, agrobacteria cultures containing the LUC reporter with proPmm and containing PmmMYB10.5b driven by the CaMV 35S promoter were mixed with a ratio of 1:1 (v/v). A mixture of LUC reporter with proPmm and SK empty vector was used as the control. Mixed cultures were injected into 4-week-old leaves of tobacco (N. benthamiana) seedlings. The LUC was activated by luciferin solution $0 . 1 \%$ Triton X-100, $0 . 3 2 ~ \mathrm { m g } ~ \mathrm { m L } ^ { - 1 }$ D-Luciferin Potassium Salt), and activities were observed by BERTHOLD NightOwl II LB 983 (Berthold Technologies, Baden-Wurttemberg, € Germany) after 2 days. For promoter activity assessment, the promoter-LUC vector and LUC vector were, respectively, injected 

into 4-week-old leaves of tobacco seedlings, and LUC activities were observed using the same method described above. LUC and REN values were detected using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay Kit (Vazyme, Nanjing, China) with the luminometer GloMax- Explorer System (Promega, Madison, WI, USA). The ratio of LUC to REN activities was used for the final quantification of relative LUC activity. Each experiment was independently repeated three times. 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA)

For protein induction, pET32a-PmmMYB10.5b-His recombinant plasmids were transformed into E. coil Rosetta-gami2 (DE3) and induced by 1 mM isopropyl- $\beta$ -d-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $4 \textrm { h }$ . The cells were collected by centrifugation at 12 000 rpm for 20 min. Then, the mycelium was resuspended by PBS buffer and collected. The target protein was purified using a His-tag Protein Purification Kit (Beyotime, Shanghai, China). For the EMSA binding reaction, the purified protein was mixed with biotin-labelled probes or competitive probes in the binding buffer using the EMSA Kit (Beyotime, Shanghai, China). The binding results were analysed by $6 \%$ non-denatured polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis, and detected by chemiluminescence method using the EMSA Kit.
