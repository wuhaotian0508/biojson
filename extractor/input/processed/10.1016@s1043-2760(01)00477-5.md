# Brassinosteroid signaling in plants

# Carsten Müssig and Thomas Altmann

In animals and humans, steroid hormones (SHs) regulate gene transcription via the binding of nuclear receptors. In addition, rapid nongenomic effects of steroids occur and appear to be mediated by plasma-membrane receptors. Plants also use steroids as signaling molecules. These brassinosteroids (BRs) show structural similarity to the SHs of vertebrates and insects. Plant mutants defective in brassinosteroid biosynthesis or perception exhibit dwarfism and reduced fertility, and reveal the need for BRs during growth and development. BR signaling in Arabidopsis thaliana and rice (Oryza sativa) – dicotyledonous and monocotyledonous models, respectively – is mediated by the receptor kinases BRI1 and OsBRI1. The extracellular domain of BRI1 perceives BRs and the signal is mediated via an intracellular kinase domain that autophosphorylates Ser and Thr residues and apparently has the potential to phosphorylate other substrates. BRI1 transduces steroid signals across the plasma membrane and mediates genomic effects.

Like animal and human steroid hormones (SHs), such as steroid sex hormones, vitamin D or the insect moulting hormone ecdysone, brassinosteroids (BRs) are cholestane derivatives (Fig. 1). Brassinolide1 (BL) is considered to be the endproduct of BR biosynthesis, because it shows the highest biological activity among BRs. In general, BRs such as BL with a 6,7-lactone function, possess higher biological activity than do 6-keto steroids. BRs lacking B-ring oxygen function show minor activity. The biological activities of intermediates correspond to their place in the biosynthetic pathway2,3. BRs elicit various physiological responses and are essential for male fertility and xylem differentiation4,5. Their growth-promoting effect results primarily from the stimulation of cell elongation and includes induction of the expression of genes encoding proteins such as xyloglucan endotransglycosylases6,7 (XETs), which are probably involved in cell-wall metabolism and loosening. Moreover, effects such as cell-wall space acidification, appear to contribute to BR-induced growth stimulation. RNA-synthesis inhibitors (e.g. actinomycin D) and protein-synthesis inhibitors (e.g. cycloheximide) interfere with BR-induced growth8. BR effects on cell division are less clear; however, the induction of CycD3 transcription by epi-brassinolide might represent a mechanism by which BRs can drive cell division9.

Steroid hormones trigger genomic and nongenomic effects in animals

A steroid effect that occurs within seconds to minutes that is not blocked by inhibitors of transcription or translation and that is mediated by steroids tethered to a membrane-impenetrable carrier protein is likely to be a nongenomic effect. Nongenomic actions of SHs in animals include ion-flux effects, neurotransmitter signaling, changes in secondmessenger levels and changes in protein-kinase activities10–12. In addition to functional evidence, membrane-binding sites for several steroids have been described, exposing binding features compatible with an involvement in rapid steroid signaling. However, direct evidence of signal transmission and the mediation of specific effects is scarce for any of the proteins. SHs can apparently act through nongenomic and genomic pathways simultaneously. Both pathways are required (e.g. for calcitriol signaling13) and might interact (e.g. via second messenger-related modulation of some steroid-induced transcriptional processes).

Genomic steroid actions are characterized principally by delayed responses (i.e. responses taking several minutes or even hours), because synthetic events that alter transcript or protein levels are required. Inhibitors of transcription or translation, such as actinomycin D and cycloheximide, should block genomic effects. Because of their lipophilic nature, SHs pass through the cell membrane by simple diffusion and bind to SH receptors (SHRs) within target cells. SHRs not bound to their ligands are associated with chaperones, keeping them in ligand-friendly conformations. Upon activation by the steroid ligand, SHRs move into the nucleus, bind (mainly as homodimers) to palindromic DNA sequences termed hormone-response elements and activate transcription by interaction with general transcription factors, transcription intermediary factors and other coactivators14,15. Some members of the nuclear receptor superfamily (which includes the SHRs) not only stimulate gene expression, but also repress transcription16–18. # BRI1 is the major brassinosteroid receptor in Arabidopsis thaliana

Attempts to identify the components of BR signal transduction in Arabidopsis thaliana using genetic techniques have resulted in the isolation of at least 25 allelic BR-insensitive mutants termed bri1-cbb2-bin. The mutants were selected by root-growth inhibition assay21 or by habitual similarity to BR-deficient mutants22,23 (Fig. 2). In contrast to BR-deficient mutants such as det2, cbb3/cpd and dwf4, the bri1 mutants could not be rescued by exogenous BR application, but retained sensitivity to other plant hormones, such as gibberellin, auxin, kinetin and jasmonic acid22. The bri1-cbb2-bin mutants all affected the BRI1 gene, and no other loci mediating (complete) BR sensitivity have yet been published.

# BRI1 encodes a plasma membrane Ser/Thr receptor-like kinase

The BRI1 gene was cloned with the use of a positional cloning approach23. BRI1 encodes a Leu-rich repeat (LRR) transmembrane receptor kinase with significant sequence identity to the ERECTA (Ref. 25) and CLAVATA1(Ref. 26) Arabidopsisgenes. Transmembrane receptor kinases are key components of various signal-transduction pathways. Animal receptor tyrosine kinases, such as the receptors for insulin and for most growth factors, autophosphorylate and phosphorylate downstream signaling proteins, whereas Ser/Thr receptor kinases mediate responses to transforming growth factor βs (TGF-βs)27. These receptor kinases are plasma-membrane proteins, so signaling is initiated at the cell boundary. Endocytosis might be required, in some cases to promote the association between activated receptors and signaling molecules28.

Plants use only receptor-like Ser/Thr kinases rather than tyrosine kinases to transduce various extracellular signals into the cell29. BRI1 was the first receptor-like kinase (RLK) described that is involved in a steroid signal-transduction pathway in plants. The predicted protein consists of several domains. A hydrophobic segment at the amino terminus presumably serves as an endoplasmic reticulum import signal. A putative Leu-zipper motif close to the signal peptide might mediate protein dimerization. This is followed by 25 tandem copies of a 24-amino-acid LRR. LRRs occur in proteins with differing functions and cellular locations in a variety of organisms30. The LRR domain of BRI1 carries 13 potential N-glycosylation sites and, as a unique feature, a 70-amino acid island occurs between the 21st and 22nd LRR. Several bri1 mutations resulting in single amino acid substitutions cluster in the island and highlight its importance for the function of BRI1 (Refs 31,32). A predicted transmembrane domain links the extracellular domains with the intracellular protein-kinase domain (KD). Several bri1 missense mutations abolishing in vivo activity were mapped to the KD, which contains all conserved protein-kinase motifs and invariant amino acids. The subcellular localization of BRI1 was demonstrated by a translational fusion with green fluorescent protein (GFP) under the control of the BRI1 promoter31. The surface of hypocotyl cells displayed fluorescence, and a plasmolysis experiment resulted in internalization of fluorescence within the plasma membrane. Thus, BRI1 is clearly a plasma-membrane-localized protein.

# The extracellular domain of BRI1 perceives brassinosteroids

The structure of BRI1 and its plasma-membrane localization suggests that BRI1 binds an extracellular ligand and transduces the signal via its intracellular KD. Domain-swap experiments with XA21 domains indicated that the extracellular domain of BRI1 perceives BRs. The domain assembly of the rice-disease-resistance receptor XA21 is very similar to that of BRI1. XA21 confers resistance to the bacterial plant pathogen Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzae race 6 (Xoo)33. An incompatible plant–pathogen interaction results in a hypersensitive response (HR), characterized by an oxidative burst, defense gene activation and cell death. In a cell-based assay, He et al.34 showed the induction of an HR in rice cells expressing full-length XA21 after inoculation with an incompatible Xoo strain, thus demonstrating the capacity of the system to function as a reporter of signaling output of the LRR–RLKs. A chimeric receptor consisting of the extracellular and transmembrane domains and 65 amino acids of the intracellular domain of BRI1 (juxtamembrane domain) fused to the KD of XA21 also induced HR after BR treatment. By contrast, constructs harboring point mutations, such as a

mutation in the 70-amino acid island of the extracellular BRI1 domain (Gly611→Glu) or a mutation in the KD of XA21 $( \mathrm { L y s 7 3 7 { \ - } { \to } G l u } )$ ) did not elicit HR. These observations provide clear evidence that BRs are perceived at the 70-amino acid island of BRI1. BL-binding to BRI1 was analyzed further by Wang et al.35 [3H]-BL binding to microsomal fractions of wild-type plants and BRI1–GFP-overexpressing plants was determined. The binding activity was dramatically increased in the membrane fractions of BR1–GFP transgenic plants owing to the rise in available binding sites. The binding affinities ( $\mathrm { ~ \textmu ~ } _ { \mathrm { d } }$ values) were consistent with physiological BL concentrations. Unlabeled BL effectively competed with $\mathrm { [ ^ { 3 } H ] }$ -BL for binding, but steroids, such as ecdysone and tetramethyl BL, did not. Castasterone, a precursor of BL, competed less effectively than did BL. In addition, proteins from wild-type and BRI1–GFP plants were immunoprecipitated with antiGFP antibodies and $\mathrm { [ ^ { 3 } H ] }$ -BL binding activities of the precipitated proteins were determined. Only extracts from BRI1–GFP displayed significant binding of the isotope-labeled steroid. Consistent with the data obtained in the rice-cell system, missense mutations in the 70-amino acid island greatly reduced BL binding activity. In summary, the 70-amino acid island of the extracellular domain of BRI1 is required for BL binding, although it is still possible that additional factors mediate binding.

The BRS1 gene encodes a carboxypeptidase-like protein that is likely to be secreted and might be involved in protein processing36. BRS1 overexpression suppresses multiple defects of bri1 extracellular domain mutants only when both a functional BRI1 KD and active BRs are present. The functional BRI1–XA21 chimera34 suggests that BRI1 does not require processing. The substrates of BRS1 might be steroid-binding proteins, which have been identified in the Arabidopsis genome sequence24.

# The intracellular domain of BRI1 is an active Ser/Thr kinase

Immunoblotting has revealed that BL treatment of Arabidopsis seedlings caused a shift of mobility of BRI1 KD protein band in SDS–PAGE to a slower migrating band35. This shift is probably a result of autophosphorylation. A BL-induced mobility shift did not occur in a bri1 mutant with a mutation in the KD. In keeping with these observations, 12 autophosphorylation sites were identified by matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization–mass spectrometry (MALDI–MS; Ref. 37). The autophosphorylation proceeds by an intramolecular reaction because an active recombinant BRI1 KD failed to transphosphorylate an inactive mutant kinase. No other substrates for the BRI1 KD transmitting the phosphorylation signal have been identified. Recombinant BRI1 KD was found to phosphorylate certain synthetic peptides and a putative consensus sequence required for peptide-substrate recognition by

BRI1 KD was deduced37. Numerous Arabidopsis proteins contain this motif – their interactions with BRI1, however, have not been hitherto tested.

A type 2C phosphatase (kinase-associated protein phosphatase, KAPP) might oppose the action of the BRI1 KD, because KAPPpotentially interacts with the KD in vitro38. Type 2C phosphatases (PP2C) function as regulators of various signal transduction pathways in plants39. PP2C enzymes, such as ABI1 and ABI2 (Ref. 40), act in a negative-feedback regulatory loop of the abscisic acid signal transduction pathway41. The KAPP protein binds the phosphorylated form of the LRR–RLK CLV1 and is part of a large complex42,43. KAPP exerts phosphatase activity on the CLV1 protein, and KAPP overexpression results in a floral phenotype reminiscent of that caused by weak clv1 alleles. Conversely, reduction of KAPP transcript levels in an intermediate clv1 mutant suppresses the mutant phenotype44. These observations suggest that KAPPcould be a negative

regulator of the CLV1 signal-transduction pathway. KAPPwas cloned by interaction with another Ser/Thr receptor-like kinase (RLK5) from A. thaliana45. Further RLK–KAPPinteractions were shown, and KAPPmight function in several RLK-initiated signaling pathways in plants46, including the BR pathway. # Perspectives

BR signaling via RLKs appears to be a common theme in plant-steroid signaling. This became apparent when OsBRI1 (the rice homolog of BRI1) was found to mediate BR sensitivity47. This system represents a new mechanism of steroid signaling, and BRI1 (in addition to other components of BR-signaling) might have animal counterparts that mediate membraneinitiated steroid effects, such as tyrosine kinase activation48,49. It might therefore be worthwhile transferring the plant model to animal systems.

# Experimental mouse genetics – answering fundamental questions about mammary gland biology

Jonathan M. Shillingford and Lothar Hennighausen

Contemporary gene-targeting techniques now make it possible to alter specific genes in the genome. As a result, a plethora of mouse models have been generated that allow researchers to dissect cell-signaling pathways involved in mammary gland development and in breast cancer. But what have we learned so far? What do these models teach us? This review presents a global picture of how the analyses and comparison of individual knockout mouse models provide important insights into basic mammary gland biology. Particular emphasis is placed upon what is currently known about the signaling pathways involved in the establishment of the mammary ductal tree, and its subsequent proliferation at pregnancy and lactation. In addition to these wellestablished pathways, we address recent data that describe the role of lesserknown genes in the development of the mammary epithelium.

Our knowledge of mammary gland biology has benefited greatly in the past decade from the development of modern mouse genetics. These tools enable us to target specific genes and to analyze the impact of their loss on cell fate and function. With these approaches, several receptors, ligands and transcription factors (Table 1) have been linked to mammary gland development and function. Although it is clear from gross morphology that these models exhibit defects in mammopoiesis, relatively little is known about the molecular events and signaling pathways that contribute to the observed phenotypes. An understanding of the individual signaling components, their actions and their ability to crosstalk with each other is necessary to ascribe a functional role to these pathways during normal mammary development. In addition, knowledge of transcription-factor target genes is desirable so that we can begin to define development on a genetic level. In this article, we present a broad overview of our current understanding of how signaling pathways

contribute to mammopoiesis. Furthermore, we highlight the importance of mammary stromal–epithelial interactions and the discovery of new genes involved in mammary gland development.

A compartmental analysis of signaling pathways The mammary gland consists of several cell types and the ratio of these cell types changes during development (Fig. 1). In the virgin, adipocytes predominate as the ductal epithelium invades the fat pad (Fig. 1a,b). During pregnancy (Fig. 1c,d), the epithelium proliferates so that at lactation (Fig. 1e) the secretory epithelium completely fills the fat pad. Once the gland is no longer suckled, the epithelium undergoes apoptosis and reverts back to a virgin-like state (Fig. 1f). A variety of steroid hormones, cytokines, growth factors and the signaling pathways that these molecules activate control the rapid changes that occur during these developmental phases. To complicate issues further, there is communication between the stromal compartment and the epithelial compartment (reviewed in Refs 1,2). Although these features make the mammary gland an interesting tissue to study, they can make interpretations complicated at best.

Studies have been performed to address these compartmentalization issues by utilizing receptor knockout mice and the technique of mammary tissue transplantation3–6. This transplantation procedure was first described in 1959 (Ref. 7) and has seen a revival in recent years. As depicted in Fig. 2, a variety of transplants is possible.
