# ABSTRACT

Heat stress (HS) has become an increasing threat to wheat productivity under global warming. However, the genetic loci for thermotolerance and the underlying molecular mechanisms remain largely unknown. In this study, genetic mapping identified a thermotolerance locus, QMpe.cau-2D, encoding fatty acid desaturase 8 (FAD8), with the transposable element (TE) insertions present in the promoter region in the thermotolerant cultivar. The expression of TaFAD8-D was negatively associated with thermotolerance. Loss-of-function mutations in TaFAD8 enhanced photosynthetic efficiency, seedling survival rate, and thousand-grain weight under HS. Transcriptome, fatty acid, and lipid profiling analyses showed that TaFAD8 mutation affected the expression of genes involved in lipid biosynthesis and metabolism to mediate the fatty acid composition and lipid remodelling, thereby maintaining chloroplast membrane fluidity and integrity under HS. TaWRKY71 negatively regulated the transcription of TaFAD8 by binding to its promoter, and mutation of TaWRKY71 reduced photosynthetic efficiency under HS. Our findings identify a beneficial TaFAD8-D haplotype, uncover its molecular mechanism and regulatory pathways in heat response, and provide a strategy for breeding climate-resilient wheat varieties. 

# 1 | Introduction

Global warming has significantly increased the frequency and duration of extreme heat stress (HS) events during the growing season, which poses severe threats to the yield of temperate cereal crops like wheat and barley. Thermotolerance in wheat is a complex agronomic trait controlled by multiple quantitative trait loci (QTLs) (Bohnert et  al.  2006); thus, 

identification and isolation of thermotolerance-associated QTLs present greater challenges compared to other agronomic traits (Sun et al. 2022). Previous investigations have demonstrated that QTLs associated with heat tolerance in wheat are distributed across multiple chromosomes (Kumar et al. 2020; Lu et  al.  2020; Wang et  al.  2021; Zhai et  al.  2021). Sharma et al. identified three QTLs correlating with maximum photochemical efficiency on chromosomes 1D and 3B (Sharma 

et al. 2017). Talukder et al. identified thermotolerance-related QTLs on chromosomes 6A and 7A, associated with postheat stress responses, such as thylakoid membrane damage, plasma membrane integrity and chlorophyll content maintenance (Talukder et al. 2014). However, genes underlying these QTLs remain largely unknown. Therefore, identifying the thermotolerance locus, elucidating the underlying molecular mechanisms and bridging the gap between this knowledge and breeding programs are crucial for developing climateresilient wheat varieties. 

Plants have evolved complex physiological and cellular responses to cope with heat stress. Among these, lipid remodelling has emerged as one of the critical mechanisms. Under HS, plants alter the saturated and unsaturated fatty acid ratio to maintain membrane fluidity and stability to reduce cellular component damage and thus enhance thermotolerance. Additionally, plants regulate lipid metabolism under HS to preserve membrane integrity and function. These HS responses and cell protection have been observed across species, like Arabidopsis, turf grasses, and castor beans (Falcone et al. 2004; Su et al. 2009; Zhang et al. 2022). Phospholipids, the main structural constituents of cell membranes, exhibit dynamic changes in response to HS. For instance, plants decrease phosphatidylethanolamine (PE) levels while maintaining phosphatidylcholine (PC) and increasing phosphatidic acid (PA) and phosphatidylserine (PS) levels in leaves under HS (Higashi et  al.  2015; Mishkind et  al.  2009; Narayanan et al. 2016). Such modifications enhance membrane thermal stability and have been observed in Arabidopsis, wheat, and tomato (Mueller et  al.  2015; Narayanan et  al.  2016; Spicher et  al.  2016). In addition, galactolipids, such as monogalactosyldiacylglycerol (MGDG) and digalactosyldiacylglycerol (DGDG), are the main lipids of the thylakoid membrane and are essential for photosystem II (PSII) stability (Jones 2007). Under HS, MGDG species were reduced, while DGDG contents were increased to stabilise the thylakoid membrane (Mueller et al. 2017). In Arabidopsis, mutants with reduced DGDG levels displayed heat-sensitive phenotypes, suggesting the role of these lipids in thermotolerance (Chen et al. 2006; Higashi et al. 2018). The DGDG synthase (DGD1), which facilitates the conversion of MGDG to DGDG within chloroplasts, upon mutation results in decreased DGDG content and manifests as a heat-sensitive phenotype (Chen et al. 2006). Moreover, mutation of genes involved in lipid metabolism can lead to thermotolerance or susceptibility. HS upregulated the transcript level of heat inducible lipase1 (HIL1), which induced the catabolism of MGDG and enhanced thermotolerance in Arabidopsis (Higashi et  al.  2018). In rice, mutation of high temperature sensitive 1 (HST1), a gene encoding $\beta$ -ketoacyl carrier protein reductase, disrupted fatty acid biosynthesis and led to heat sensitivity (Chen et al. 2021). 

The relative abundance of lipid species is tightly regulated by the availability and composition of fatty acids, which are synthesised and modified by fatty acid desaturases (FADs) (Xiao et  al.  2022; Zhao et  al.  2019). FADs catalyse the introduction of double bonds in fatty acid chains, with distinct FADs targeting specific carbon positions. FAD gene families are classified into ω-3 FAD (FAD3, FAD7, FAD8) and ω-6 FAD (FAD2, FAD6) families (Liu et  al.  2015). In Arabidopsis, mutations in 

FAD2 resulted in hypersensitivity to ER stress, while mutations in FAD6 compromised salt tolerance (Nguyen et al. 2019; Zhang et  al. 2009). In contrast, overexpression of FAD3 in tobacco, tomato, and Arabidopsis enhanced plant tolerance to cold, heat, drought, and salt stresses (Matos et  al.  2007; Shi et al. 2018; Wang et al. 2010). FAD7 and FAD8 share the most similar amino acid sequences (Dampanaboina et  al.  2019). In tomato, soybean, rice, and Arabidopsis, expression of FAD7 and FAD8 was increased under cold stress but decreased under high temperatures (Soria-Garcï et al. 2019; Wang et al. 2010, 2019). The stability of the FAD8 protein has been demonstrated to decrease under elevated temperatures, indicating the presence of post-transcriptional level regulatory mechanisms (Matsuda et al. 2005). Loss of function of FAD7 and FAD8 in tobacco and Arabidopsis enhances thermotolerance (Murakami et al. 2000; Soria-Garcï et al. 2019). Overexpression of FAD7 in tomato enhanced cold tolerance, while downregulation of FAD7 increased thermotolerance (Wickramanayake et al. 2020; Yu et al. 2009). 

This study aimed to identify thermotolerance loci in wheat, elucidate the molecular mechanisms underlying thermotolerance regulation, and assess their potential application in wheat breeding. Using genetic mapping analysis, we identified a FAD gene, TaFAD8, as a negative regulator of wheat thermotolerance. Natural variation in the promoter of TaFAD8-D, with or without transposable element (TE) insertions, resulted in differential basal expression of TaFAD8-D. The introgression of the Tafad8 loss-of-function mutation enhanced photosynthetic efficiency under HS. Moreover, we identified a transcription factor TaWRKY71 that repressed TaFAD8 expression through binding to the W-box motif in the TaFAD8 promoter. The lossof-function mutation of TaFAD8 regulated downstream genes involved in lipid biosynthesis, thereby modulating lipid compositions, especially for thylakoid membranes, and thus maintained membrane fluidity and the integrity of the chloroplast and thylakoid, enhancing photosynthetic efficiency under HS.

# 2.3 | TaFAD8 Acts as a Negative Modulator of Thermotolerance

To explore the role of TaFAD8 in thermotolerance in wheat, we used CRISPR/Cas9 technology to generate TaFAD8 lossof-function mutants in the wild-type (WT) cultivar Fielder. A single guide RNA (sgRNA) specifically targeting the Nterminal of TaFAD8 was designed to target A, B, and D genomes (Figure  3A). Three independent Tafad8 mutants were obtained (Figure 3A). Tafad8-1 and Tafad8-2 carried frameshift mutations in all A, B, and D homologues of TaFAD8, whereas Tafad8-3 had a frameshift mutation only in the D homologue (Figure 3A and Table S5). Under CK $( 2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ , no significant differences in plant morphology or above-ground fresh weight were observed between the WT and Tafad8 mutants at 7 days after germination (Figure  3B; Data  S4A). However, loss-of-function mutations in TaFAD8 or TaFAD8-D enhanced thermotolerance compared to the WT, with significantly higher survival rates after HS $( 4 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ (Figure 3C). Furthermore, the Fv/Fm ratio was similar between WT and mutants under CK, while HS $4 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 1 h) caused a greater reduction in the Fv/Fm ratio in the WT compared to Tafad8 mutants, consistent with the observation in CS and TAM107 (Figure  3D,E). HS reduced fresh weight but increased electrolyte leakage in both genotypes, while the impact was more severe in the WT than in the Tafad8 mutants (Figure S4A,B). 

To evaluate the impact of TaFAD8 on grain yield, we conducted a field experiment applying heat stress at the post-anthesis stage to assess yield-related traits in WT, Tafad8-1, and Tafad8-2. 

Consistent with the effects observed at the seedling stage, the Fv/Fm ratio of the flag leaf was higher in the Tafad8 mutants than in the WT under HS, while no significant difference was observed under normal conditions (Figure  S5A). Notably, thousand-grain weight (TGW) was significantly higher in the Tafad8 mutants than in the WT under HS, while no significant differences between the two genotypes were observed under normal conditions (Figure 3F). Other agronomic traits, including plant height, spike length, number of spikes and tillers, showed no significant differences between genotypes and treatments (Figure S5B–E). 

To further investigate the impact of TaFAD8 on thermotolerance across different genetic backgrounds, we introduced the loss-of-function Tafad8 allele into NingChun 4 (NC4), a highyield spring wheat variety. Under HS $( 4 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ , the NIL-fad8ABDmut seedlings exhibited a higher Fv/Fm ratio compared to NC4 (Figure S6A,B), suggesting an improved Fv/Fm ratio under HS. These results indicated that downregulation of TaFAD8 had the potential to enhance thermotolerance in different genetic backgrounds. 

# 2.4 | TaFAD8 Affects Fatty Acid Composition and Lipid Remodelling Under Heat Stress

FADs are enzymes responsible for the synthesis of unsaturated and polyunsaturated fatty acids (Xiao et al. 2022). To investigate the role of TaFAD8 in fatty acid biosynthesis and its response to HS, we analysed changes in fatty acid composition in the leaf under CK $( 2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ and $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ of HS $( 4 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ , as well as in the grain under CK in WT and Tafad8-1 and Tafad8-2 mutants. In both leaf and grain, total fatty acid content remained unchanged between genotypes or treatments (Figure S7A,B). However, Tafad8 mutations significantly altered the fatty acid composition in the leaf (Figure 4A and Figure S7C). Under both CK and HS, the level of C18:2 was significantly higher in the mutants compared to the WT in both leaf and grain, while the level of C18:3 was only markedly reduced in the Tafad8 mutants in the leaf, but not grain (Figure 4A and Figure S7C,D). Consequently, the ratio of C18:2/C18:3 was higher in the Tafad8 mutants than in the WT, suggesting that TaFAD8 plays a key role in catalysing the conversion of C18:2 to C18:3 (Figure 4A and Figure S7C,D). A similar effect of TaFAD8 on the ratio of C18:2/C18:3 was observed in the leaves of NIL-CS and NIL-TAM107. The ratio of C18:2/C18:3 was higher in NIL-TAM107 than in NIL-CS under both CK and HS (Figure  S7E,F). Apart from C18:2 and C18:3, the levels of other fatty acids in both leaf and grain were not significantly affected by TaFAD8 or HS (Figure S7C,D). 

We further quantified lipid species in the leaf of WT and Tafad8-1 using liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry (LC–MS). # 2.5 | Mutation of TaFAD8 Enhanced Thermotolerance by Modulating the Transcriptome, Cellular Homeostasis and Chloroplast Integrity Under Heat Stress

To investigate the molecular basis of TaFAD8 in regulating thermotolerance and photosynthetic efficiency, we conducted transcriptome analysis for leaf samples from 7-day-old WT and Tafad8-1 seedlings under CK $( 2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ and after 4 and $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ of HS $( 4 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ conditions. The principal component analysis (PCA) revealed that the duration of HS explained most of the transcriptomic variation (Figure  S9A). Compared to WT, only 60 genes were differentially expressed $\mathrm { | l o g 2 F C | } \geq 1$ , $p { < } 0 . 0 5 $ ) in the Tafad8 mutant under CK. However, under HS, the number of TaFAD8-regulated genes significantly increased, with 704 differentially expressed at 4 h and 1077 at $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ (Figure 5A and Table S7), indicating distinct thermal responses between WT and the Tafad8 mutant. Gene Ontology (GO) analysis of TaFAD8- regulated genes revealed that genes involved in stress response, lipid biosynthesis and metabolism, and hormone signalling pathways were significantly enriched, primarily functioning as oxidoreductase, protein dimerization, and glutathione transferase on plasma and thylakoid membranes (Figure S9B–D). 

Given that a large number of genes involved in lipid biosynthesis, metabolism, and transport were dysregulated by the Tafad8 mutation, we analyzed the transcriptional changes of all lipidrelated genes between treatments and genotypes. We identified 47 genes that were regulated by HS or TaFAD8 (Figure 4F and Table S9). The transcript levels of genes involved in PC biosynthesis and metabolism, such as TaFAD2, TaFAD3, TaCCT1, and TaNMT1, were similar between WT and Tafad8 mutant under CK condition (Figure 4F) (Caldo et al. 2019; Chen, Wang, et al. 2018; Okuley et al. 1994; Park et al. 2023). However, under HS, their expression levels were higher in the WT than in the Tafad8 mutant. This was consistent with the higher levels of PC(16:0/18:3), PC(18:2/18:3), and PC(20:0/18:3) in the WT compared to the mutant under HS, while PC(18:0/18:2) and PC(18:2/18:2) levels were lower in the WT (Figure 4F). In contrast, the transcript levels of TaFAD4, an enzyme that introduces a Δ3-trans double bond in palmitic acid in plastid PG, were similar between WT and mutant under CK, whereas its expression 

was more strongly downregulated in the mutant than in the WT under HS, correlated with an increase in PG (34:0) (Figure 4F) (Chen, Guo, et al. 2023). Among genes involved in PE biosynthesis and metabolism, the expression of TaPSD3 and TaPECT1, catalyzing PS to PE and DAG to PE, was similar between WT and Tafad8 mutant under CK (Mizoi et  al.  2006; Nerlich et al. 2007). However, HS significantly downregulated TaPSD3 expression in the mutant but upregulated TaPECT1 expression in the WT (Figure 4F). The changes in the expression of these genes appear to be consistent with the alterations observed in the lipidome. However, the closest homologue of TaFAD8 in wheat, TaFAD7, was not differentially expressed between WT and Tafad8 under either CK or HS (Figure S9E). Together, the mutation of TaFAD8 may affect fatty acid and lipid remodeling, thereby altering membrane composition and modulating membrane stability and fluidity under HS. 

To further investigate the thermoresponse of TaFAD8-regulated genes, we classified TaFAD8-regulated genes into ten distinct gene expression profiles (C1–C10). Among them, six expression profiles (C1–C6) exhibited substantial interaction between TaFAD8 and HS (Figure 5B and Table S7), reflecting differential thermoresponses and regulatory mechanisms by TaFAD8. In contrast, genes in C4 and C5 were significantly upregulated in the WT at $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ of HS but showed a mild HS response in the Tafad8 mutant (Figure 5B). Notably, genes in C3 were significantly upregulated by HS in the WT at both 4 and $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ of HS, whereas these genes were upregulated in the Tafad8 mutant at $4 \mathrm { h }$ of HS but downregulated at $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ of HS (Figure 5B), suggesting HS acclimation of the Tafad8 mutant. In addition, genes in C2 and C4 exhibited the opposite HS response between WT and Tafad8, suggesting an HS acclimation of the Tafad8 mutant. GO analysis revealed that genes in C1 and C2, which were either specifically upregulated by HS or responded earlier in the Tafad8 mutant compared to the WT, were enriched in glutathione metabolism (i.e., Glutathione S-Transferase and Peroxidase), negative regulation of ethylene-activated signalling, and chromatin silencing (i.e., Ethylene-Responsive Transcription Factor and Nuclease) (Figure 5B and Table S8). This suggests that the Tafad8 mutant may suppress transcription and enhance cellular detoxification to mitigate misfolded protein accumulations and ROS damage under HS. Genes in C5 and C6, which were upregulated in the WT but not in the mutant, as well as genes in C3 and C4, which showed sustained upregulation in the WT but only transient activation in the mutant, were involved in calcium-mediated signalling (i.e., Calmodulin, Calcium-binding Protein, Calcium-transporting ATPase), oxylipin and lipid biosynthesis (i.e., Fatty Acid Hydroxylase, Non-Specific Lipid-Transfer Protein, Phospholipase A1), and stress responses (i.e., Late Embryogenesis Abundant Protein, Aquaporin, Dehydrin) (Figure  5B and Table  S8). # 2.6 | TaWRKY71 Interacts With TaFAD8 Promoter to Regulate TaFAD8 Basal Expression and Thermotolerance

To elucidate the regulatory mechanisms of TaFAD8 in wheat thermotolerance, we used the wGRN database to predict potential regulatory networks targeting TaFAD8-A, TaFAD8-B, and TaFAD8-D (Chen, Guo, et  al.  2023). Within their common putative regulatory network, we examined the thermoresponsiveness of these genes in WT plants using RNA-seq data. A total of 34 genes were differentially expressed in response to HS (Figure S11). Several transcription factors potentially involved in stress regulation were identified, including ethylene-responsive transcription factors and bHLH transcription factors. The transcript levels of TaWRKY71 gradually increased during HS, displaying a negative correlation with TaFAD8 expression (Figure  6B). The results confirmed that TaWRKY71-GST bound specifically to the W-box motif in the TaFAD8 promoter (Figure 6C,D). In addition, we performed LUC reporter assays of TaWRKY71 and the TaFAD8-D promoter from TAM107 (pro-TaFAD8- $. D ^ { T A M I O 7 } )$ ) and CS (pro-TaFAD8- $. D ^ { C S }$ ), along with a mutated TaFAD8-D promoter of TAM107 (pro-TaFAD8-DmTAM107) without TE insertion. To determine the role of TaWRKY71 in wheat thermotolerance, we generated TaWRKY71 knockout lines, Tawrky71-1 and Tawrky71-2, carrying frameshift mutations in the A, B, and D alleles (Figure S13A and Table S10). The transcript level of TaFAD8 was significantly higher in the Tawrky71 mutants under CK and HS (Figure 6G). These results indicated that TaWRKY71 played a crucial role in downregulating TaFAD8 expression under HS and fine-tuning the C18:2/C18:3 ratio and lipid metabolism to confer thermotolerance in wheat. 

# 2.7 | Identification and Distribution of TaFAD8-D Haplotype in Wheat

To investigate the genetic variation of TaFAD8-D in natural wheat populations, we analysed nucleotide polymorphisms in 1280 resequenced wheat accessions from five continents, including Asia, Europe, North and South America, Oceania, and Africa (Table  S11). The majority of accessions $( 7 9 . 7 6 \% )$ belonged to haplotype 1 (Hap1), which included the heat-susceptible cultivar CS (Figure  7A). # 3 | Discussion

Previous studies have demonstrated that FADs play a crucial role in stress resilience, such as low and high temperatures,

drought, and salt stress (Dong et  al.  2016; Gibson et  al.  1994; Nishiuchi et  al.  1997). In this study, we identified TaFAD8 as a key regulator underlying thermotolerance. Natural variation in the promoter of TaFAD8-D, with transposable element (TE) insertions, resulted in differential basal expression of TaFAD8. The transcription factor, TaWRKY71, binds to the TaFAD8 promoter to repress its expression and enhance thermotolerance. Loss-of-function mutation of TaFAD8 influences the expression of lipid metabolism-related genes, leading to alterations in fatty acid and lipid composition, maintaining the integrity and stability of chloroplasts and thylakoid membranes, thus improving photosynthetic efficiency under HS (Figure 8). In rice and Arabidopsis, expression of FAD8 was up-regulated under cold 

but down-regulated under high temperature; the Arabidopsis fad8 mutant conferred thermotolerance, and the rice fad8 mutant was susceptible to cold (Falcone et al. 2004; Nair et al. 2009; Román et  al.  2015; Tovuu et  al.  2016). These findings suggest that the role of FAD8 might be conserved between monocots and dicots. 

In wheat, there are 68 TaFAD genes, each with distinct gene structures and functions (Hajiahmadi et al. 2020). In tomato and tobacco, mutation of FAD7 enhanced photosynthetic efficiency under HS through regulating lipid remodelling and membrane fluidity (Hiremath et  al.  2017; Wickramanayake et al. 2020). We demonstrate that TaFAD8 plays an important role in catalysing the conversion of C18:2 to C18:3. C18:2 and C18:3 are essential for maintaining photosynthetic efficiency under high temperatures (Sharma et al. 2020). We found that the ratio of C18:2 to C18:3 was higher in the Tafad8 mutant compared to WT under CK, while the Fv/Fm ratio was similar between both genotypes (Figure 3). However, the Fv/Fm ratio was higher in Tafad8 than in the WT under HS, suggesting that the C18:2 and C18:3 balance is crucial to preserve photosynthetic efficiency under HS (Figure  3). A higher level of C18:3 relative to C18:2 could increase fluidity (Narayanan et  al.  2016). Under HS, a higher level of C18:3 to C18:2 in the WT could cause membranes to be overly fluid and unstable, whereas a lower level of C18:3 to C18:2 in the Tafad8 mutants could maintain membrane fluidity and integrity, as we observed a looser distribution of thylakoid membranes in the WT than in mutants under HS (Figure  4 and Figure  5). Moreover, C18:3 is more prone to oxidation due to its three double bonds, making it susceptible to ROS generated under HS (Allakhverdiev et  al.  2008; Chan et  al.  2012). In Tafad8 mutants, a lower level of C18:3 relative to C18:2 may protect membrane lipids and photosynthetic components from excessive ROS damage under HS. Furthermore, the ratio of bilayer to non-bilayer lipid (DGDG/MGDG and PC/PE) was higher in the mutant than in the WT under HS, suggesting that lipid remodelling in the Tafad8 mutant may increase the proportions of MGDG and PE to reduce membrane permeability of chloroplast, plasma, and other cellular membranes under HS (Figure  4). In Arabidopsis, the remodelling of galactolipids from MGDG to DGDG enhances dehydration tolerance by limiting membrane fusion and promoting more stable inter-membrane stalk structures (Chng et  al.  2022). Increased DGDG content and DGDG/MGDG ratio in maize retarded leaf senescence and maintained stable chloroplast ultrastructure under drought stress (Chen, Salari, et al. 2018). Together, our results demonstrate that mutation of TaFAD8 increased the ratio of C18:2 to C18:3, DGDG to MGDG, and PC to PE, reducing membrane permeability and fluidity under HS and maintaining the membrane integrity of chloroplasts and other cellular components, which in turn stabilises electron transport chains, minimises photoinhibition, and $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ homeostasis. 

The photosynthetic activity of plants is generally reduced under stress conditions. Most of the neutral membrane lipids that constitute the photosynthetic membrane are unsaturated (Boudière et  al.  2014). Under CK, the Fv/Fm ratio was similar between WT and Tafad8 mutants, while it was higher in Tafad8 than in WT under HS (Figure 3). This result suggests that the mutation 

of TaFAD8 improves the photosynthetic efficiency of PSII, the most thermal-sensitive component, under HS. Transcriptome analysis showed that the expression of genes involved in PSII was more strongly down-regulated or up-regulated in the Tafad8 mutant compared to WT under HS, suggesting that plants may down-regulate the transcription of photosynthesisrelated genes to maintain photosynthetic efficiency under HS (Figure 5). Moreover, HS upregulated the expression of glutathione metabolism-related genes earlier and stronger in the Tafad8 mutant compared to the WT, suggesting that the Tafad8 mutant could avoid excessive ROS accumulation under HS and thus prevent damage to photosynthetic pigments and proteins and photoinhibition (Figure 5). Furthermore, the mutations of TaFAD8 maintained the expression of calcium-mediated signalling genes and cellular $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ levels under HS, suggesting more stable $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ homeostasis, which is essential for $\mathrm { C O } _ { 2 }$ uptake and fixation and PSII stability (Figure 5). 

We identified nine haplotypes of TaFAD8-D using 1280 resequenced wheat accessions (Figure  7). Hap4, associated with thermotolerance, contains a 3534-bp TE insertion in its promoter region. This haplotype was more prevalent in Aegilops tauschii and spelt wheat than in modern wheat, suggesting that Hap4 likely originated from Aegilops tauschii and was introduced into hexaploid wheat (Figure  7). Importantly, although many stress regulators exhibit trade-offs between growth and stress adaptation (Paliwal et  al.  2012), such as faster growth, fewer grains, but enhanced stress resistance. Our field trials revealed no significant differences in grain-yield-related traits between WT and Tafad8 mutants under normal conditions (Figure S5). However, under HS, the Tafad8 mutant exhibited a higher grain yield compared to the WT, highlighting the potential of TaFAD8 mutation for breeding thermotolerant wheat varieties (Figure 3). Based on these findings, we introgressed the loss-of-function TaFAD8 alleles into NingChun 4 (NC4), a high-yield wheat variety. Notably, the NILs carrying the TaFAD8 mutations displayed higher photosynthetic efficiency under HS, further supporting its role in improving thermotolerance (Figure S6). In conclusion, our findings unravel the molecular mechanism and regulatory pathway of TaFAD8 in regulating thermotolerance in wheat and highlight the potential breeding value of utilising TaFAD8 mutation to maintain grain yield to cope with global warming, and also demonstrate the future potential of editing target genes to improve heat tolerance in wheat and other crops. 

# 4 | Materials and Methods

# 4.1 | Plant Materials and Genetic Resources

The study utilised contrasting wheat genotypes: thermotolerant TAM107 and heat-sensitive LZ238. A recombinant inbred line (RIL) population was developed through seven generations of self-fertilisation following hybridization. For fine-mapping QMpe.cau-2D, $\mathrm { B C } _ { 3 } \mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ near-isogenic lines (NILs) in the Chinese Spring (CS) background were constructed, carrying introgressed chromosome 2D segments from TAM107. CRISPR/Cas9 technology was used to generate homozygous knockout mutants, Tafad8-1/2/3 and Tawrky71-1/2, in the Fielder background carrying the Hap1 haplotype of the TaFAD8 gene. Mutants were validated by sequencing. In addition, $\mathrm { B C } _ { 2 } \mathrm { F } _ { 2 }$ NILs derived from 

Tafad8-1 and the high-yield cultivar Ningchun4 (NC4) were developed to assess the breeding value of TaFAD8. Additional methodological details can be found in the Data S3. 

# 4.2 | Experimental Design and Phenotyping

Seedlings were cultivated in Pindstrup substrate under controlled conditions (CK) at $2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in growth chambers, $1 6 \mathrm { h }$ light cycles and $6 0 \%$ relative humidity. For the evaluation of the survival rate, plants were exposed to heat stress (HS) at $4 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 4 days (LZ238, TAM107, Tawrky71 mutants, and WT) or for 6 days (Tafad8 mutants and WT), followed by a 4-day recovery period under control conditions. Field trials at China Agricultural University simulated post-anthesis heat stress using transparent tents. Phenotypic evaluation included photosynthetic efficiency (Fv/Fm ratio), measured via FluorCam and MINI-PAM after dark adaptation; thermotolerance traits (seedling survival rate, electrolyte leakage, and biomass loss) were measured in 7-day-old seedlings in a growth chamber after HS of different durations. The agronomic parameters (plant height, spike length, and thousand-grain weight) for plants grown in the field were quantified using camera-assisted phenotyping. Chloroplast ultrastructure was analysed by transmission electron microscopy (TEM). Additional methodological details can be found in the Data S3. 

# 4.3 | Genomic and RNA-Seq Analyses

QTL mapping was performed using composite interval mapping (WinQTLCart v2.5) with a LOD threshold $\geq 2 . 5$ . Bulked segregant analysis (BSA) was conducted by resequencing extreme high/low Fv/Fm pools with variant calling and allele frequency divergence calculations. Whole-genome resequencing data were used to analyse sequence differences in candidate genes. To identify polymorphisms in candidate genes, we aligned genomic sequences between CS and TAM107 obtained from resequencing data. We focused on the nucleotide variations within the 2-kb promoter and gene coding regions  of the annotated gene, as these regions have garnered the most attention. Genes with identical sequences were excluded, while those exhibiting sequence variations were retained for subsequent analysis. Transcriptomic profiling of NILs and mutants under heat stress (RNA-seq on Illumina NovaSeq 6000) identified differentially expressed genes (DESeq2/EdgeR, FDR $< 0 . 0 5 )$ . Additional methodological details can be found in the Data S3. 

# 4.4 | Lipid and $\mathbf { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ Measurements

Lipid metabolism was assessed by gas chromatography (Agilent HP7890A) for fatty acid composition in Tafad8 mutants' leaves and grains, complemented by lipidomic profiling following established protocols. The extraction, detection, and quantitative analysis of metabolites in the Tawrky71 mutants, CS, and TAM107 leaves were performed by Wuhan Metware Biotechnology Co. Ltd. (www.metware.cn). Non-invasive micro-test technology (NMT-YG-100) monitored real-time $\mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ flux in leaves under $4 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Detailed protocols can be found in the Data S3. 

# 4.5 | Molecular Analyses

The dual-luciferase assay was conducted to compare TaFAD8-D promoter activity in Nicotiana benthamiana. Electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) confirmed the binding of TaWRKY71 to the TaFAD8-D promoter. Real-time quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) quantified the expression of the target gene. Additional methodological details can be found in the Data S3. 

# 4.6 | Evolutionary and Haplotype Analysis

Phylogenetic analysis of TaFAD8 homologs was constructed using MEGA6. Haplotype analysis of TaFAD8-D in 1280 wheat accessions was performed using the PEGAS R package. Additional methodological details can be found in the Data S3. 

# 4.7 | Statistical and Technical Validation

All experiments included at least three biological replicates and experimental replicates. Key analytical tools included QTL Cartographer for genetic mapping, SnpEff for variant annotation, and STAR for RNA-seq alignment. Detailed protocols are provided in Data S3.
