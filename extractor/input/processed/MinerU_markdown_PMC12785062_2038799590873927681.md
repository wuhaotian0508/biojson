# ABSTRACT

Transitory starch in rice leaves is critical for temporary carbon storage, plant growth, and subsequent grain filling, yet the regulatory mechanisms underlying its biosynthesis remain poorly understood. Here, we found that Leaf Starch Deficiency 3 (LSD3), a rice glutamyl–tRNA reductase (GluTR), interacts with GluTR-binding protein (GluTRBP), which in turn associates with granule-bound starch synthase II (GBSSII), the key enzyme responsible for amylose synthesis in leaves. The LSD3–GluTRBP module maintains both the protein stability and enzymatic activity of GBSSII. The lsd3 and glutrbp mutants exhibited markedly reduced GBSSII activity, resulting in significant decreases in leaf starch accumulation. Moreover, these mutants displayed severe defects in carbon-source transport from leaves to developing grains, accompanied by strong downregulation of genes associated with carbon-source allocation. Consequently, the lsd3 and glutrbp mutants showed reduced endosperm-storage starch content and severely compromised grain quality and yield. Notably, overexpression of GBSSII partially rescued defective phenotypes in both leaves and grains of the mutants. In summary, our findings establish that the LSD3–GluTRBP module acts upstream of GBSSII and plays a central role in transitory starch biosynthesis as well as in determining grain quality and yield in rice. These findings provide new molecular targets for simultaneously improving both grain yield and quality in rice. 

Key words: transitory starch, source–sink regulation, granule-bound starch synthase II, GBSSII, grain yield and quality, rice, Oryza sativa L.

# INTRODUCTION

Rice (Oryza sativa L.) is one of the most important food crops worldwide. Starch constitutes more than $80 \%$ of grain endosperm weight and serves as a central determinant of both rice quality and yield (Dong et al., 2024). Endosperm-storage starch consists of amylose and amylopectin (Denyer et al., 1997; Vrinten and Nakamura, 2000b; Seung and Smith, 2019). Amylose is synthesized by granule-bound starch synthase (GBSS), whereas amylopectin is produced through the coordinated activity of starch synthases, starch branching enzymes, and starch debranching enzymes (Bahaji et al., 2014). Substantial research has clarified the biosynthetic pathways 

and regulatory networks of endosperm starch as well as the influence of its composition on rice yield and quality (Cai et al., 2018; Cao et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2022). 

Beyond the large starch reserves in the endosperm, rice also accumulates starch in leaves and stem sheaths. During the day, photosynthetic assimilates are stored transiently as starch within the chloroplasts of mesophyll cells; this starch is subsequently degraded into sucrose at night to sustain plant growth (Westgeest et al., 2023). This dynamic process produces diurnal fluctuations in leaf starch content that follow circadian rhythms. During grain filling, assimilates from leaves and stem sheaths are translocated as sucrose to endosperm cells, where

they are used to synthesize storage starch (Dong et al., 2024). Therefore, the biosynthesis and degradation of transitory starch in leaves also modulate endosperm starch accumulation, ultimately influencing grain quality and yield. 

Transitory starch biosynthesis in chloroplasts begins with ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ fixation via the Calvin cycle, producing triose phosphates that are subsequently converted to fructose-6-phosphate via a series of enzymatic reactions. Fructose-6-phosphate is further catalyzed into glucose-6-phosphate, glucose-1-phosphate, and ultimately adenosine diphosphate glucose, the direct precursor for amylose and amylopectin synthesis mediated by multiple starch-synthesis-related enzymes (Umemoto et al., 2002; Satoh et al., 2003; Bahaji et al., 2014). In Arabidopsis, the starch-binding protein PTST2 was shown to interact with soluble starch synthase 4, mediate its binding to substrate glucan, and initiate the formation of starch particles in leaves (Seung et al., 2017). Similarly, AtPTST1 directs GBSS to starch particles for amylose synthesis, and mutation of PTST1 blocks amylose synthesis in leaves (Seung et al., 2015). In rice, GBSS-binding protein 1 (OsGBP1), an ortholog of AtPTST1, was demonstrated to mediate the binding of granule-bound starch synthase I (GBSS I) and GBSSII to developing starch granules (SGs) in both leaves and endosperm, thereby influencing transitory and storage starch biosynthesis as well as grain quality (Wang et al., 2020a). A recent study indicated that two starch-binding proteins, OsLESV and OsESV1, jointly maintain the stability and enzymatic activity of GBSSI and GBSSII, assist their targeting to SGs, and thus regulate the biosynthesis of both transitory and storage starch, ultimately affecting rice grain quality and yield (Dong et al., 2024). However, the detailed molecular mechanisms underlying transitory starch biosynthesis in rice leaves and its precise contribution to grain quality and yield remain unresolved. 

Glutamyl–tRNA reductase (GluTR) plays an essential role in chlorophyll biosynthesis. It catalyzes the reduction of glutamyl–tRNA to glutamic-1-semialdehyde, which is then converted into the chlorophyll precursor 5-aminolevulinic acid by glutamic-1-semialdehyde aminotransferase (Verkamp et al., 1992; Bougri and Grimm, 1996; Vothknecht et al., 1996, 1998; Zeng et al., 2020). In rice, mutations in OsHemA (the gene encoding GluTR) result in a pale-green leaf phenotype accompanied by pronounced reductions in both chlorophyll and carotenoid contents (Zeng et al., 2020). Thus far, the potential role of GluTR in transitory starch metabolism in leaves, and its downstream impact on grain quality and yield, has not been investigated. 

In this study, we characterized a novel leaf starch-deficiency mutant, lsd3. Genetic and molecular analyses demonstrated that LSD3 encodes GluTR and physically interacts with GluTRbinding protein (GluTRBP) to form a molecular module. This module maintains the stability and enzymatic activity of GBSSII, contributing to the biosynthesis of leaf transitory starch and ultimately influencing rice grain quality and yield. Moreover, we found that LSD3 and GluTRBP play critical roles in source–sink carbohydrate partitioning in rice. These findings advance the understanding of the molecular mechanisms underlying rice leaf transitory starch biosynthesis and provide valuable insights for the genetic improvement of grain quality and yield.

# LSD3 is involved in transitory starch biosynthesis in rice

To investigate the molecular mechanism of leaf transitory starch biosynthesis in rice, we identified a novel mutant, leaf starch deficiency 3 (lsd3), from an ethyl methanesulfonate (EMS)-induced mutant library of the japonica cultivar Zhonghua11 (ZH11). Iodine staining of leaves revealed that the color intensity of lsd3 was markedly lighter than that of the wild type, indicating reduced starch accumulation in lsd3 leaves (Figure 1A). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) analyses further showed a pronounced reduction in both the size and number of SGs within chloroplasts of lsd3 mesophyll cells relative to the wild type (Figure 1B and 1C). Quantitative assays of total starch and amylose contents in leaves at the end of the day confirmed that both were significantly lower in lsd3 (Figure 1D and 1E). Additionally, lsd3 leaves displayed a pale-green phenotype associated with reduced chlorophyll content (Supplemental Figure 1A and 1B). 

To clone the gene responsible for the lsd3 mutant phenotype, we first screened the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population derived from a cross between lsd3 and Nanjing11 (an indica variety); we selected 126 individuals with leaf iodine staining similar to that of lsd3. The mutation locus was initially mapped to the long arm of chromosome 10, between the markers RM25664 and RM228 (Figure 1F). Subsequently, we employed the Mut-Map strategy to identify the candidate gene for LSD3. A total of seven single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) with an index ${ \ge } 0 . 8$ were detected on chromosome 10, among which only one SNP (C to T) with an index near 1.0 was located within the primary mapping interval (Figure 1F; Supplemental Figure 2A and 2B). This SNP was located in the third exon of $L O C \_ O s 1 0 g 3 5 8 4 0$ in lsd3 and caused a leucine (L484) to phenylalanine (F484) substitution in the encoded protein (Figure 1F). The protein encoded by LOC_Os10g35840 has been annotated as rice glutamyl–tRNA reductase (GluTR), a 538–amino-acid protein comprising the GluTR-N, NADPH-binding, and dimerization domains. The L484F substitution in the lsd3 mutant occurs within the dimerization domain of GluTR, potentially impairing dimer formation and disrupting normal function (Supplemental Figure 2C–2E). To confirm that LOC_Os10g35840 is responsible for the lsd3 phenotype, we constructed a complementary vector containing its full-length genomic sequence with the native promoter and introduced it into the lsd3 mutant. We also generated two independent CRISPR–Cas9-edited mutants, lsd3-cr1 and lsd3-cr2, in the ZH11 background (Supplemental Figure 2F). In the complementary lines (Com-1, Com-2), iodine staining intensity, the number of SGs per chloroplast in mesophyll cells, leaf coloration, chlorophyll content, LSD3 transcript levels, and leaf total starch and amylose contents were restored to wild-type levels. In contrast, lsd3-cr1 and lsd3-cr2 showed a leaf starch-deficiency phenotype similar to that of the lsd3 mutant (Figure 1A– 1E and 1G; Supplemental Figure 1A and 1B). Therefore, the gene LOC_Os10g35840 (LSD3), encoding GluTR, is responsible for the phenotypic defects of lsd3.

# GluTRBP interacts with LSD3 and regulates transitory starch biosynthesis in rice

To further investigate the mechanism of LSD3 function in rice leaf starch biosynthesis, we identified GluTRBP (LOC_Os08g15500), which interacts with LSD3 in yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) assays (Figure 2A; Supplemental Figure 3B and 3C). # LSD3–GluTRBP module regulates endosperm starch biosynthesis and affects rice quality and grain yield

We next examined grain quality– and yield-related traits in lsd3 and glutrbp mutants along with their wild-type counterpart. Compared with the wild type, mature grains of both lsd3 and glutrbp displayed a significantly higher chalky-grain rate. Notably, glutrbp grains exhibited an almost completely flouryendosperm phenotype (Figure 3A and 3C). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) revealed that mature endosperm of the wild type was densely packed with polyhedral SGs, whereas SGs in lsd3 and glutrbp were irregularly spherical and loosely arranged (Figure 3B). The total starch and amylose contents in the endosperm of lsd3 and glutrbp were significantly lower than in the wild type, while protein and soluble sugar levels were markedly elevated. These alterations were more pronounced in glutrbp than in lsd3 (Figure 3D–3G). In contrast, the grain appearance, SG morphology, and contents of starch, protein, and soluble sugar in the endosperm of LSD3 complementary lines were restored to wild-type levels (Supplemental Figure 4A–4G). We also analyzed the pasting properties of endosperm starch using a rapid viscosity analyzer. The starch pasting properties of lsd3 and glutrbp were significantly reduced compared with the wild type; glutrbp showed the lowest viscosity (Figure 3H). # Mutation of LSD3 and GluTRBP compromises source– sink carbohydrate partitioning

Before heading, a portion of the photosynthates in leaves is converted into transitory starch, which provides energy for plant growth and development through its subsequent catabolism. Excess photoassimilates are translocated to the stem sheath and converted into starch for temporary storage. After heading, this starch in the stem sheath is degraded and transported to the grains as sucrose, where it is ultimately synthesized into storage starch in the rice endosperm (Yang et al., 2001; Smith and Zeeman, 2020b). We examined the starch and non-structural carbohydrate (NSC) contents in the stem sheaths of lsd3, glutrbp, and the wild type. Iodine staining of semi-thin sections from seedling bases revealed that both lsd3 and glutrbp accumulated fewer and smaller SGs compared with the wild type (Figure 4A). Furthermore, SEM was used to observe SG accumulation in the internodes of lsd3, glutrbp, and wild type at three developmental stages: before grain filling (5 days before flowering [− 5 DAF]), during grain filling (10 days after flowering [10 DAF]), and at the end of grain filling (25 DAF). In the wild type, internode cells were densely packed with SGs before grain filling; SGs gradually decreased during grain filling, and by the end of grain filling (25 DAF), almost none remained (Figure 4B). In contrast, lsd3 and glutrbp internode cells stored very few SGs prior to grain filling, showed only limited depletion during grain filling, and still contained detectable SGs at the end of grain filling (Figure 4B). Quantitative measurements of NSC, starch, and sucrose contents in stems further supported these observations. Before grain filling, NSC, starch, and sucrose levels in lsd3 and glutrbp stems were significantly lower than those in the wild type. After grain filling, although NSC, starch, and sucrose contents in the mutants decreased markedly compared with their pre-filling levels, they remained significantly higher than those of the wild type (Figure 4C–4E). These results suggest that lsd3 and glutrbp plants not only stored less carbon in the stem sheath but also exhibited a reduced capacity to mobilize stored carbon for dry matter (primarily starch) accumulation in grains.

# Overexpression of GBSSII partly rescues the lsd3 and glutrbp phenotypes

Given that the LSD3–GluTRBP module likely regulates leaf transitory starch biosynthesis through interaction with GBSSII, we overexpressed GBSSII in lsd3 and glutrbp mutant backgrounds under the control of the maize Ubiquitin 1 promoter (Figure 6A and 6B). GBSSII overexpression (OE) lines displayed darker green leaves and significantly higher chlorophyll contents compared with lsd3 and glutrbp mutants, although these traits did not fully return to wild-type levels (Supplemental Figure 10A–10D). Iodine staining and starch quantification assays confirmed that leaf starch contents in the OE plants were significantly higher than those of the respective mutants and were either near or comparable to wild-type levels (Figure 6C–6E). 

Additionally, compared with lsd3 and glutrbp, the GBSSII OE lines showed a significantly lower chalky-grain rate and degree of chalkiness, although these traits were not fully restored to wild-type values (Figure 6F–6H, 6L, and 6M). Total starch and amylose contents in the endosperm of OE lines were also significantly elevated, exhibiting full or partial recovery to wild-type levels (Figure 6I, 6J, 6N, and 6O). The 1000-grain weight of OE lines in the glutrbp background was restored to wild-type levels, whereas no significant recovery was observed in the lsd3 background (Figure 6K and 6P). Furthermore, we overexpressed GBSSII in the ZH11 background (Supplemental Figure 11A). These OE lines accumulated significantly more total starch in both leaves and endosperm compared with the wild type (Supplemental Figure 11B, 11D, and 11F). However, endosperm amylose content did not significantly differ from the wild type (Supplemental Figure 11G). The OE lines showed a significantly higher chalky-grain rate and increased 1000-grain weight (Supplemental Figure 11C, 11E, and 11L). Nevertheless, due to a reduction in grain number per panicle, no significant difference in yield per plant was detected between OE lines and the wild type (Supplemental Figure 11J and 11M). These results indicate that GBSSII plays an essential role in starch biosynthesis in both rice leaves and endosperm, and that OE of GBSSII can fully or partially rescue the starch-deficient phenotypes of lsd3 and glutrbp mutants. Furthermore, our findings establish that the LSD3– GluTRBP module functions upstream of GBSSII, modulating its protein stability and enzymatic activity to regulate leaf transitory starch biosynthesis, thereby influencing grain quality and yield in rice.

Although the biosynthesis and regulation of storage starch in rice endosperm are relatively well characterized, the biosynthesis of transitory starch in leaves and its effect on rice quality and yield remain insufficiently investigated. In Arabidopsis, the CBM48-domain proteins AtPTST1 and AtPTST2 play critical roles in leaf amylose biosynthesis; mutations in either gene disrupt this process (Seung et al., 2015). AtESV1 and AtLESV also participate in leaf starch metabolism; AtESV1 may regulate leaf starch degradation by modulating the activities of glucan water dikinase and phosphoglucan water dikinase (Feike et al., 2016; Malinova et al., 2018). In contrast, the molecular mechanisms underlying transitory starch biosynthesis and metabolism in rice leaves, and their contributions to grain yield and quality, remain less clearly defined. 

In this study, we demonstrated that rice GluTR (LSD3) and its interacting protein GluTRBP coordinately regulate the biosynthesis of both leaf transitory starch and endosperm-storage starch. The lsd3 and glutrbp mutants exhibited significantly reduced total starch and amylose contents in both leaves and endosperm, increased grain chalkiness, and decreased 1000-grain weight and grain yield per plant (Figures 1A–1E, 2E–2I, 3A–3E, 3M, and 3N). The physicochemical properties of endosperm starch were also markedly altered compared with the wild type (Figure 3H and 3I). Notably, the EMSinduced lsd3 mutant and gene-edited glutrbp mutants exhibited more severe phenotypes, including a higher chalkygrain rate and greater reductions in endosperm starch and amylose, than the gene-edited mutants lsd3-cr1 and lsd3-cr2 (Figures 1D, 1E, 2H, 2I, and 3A–3E; Supplemental Figure 4). These phenotypic differences may reflect variations in mutation type, genomic location, or mutational strength (Figure 1F and 1G; Supplemental Figures 2F, 3F, and 3G). We also attempted to generate LSD3–GluTRBP double mutants but were unsuccessful despite repeated efforts. Given that both LSD3 and GluTRBP are single-copy genes in the rice genome (Supplemental Figures 2E and 3C), this outcome suggests that the double mutant is lethal. Alternatively, complete loss of function of either LSD3 or GluTRBP alone could induce lethality; this hypothesis requires further investigation. Other rice proteins such as OsLESV, OsESV1 (homologs of AtESV1 and AtLESV), and OsGBP1 (GBSSI Binding Protein 1, the homolog of AtPTST1) have also been reported to regulate transitory starch biosynthesis. Mutants of these genes showed impaired starch biosynthesis in both leaves and endosperm, along with reduced rice quality and yield (Wang et al., 2020a; Dong et al., 2024). Together, these findings highlight lsd3 and glutrbp as valuable models for dissecting the molecular mechanisms of transitory starch biosynthesis and its influence on endosperm starch metabolism and grain quality. Further studies of the LSD3– GluTRBP module will enhance our understanding of transitory starch biosynthesis and source–sink carbon allocation in rice. 

# The LSD3–GluTRBP module stabilizes GBSSII to promote leaf starch biosynthesis

Both endosperm-storage starch and leaf transitory starch consist of amylose and amylopectin. In rice, GBSSI, encoded by the Waxy gene, is responsible for amylose biosynthesis in the endosperm (Martin and Smith, 1995), whereas GBSSII mediates amylose biosynthesis in leaves (Vrinten and Nakamura, 2000a). Consistent with these functions, we found that mutation of GBSSII significantly reduced both total starch and amylose contents in rice leaves and endosperm (Figure 5J–5M, 5Q, and 5R), whereas OE of GBSSII markedly increased leaf starch content (Supplemental Figure 11A, 11B, and 11D). Numerous transcription factors and non-enzymatic proteins have been reported to regulate endosperm amylose biosynthesis through diverse mechanisms, including activating or repressing Waxy transcription, modulating Waxy pre-mRNA splicing efficiency, mediating GBSSI binding to developing SGs, or maintaining GBSSI protein stability and enzymatic activity (Wang et al., 2013, 2020a; Dong et al., 2024). In contrast, mechanisms that regulate leaf amylose biosynthesis via GBSSII remain poorly characterized. In this study, we showed that GluTRBP directly interacts with GBSSII (Figure 5A–5C) and, together with LSD3, stabilizes GBSSII and enhances its enzymatic activity (Figure 5F–5I); notably, LSD3 itself does not directly interact with GBSSII (Figure 5A; Supplemental Figure 8A). Mutation of either GluTRBP or LSD3 phenocopied the gbssII mutant, leading to significant reductions in amylose and total starch contents in both leaves and endosperm (Figures 1A–1E, 2E–2J, 3A–3E, and 5J–5R). Importantly, OE of GBSSII fully or partially rescued the starch-deficient phenotypes of lsd3 and glutrbp mutants (Figure 6), confirming that GBSSII acts downstream of the LSD3–GluTRBP module. The LSD3– GluTRBP module shares functional similarities with OsLESV and OsESV1, which also interact with GBSSII to maintain its stability and activity in regulating leaf amylose and total starch biosynthesis. Additionally, OsLESV and OsESV1 can interact with GBSSI to stabilize the protein and assist both GBSSI and GBSSII in targeting SGs (Dong et al., 2024). These findings suggest that the LSD3–GluTRBP module plays a distinct role in regulating transitory starch biosynthesis in leaves upstream of GBSSII (Figure 7). 

LSD3 encodes rice GluTR (Supplemental Figure 2C–2E), an enzyme that catalyzes the NADPH-dependent reduction of glutamyl–tRNA. This reaction represents a rate-limiting step in ALA formation and chlorophyll biosynthesis (Verkamp et al., 1992; Bougri and Grimm, 1996; Vothknecht et al., 1996, 1998; Zeng et al., 2020). GluTR activity is stringently regulated at both transcriptional and posttranslational levels (Tanaka and Tanaka, 2007). In Arabidopsis, caseinolytic protease (Clp) substrate adaptor ClpS1, the chaperone ClpC1, and GluTR-binding protein (GBP) interact with GluTR. The Clp protease system mediates GluTR1 degradation, whereas GBP protects GluTR1 from Clp-mediated degradation, and the absence of GBP decreases GluTR1 stability (Apitz et al., 2016). Consistent with previous reports (Zeng et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2022), lsd3 and glutrbp mutants exhibited pale-green leaves and reduced chlorophyll contents compared with the wild type (Supplemental Figures 1A, 1B, 3H, and 3I). # The LSD3–GluTRBP module influences carbon-source allocation in rice

The efficient distribution and utilization of photosynthetic assimilates between sources and sinks are essential for maximizing crop yield and ensuring grain quality. Photoassimilates, primarily in the form of sucrose, are transported long distances to sink organs via phloem sieve tubes (Eom et al., 2012; Wu et al., 2021). In rice, excess photoassimilates are transiently stored as starch in stems and leaf sheaths before grain filling. During the grain-filling stage, these starch reserves are degraded into sucrose and delivered to the endosperm for storage-starch biosynthesis (Yang et al., 2001; Smith and Zeeman, 2020b). In this study, lsd3 and glutrbp mutants contained significantly lower levels of NSCs, starch, and sucrose in stem sheaths prior to grain filling, likely due to impaired leaf transitory-starch biosynthesis (Figure 4A–4E). Interestingly, after grain filling, residual NSC, starch, and sucrose in mutant stems were significantly reduced compared with pre-filling levels but remained markedly higher than in the wild type (Figure 4A–4E). This finding suggests that the limited carbon reserves stored in the leaf sheaths and stems of lsd3 and glutrbp plants cannot be efficiently mobilized for grain dry-matter (mainly starch) accumulation. This conclusion was further supported by leaf luciferin feeding experiments, which demonstrated weakened allocation of carbohydrates from leaves through stems to grains in lsd3 and glutrbp mutants (Figure 4F and 4G). We speculate that transport of carbohydrate (flow) is impaired in lsd3 and glutrbp mutants. Alternatively, this phenomenon could reflect a reduced carbon demand in the grain (sink) of 

the mutants. Further investigations will be necessary to resolve these possibilities. 

Carbohydrate transport in plants is mediated by specialized proteins: sucrose transporters (SUTs) facilitate apoplastic sucrose unloading (Julius et al., 2017; Nieberl et al., 2017; Braun, 2022), whereas SWEET proteins mediate sucrose efflux from phloem parenchyma cells into the apoplast (Chen, 2014; Braun, 2022). Additionally, cell-wall invertase, encoded by GIF1, catalyzes the hydrolysis of sucrose into glucose and fructose, representing a key step in sucrose unloading (Wang et al., 2008). We found that the expression of SUTs, OsSWEETs, and GIF1 was significantly downregulated in the lsd3 and glutrbp mutants (Figure 4H), which may contribute to their reduced carbonsource transport efficiency. Collectively, these results indicate that the LSD3–GluTRBP module plays an important role in carbon-source allocation in rice, although the detailed regulatory mechanisms remain to be clarified. 

In conclusion, we demonstrate that the LSD3–GluTRBP module regulates transitory starch biosynthesis in rice leaves by maintaining the protein stability and enzymatic activity of GBSSII. This module also influences the allocation of carbon sources between source and sink organs, ultimately affecting rice grain yield and quality (Figure 7). Our findings advance the understanding of both transitory starch biosynthesis and source–sink relationships in rice and provide a theoretical foundation for the genetic improvement of high-yield, highquality rice varieties. 

# METHODS

# Plant materials and growth conditions

The lsd3 mutant was induced by EMS in the japonica rice cultivar Zhonghua11 (ZH11). CRISPR–Cas9 mutants of LSD3, GluTRBP, and GBSSII were generated in the ZH11 background. All plants used in this study were cultivated either in paddy fields or in a greenhouse at the China National Rice Research Institute, Hangzhou, China. 

# Mapping of the LSD3 gene

A total of 126 individuals with lighter iodine-stained leaves resembling lsd3 were selected from the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population derived from lsd3 $\times$ Nanjing11 (indica) to initiate primary mapping of the LSD3 locus. Polymorphic simple sequence repeat markers were used to link the locus to LSD3. The Mut-Map method was subsequently applied to refine the mapping. A recessive pool of 50 individuals with lighter iodine-stained leaves was selected from the $\mathsf { F } _ { 2 }$ population derived from $1 s d 3 \times 2 \mathsf { H } 1 1$ , and ZH11 served as the dominant pool. Whole-genome sequencing of both pools was conducted on the Illumina HiSeq PE150 platform (Novogene, Beijing, China) to identify candidate genes. The primers used for mapping are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Vector construction and plant transformation

For complementation experiments, the genomic sequence of LSD3, including its native promoter, was amplified from ZH11 and cloned into the pCAMBIA1300 vector. To obtain gene-edited mutants of LSD3, GluTRBP, and GBSSII, the corresponding single guide RNA target sites were inserted into the BGK03 vector (Biogle, Hangzhou, China). The GBSSII cDNA sequence, driven by the 35S promoter, was cloned into the binary pKK-35S vector (Biorun, Wuhan, China) to construct the OE vector. The complementation vector was transformed into lsd3, the CRISPR–Cas9 vectors were introduced into ZH11, and the GBSSII OE vector was transformed into lsd3-cr1, glutrbp-1, and ZH11 by Agrobacterium-mediated transformation. Primer sequences used for vector construction and detection are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Microscopy analysis

The morphology and number of SGs in chloroplasts of mesophyll cells from flag leaves were examined using TEM (Hitachi H-7650, Tokyo, Japan). To assess SG morphology in the endosperm, cross-sections of mature grains were analyzed by SEM (Hitachi SU-8010, Tokyo, Japan). Semi-thin sections of seedling bases were prepared and stained with $\mathsf { I } _ { 2 } \mathrm { - } \mathsf { K } \mathsf { I }$ , and SGs accumulated in leaf sheaths were observed under a light microscope. Transverse sections of the middle stem internode were collected at early, middle, and late stages of grain filling, and SGs temporarily stored in stem parenchyma cells were observed by SEM. 

# Iodine staining and determination of NSC, starch, and sucrose contents in leaf and stem

The starch content of rice flag leaves or leaves of 1-month-old seedlings at the end of the day was analyzed by iodine staining, following the method of Dong et al. (2024), to quantify NSC, total starch, amylose, and sucrose. Flag leaves or 1-month-old seedling leaves and the main stem (excluding the panicle and top node) were ground into powder with liquid nitrogen and oven-dried at $1 0 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to a constant weight. NSC and sucrose contents were determined using a total sugar assay kit (BoxBio, Beijing, China) and a plant sucrose assay kit (BoxBio), respectively. Total starch content was measured with a starch detection kit (Megazyme K-TSTA; Wicklow, Ireland). For amylose determination, SGs were first extracted from leaves or stems, and the percentage of amylose in total starch was quantified using an amylose detection kit (Megazyme K-AMYL). 

# Analyses of physicochemical properties of endosperm starch

The total starch and amylose contents of mature grains were measured using a starch determination kit, K-TSTA (Megazyme), and an amylose detection kit, K-AMYL (Megazyme), respectively. Protein and soluble sugar contents were determined according to the methods of Fang et al. (2022). To assess starch pasting properties, 3 g of endosperm flour was weighed, mixed in an aluminum tank with $2 5 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of ${ \mathsf { d o H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ , and analyzed for gelatinization characteristics using a rapid viscosity analyzer (Newport Scientific, Narrabeen, Australia). To determine the chain-length distribution of amylopectin, 5 mg of endosperm flour was digested with isozymes (Megazyme) and subsequently analyzed using a PA800 Plus capillary electrophoresis system (Beckman Coulter, Brea, CA, USA). 

# RNA extraction and RT–qPCR analysis

Total RNA was extracted from different plant tissues using TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA). RNA was reverse transcribed into cDNA with the ReverTra Ace qPCR RT kit (Tokyo, Osaka, Japan). qPCR was performed using SYBR Green Real-Time PCR Master Mix (Toyobo). The Ubiquitin gene (Os03g0234200) served as the internal reference. Primers used for qPCR are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Subcellular localization

The coding sequences of LSD3 and GluTRBP without termination codons were cloned into pAN580 vectors. The full-length GBSSII sequence, also lacking a stop codon, was cloned into the pA7- CFP vector for co-localization analysis. The fusion constructs and control vector were expressed in rice protoplasts. Fluorescence signals were observed using confocal laser scanning microscopy (Carl Zeiss, Jena, Germany). The primers used are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# Y2H, BiFC, pull-down, and co-IP assays

For Y2H assays, the coding sequences of LSD3, GluTRBP, and GBSSII were cloned into pGADT7 (prey) or pGBKT7 (bait) vectors, and the fusion constructs were transferred into the GAL4 Y2H system (Clontech, Dalian, China). For BiFC assays, the coding sequences of LSD3, GluTRBP, and GBSSII were inserted into cCFP or nCFP vectors. The resulting constructs were introduced into rice protoplasts, and fluorescence was observed using confocal laser scanning microscopy (Carl Zeiss). For in vitro pull-down assays, the cDNA sequences of LSD3 and GBSSII were cloned into the pGEX-4T-1 vector to generate GST–LSD3 and GST–GBSSII fusion constructs, respectively. The cDNA sequence of GluTRBP was cloned into the pET28a vector to generate a His–GluTRBP fusion construct. The above fusion vectors were expressed in Escherichia coli DE3 cells for in vitro protein purification. For the pull-down assay method, we followed the protocol of Zhang et al. (2015). For co-IP assays, to verify the interaction between LSD3 and GluTRBP in vivo, 35S:LSD3–GFP and Ubi:1390–GluTRBP–FLAG fusion vectors were constructed and transiently co-expressed in rice protoplasts. Total proteins extracted from protoplasts were immunoprecipitated using anti-GFP magnetic beads (AT0089). Input and co-immunoprecipitated proteins were detected with anti-FLAG (Sigma-Aldrich) and anti-GFP (Sigma-Aldrich)

antibodies, respectively. The interaction between GluTRBP and GBSSII was tested via the same methods, using 35S:GluTRBP– GFP and Ubi:GBSSII–FLAG fusion vectors. Primers utilized for vector construction in these experiments are listed in Supplemental Table 1. 

# 5,6-CFDA feeding experiment

To compare photoassimilate uptake in the panicles of wild type and mutant plants, panicles with flag leaves were placed in water, and the flag leaves were incubated in an aqueous solution containing 5,6-CFDA $( 5 0 \mu \ g / \mu \ l )$ for 24 h at room temperature. Fluorescence signals in flag leaves, flag leaf sheaths, stems, panicle neck nodes, and hulls were then examined under a fluorescence microscope. 

# Western blot and GBSS enzyme activity analysis

For western blotting, the tip of the flag leaf was ground into powder with liquid nitrogen and placed in a $1 . 5 \mathrm { - } \mathsf { m } |$ centrifuge tube containing $8 0 0 ~ \mu \up$ of extraction buffer for ice-bath homogenization, followed by centrifugation to obtain total protein. Equal amounts of total protein were separated by sodium dodecyl sulfate–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS–PAGE) and transferred to a polyvinylidene fluoride membrane. $\beta$ -actin served as an internal control, and immunoblotting was performed using appropriate antibodies. GBSS enzyme activity was measured using total protein extracts from flag leaves, following the manufacturer’s instructions for the commercial GBSS assay kit (Solarbio, Beijing, China). 

# Cell-free degradation assay

The cell-free degradation assay was performed as described by Wang et al. (2020b). Briefly, $2 0 0 ~ { \mu \ g }$ of leaf-protein extract was incubated with $2 \mu \ g$ of purified GST- or His-tagged recombinant protein in degradation buffer. The protease inhibitor MG132 (Sigma-Aldrich) was added to suppress protein degradation. The abundances of LSD3, GluTRBP, and GBSSII were monitored by immunoblotting at designated time points; $\beta$ -actin was used as the loading control.
