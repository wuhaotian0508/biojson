# a b s t r a c t

Higher-plant chloroplast membranes are composed primarily of four characteristic lipids, namely monogalactosyldiacylglycerol, digalactosyldiacylglycerol, sulfoquinovosyldiacylglycerol (SQDG), and phosphatidylglycerol. Among them, SQDG is the only sulfur-containing anionic glycerolipid and is the least prevalent component of photosynthetic membrane lipids. SQDG biosynthesis is mostly mediated by UDP-sulfoquinovose synthase (SQD1) and SQDG synthase (SQD2). Recently, another essential gene for SQDG synthesis, UGP3, was identified using transcriptome coexpression analysis and reverse genetics. UGP3 is a novel plastid UDP-glucose pyrophosphorylase that supplies UDP-glucose to SQD1 in plastids. In Arabidopsis, SQDG is dispensable under normal growth conditions but important in certain environments, particularly phosphate-depleted conditions. The function of SQDG under phosphate-limited growth conditions is highly correlated with the regulation of other plant glycerolipid biosyntheses. This review summarizes recent research defining the mechanism for SQDG biosynthesis and its biological function in higher plants, particularly under phosphate-starved conditions.

# 1. Introduction

In many eukaryotic and prokaryotic species, cell membranes are mainly composed of phospholipids. However, in the chloroplasts from mosses, ferns, algae, and plants, as well as the photosynthetic membranes of some bacteria (and all cyanobacteria), phospholipids constitute a minor proportion of membrane lipids. Instead, the following four lipids comprise the major proportion of photosynthetic tissues: two galactolipids, monogalactosyldiacylglycerol

The three glycolipids, MGDG, DGDG, and SQDG, constitute about $9 0 \%$ of chloroplast membrane lipids [1], maintaining photosynthetic activity by being directly assembled into the photosynthetic machinery or forming the thylakoid membrane structure for photosynthesis [2]. PG is the only significant thylakoid membrane phospholipid and is essential for the photosynthetic ability of higher plants and cyanobacteria [3–5]. In plants and bacteria, the relative proportion of lipids often changes depending on growth conditions. Thus, the balance of all membrane lipids should be considered when discussing plant SQDG.

The sulfolipid SQDG is a relatively minor lipid compared to the galactosylglycerides comprising about $4 \mathrm { - } 7 \%$ of total leaf lipids. However, the presence of SQDG does not always correlate with photosynthetic ability [11–18]. SQDG was later shown to be conditionally important under certain environmental conditions.

SQDG was discovered by A.A. Benson in the late 1950s using radioisotope labeling [19]. One of the remarkable characteristics of this lipid is its unique sugar head group, a 6-deoxy-6-sulfoglucose, referred to as sulfoquinovose (Fig. 1). Over the past 50 years, many experiments have been directed to elucidating the plant sulfolipid biosynthetic pathway. In the years following SQDG discovery, Benson and his colleagues conducted most of the SQDG research, and substantial progress was made [20,21]. The SQDG structure was thus determined as 6-sulfo-6-deoxy-a-D-glucosyl (1->10 )-20 -30 -di-O-acyl-D-glycerol, which was later confirmed by mass spectrometric analysis [22–24]. The biosynthetic intermediate sulfoquinovose-nucleotide sugar was observed in Chlorella soluble extract after labeling with $[ ^ { 3 5 } \mathsf { S } ]$ -sulfate [25]. The sulfoquinovose sugar nucleotide was suggested to be formed from a 4- keto-6-deoxy-a-D-glucose nucleotide or similar molecule [26], and sulfoquinovose was chemically synthesized by sulfite and methyl 5,6-glucoseenide [27]. After an almost 30-year interval, a more detailed hypothesis of the sugar nucleotide biosynthetic pathway was proposed based on genetic experiments [28,29]. A sugar nucleotide-modifying enzyme was clearly shown to be crucial for sulfolipid synthesis in the purple bacterium Rhodobacter sphaeroides [28,29]. This finding was the first breakthrough in identifying genes involved in the SQDG biosynthetic pathway. Previous reviews are recommended for more details regarding SQDG [8– 10,21,30–37].

# 2. Biosynthesis of sulfoquinovosyldiacylglycerol

Early studies widely perceived chloroplasts to be capable of synthesizing SQDG if exogenous sulfate was supplied to the chloroplasts [38–46]. Therefore, chloroplasts were thought to contain all of the necessary enzymes and substrates for producing SQDG. This hypothesis was confirmed by measuring SQDG synthase activity in chloroplast envelopes using an artificially generated sulfur donor substrate [47]. Using that assay method, SQDG synthase localization was determined to be on the stroma side of the chloroplast inner envelope membrane [48,49]. Although the details of the SQDG biosynthetic pathway had been proposed [44], final confirmation required that the corresponding enzymes were identified at the molecular level and then biochemically characterized. The first

breakthrough was made by genetic analyses in R. sphaeroides, which identified four genes (sqdA, sqdB, sqdC, and sqdD) were determined to be necessary for SQDG biosynthesis [28,29]. Among them, SqdB was similar to enzymes that modify the nucleotide sugar moiety, and sqdB is the only gene that has apparent orthologs in every SQDG-producing organism—namely, in the cyanobacterium Synechococcus sp. PCC 7942 [50], in plants such as Arabidopsis and spinach [51,52], and in the alga Chlamydomonas reinhardtii [53,54].

In plants and algae, the sqdB ortholog was designated SQD1. In Arabidopsis and spinach, SQD1 is a soluble enzyme that localizes to the chloroplast stroma [51,52]. The reaction mechanism for SQDG synthesis was proposed based on the reaction intermediate and the structural similarity between Arabidopsis SQD1 (AtSQD1) and other sugar nucleotide-modifying enzymes [44,55]. This mechanism was later supported by detailed structural analysis of recombinant AtSQD1 using X-ray crystallography, which demonstrated that AtSQD1 is a dimeric protein containing $\mathsf { N A D } ^ { + }$ and UDP-glucose, but biochemical evidence for the mechanism remained unknown [56]. In 2001, the synthesis of UDP-sulfoquinovose from UDP-glucose and sulfite catalyzed by SQD1 was finally demonstrated by biochemical analysis of recombinant Arabidopsis SQD1 expressed in Escherichia coli [57]. However, because the reaction rate of the recombinant enzyme was much slower than expected, it was hypothesized that proteins and/or cofactors might be required, or that the actual in vivo sulfur donor was not sulfite [57].

To further understand SQD1 activity in vivo, spinach SQD1 was purified from the chloroplast stroma and identified as a 250-kDa complex [52]. The affinity for sulfite was at least 4-fold higher than recombinant spinach SQD1, whereas that for UDP-glucose was similar. Further analyses clearly showed that ferredoxin-dependent glutamate synthase (FdGOGAT) co-purified and was tightly associated with SQD1 as shown in Fig. 2 [58]. FdGOGAT and SQD1 proteins are both present in the same subcellular compartment, the chloroplast stroma [51,52,59,60]. FdGOGAT is a flavincontaining protein that can reversibly bind sulfite, suggesting that the protein efficiently delivers sulfite to the SQD1 active site (Fig. 2B) [61,62]. This was also supported by tentative modeling of cyanobacterial FdGOGAT and AtSQD1 into a plausible complex (Fig. 2A) [58]. In this model, the predicted SQD1 sulfite channel is directed at the FMN cofactor in the FdGOGAT FMN-binding domain. Sulfite is a cytotoxic compound and is maintained at a very low level in plant cells. Therefore, FdGOGAT interaction with SQD1 would channel sulfite directly to SQD1 and would be an efficient way to overcome this problem. Indeed, leaf SQDG content is decreased by about $2 0 { - } 2 5 ~ \mathrm { m o l \% }$ of total lipids in glu1 knockout mutants compared to wild type (Shimojima et al., unpublished data). The amount of FdGOGAT is estimated to be 10-fold higher than that of SQD1 in plastids [58]. Therefore, most of the FdGOGAT may play a role as a glutamate synthase without SQD1 interaction, but all SQD1 proteins interact with FdGOGAT because, at least in spinach, SQD1 only exists as a 250-kDa complex [58]. However, the affinity of FdGOGAT for sulfite could be too low to explain the efficient binding and delivery of sulfite to SQD1 [62,63]. Recently, FdGOGAT1 was demonstrated to be targeted to both mitochondria and the chloroplasts, and in the mitochondria FdGOGAT physically interacts with mitochondrial serine hydroxymethyltransferase, and this interaction is necessary for photorespiratory enzymatic activity [64]. Considering that FdGOGAT has several moonlighting functions other than in glutamate synthesis, we must carefully examine the possible role for FdGOGAT in SQDG biosynthesis.

This gene was found in an operon, in which another unknown sqdX gene was found. In R. sphaeroides, sqdX was distinct from the SQDG synthase gene sqdD but similar to other lipid glycosyltransferases [67]. Analyses of an sqdX knockout mutant of Synechococcus sp. PCC 7942 clearly showed the lack of SQDG, and thus SqdX was identified to be cyanobacterial SQDG synthase [66]. Later, the sqdX ortholog was found in plants and designated SQD2 [65]. The Arabidopsis sqd2 knockout mutant showed complete loss of SQDG, and biochemical analyses of recombinant SQD2 protein produced SQDG by coexpression with SQD1, clearly indicating that SQD2 is SQDG synthase in plants [64]. SQD2 was also confirmed to be localized inside the plastid [65], supporting previous biochemical data [48,49].

The substrate for SQD1 is UDP-glucose, and SQD1 is localized inside plastids [57]. The source of UDP-glucose has been a longstanding question because of the very low UDP-glucose level in plastids [30,68]. Recently, the gene involved in UDP-glucose synthesis in plastids was identified using transcriptome coexpression analysis [69]. The previously uncharacterized UGP3, which is highly coexpressed with SQD1 and SQD2, was selected from the ATTEDII database using a coexpression gene search program on the RIKEN PRIMe website [70–72]. The lack of SQDG in the Arabidopsis ugp3 knockout mutant was confirmed either under normal or phosphate-depleted growth conditions [69]. UGP3 is a plastid UDP-glucose pyrophosphorylase that converts glucose-1-phosphate to UDP-glucose in the stroma [69]. Although UDP-glucose is an important metabolite used to synthesize various kinds of glycosides and saccharides, there were no significant differences in these compounds between wild type and ugp3 mutants. This suggests that UGP3 is not involved in the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites and cellulose, but rather is likely to be specifically involved in SQDG synthesis. The UGP3 substrate, glucose-1-phosphate, is a substrate for ADP-glucose pyrophosphorylase in starch synthesis [73]. A plastid pool of glucose-1-phosphate could be a branching point between starch and SQDG biosynthesis, but more careful investigation using some starch-modified mutants will be needed.

Plant SQDG biosynthesis is summarized in Fig. 3. As mentioned previously, SQDG assembly in chloroplasts mainly requires UDPglucose pyrophosphorylase (UGP3), UDP-sulfoquinovose (UDP-SQ) synthase (SQD1), and SQDG synthase (SQD2). UGP3 produces UDP-glucose for SQD1, which is responsible for the biosynthesis of the unique head group donor, and SQD2 transfers sulfoquinovose to diacylglycerol to produce SQDG [51,57,65,69]. These enzymes are metabolic enzymes and indispensable for plant SQDG biosynthesis. However, more experiments are needed to determine the regulation by protein–protein interactions and the subsequent modification of the enzyme and its activity.

# 3. Biological functions of SQDG and its biosynthetic regulation

SQDG function was initially mostly analyzed based on its unique molecular characteristics or photosynthetic function owing to the widespread SQDG presence in photosynthetic organisms as summarized in Table 1 [74–82]. However, biological evidence corroborating these in vitro experiments is still insufficient.

Nevertheless, there have been some very detailed molecular structural studies recently [summarized in 2]. These show that Photosystem II contains 14 lipid molecules of which 3 are SQDG. These are arranged with the head groups orientated to the stroma. One of the SQDG molecules lines a prominent cavity near the $\mathbf { Q } _ { \mathrm { B } }$ site and may play an important function under normal growth conditions [2]. SQDG is also present in the crystal structure of the purified cytochrome $b _ { 6 } – f$ complex [2].

Plant membrane lipid composition is often affected by environmental factors, such as salt exposure [83–86], drought stress [87,88] or cold stress [89,90], suggesting that SQDG is conditionally important in plants. Among them, phosphate (Pi) starvation is one of the most important environmental factors affecting SQDG content because the effect is widely observed in bacteria and plants. Pi is an essential nutrient for development and reproduction in most organisms including plants. It is crucial for signaling cascades and is also an indispensable component of nucleic acids and phospholipids [91]. Plant root surfaces assimilate soluble Pi from the soil or growth medium. The availability of soluble Pi is often limited, however, because Pi primarily exists as insoluble salts such

as calcium phosphate or aluminum phosphate. Therefore, striking changes in morphology and biochemical processes are observed in plants under Pi-limited growth conditions. For example, the number of root hairs increases for efficient Pi uptake [92]. Changes in gene expression upon Pi starvation, measured using an A. thaliana whole-genome Affymetrix gene chip, correlated well with the biochemical processes of Pi starvation [93].

Two important changes in membrane lipid composition are adopted under Pi starvation. First, plastid DGDG levels increase, and the excess DGDG is transferred to the plasma, mitochondrial, and tonoplast membranes presumably by direct contact between these membranes [94–96]. Thus, phospholipids are replaced with DGDG outside of plastids to avoid consuming limited Pi for membrane lipid synthesis. Indeed, in Arabidopsis, several genes for galactolipid biosynthesis (MGD2/3, DGD1/2) are activated upon Pi starvation [97–99]. Second, SQDG content increases in bacterial and plant membranes under Pi starvation, while a phospholipid, PG, decreases [5,23,50,51,100–103]. SQDG may partially replace PG to maintain the anionic surface charge of plastid thylakoid membranes because both SQDG and DGDG are anionic lipids. Indeed, sqd2 mutants grew at similar rates to wild type under normal growth conditions, but the growth was retarded under Pi-deficient conditions [65]. In this mutant, PG content remained high even under Pi depletion, suggesting that PG can substitute for most of the SQDG function. On the other hand, analyses of PG-deficient mutants clearly demonstrated that SQDG cannot entirely compensate for PG function because the mutants showed considerable growth and photosynthetic defects under normal growth conditions [4,104,105]. An Arabidopsis mutant with low PG and no SQDG had considerably reduced total anionic lipids and more severe impairment of growth and photosynthesis than in either mutant alone, suggesting that these anionic lipids are essential for photosynthesis [103]. Marine cyanobacteria reduce their cellular phosphorus content by substituting SQDG for PG when environmental phosphorus is scarce [106,107], suggesting the importance of SQDG for marine cyanobacteria surviving under naturally occurring low-phosphorus conditions.

Genes involved in membrane lipid remodeling under Pi-limited growth conditions are coactivated in response to Pi starvation. Kobayashi et al. showed that transport of a plant hormone auxin is important for DGDG accumulation under Pi starvation [108]. Indeed, Pi starvation-induced gene expression, including galactolipid and SQDG synthesis and phospholipid degradation, was substantially reduced in auxin signaling mutants, slr-1, a gain-of-function mutant of IAA14 (a repressor of auxin signaling), and arf7arf19, a loss-of-function mutant of auxin response factors ARF7 and ARF19, resulting in suppressed accumulation of DGDG and SQDG [109]. In these mutants, free Pi content was high even under Pideficient conditions, suggesting that Pi homeostasis during Pi starvation is altered in these mutants. These results provided a better understanding of the involvement of auxin signaling mediated by

IAA14 and ARF7/19 in membrane lipid remodeling and other Pi starvation responses.

Recently, a new SQDG function was proposed in the green algae C. reinhardtii [110,111]. SQDG is utilized as a major internal sulfur source for protein synthesis in the early phase of sulfur starvation, and thus SQDG functions as a sulfur storage lipid in cells [110,111]. In plants, however, direct evidence of SQDG as a major component of the S cycle has not been reported to date. In the seed plant Brassica napus, sulfate was thought to be a major sulfur storage compound because it contained $4 2 \%$ to almost $7 0 \%$ of the total leaf sulfur and almost completely disappeared when the external sulfur supply was cut off [112]. On the other hand, in the floating weed Lemna minor, Rubisco protein was proposed to be a sulfur source [113]. However, both reports lack direct evidence such as sulfur redistribution from the storage compounds to other compounds upon sulfur deficiency, suggesting that SQDG could be a sulfur storage lipid in plants as well as green algae. Indeed, it is of interest that the role of SQDG as a major component of the S cycle was proposed some thirty years ago [114]. These aspects were also included in a review on SQDG biosynthesis [9].

# 4. Concluding remarks

Half a century has passed since the first discovery of SQDG, but I could only partially summarize the long and great history of sulfolipid research in this review. Substantial advances in understanding the mechanisms of sulfolipid biosynthesis and function have been accomplished since the initial discovery by A.A. Benson. Both genetic and biochemical techniques, which have improved remarkably in the past decade, allowed identification of most major genes involved in sulfolipid biosynthesis. In addition, regulation of these genes in plastids has been analyzed under a variety of growth conditions, particularly under Pi-depleted growth conditions. SQDG biosynthesis needs further research to determine whether this pathway is regulated directly by other protein factors. Moreover, a deeper understanding of mechanisms underlying SQDG biosynthesis will be achieved by discovering more regulatory factors through screening SQDG-deficient mutants and using advanced techniques for biochemical analyses. Several recent findings suggest that SQDG biosynthesis is not an isolated biosynthetic pathway in plastids. Progress in the next decade may uncover plausible metabolic networks involving the biosynthesis of starch, sulfolipid, and glutamate.
