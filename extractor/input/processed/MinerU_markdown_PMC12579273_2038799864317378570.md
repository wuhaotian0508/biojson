# The 13-lipoxygenase GmLOX6 is involved in JA biosynthesis and serves as a positive regulator of salt stress tolerance in soybean

# SUMMARY

Salinity represents a major abiotic stressor that significantly impairs soybean growth and yield. Although jasmonic acid (JA) has been firmly established as a key regulator of plant defense against salt stress, the precise functions of lipoxygenase (LOX) genes responsible for initiating JA biosynthesis remain poorly defined. Here, a comprehensive genome-wide analysis of the soybean LOX gene family was performed, and a detailed functional characterization of GmLOX6 was carried out. Subcellular localization confirmed that GmLOX6 is targeted to chloroplasts, while enzymatic assays demonstrated that it acts as a 13-LOX enzyme with a strong preference for $\pmb { \alpha }$ -linolenic acid as substrate. To clarify its role under salt stress, we generated both overexpression and CRISPR/Cas9-mediated knockout lines of soybean. Phenotypic and molecular evaluations revealed that GmLOX6 facilitates JA production under salt stress, thereby contributing to enhanced JA accumulation. This elevation in JA levels was associated with improved salt tolerance through multiple physiological adaptations, including the activation of antioxidant enzymes for the detoxification of reactive oxygen species (ROS), enhanced ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ extrusion to preserve ionic balance, and reinforced membrane stability. Moreover, GmRWP-RK11 was identified as a transcriptional repressor of GmLOX6. Functional disruption of GmRWP-RK11 via CRISPR/Cas9 conferred greater salt tolerance, further supporting its negative regulatory role. Collectively, these findings uncover a novel regulatory axis in which GmLOX6-mediated JA biosynthesis enhances soybean resistance to salinity through modulation of ROS homeostasis and ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ transport. These insights provide an expanded understanding of the transcriptional and biochemical mechanisms underpinning JA-driven stress adaptation in soybean. 

Keywords: GmLOX6, JA biosynthesis, ROS scavenging, $\mathbb { N } \mathfrak { a } ^ { + } / \mathbb { K } ^ { + }$ homeostasis, salt stress, soybean. 

# INTRODUCTION

Soil salinization has become a critical agricultural and ecological challenge requiring urgent attention, impacting nearly $7 \%$ of the Earth’s terrestrial area and approximately one-third of irrigated farmland, severely constraining plant productivity (Chele et al., 2021; Shabala, 2013). Soybean (Glycine max), a high-value vital dicotyledonous crop and key source of protein and oil (Kou et al., 2022), is highly sensitive to salt stress, with yield declining sharply under saline conditions. As global food demand is expected to rise by $7 0 - 1 1 0 \%$ by 2050 to accommodate population growth (Tilman et al., 2011), enhancing soybean tolerance to salinity has become a key objective for safeguarding food security. 

Salt stress imposes multifaceted physiological and biochemical constraints on soybean, mainly through ion 

toxicity, osmotic stress, and oxidative damage resulting from excessive ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ accumulation (Yang & Guo, 2018). The upward translocation of sodium from roots to leaves further disrupts photosynthesis and accelerates yield losses (Rasheed et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023). To counter these challenges, soybean plants deploy diverse adaptive strategies. One primary defense involves the activation of antioxidant systems comprising both enzymatic components, such as superoxide dismutase (SOD), peroxidase (POD), catalase (CAT), and ascorbate peroxidase (APX), and other molecules including ascorbic acid (AsA) and glutathione (GSH) (Ahmad et al., 2010; Foyer & Noctor, 2011). For instance, overexpression (OE) of GmMYB46 has been shown to enhance salt–alkali tolerance by upregulating transcripts of antioxidation-related genes (Liu et al., 2021). 

Another vital adaptive response involves maintaining intracellular ion balance. Genes such as GmSOS1, GmCHX1, and GmNHX1 mediate ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ efflux or vacuolar sequestration to achieve ionic homeostasis (Chen et al., 2015; Jia et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2019; Zhang, Cao, et al., 2022; Zhang, Ng, et al., 2022). These processes are orchestrated by transcription factors (TFs) and hormonal signaling networks that collectively fine-tune stress adaptation (Feng et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2019). Despite this progress, the regulatory complexity underlying soybean salt tolerance remains incompletely resolved. 

The jasmonic acid (JA) signaling cascade has recently gained prominence as a central regulator of plant adaptation to salinity, drought, and temperature extremes (Zhang et al., 2017). Elevated endogenous JA levels have been consistently associated with improved salt tolerance across species, including maize (Shahzad et al., 2015) and Arabidopsis thaliana (Prerostova et al., 2017). In rice, for example, the JA-related protein OsJAZ9 enhanced salt tolerance by interacting with the TF OsbHLH062 to regulate ion homeostasis and downstream stress responses (Wu et al., 2015). Exogenous JA application has similarly been reported to alleviate salt-induced damage by stimulating antioxidant activity, restoring ionic balance, and interacting synergistically with other phytohormones (Farhangi-Abriz & Ghassemi-Golezani, 2018). In soybean, JA treatment has been shown to induce the expression of stress-responsive hormones and antioxidant enzyme genes, such as FeSOD, POD, CAT, and APX in response to salt stress (Sheteiwy et al., 2021). However, the molecular framework governing JA-mediated salt tolerance in soybean remains insufficiently understood. 

Lipoxygenases (LOXs) are non-heme iron-dependent dioxygenases, that are generally classified as 9-LOXs and 13-LOXs (Feussner & Wasternack, 2002; Kolomiets et al., 2001). The biosynthesis pathway of JA begins in the chloroplast with the 13-LOX-dependent oxidation of linolenic acid (LeA), which is subsequently converted by allene oxide synthase (AOS) and allene oxide cyclase into 12-oxophytodienoic acid (OPDA), the direct precursor of JA (Wasternack, 2007; Wasternack & Feussner, 2018). Accumulating evidence suggests that LOX enzymes play a critical role in plant abiotic stress. In peanut, LOX genes are significantly upregulated by salt stress (Mou et al., 2022). In comparison, DkLOX3 in persimmon and CaLOX1 in chili pepper have been shown to facilitate the detoxification of reactive oxygen species (ROS) (Hou et al., 2015; Lim et al., 2015). In cotton, silencing GhLOX12/13 heightens salt sensitivity, as evidenced by chlorophyll degradation, increased ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ accumulation, and reduced activity of antioxidant enzymes (Shaban et al., 2018). In Arabidopsis, the isoforms AtLOX2, AtLOX3, AtLOX4, and AtLOX6 have been identified as the primary 13-LOXs involved in JA production (Bannenberg et al., 2009). Only AtLOX6 is indispensable for 

stress-induced JA accumulation in roots, as AtLOX6- deficient mutants display reduced JA levels under stress and compromised resistance to both biotic and abiotic challenges (Grebner et al., 2013). 

LOX enzymes play integral roles in JA biosynthesis and in affecting ROS detoxification, ion homeostasis, and stress-responsive gene expression under abiotic stresses such as salt. However, in soybean, the functions of LOX genes in salt adaptation and their involvement in the JA pathway remain poorly defined. In this study, we conducted a genome-wide characterization of the soybean LOX gene family, uncovering their distinct expression profiles across various tissues. Among these, GmLOX6 emerged as a prominent candidate for mediating salt stress adaptation due to its responsiveness to both NaCl exposure and JA signaling. To explore its functional role, we developed transgenic soybean lines overexpressing or lacking GmLOX6. These results demonstrate that GmLOX6 confers enhanced salt tolerance by modulating JA biosynthesis, facilitating ROS detoxification, and maintaining $\mathsf { N a } ^ { + } / \mathsf { K } ^ { + }$ homeostasis. Furthermore, we identified GmRWP-RK11 as a transcriptional repressor that binds to the GmLOX6 promoter and downregulates its expression, thereby regulating JA biosynthesis. This study establishes GmLOX6 as a key regulator in the JA-mediated stress response pathway, fills the knowledge gap of LOX genes in soybean, and provides a mechanistic foundation for the development of salt-tolerant soybean cultivars.

# Genome-wide GmLOX identification and analysis

These genes encode proteins ranging from 456 to 928 amino acids in length, with coding sequences between 1368 and 2784 bp. Predicted molecular weights span from 51.95 to 104.34 kDa, and calculated isoelectric points (pI) range from 4.97 to 6.55 (Table S1). Phylogenetic analysis, conducted using LOX orthologs from A. thaliana (AtLOX1–6), Oryza sativa (OsLOX1–9), Zea mays (ZmLOX1–7), and Phaseolus vulgaris (PvLOX1–7), classified the soybean LOXs cluster into three major groups: 9-LOX, 13-LOX type I, and 13-LOX type II. # GmLOX6 encodes a chloroplast-localized 13-LOX enzyme

Domain analysis classified GmLOX6 within the 13-LOX type II subfamily, containing both the canonical LOX and PLAT domains (Figure 1B). To investigate its subcellular localization, a GmLOX6::GFP fusion construct was generated and transiently expressed in Arabidopsis mesophyll protoplasts. Confocal imaging revealed GFP fluorescence co-localizing with chlorophyll autofluorescence, confirming that the GmLOX6 protein is targeted to chloroplasts (Figure 1D). 

To further evaluate enzymatic function, we expressed a His-tagged recombinant GmLOX6 protein in Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3) cells, with empty pET-22b vector-transformed cells serving as controls. SDS-PAGE and Western blot analyses detected a protein band of the expected molecular size (Figure 1E). Enzyme kinetics were evaluated using LeA and linoleic acid (LA) as substrates. Eadie–Hofstee plot analysis yielded a $K _ { \mathsf { m } }$ of 19.32 nmol ${ \mathsf { L } } ^ { - 1 }$ and a $V _ { \sf m a x }$ of 76.41 nmol $\mathsf { m i n } ^ { - 1 } \mathsf { \mu 9 } ^ { - 1 }$ for LeA, while LA showed a $K _ { \mathfrak { m } }$ of 95.8 nmol ${ L ^ { - 1 } }$ and a $V _ { \mathrm { { m a x } } }$ of $3 . 2 6 ~ \mathsf { n m o l } ~ \mathsf { m i n } ^ { - 1 } ~ \mu \mathsf { g } ^ { - 1 }$ (Figure 1F,G). These data confirm that GmLOX6 has a strong substrate preference for LeA, consistent with its predicted role in JA biosynthesis. 

# GmLOX6 enhances soybean salt tolerance and photosynthetic efficiency

To elucidate the functional role of GmLOX6 in salt tolerance, we developed transgenic soybean lines in the DN50 background, including OE lines (GmLOX6-OE, OE7, and OE13) and CRISPR/Cas9-mediated knockout mutants (- gmlox6-cr, m2, and m5) (Figures S3 and S4). Under normal conditions, OE lines revealed enhanced vegetative performance, with increases of $1 4 . 1 \%$ in root length, $3 1 . 4 \%$ in root biomass, $3 2 . 2 \%$ in shoot biomass, and $2 1 . 6 \%$ in plant height compared with wild-type (WT) plants. Knockout lines displayed growth impairments, with reductions of 

8.1, 9.1, 24.6, and $1 9 . 8 \%$ in these respective traits (Figure 2A,B). Upon exposure to salt stress, WT plants exhibited severe wilting and chlorosis by Day 7. In comparison, GmLOX6-OE lines retained green foliage, while gmlox6-cr plants exhibited complete wilting and yellowing as early as Day 5 (Figure 2A). Morphological metrics confirmed that GmLOX6-OE lines maintained superior growth under salt stress, whereas gmlox6-cr lines suffered pronounced inhibition (Figure 2B). After 3 days of salt treatment, photosynthetic performance was evaluated across genotypes (Figure 2C). GmLOX6-OE plants exhibited improved photosynthetic parameters, while gmlox6-cr lines showed declines relative to WT. Specific indicators, including minimal fluorescence (Fo), maximum quantum yield (Fv/Fm), chlorophyll content index (Chl-index), and anthocyanin index (Ant-index), reflected these trends (Figure 2D). Furthermore, statistical evaluation of seedling survival revealed significant differences among genotypes. GmLOX6-OE lines maintained a survival rate of $8 7 . 1 \%$ under salt stress, significantly higher than WT, whereas knockout lines showed only $3 7 . 8 \%$ survival (Figure 2E). Collectively, these findings establish that GmLOX6 enhances salt tolerance in soybean by sustaining photosynthetic function and promoting biomass accumulation under stress conditions.

# GmLOX6 augments JA accumulation to promote salt tolerance in soybean

JA content, whereas gmlox6-cr mutants showed reduced JA levels relative to WT. These differences became more pronounced under salt stress, with JA concentrations increasing across all genotypes, albeit to varying extents. This pattern suggests that JA biosynthesis is an integral component of the salinity response, with GmLOX6 playing a facilitative role in amplifying this response.

# GmLOX6 facilitates ROS detoxification and maintains ion homeostasis during salt stress

To investigate the physiological functions of GmLOX6 under saline conditions, we assessed ROS levels, membrane integrity, and ion homeostasis across different genotypes (Figure 5). Histochemical staining with diaminobenzidine (DAB) and nitroblue tetrazolium (NBT) indicated that GmLOX6-OE plants accumulated significantly lower levels of ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ and $0 { _ 2 } ^ { - }$ compared with WT under salt stress, whereas the gmlox6-cr lines displayed markedly elevated ROS levels (Figure 5A). Quantitative measurements corroborated these staining results, confirming that GmLOX6 OE improves cellular redox balance during stress (Figure 5C). Membrane stability, assessed through Evans blue and propidium iodide (PI) staining, revealed that GmLOX6-OE lines sustained minimal membrane damage, while gmlox6-cr mutants exhibited severe membrane disruption under salt treatment (Figure 5A,B). The observed differences were supported by measurements of malondialdehyde (MDA), where lipid peroxidation levels were significantly reduced in OE lines but elevated in knockout lines relative to WT under stress conditions (Figure 5C). These findings suggest that GmLOX6 enhances tolerance to salt stress by reducing oxidative damage and preserving membrane integrity. 

To further dissect the antioxidant response, we quantified the activity of key ROS-scavenging enzymes and measured non-enzymatic antioxidant levels (Figure 5D). Under control conditions, antioxidant enzyme activities were comparable across genotypes. However, upon salt exposure, GmLOX6-OE plants showed markedly higher activities of SOD, POD, CAT, and GSH reductase (GR) compared with WT, while gmlox6-cr lines displayed suppressed enzyme activity. Additionally, levels of non-enzymatic antioxidants such as AsA and reduced GSH were significantly elevated in GmLOX6-OE plants under stress. This coordinated upregulation of both enzymatic and non-enzymatic ROS defense systems contributes to the superior oxidative stress resilience observed in GmLOX6-OE lines. 

Salt stress also disrupts ion homeostasis, primarily due to excessive ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ accumulation that exacerbates oxidative stress and membrane damage. Using CoroNa Green dye, we visualized intracellular ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ distribution after $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ of salt treatment. GmLOX6-OE roots exhibited weaker fluorescence intensity, indicating lower ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ accumulation, whereas gmlox6-cr roots displayed stronger fluorescence (Figure 5B). Ion quantification confirmed these patterns: 

OE lines accumulated lower ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ and $\mathsf { K } ^ { + }$ concentrations than WT, whereas knockout plants accumulated higher levels (Figure 5E). Critically, the ${ \mathsf { K } } ^ { + } / { \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ ratio, a critical indicator of salt tolerance, was significantly higher in GmLOX6-OE lines (5.28) compared with knockout plants (2.35), highlighting their improved ionic balance under salt stress (Figure 5E). Together, these data demonstrate that GmLOX6 enhances salinity tolerance by promoting ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ efflux, maintaining ionic homeostasis, and minimizing oxidative and membrane damage. 

# GmRWP-RK11 directly binds the GmLOX6 promoter and represses its transcription

To explore upstream regulatory factors modulating GmLOX6 expression, we cloned the GmLOX6 promoter and screened for candidate TFs using molecular docking simulations. Among the potential regulators, Glyma.06G054900 (GmRWP-RK11), a member of the plantspecific RWP-RK TF family (Amin et al., 2023), showed the highest ipTM $^ +$ pTM interaction score (0.60), suggesting strong binding potential to the GmLOX6 promoter (Table S4). One amino acid Arg-33 present in GmRWP-RK11 was predicted to play a crucial role in GmLOX6 promoter recognition (Figure S5). 

To validate this interaction, we employed a yeast onehybrid (Y1H) assay, which confirmed direct binding of GmRWP-RK11 to the GmLOX6 promoter (Figure 6A). This interaction was further substantiated by electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA), where a specific shift in the DNA-protein complex was observed upon incubation with GmRWP-RK11, and binding was competitively inhibited by excess unlabeled probe. M2 (AAAAGGCA) and M3 (CAGCA) were the core motifs of GmRWP-RK11 binding to the GmLOX6 promoter (Figure 6B). Finally, a dualluciferase reporter assay in planta demonstrated that coexpression of 35S::GmRWP-RK11 significantly repressed the activity of the GmLOX6 promoter, as indicated by reduced luminescence from the GmLOX6pro::LUC construct (Figure S6; Figure 6C,D). These findings collectively demonstrate that GmRWP-RK11 acts as a negative regulator of $G m L O X6 ,$ directly binding its promoter and suppressing transcriptional activation.

# DISCUSSION

# Comprehensive characterization of the soybean GmLOX gene family

Soybean, a moderately salt-sensitive crop, suffers substantial yield and quality losses when exposed to salinity (Phang et al., 2008). LOXs are widely recognized for their involvement in plant development and stress responses. Although LOX family members have been extensively studied in model and crop species like Arabidopsis (Bannenberg et al., 2009), rice (Umate, 2011), cotton (Shaban et al., 2018), and tomato (Mariutto et al., 2011), a systematic genome-wide survey coupled with functional analysis of soybean LOX genes has remained limited. In the present study, we identified 39 putative soybean LOX (GmLOX) proteins and categorized them into three major phylogenetic groups, with the 13-LOX Type II clade comprising the largest cluster (Figure 1A). Multiple sequence alignment 

confirmed the presence of conserved LOX and PLAT domains across GmLOX proteins (Figure 1B), consistent with structural features previously reported for LOXs in other plant systems (Song et al., 2016). 

Expression profiling revealed three distinct tissuespecific patterns among the GmLOX genes: two were predominantly expressed in seeds, 14 in roots, and 23 in flowers and pods (Figure S2), suggesting substantial functional diversification. In particular, root-specific GmLOX expression implies potential roles in root development and stress adaptation. These patterns underscore the importance of spatial regulation in GmLOX-mediated physiological processes. 

Promoter analysis revealed that GmLOX genes harbor multiple cis-regulatory elements linked to abiotic stress and hormone responses. Among these, the TGACG motif, CGTCA motif, ABRE, TC-rich repeats, LTR, and G-box were frequently identified (Figure S1). These motifs are known to mediate transcriptional responses to environmental stimuli such as drought and low temperatures (Sakuma et al., 2002), further supporting the idea that GmLOXs are integrally involved in stress signaling networks. This conclusion aligns with earlier findings that demonstrate the functional plasticity of LOX genes across species (Shaban et al., 2018; Upadhyay et al., 2019). 

# GmLOX6 positively modulates soybean salt stress responses

Previous studies in Arabidopsis have established AtLOX6 as a participant in JA biosynthesis and abiotic stress responses (Grebner et al., 2013). Phylogenetic and sequence analyses revealed strong homology between GmLOX6 and AtLOX6 (Figure 1A,B). Functionally, GmLOX6 encodes a chloroplast-localized 13-LOX that utilizes LeA as its substrate and is strongly inducible by both JA and salt stress (Figure 1C–G). GmLOX6 was induced by NaCl to reach the peak at 6 h (Figure 1C). The same phenomenon was also observed in the CaLOX1 gene in pepper (Lim et al., 2015). These findings support the notion that GmLOX6 is involved in the JA pathway and stress response, which is consistent with AtLOX6. Exposure to salt stress typically suppresses soybean growth, reducing parameters such as plant height, leaf size, and biomass accumulation (Kataria et al., 2019; Shu et al., 2017). In our study, OE of GmLOX6 significantly improved growth performance and survival under saline conditions, whereas knockout lines were more susceptible (Figure 2A,B,E).

# GmLOX6 contributes to JA biosynthesis under salt stress

The JA and its derivatives are key mediators of plant growth, defense, and stress adaptation. Numerous studies have highlighted JA’s role in mitigating salt damage in various species, including wheat (Qiu et al., 2014), Arabidopsis (Kuo et al., 2020), and tomato (Shang et al., 2024). In this study, GmLOX6 showed strong responsiveness to JA treatment, suggesting its involvement in the JA-associated signaling pathway (Figure 1C). Transcriptomic analysis comparing GmLOX6-OE and WT plants under salt stress identified a significant enrichment of DEGs within the a-LeA metabolism pathway (Figure 3C), which constitutes the upstream precursor to JA biosynthesis. JA synthesis begins on chloroplast membranes, where 13-LOX converts $\pmb { \mathcal { X } }$ -LeA to 13-HPOT. This product is then processed by AOS into an unstable allene oxide, which cyclizes to form 12-OPDA. Subsequent $\beta$ -oxidation in the peroxisome catalyzed by ACX produces JA precursors (Li et al., 2021; Wasternack & Feussner, 2018). 

Consistent with this pathway, we detected significant upregulation of key biosynthetic genes including PLA2, LOX, AOS, and ACX in GmLOX6-OE plants (Figure 3D,E). Quantification of endogenous JA levels further supported these molecular findings. Compared with WT, GmLOX6- OE plants accumulated higher amounts of JA than WT under both control and salt-stressed conditions, while gmlox6-cr lines showed lower JA levels (Figure 4C). Application of exogenous JA during salt treatment further enhanced shoot growth and photosynthetic parameters in all genotypes, with GmLOX6-OE lines displaying the most robust growth responses (Figure 4). These results collectively indicate that GmLOX6 enhances salt tolerance by stimulating JA biosynthesis. A similar mechanism has been described in tomato, where the TF SlWRKY81 increases tolerance to saline-alkaline stress by modulating JA signaling via interaction with SlJAZ1 (Shang et al., 2024). 

# GmLOX6 promotes ROS detoxification and ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ balance in soybean

Prior studies have demonstrated that exogenous JA improves salt stress resilience in soybean by modulating ROS homeostasis, ionic equilibrium, and the expression of antioxidant genes, including POD, CAT, and APX (Farhangi-Abriz & Ghassemi-Golezani, 2018). Our findings suggest that GmLOX6-mediated JA accumulation elicits a similar physiological response. Salt stress disrupts cellular ion balance, particularly via excessive ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ accumulation, leading to membrane damage and elevated ROS production (Hasegawa, 2013; Mittler et al., 2011). Although early ROS generation can function as a signaling cue (Swanson & Gilroy, 2010), persistent oxidative stress is deleterious. DAB and NBT staining revealed substantially reduced ROS accumulation in GmLOX6-OE plants under saline conditions (Figure 5A), a trend further supported by quantitative reductions in ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ and $0 { _ 2 } ^ { - }$ levels (Figure 5C). Furthermore, membrane integrity was better preserved in GmLOX6-OE lines, as evidenced by reduced uptake of Evans blue and PI stains, whereas gmlox6-cr lines showed more severe membrane damage (Figure 5A,B). Correspondingly, levels of the lipid peroxidation biomarker MDA were decreased in GmLOX6-OE lines and elevated in knockout lines (Figure 5C). 

Plants have evolved complex antioxidant defenses with enzymatic and non-enzymatic factors to neutralize excessive ROS generated under abiotic stress (Ahmad et al., 2010). Enzymatic players, such as SOD, CAT, APX, and GR act synergistically to detoxify ROS, while molecules like GSH, AsA, and flavonoids maintain cellular redox homeostasis (Miller et al., 2010; Yang & Guo, 2018). In this study, salt exposure increased the activities of POD, SOD, CAT, and GR in all genotypes, but the greatest induction was observed in GmLOX6-OE lines (Figure 5D). Levels of AsA and GSH, key metabolites of the AsA–GSH cycle that play a central role in ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 }$ detoxification (Foyer & Noctor, 2011), were also significantly higher in GmLOX6-OE plants under salt stress (Figure 5D). These results collectively suggest that GmLOX6 enhances antioxidant defense capacity, thereby promoting ROS scavenging under salt stress. 

Another critical factor in salt tolerance is maintaining a favorable $\mathsf { N a } ^ { + } / \mathsf { K } ^ { + }$ ratio (Chen et al., 2015). Using CoroNa Green fluorescence, we observed significantly lower ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ accumulation in root tips of GmLOX6-OE plants compared with WT and gmlox6-cr lines (Figure 5B,E). This pattern was consistent with direct quantification of root ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ content, supporting the hypothesis that GmLOX6 facilitates ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ efflux under salt stress. Moreover, GmLOX6-OE lines maintained significantly higher ${ \mathsf { K } } ^ { + } / { \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ ratios under salt stress (Figure 5E), a widely recognized hallmark of salt tolerance (Liang et al., 2024). These results align with 

previous findings on GmNHX1, which contributes to salt tolerance by promoting ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ efflux and maintaining ionic balance (Sun et al., 2019, 2021). 

These results demonstrate that GmLOX6 enhances salt tolerance by promoting JA biosynthesis, strengthening antioxidant defenses, maintaining ionic homeostasis, and reducing cellular damage. Previous studies have reported similar roles for LOX genes in other species, for example, DkLOX3 in persimmon, CaLOX1 in pepper, and GhLOX12/13 in cotton, which were shown to mediate salt tolerance through enhanced ROS scavenging (Hou et al., 2015; Lim et al., 2015; Shaban et al., 2018). GmLOX6 not only participates in ROS detoxification but also contributes to JA biosynthesis and ion regulation under salt stress, revealing a broader functional spectrum. Most research on soybean LOX genes has focused on expression profiles under stress (Zhang, Cao, et al., 2022; Zhang, Ng, et al., 2022). In comparison, this study provides mechanistic insight into the functional role of GmLOX6 in salinity responses, expanding current understanding of LOX gene diversity. 

Under salt stress, respiratory burst oxidase homologs are rapidly activated to generate ROS, which, together with $\mathsf { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ signaling, modulates cellular ion homeostasis (Hasanuzzaman et al., 2021). Concomitantly, JA biosynthesis is upregulated (Yu et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2014). The resulting accumulation of JA-Ile facilitates binding of its bioactive form, $\left( + \right)$ -7-iso-JA-Ile, to the COI1 subunit of the ${ \mathsf { S C F } } ^ { \mathsf { C O I 1 } }$ E3 ubiquitin ligase, triggering 26S proteasomemediated degradation of JAZ repressors (Browse, 2009; Fonseca et al., 2009; Sheard et al., 2010). In rice, OsJAZ9 is targeted by ${ \mathsf { S C F } } ^ { \mathsf { C O I 1 } }$ and interacts with OsbHLH062 to regulate the expression of ion-transporter genes (Wu et al., 2015). Recent studies have found that in apple, JA induces 26S proteasome-dependent degradation of MdJAZ5 and MdJAZ10, releasing MdWRKY9 from the JAZ–WRKY complex to activate MdNHX1 and MdSOS2, thereby restoring ion homeostasis and enhancing salt tolerance (Zhao et al., 2025). Similarly, elevated JA levels in tomato relieved SlJAZ1-mediated repression of SlWRKY81, enabling activation of SISPDS2 and SINHX4, which promote ${ \sf K } ^ { + }$ uptake, ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ efflux, and ROS scavenging (Shang et al., 2024). ${ \mathsf { S C F } } ^ { \mathsf { C O I 1 } }$ -dependent degradation of JAZ proteins also liberates MYC TFs (Sheard et al., 2010; Yan et al., 2009). For example, MYC2 directly regulates CHS1, RR26, and CAT2 genes involved in ROS detoxification under abiotic stress (Song et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023). Collectively, these studies illustrate how JA signaling modulates salt stress adaptation by adjusting ROS dynamics and $\mathsf { N a } ^ { + } / \mathsf { K } ^ { + }$ homeostasis through the antagonistic interaction between MYC2 and JAZ repressors. In soybean, GsJAZ2 has been shown to enhance salt tolerance, potentially by modulating NHX1-mediated ${ \mathsf { N } } { \mathsf { a } } ^ { + }$ transport (Zhu et al., 2012). In this study, GmLOX6 was rapidly 

activated under salt stress, catalyzing the initial step of JA biosynthesis and directly increasing JA and $\left( + \right)$ -7-iso-JA-Ile levels. We propose that this JA accumulation may stimulate ${ \mathsf { S C F } } ^ { \mathsf { C O I 1 } }$ -mediated degradation of JAZ repressors, releasing downstream TFs such as MYC and WRKY. These TFs likely regulate genes involved in ROS detoxification and ion transport, contributing to enhanced salt tolerance in soybean. However, further experimental validation is required to fully elucidate the specific downstream components of the GmLOX6-mediated JA signaling cascade. 

# GmRWP-RK11 negatively regulates GmLOX6 expression at the transcriptional level

The RWP-RK family is unique to plants and plays pivotal roles in nitrate signaling, gametogenesis, and nodulation (Amin et al., 2023). Emerging evidence also implicates that RWP-RK TFs are involved in regulating stress-responsive genes. For example, the OsNLP4-OsMADS27 regulatory module has been shown to enhance salt tolerance in rice (Alfatih et al., 2022). In soybean, several GmRWP-RK genes, including GmRWP-RK11, are notably induced under abiotic stresses such as salinity (Amin et al., 2023). In this study, GmRWP-RK11 was found to be preferentially expressed in roots (Figure S7), consistent with the expression pattern of GmLOX6. Under salt stress, GmRWP-RK11 expression peaked at $2 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ , where in response to JA treatment, its expression showed a biphasic pattern with peaks at 3 and $1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ }$ , followed by declines at 6 and $2 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ (Figure 6F,G). These indicate that GmRWP-RK11 may play a role in both salt stress and JA signaling pathways in soybean. Molecular docking, Y1H, and EMSA assays confirmed that GmRWP-RK11 directly binds to the promoter of GmLOX6 (Figure S5; Figure 6A,B). Dual-luciferase reporter assays demonstrated that this interaction represses GmLOX6 transcriptional activity (Figure 6C,D). The expression level of GmLOX6 reached its maximum at 6 h following salt and JA treatments, when GmRWP-RK11 expression was relatively low (Figures 1C and 6F,G), further supporting the inhibitory role of GmRWP-RK11. Functional validation using soybean hairy root systems demonstrated that knocking out GmRWP-RK11 enhanced growth under both control and saline conditions, indicative of increased salt tolerance (Figure 6H,I). Moreover, higher GmLOX6 expression levels and greater JA content were also detected in the roots of the GmRWP-RK11 gene knockout lines compared with WT (Figure 6J,K). These findings suggest that GmRWP-RK11 functions as a transcriptional repressor of GmLOX6, and disruption of this repression enhances JA biosynthesis and salt tolerance in soybean.

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Identification and evaluation of soybean LOX genes

To identify LOX genes in soybean, BLASTP searches were conducted using A. thaliana LOX protein sequences against databases hosted on NCBI (http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov) and Phytozome (https://phytozome.jgi.doe.gov/pz/portal.html), applying an E-value threshold of $< 1 0 ^ { - 1 0 }$ . Conserved domains characteristic of LOX proteins including PLAT (PF03790) and LOX (PF00391) were validated using the SMART database (http://smart.emblheidelberg.de). Candidate genes were classified based on chromosomal localization and sequence homology, and their physicochemical properties, including molecular weight, pI, and sequence length, were determined with ExPASy tools (https://web.expasy.org/tools/). 

For phylogenetic analysis, LOX protein sequences from G. max, O. sativa, Z. mays, A. thaliana, and P. vulgaris were aligned, 

and a neighbor-joining tree was constructed using MEGA5.0 (Edgar, 2004) with 1000 bootstrap replicates. The tree was visualized and enhanced with iTOL (http://itol.embl.de). Multiple sequence alignments were performed using SnapGene. 

Promoter regions (2 kb upstream of the start codon) of GmLOX genes were analyzed using the PlantCARE database (https://bioinformatics.psb.ugent.be/webtools/plantcare/html/) to predict cis-regulatory elements, and the promoter features were graphically displayed using IBS software. Transcriptomic data for GmLOX genes across various tissues were retrieved from Phytozome and visualized using TBtools. 

# Subcellular localization analysis of GmLOX6

The coding sequence of GmLOX6 was cloned into the GFP-tagged vector pSOY1 under the control of the CaMV35S promoter to generate a 35S::GmLOX6-GFP construct (primer sequences in Table S2). This construct and an empty vector control were transiently expressed in Arabidopsis mesophyll protoplasts via polyethylene glycol-mediated transformation with calcium (Yoo et al., 2007). Following a 16-h incubation, cells were imaged using confocal laser scanning microscopy (Olympus FV1200). Chlorophyll autofluorescence $( \mathsf { E x } \cdot 6 4 0 \ \mathsf { n m } )$ served as a marker for chloroplasts, enabling assessment of the subcellular localization of GFP-tagged GmLOX6. 

# Analyses of enzyme kinetics

The enzymatic activity of purified LOX protein was quantified using a spectrophotometric assay adapted from Liu and Han (2010). Each reaction mixture (3 ml total volume) included varying concentrations of LA or LeA $( 1 6 . 5 - 3 3 0 ~ \mu \mathsf { M } )$ , $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ buffer at different pH levels, and $5 ~ { \mu \mu }$ of enzyme solution. Control reactions were performed using ${ \mathsf { d } } { \mathsf { d } } { \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ instead of enzyme. The formation of hydroperoxides was monitored by measuring absorbance changes at 234 nm $\large ( \Delta \mathsf { A } \min ^ { - 1 } \large )$ at $\mathsf { 2 5 ^ { \circ } C }$ . Enzyme activity was calculated based on the molar extinction coefficient $( \varepsilon = 2 5 0 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m } ^ { - 1 } ~ \mathsf { c m } ^ { - 1 } )$ , with one unit defined as the amount of enzyme catalyzing the formation of $1 \ \mu \mathrm { m o l }$ of hydroperoxide per minute. All assays were conducted in triplicate to ensure reproducibility. 

# Generation and validation of GmLOX6 OE and knockout lines

To construct OE lines, the full-length coding sequence of GmLOX6 was amplified and cloned into the pSOY1 vector (driven by the CaMV35S promoter) at the EcoRI/BamHI restriction sites. CRISPR/Cas9 knockout constructs were designed using CRISPR-GE (http://skl.scau.edu.cn/) and included target sequences specific to GmLOX6. Both constructs were introduced into the Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain EHA105 through freeze-thawing, followed by transformation into soybean cultivar DN50 via cotyledonary node transformation (Liu et al., 2012). 

$\mathsf { T } _ { 2 }$ homozygous OE lines (OE-7, OE-13) were identified through PCR screening, while knockout lines (m2, m5) were confirmed by sequencing the targeted loci and verifying amino acid alterations introduced via CRISPR/Cas9. Gene expression levels in WT, OE-7, and OE-13 lines were quantified using reverse transcription-quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR). To validate the knockout efficiency, the mutated sequences in m2 and m5 lines were aligned with the WT sequence using DNAMAN v.6 (Lynnon Biosoft, CA, USA). 

# qPCR

Total RNA was extracted from samples ground in liquid nitrogen with TRIzol (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA), following the manufacturer’s protocol. Gene-specific primers for GmLOX6 were designed using Primer3 Plus (https://www.primer3plus.com/) in conjunction with Primer-BLAST to ensure specificity. Gene expression analysis was performed with three biological replicates, with GmACTIN4 serving as the reference. Relative transcript abundance was calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathsf { C T } }$ method (Livak & Schmittgen, 2001). A complete list of primer sequences used in this study is provided in Table S3. 

# Plant materials and treatments

The soybean cultivar DN50, along with its derivative lines overexpressing GmLOX6 (OE-7, OE-13) and knocking out lines (m2, m5) was used in all experiments. Seeds were germinated in vermiculite, and cotyledonary node segments were stabilized with sponge blocks before being transferred into black six-cell hydroponic containers filled with half-strength Hoagland’s nutrient solution (pH 5.5). Plants were cultivated in a controlled environment chamber set to $2 6 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } / 2 4 ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ of day/night temperature, 16 h light/8 h dark photoperiod, and $7 5 \%$ relative humidity. 

Salt and JA treatments were initiated once trifoliate leaves had fully expanded. Plants were treated with 120 mmol ${ \mathsf { L } } ^ { - 1 }$ NaCl and/or $0 . 2 ~ \mu \mathrm { m o l } ~ \mathsf { L } ^ { - 1 }$ JA, and root samples were harvested at 0, 1, 3, 6, 12, and 24 h post-treatment, flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen, and stored at $\mathtt { - 8 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ for RNA extraction and expression analysis. In parallel, WT, OE, and knockout lines were exposed to 120 mmol ${ \mathsf { L } } ^ { - 1 }$ NaCl to evaluate stress-related phenotypes. Growth parameters were documented daily via imaging. For exogenous JA treatment under salt stress, $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ JA solution was foliarapplied, and water-sprayed plants served as controls. Growth performance was evaluated through morphological assessment and quantitative measurements. 

# Biochemical and physiological assays

Following 7 days of salt stress, plant height and root length of 10 individual plants per treatment were recorded. Roots were excised, rinsed with distilled water, and gently dried with filter paper. Shoot and root fresh weights were determined, after which 

samples were dried at $\mathtt { 1 0 5 ^ { \circ } C }$ for 15 min to inactivate enzymatic activity and oven-dried at ${ 8 0 ^ { \circ } } \complement$ until constant weight for dry biomass determination. Survival rate was recorded 6 days after recovery. All measurements were recorded from three biological replicates. 

Chlorophyll fluorescence and related physiological traits of three plants per line were measured using the TraitExplorerPE plant photosynthesis phenotyping platform (PhenoTrait, Beijing, China). Each measurement was performed in triplicate. The measured parameters included initial fluorescence (Fo), maximum quantum yield of PSII (Fv/Fm), chlorophyll content index (Chlindex), and anthocyanin content index (Ant-index). 

To evaluate oxidative stress and antioxidant responses, the concentrations or activities of AsA, GSH, hydrogen peroxide $( \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } 0 _ { 2 } ) ,$ superoxide anion $( 0 _ { 2 } ^ { - } )$ , MDA, POD, SOD, CAT, and GR were determined using commercial assay kits (Suzhou Grace Biotechnology Co., China; product codes G0206W–G1208W). Three plants were selected from each line for measurement, and assays were carried out in triplicate. 

Histochemical staining was performed to visualize ROS and cell viability. Leaves from WT and transgenic lines, subjected to 0 or $^ { 1 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ salt stress, were stained overnight in $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m l }$ of NBT, DAB, or Evans blue solution. Stained tissues were cleared by boiling in $7 5 \%$ ethanol with $5 \%$ glycerol. Root samples were stained with PI and CoroNa Green and visualized using laser scanning confocal microscopy. 

The JA quantification was performed in root tissues collected from WT, OE, and knockout lines under both control and salt stress conditions. Three plants from each line were selected for measurement and three biological replicates were conducted. Samples $_ { ( 0 . 2 \ 9 ) }$ were homogenized in liquid nitrogen, spiked with $1 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of internal standard solution, and extracted using 1 ml of solvent. After centrifugation at low temperature, the supernatant was concentrated, redissolved, filtered, and transferred into chromatographic vials. Quantification was performed via ultra performance liquid chromatography (ExionLCTM AD) coupled with tandem mass spectrometry (QTRAP 6500+), and data analysis was conducted by Metware Biotechnology Co. Ltd. (Wuhan, China). All measurements were performed in triplicate. 

# RNA-seq

At the seedling stage, when the first trifoliate leaves of WT and OE13 plants were fully expanded, the roots were collected $^ { 2 4 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ after salt treatment for RNA sequencing, performed by Tianjin Jizhi Gene Technology Co., Ltd. (Tianjin, China). Differential expression analysis was conducted using a significance threshold of $P < 0 . 0 5$ and an absolute $\lVert \mathsf { o g } _ { 2 }$ Fold Change| ≥1. Gene ontology and KEGG pathway enrichment analyses were performed with the cluster Profiler package (Wu et al., 2021). 

# Molecular docking

Putative upstream TFs of GmLOX6 were predicted using molecular docking conducted by Kejing Biotechnology Co. Ltd. (Hefei, China). Three-dimensional structural models of TF proteins and the GmLOX6 promoter DNA were generated, and protein–DNA interactions were assessed with the HDOCK server. The top 10 candidates, ranked by binding affinity, were further analyzed with AlphaFold 3 to assess structural compatibility based on ipTM (interface predicted Template Modeling score) and pTM (predicted Template Modeling score) scores (Table S4). Highscoring TFs were selected for subsequent experimental validation. 

# Dual-luciferase reporter assay

The GmRWP-RK11 coding region was cloned into the pGreenII 62- SK vector, while the GmLOX6 promoter was inserted upstream of the luciferase reporter in the pGreenII 0800 vector using BamHI and KpnI restriction sites. Recombinant plasmids were transformed into A. tumefaciens GV3101 (harboring pMP90 and pSoup-p19). Leaf infiltration of 4-week-old Nicotiana benthamiana was performed using Agrobacterium suspensions carrying either 0800-GmLOX6pro+SK empty vector or 0800-GmLOX6pro+SK-GmRWP-RK11. After $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { ~ h ~ } }$ in the dark, luminescence signals were detected using a Tanon 5200 imaging system (Shanghai, China). Firefly (LUC) and Renilla (REN) luciferase activities were quantified with a dual-luciferase assay kit (Beyotime, Shanghai, China). Data were collected from four biological replicates, and primer sequences are provided in Table S3. 

# EMSA

To assess the binding of GmRWP-RK11 to the GmLOX6 promoter, its coding sequence was cloned into the pET32a vector to generate a $6 \times$ His-tagged fusion protein. Protein expression was induced in E. coli by IPTG (Transgenic Biotechnology Company, Beijing, China). Moreover, the EMSA was performed using the LightShift Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (Beyotime/GS009, Shanghai, China). Biotin-labeled DNA probes derived from the GmLOX6 promoter region are listed in Table S5. 

# Y1H assay

The open reading frame of GmRWP-RK11 was inserted into the pGADT7 vector (pGADT7-GmRWP-RK11), while a 2-kb fragment of the GmLOX6 promoter was cloned into the pAbAi vector (pAbAi-GmLOX6pro). These constructs were co-transformed into the Y1H Gold yeast strain. Positive colonies were serially diluted $( 1 0 ^ { - 1 }$ to $1 0 ^ { - 4 } )$ and plated on SD/ Leu/–Ura medium supplemented with or without $2 0 0 \mathrm { \ m g \ L } ^ { - 1 }$ Aureobasidin A (AbA), followed by incubation at $\mathfrak { 3 0 ^ { \circ } C }$ for 3–5 days. Controls included the positive control (pGADT7-rec-53+pAbAi-p53) and negative controls (pGADT7+pAbAi-GmLOX6pro and pGADT7+pAbAi-p53). Primer sequences are provided in Table S3. 

# CRISPR/Cas9-mediated knockout of GmRWP-RK11 in soybean hairy roots

The CRISPR/Cas9 construct targeting GmRWP-RK11 was introduced into A. tumefaciens K599 via electroporation. Soybean hypocotyls from the DN50 cultivar were transformed following established protocols (Toth et al., 2016) to generate transgenic hairy roots. Roots induced by untransformed K599 were used as negative controls. Transformation was verified by PCR screening, and non-transgenic roots were excluded. For phenotypic evaluation, 10–20 independent root lines per genotype were subjected to 0 or 120 mmol ${ \mathsf { L } } ^ { - 1 }$ NaCl for 5 days, after which root and shoot lengths were recorded as growth indices. 

# Statistical analysis

All statistical analyses were performed with Origin 2022 and SPSS 26.0. Differences between two groups were evaluated using Student’s t-test, with significance thresholds indicated by $P < 0 . 0 5$ (*) and $P < 0 . 0 1$ (**). For comparisons involving more than two groups, Duncan’s multiple range test was employed; statistically distinct groups are marked with different lowercase letters $( P < 0 . 0 5 )$ .
