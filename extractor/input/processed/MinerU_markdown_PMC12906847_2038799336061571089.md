# ABSTRACT

The interaction between plants and pathogens has long been an actively researched field of plant immunity. In this study, we found an Aa593/CmNAC29-CmNCED3 module associated with the interaction between chrysanthemum and the fungal pathogen Alternaria alternata. Here, we identified a transcription factor, CmNAC29, in chrysanthemum that promotes abscisic acid (ABA) biosynthesis by directly regulating CmNCED3, a key rate-limiting enzyme gene for ABA synthesis, thereby determining the susceptibility of plants to A. alternata. On invading plants, pathogens secrete effectors that contribute to regulating plant immune responses. Interestingly, we screened a novel effector, Aa593, secreted by A. alternata that targets CmNAC29 in the plant cell nucleus. By interacting with CmNAC29 and thus enhancing its transcriptional activation activity, Aa593 promotes ABA biosynthesis and subsequently weakens plant resistance to A. alternata. Furthermore, genetic transformation assays indicated Aa593 contributes to the virulence of A. alternata. Our findings in this study have enabled us to elucidate how fungal effectors target plant hormone signalling pathways to promote their virulence and lay the theoretical foundations for further studies on the pathogenic mechanism of necrotrophic fungi. 

# 1 | Introduction

In the continually evolving arms race between plants and pathogens, plants have evolved a suite of defensive strategies, among which pathogen-associated molecular pattern-triggered immunity (PTI) and effector-triggered immunity (ETI) constitute two main layers of the immune system in plants (Wang, Feng, et  al.  2020; Ngou et  al.  2022). PTI triggers a series of immune responses, including reactive oxygen species (ROS) 

bursts, calcium $( \mathrm { C a } ^ { 2 + } )$ influx, mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) activation and transcriptional reprogramming (Couto and Zipfel 2016; Yu et al. 2017). ETI is often accompanied by cell death (Cui et al. 2015). Rather than functioning independently, these two layers of the immune system act synergistically in regulating plant immune responses (Ngou et  al.  2021; Yuan et al. 2021). In this regard, transcriptional regulation has been established to play a key role in the disease resistance strategies adopted by plants.

NAC proteins comprise a unique family of transcription factors in plants. As transcriptional activators or repressors, NACs can mediate the regulatory process of plant immunity via an array of different pathways, including those associated with plant hormone signalling, ROS, and the hypersensitive responses (Bian et al. 2020). For example, NTL9 in Arabidopsis thaliana increases endogenous levels of salicylic acid in plants by positively regulating salicylic acid synthesis pathway genes, enhancing disease resistance (Zheng et al. 2015). Furthermore, several BnaNACs in Brassica napus L. play roles in the defence against pathogens as positive regulators of ROS metabolism and hypersensitive responses (Niu et al. 2014, 2016; Chen et al. 2017; Yan et al. 2018). In A. thaliana, ANAC029/NAP regulates leaf senescence via the abscisic acid (ABA) biosynthesis pathway (Guo and Gan 2006; Yang et al. 2014) and negatively responds to salt stress (Seok et al. 2017). Moreover, TgNAP, a homologue of ANAC029/ NAP in Tulipa gesneriana, positively regulates petal senescence (Meng et  al.  2022). However, although broadly characterised, the response mechanism of ANAC029/NAP and homologous proteins to biotic stress has not been reported in detail. 

Pathogens secrete a diverse array of effectors into plants to target the immune components and thus attenuate the immune defenses of plants via multiple pathways (Wang, Pruitt, et al. 2022). Transcriptional reprogramming in plants has been identified as an important mechanism whereby effectors disable the plant immune system and disrupt normal physiological activities. For example, Xanthomonas outer protein S (XopS), an effector produced by the bacterium Xanthomonas campestris pv. vesicatoria (Xcv), stabilizes the transcription factor CaWRKY40a, which negatively regulates resistance to Xcv in Capsicum annuum, interfering with stomatal immunity and promoting pathogen infection (Raffeiner et  al.  2022). RXLR effectors secreted by oomycetes enter the plant cell nucleus and therein target key transcriptional complexes to suppress plant immunity (Wang et al. 2017; Qiu et al. 2023). However, few studies have examined the transcriptional regulation in plants targeted by effectors secreted by necrotrophic fungi. Consequently, the corresponding mechanisms have yet to be sufficiently elucidated. 

Species in the genus Alternaria are a commonly occurring group of necrotrophic fungi with a worldwide distribution and broad host range that pose a serious threat to the yield and quality of many important commercial crops (Fernandes et  al.  2023). Chrysanthemum morifolium is one of the four major cut flowers worldwide with ornamental, medicinal, and edible values, and accordingly has considerable market potential (Song et al. 2023). However, black spot disease caused by Alternaria alternata has a particularly detrimental impact (Xin et al. 2021). At present, little is known about the pathogenic mechanisms. In our previous study, WGCNA (Weighted Gene Co-expression Network Analysis) and gene co-expression network construction revealed that a transcript factor CmNAC29 occupies a central position in the network topology and consistently exhibited an up-regulated trend during the entire infection process by A. alternata (Liu, Xin, Liu, Song, Guan, et  al.  2020). Here, we found CmNAC29 regulates the expression of CmNCED3, a key component involved in ABA synthesis, thereby increasing the susceptibility of chrysanthemum to A. alternata. Furthermore, CmNAC29 is targeted by the effector Aa593 secreted by A. alternata, which, by enhancing ABA biosynthesis, facilitates the 

colonisation of A. alternata. These findings provide insights into the invasive process of $A$ . alternata and accordingly represent a valuable reference for further studies seeking to elucidate the pathogenic mechanisms of necrotrophic fungi. As such, they will contribute to augmenting our current understanding of the mechanisms underlying the regulation of plant–fungal interaction networks. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | CmNAC29 Negatively Regulates the Plant Resistance to A. alternata

On the basis of RNA-seq data of chrysanthemum leaves inoculated with A. alternata at different time points, we identified a differentially expressed gene (DEG), DN73063c1_g1, as a homologue of ANAC029 in A. thaliana (Liu, Xin, Liu, Song, Guan, et  al.  2020), which is designated CmNAC29 (Figure  S1a). We cloned CmNAC29 from chrysanthemum ‘Jinba’ (Figure  S1b). The amino acid residues 9–134 at the N-terminus of CmNAC29 were identified as comprising the NAM domain, which is a typical conserved domain of NACs (Figure  S1c). Consistent with the RNA-seq results, high levels of CmNAC29 expression were continuously induced during A. alternata infection by Real-Time Quantitative Reverse Transcription PCR (RT-qPCR) verification (Figure  1a), suggesting the probable involvement of CmNAC29 in a network associated with the response to A. alternata. Moreover, CmNAC29 exhibits high expression in roots and leaves (Figure S1d) and possesses transcriptional activation activity, with the activation region situated at the C-terminal amino acid residues 135–283 (Figure 1b). Subcellular localization analysis in Nicotiana benthamiana showed that CmNAC29 predominantly localises in the cell nucleus (Figure 1c). 

To determine the function of CmNAC29 in response to A. alternata infection, we generated $p 2 \times 3 5 S \mathrm { . }$ ::amiR-CmNAC29 interference lines of chrysanthemum (Figures 1d, S1e). Inoculation experiments revealed that compared with wild-type (WT) plants, there was a significant retardation of the rate of disease development in the interference lines, and the lesion areas were significantly reduced (Figure 1e,f). These findings indicate that CmNAC29 functions as a negative regulator by promoting the susceptibility of chrysanthemum to A. alternata. 

# 2.2 | CmNAC29 Activates the Transcription of CmNCED3 to Enhance the ABA Content in Plants

To further understand the function of CmNAC29, we performed RNA-seq analysis on the leaves of wild-type and amiR-CmNAC29 (amiR) interference plants, both with and without A. alternata at 2days post-inoculation (dpi). Since CmNAC29 functions as a transcriptional activator, we intersected the downregulated gene sets from two comparisons: WT vs. amiR (Mock) and WT-In vs. amiR-In (Inoculation), and thus identified 938 DEGs (Figure S2a,b). These DEGs were classified by COG (Clusters of Orthologous Groups) and KEGG (Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes) to clarify the types of functions. COG analysis revealed that “Signal transduction mechanisms” and “Secondary metabolite biosynthesis, transport, and catabolism” were predominantly enriched (Figure S2c).

KEGG pathway analysis further indicated that the DEGs were associated with “Plant hormone signal transduction” and “Carotenoid biosynthesis” (Figure S2d). Given that carotenoid biosynthesis intermediates serve as precursors for abscisic acid (ABA) synthesis 

(Chen et al. 2023; Ye et al. 2024) and the Arabidopsis homologue of CmNAC29, NAP/ANAC029, has been reported to be involved in ABA biosynthesis (Yang et al. 2014), we focused on DEGs in this pathway. Compared with WT, interference plants indeed exhibited 

significant downregulation of CmABA4, CmNCED3, CmNCED5, CmABA2, CmAAO3 and CmABA3 following A. alternata infection (Figure S2e), consistent with RT-qPCR (Figure S2f). Notably, CmNCED3 displayed the most pronounced differential expression between WT and interference lines, both before and after pathogen inoculation. This finding aligns with previous studies demonstrating that NCED3 is the dominant isoform among NCEDs in mediating ABA biosynthesis and stress responses (Iuchi et al. 2001; Tan et al. 2003). Therefore, we selected CmNCED3 as a candidate target of CmNAC29. 

Yeast one-hybrid (Y1H) and electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) analyses revealed that CmNAC29 binds directly to the ‘CACG’ cis-acting element in the promoter of CmNCED3 (Figure 2a,b). To analyse the binding of CmNAC29 and CmNCED3 promoters in  vivo, we constructed CmNAC29-overexpression (OE) transgenic chrysanthemum lines. Compared with WT, OE-CmNAC29 plants were more resistant to A. alternata (Figure S3). Consistent with Y1H and EMSA, chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP)-qPCR results demonstrated that CmNAC29 specifically enriches in the segment (P1) containing the ‘CACG’ element in the CmNCED3 promoter (Figure 2c). Further, dual-luciferase reporter assays showed that compared with control, a clear increase in luciferase/Renilla luciferase (LUC/REN) appeared in the presence of CmNAC29 (Figure 2d–f). Collectively, these findings indicate that CmNAC29 binds directly to the promoter of CmNCED3 and thereby activates its transcription. 

To determine whether changes in the expression of CmNAC29 influence the ABA content in plants, we measured the ABA levels in WT and CmNAC29-transgenic lines. Consistent with speculation, both prior to and after inoculation with A. alternata, the ABA contents in amiR-CmNAC29 lines were significantly lower than those in WT (Figure 2g). Conversely, OE-CmNAC29 lines exhibited the opposite pattern, with elevated ABA accumulation under both conditions (Figure  2h). Moreover, CaLCuV-amiR-CmNCED3 plants generated by virus-induced gene silencing (VIGS) enhanced disease resistance against A. alternata (Figures  S4, 2i,j). To further characterise the role of ABA in A. alternata infection, we sprayed WT and CmNAC29- transgenic plants with water or ABA prior to inoculation with A. alternata (Figure S5). At 2 dpi, compared with the control plants treated with water, there was a significant increase in susceptibility to A. alternata in plants sprayed with ABA. Moreover, the disease lesion of amiR-CmNAC29 plants sprayed with ABA was similar to that of WT sprayed with water. These results indicate that ABA can compensate for the loss of susceptibility of amiR-CmNAC29 plants, and the sensitivity of CmNAC29-mediated to A. alternata depends on ABA (Figure 2k,l). Additionally, the disease symptom of OE-CmNAC29 lines after ABA treatment further supports the above conclusion (Figure $2 \mathrm { m , n }$ ). Collectively, ABA contributes to promoting the infection of A. alternata in chrysanthemum, and CmNAC29 enhances ABA biosynthesis in plants by directly activating the expression of CmNCED3. 

# 2.3 | A. alternata Effector Aa593 Targets CmNAC29 in the Plant Cell Nucleus

To gain further insights regarding the mechanism of action of CmNAC29, a Y2H library prepared from total RNA extracted 

from chrysanthemum leaves infected with $A$ . alternata was screened. Interestingly, we identified several predicted effectors from A. alternata on the basis of the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI)-BLAST search and bioinformatics (Table S2). Using a tobacco transient expression system, we identified an effector CC77DRAFT_360593 (Aa593) capable of inducing cell death in N. benthamiana (Figure S6). Consistent with the library screening results, yeast two-hybrid (Y2H) validation preliminarily confirmed an interaction between Aa593 and CmNAC29 (Figure  S7). Further, bioinformatics analysis showed that the Aa593 lacks a transmembrane structure and has no glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI)-anchored site, no mitochondrial localization signal, and no conserved domain, apart from a signal peptide (SP) segment spanning amino acid residues 1–17 at the N terminus (Figure S8). These findings demonstrate that Aa593 represents a novel, previously uncharacterized effector worthy of further functional investigation. 

To verify whether Aa593 interacts with CmNAC29 in plants, we performed luciferase complementation imaging (LCI), bimolecular fluorescence complementation (BiFC) and coimmunoprecipitation (Co-IP) assays. LCI results revealed that the signal was observed when CmNAC29-nLUC and cLUC-Aa593 were co-injected into tobacco leaves, as well as the positive control (Figure  3a). Consistent with the above, BiFC analysis revealed an overlap of yellow fluorescence and red fluorescence (nuclear marker) signals in the presence of both CmNAC29-Vn and Aa593-Vc (Figure 3b), which was confirmed by the Co-IP assay (Figure 3c). Further, GST pull-down assay characterised the interaction in vitro. The specific band of Aa593-His was detected only in the presence of both Aa593-His and GST-CmNAC29 (Figure  3d). These findings thus provide evidence to indicate a physical interaction between Aa593 and CmNAC29 in the nucleus of plant cells. 

# 2.4 | The Characteristic Analysis of Aa593 as an Effector

Compared with 0 hpi, Aa593 was significantly up-regulated at 6 hpi (Figure  4a), which indicates that Aa593 may have responses during pathogen infection. To examine the function of the SP of Aa593, yeast secretion trap and 2, 3, 5-triphenyl tetrazolium chloride (TTC) assay were performed. When cultured on YPRAA medium, yeast harbouring the Aa593-FL and Aa593-SP, along with the positive control, grew normally, whereas those transformed with Aa593-ΔSP and the negative controls were characterised by inhibited growth (Figure 4b). In addition, the TTC assay showed that a positive red coloration, as the positive control, was detected in samples of Aa593-FL and Aa593-SP (Figure  4b). To further visually demonstrate that Aa593 can be secreted from A. alternata and translocate to the plant cells, we injected the leaves of Chrysanthemum seticuspe with hyphal suspension of A. alternata containing pAa593::Aa593-GFP (Figure 4c). At 24 hpi, green fluorescence was observed in the nucleus and other subcellular structures of leaf cells (Figure 4d). Further, the subcellular localization of Aa593 in N. benthamiana revealed that both the full-length Aa593 and the Aa593-ΔSP proteins were distributed throughout tobacco cells (Figure 4e). These findings indicate that Aa593 can be delivered into the plant cells.

# 2.5 | Aa593 Contributes to the Virulence of A. alternata

To assess the contribution of Aa593 to the virulence of A. alternata, we generated Aa593-deleted mutant (ΔAa593) and complementation (ΔAa593-C) strains (Figures  S11a,b, 5a). Compared with wild-type F20 (F20-WT) and ΔAa593-C, no 

appreciable changes in the morphology of ΔAa593 mutant colonies or hyphae were observed; however, the hyphae of mutants exhibited reduced rates of growth (Figure  S11c–e). Furthermore, at 2 dpi, the leaves of chrysanthemum inoculated with the ΔAa593 mutants appeared to have significantly less pronounced disease symptoms than those inoculated with F20-WT or ΔAa593-C. In addition, the lesion areas were notably smaller than those of leaves infected with F20-WT or ΔAa593-C (Figure  5b,c). When chrysanthemum leaves were inoculated with varying mycelial amounts of ΔAa593 mutants, the disease severity and lesion area still did not reach those caused by the normal mycelial amount of F20-WT (Figure  S12a,b). Further correlation analysis conducted separately between the lesion area and biomass for the ΔAa593 mutants revealed that the relationship was not strictly proportional; rather, it displayed a positive correlation with slopes $< 1$ (Figure  S12c). Additionally, RT-qPCR analysis revealed that the expression of immune-related marker genes in chrysanthemum leaves inoculated with the ΔAa593 mutant was significantly higher than in those inoculated with F20-WT (Figure  S12d). These results demonstrate that the loss of pathogenicity in the ΔAa593 mutant is primarily due to a weakened ability to suppress plant immunity, rather than simply impaired growth. To further confirm whether Aa593 

facilitates A. alternata infection, we constructed two OE-Aa593 chrysanthemum lines (Figure 5d). Compared with WT, overexpression lines showed more severe disease symptoms (Figure  5e) and significantly larger lesion areas (Figure  5f). Similar results were obtained following transient overexpression of Aa593 in N. benthamiana and stable overexpression of Aa593 in A. thaliana (Figure  S13). Collectively, Aa593 contributes to the full virulence of A. alternata. 

# 2.6 | Aa593 Facilitates A. alternata Infection by Hijacking the CmNAC29-CmNCED3 Module

The above results suggest that Aa593 exerts its virulence via the CmNAC29-mediated regulation pathway. To support the supposition, we analyzed the expression of CmNCED3 in WT and OE-Aa593 chrysanthemum lines. Compared with WT, there was a significantly up-regulated expression of CmNCED3 in OE-Aa593 plants (Figure  6a), as well as elevated levels of endogenous ABA (Figure  6b). These results thus provided evidence for a causal link between Aa593 and CmNCED3. Transcriptional activity analysis further reveals that Aa593 exhibits transcriptional self-activation activity in yeast (Figure  S14). However, Y1H assays showed that Aa593 does not bind directly to the promoter of CmNCED3 (Figure  6c), indicating that Aa593 may indirectly regulate the expression of CmNCED3. To assess the possibility, we performed a chrysanthemum protoplast transient assay (Figure 6d). The LUC/ REN ratio was significantly higher when both p35S::Cm-NAC29 and p35S::Aa593 were present, compared to the ratio observed with p35S::CmNAC29 alone (Figure 6e). The results indicate that Aa593 enhances the transcriptional activity of CmNAC29 on the downstream gene CmNCED3. To gain further insights into the mechanisms whereby Aa593 influences the regulation of the downstream gene targeted by CmNAC29, we performed the transcriptional activity analysis in chrysanthemum protoplasts. As shown in Figure 6f,g, we detected a significant increase in the LUC/REN value in the presence of both p35S::GAL4DB-CmNAC29 and p35S::GAL4DB-Aa593, indicating that Aa593 contributes to enhancing the transcriptional activation of CmNAC29. To examine whether Aa593 affects the DNA-binding ability of CmNAC29, we performed an EMSA. Notably, with the addition of graded amounts of Aa593-His, the bound bands remained unchanged (Figure 6h), which suggests that Aa593 does not alter the binding ability of CmNAC29 to the promoter of CmNCED3. 

To determine whether ABA can compensate for the lost virulence of the ΔAa593 mutants, we pretreated chrysanthemum with water or ABA, and thereafter inoculated it with F20-WT or ΔAa593 mutants. At 2 dpi, compared with the water-sprayed control plants, chrysanthemum leaves infected by F20-WT or ΔAa593 mutants following exogenous ABA application all exhibited more serious symptoms, which were consistent with previous results (Figure  2k–n). Moreover, the severity of disease in the ABA-treatment leaves inoculated with ΔAa593 mutants was similar to that of the control leaves inoculated with F20-WT following spraying with water (Figure  6i,j). These findings suggest that exogenously applied ABA can compensate for the attenuated virulence of the ΔAa593 mutants. To explore whether the toxicity of Aa593 

depends on ABA, we treated N. benthamiana with fluridone (an ABA synthesis inhibitor) and injected the treated leaves with Agrobacterium suspension containing p35S::Aa593. RTqPCR results showed that the overexpression of Aa593 inhibited the expression of immune marker genes NbPR1, NbPR2, NbGLNb, NbSOBIR1, NbLOX and NbHSR203J in tobacco without fluridone. However, after fluridone treatment, the expression of these genes showed an upward trend, and the degree of inhibition by Aa593 was reduced, in which the expression of NbSOBIR1, NbLOX, and NbHSR203J was no longer inhibited by Aa593 (Figure  S15). These results indicate that the virulence of Aa593 depends partly on the ABA pathway. 

Subsequently, to further elucidate the dependency between Aa593 and CmNAC29, we inoculated chrysanthemums with F20-WT or ΔAa593 mutant and performed RT-qPCR. Compared with inoculation with F20-WT, the expression level of CmNAC29 in the leaves of ΔAa593 mutant-inoculated chrysanthemum was lower, as well as the ABA content (Figure  7a,b), further supporting the connection between Aa593 and CmNAC29. Then, we conducted transient transformation experiments using protoplasts from WT and amiR-CmNAC29 interference lines. Compared with the empty vector, the LUC/REN values were significantly increased in both wild-type and amiR-CmNAC29 protoplasts when p35S::Aa593 and pCmNCED3::LUC plasmids were introduced, further supporting Aa593's role as a transcriptional activator. Additionally, compared with WT, the LUC/ REN ratio was significantly reduced in amiR-CmNAC29 protoplasts after introducing p35S::Aa593 plasmid, though it did not decrease to the same level as the empty vector (Figure 7c), indicating that Aa593 partially depends on CmNAC29 to regulate the transcription of CmNCED3. Furthermore, compared with WT, the amiR-CmNAC29 plants showed significantly higher resistance regardless of whether inoculated with F20-WT or ΔAa593 mutant, consistent with previous results (Figure 1d–f); moreover, the lesion area of amiR-CmNAC29 plants was further reduced after inoculation with ΔAa593 mutant compared with inoculation with F20-WT (Figure  7d). We further conducted transient interference expression of CmNAC29 in OE-Aa593 chrysanthemum plants (Figure S16) and performed inoculation experiments. The results showed that knockdown of CmNAC29 expression inhibited Aa593-mediated pathogenicity (Figure 7e). Collectively, our findings indicate that Aa593 indirectly contributes to inducing the production of ABA in plants and promotes the infection of A. alternata by hijacking the CmNAC29- CmNCED3 module.

# 3.3 | Aa593 Promotes Virulence of A. alternata by Targeting CmNAC29-Mediated ABA Pathway

To establish infections in host plants, pathogens secrete effectors that facilitate their invasion and nutrient acquisition, promoting growth and colonisation. Several effectors contribute to the virulence of Alternaria species (Duhan et  al.  2021; Wang, Zhang, et al. 2022; Wang et al. 2023; Qiu, Halterman, et al. 2024; Qiu, Zhang, et al. 2024; Xiao et al. 2024). However, the mechanisms by which Alternaria effectors interact with host proteins remain poorly understood. 

In this study, we identified a novel effector, Aa593, in A. alternata, which interacts with chrysanthemum CmNAC29, advancing our understanding of the interactions between necrotrophic fungi and plants. Aa593 targets this nuclearlocalised transcription factor in plants to promote A. alternata infection; it also localises to the nucleus in fungal cells (Figure  4c–e), suggesting that nuclear localization may be 

critical for its function. To explore this further, we attempted to construct a nuclear localization-deficient mutant of Aa593. However, despite using multiple nuclear localization signal (NLS) prediction tools (such as cNLS Mapper, NucPred, and NLStradamus), we could not identify any typical NLS in its amino acid sequence. Future investigations may require the introduction of an efficient nuclear export signal to disrupt its nuclear localization. Given its nuclear presence in fungi, we hypothesized that Aa593 functions as a transcriptional regulator. Supporting this hypothesis, Aa593 exhibited transcriptional activation activity in yeast (Figure  S14), indicating that it may act as a transcriptional activator during A. alternata infection, potentially enhancing virulence by modulating pathogenicity-related genes—though additional evidence is needed. Furthermore, the expression of Aa593 induced cell death in both tobacco and chrysanthemum leaves (Figure  4f,g), which may facilitate fungal invasion. These findings align with earlier reports on necrotrophic effectors promoting disease susceptibility in plants (Oliver and Solomon 2010; Li et al. 2019; Sung et al. 2021). Additionally, ΔAa593 mutants showed slower hyphal growth rates and significantly impaired pathogenicity (Figures  5, S11). We speculate that Aa593 may play a role in regulating the vegetative growth cycle of A. alternata, which influences toxin accumulation during this phase, thereby partially affecting virulence. Further investigation is required to elucidate the precise relationship between vegetative growth and virulence. 

Effectors promote virulence by interfering with hormone synthesis or response pathways. For example, AvrE, an effector produced by the bacterium Pseudomonas syringae, induces the production of ABA in host plants, resulting in stomatal closure and generating an aqueous environment conducive to bacterial reproduction (Hu et al. 2022). An RXLR effector, PlAvh202, inhibits ethylene production in Litchi chinensis and promotes the colonisation of Peronophythora litchii (Li et al. 2023). In addition, effectors can also promote the virulence of pathogens by suppressing the transduction of hormone signals, such as salicylic acid (Liu et  al.  2014; Qi et  al.  2022), auxin (Chen et  al.  2007), and ABA (Peng et al. 2019; Hu et al. 2022). Here, we found that by interacting with CmNAC29, Aa593 indirectly hijacks the ABA synthesis pathway in plants, thereby causing a hormonal imbalance and promoting A. alternata infection. # 4 | Methods

# 4.1 | Plant Materials and Growth Conditions

The chrysanthemum ‘Jinba’ and A. thaliana (Col-0) were used for genetic transformation and ABA treatment. C. seticuspe, because of its thin leaves, was used for the observation of Aa593 secretion. N. benthamiana was employed for subcellular protein localization, protein interaction assays, and functional characterisation of Aa593. All plant materials were cultivated in a 1:3 mixture of nutrient soil and vermiculite, maintained at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with $7 0 \%$ relative humidity under a 16/8 h (light/dark) photoperiod. 

# 4.2 | Gene Cloning and Sequence Analysis

The coding sequence (CDS) of CmNAC29 was cloned and ligated into a pEASY-Blunt cloning vector (TransGen, Beijing, China). We downloaded the amino acid sequences of genes with high similarity in the TAIR and NCBI databases. The amino acid sequences of several typical NACs were selected for alignment and conserved domain analysis. The alignment was performed using ClustalX 1.81, and a phylogenetic tree was constructed by MEGA5.1. 

# 4.3 | RT-qPCR Analysis

RNA was extracted using an RNA isolation kit (Huayueyang Biotechnology, Beijing, China) and was reverse-transcribed to cDNA using the Evo M-MLV RT Kit with gDNA Clean for qPCR II (Accurate Biotechnology, Changsha, China). RTqPCR was performed with the Roche LightCycler480 II (Roche, 

Basel, Switzerland) using the SYBR Green Premix Pro Taq HS qPCR Kit (Accurate Biotechnology, Changsha, China). CmEF1α (GenBank: KF305681), AaActin (Liu, Xin, Liu, Song, Liao, et  al.  2020) and NbEF1a (GenBank: Niben101Scf34389) worked as internal reference genes, and relative transcription levels were calculated using the $2 ^ { - \Delta \Delta \mathrm { C t } }$ method (Livak and Schmittgen 2001). Samples were analysed using three biological replicates, with three technical replicates. 

# 4.4 | Creation of Transgenic Plants

The primers (Table  S1) were designed by the Web MicroRNA Designer (http://wmd3.weigelworld.org/cgi-bin/webapp.cgi) to amplify amiR-CmNAC29 interference fragments (Schwab et  al.  2006). The PCR products were then inserted into a pORE- $2 \times 3 5 \mathrm { S } \mathrm { - } \mathrm { R } 4$ vector. To obtain OE-CmNAC29 lines or OE-Aa593 lines of chrysanthemums, the full-length CDS of CmNAC29 or Aa593 was inserted into a pORE-R4-35AA vector. These vectors were subsequently transformed into A. tumefaciens EHA105, and transgenic plants were obtained via leaf disc transformation (Li et al. 2015). For the transformation of A. thaliana, the pK7WGF2-Aa593-ΔSP-mRFP1 vector was transformed into wild-type Col-0 using the floral dip method (Clough and Bent 1998). 

# 4.5 | Inoculation and Monitoring of Lesion Development

F20 (a pathogenic strain of A. alternata) was cultured on potato dextrose agar (PDA) medium at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 days. The fungal cakes (approximately 5 mm in diameter) were then excised from the colony edge and transferred into potato dextrose water (PDW) medium, followed by incubation at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ with shaking at 200 rpm for 1 day. Subsequently, 10 well-grown hyphal balls were crushed and cultured in $1 0 0 \mathrm { m L }$ of fresh PDW for an additional 1 day under identical conditions. 1 mL of hyphal suspension was collected and inoculated on the reverse side of the third fully expanded leaf, with each leaf being inoculated at two points. Then the plants were grown at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , and the inoculated leaves were photographed at 2 dpi. For the inoculation of $A$ . thaliana, $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of hyphal suspension was enriched, with each leaf being inoculated at a single point. 

# 4.6 | Yeast Transactivation Assay

The CDS of CmNAC29 was inserted into a BD vector. BD-CmNAC29, along with controls, was severally transformed into the yeast strain Y2H. Yeast cells harbouring the BD-CmNAC29 or BD constructs were cultured on SD/−Trp medium, whereas those containing pCL1 were cultured on SD/−Leu medium at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 days. Thereafter, the growing yeast cells were inoculated into SD/−His/−Ade medium with or without X-α-gal. To avoid potential false positives caused by the transcriptional activation activity of full-length CmNAC29 in subsequent Y2H library screening, CmNAC29 was cleaved into N- (containing the NAM domain) and C-terminal fragments to further identify the region responsible for transcriptional activation. Other BD vectors were constructed and transformed as described above. 

# 4.7 | RNA-Seq Analysis

The leaves of WT and amiR-CmNAC29 plants before and after inoculation with A. alternata were collected. For each line, three biological replicates were analysed. RNA-seq was performed by Annoroad Gene Technology Co. Ltd. (Beijing, China) using Illumina Novaseq 6000. PCA was operated using R package models (http://www.r-project.org/). Analysis of differential expression was performed using DESeq2. Screening of DEGs was based on of FDR $\leq 0 . 0 5$ and Fold Change $\geq 1 . 2$ . All RNA-seq analysis was performed using BMKCloud (https://www.biocl oud.net). 

# 4.8 | Y1H

The CDS of CmNAC29 and Aa593-ΔSP were cloned and inserted into an AD vector, respectively. The promoter of CmNCED3 and its mutation fragments were inserted into the pHIS2 vector, respectively. The yeast strain Y187 was co-transformed with the above pHIS2 and AD vectors, and the transformants were cultured on SD-Trp/-His/−Leu medium at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 days. Thereafter, the growing yeast cells were inoculated into SD-Trp/-His/−Leu medium with or without $1 8 0 \mathrm { m M }$ 3-amino-1, 2, 4-triazole (3-AT). 

# 4.9 | ChIP-qPCR

Two grams of leaves from WT and OE-CmNAC29 chrysanthemum lines were collected. After crosslinking with formaldehyde, chromatin was extracted and fragmented. A total of $5 \mu \mathrm { g }$ anti - GFP recombinant rabbit monoclonal antibody (G10362, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Shanghai, China) was used for immunoprecipitation to obtain protein-DNA complexes. After de-crosslinking, the DNA was purified and dissolved for qPCR analysis (Wang, Sun, et al. 2020). 

# 4.10 | Y2H Library Screening Analysis

Chrysanthemum leaves at different time points after inoculation with A. alternata were collected to construct a Y2H cDNA library. BD vector harboring the CmNAC29 gene segments 

without transcriptional activation activity was used as a bait and co-transformed with the library plasmids into Y2H, and then cultured on SD-Trp/-His/−Leu/−Ade medium at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . The growing yeast plaques were used as templates for PCR amplification, and PCR products $> 5 0 0$ bp were submitted for sequencing. The sequences thus obtained were analyzed by BLAST against  the NCBI or TAIR databases. The full-length CDSs of candidate effector genes were cloned, and the relevant protein information was predicted by the TMHMM 2.0, big-PI Fungal Predictor, signalP 5.0, ExPASy-ProtParam, NCBI-Conserved Domains, and SMART websites. 

# 4.11 | Subcellular Localization, BiFC, and LCI

To observe the subcellular localization, pENTR1A-CmNAC29 was constructed and recombined with a pEarleyGate104-EYFP vector using LR Clonase II (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA). Additionally, pK7WGF2-Aa593-FL-mRFP1 and pK7WGF2- Aa593-ΔSP-mRFP1 (without signal peptide) vectors were constructed using the Ready-to-Use Seamless Cloning Kit (Sangon Biotech, Shanghai, China). These vectors were transformed into A. tumefaciens GV3101, and the corresponding suspensions were subsequently inoculated into N. benthamiana. For BiFC assays, we constructed the vectors pYL1300-CmNAC29-Vn-Aa593-Vc, pYL1300-CmNAC29-Vn-Vc and pYL1300-Vn-Aa593-Vc, according to the procedure described previously (Han et al. 2022). A. tumefaciens suspensions harbouring different vectors were respectively injected into N. benthamiana. At 2 dpi, all fluorescence signals were examined under a LSM900 confocal laser scanning microscope (Zeiss, Oberkochen, Germany). 

For LCI assays, pCAMBIA1300-CmNAC29-nLUC and pCAMBIA1300-cLUC-Aa593 were constructed and separately transformed into GV3101. All groups were inoculated into $N .$ . benthamiana. At 2 dpi, the leaves were sprayed with a 1 mM solution of fluorescein sodium salt (Sigma, Mannheim, Germany), and LUC activity was assessed using a Tanon 5200 CCD camera (Tanon Science, Shanghai, China). 

# 4.12 | Co-IP

Co-IP assay was conducted as previously described (Li et al. 2018). Briefly, N. benthamiana expressing proteins of interest were treated with MG132 (M7449, Sigma); total proteins were extracted, followed by immunoprecipitations with anti-HA affinity gel (88836, Thermo Fisher Scientific). CmNAC29- $3 \times$ FLAG and Aa593-3×HA were detected by western blotting using anti-FLAG (A8592, Sigma) and anti-HA (BP5015-02, ACE Biotechnology, Changzhou, China). 

# 4.13 | GST Pull-Down

To verify the interaction in  vitro, we constructed pGEX-5X-1- CmNAC29 (containing GST-tag) and pET-32a-Aa593-ΔSP (containing His-tag) vectors. These constructs and pGEX-5X-1 empty vectors were transformed into Escherichia coli BL21 (DE3) and induced to obtain recombinant proteins by isopropyl- $\cdot \beta$ -Dthiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 6 h. Aa593-ΔSP-His 

was incubated with GST-CmNAC29 or GST at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 h. Then, GST magnetic beads (Thermo Fisher Scientific) were added and incubated overnight at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The proteins were detected using GST and His antibodies (Thermo Fisher Scientific). 

# 4.14 | Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assays

The promoter of CmNCED3 was inserted into a pGreenII0800- LUC vector and subsequently transformed into GV3101 containing pSoup. A. tumefaciens suspensions harbouring the p35S::CmNAC29-GFP or p35S::GFP and pCmNCED3::LUC were mixed and injected into N. benthamiana. At 2 dpi, the leaves were sprayed with fluorescein sodium salt and then photographed using a Tanon 5200 CCD camera (Tanon Science). 

To assess the effects of Aa593 on the transcriptional regulation of CmNAC29, we constructed R4-FVuv-CmNAC29 and R4-FVuv-Aa593 vectors. These constructs or empty vectors and pCmNCED3::LUC were mixed and transformed into wildtype chrysanthemum protoplasts (Wang et  al.  2021). To determine whether Aa593 alters the transcriptional activity of CmNAC29, we used LR Clonase II (Invitrogen) to recombine pENTR1A-CmNAC29 with p35S::GLAL4DB, thereby obtaining p35S::GLAL4DB-CmNAC29. The controls, p35S::GLAL4DB-CmNAC29, or p35S::Aa593 + p35S::GLAL4DB-CmNAC29 were mixed with pGreen0800-LUC-p35S::REN vector (Huang, Jiang, et  al.  2023) in equal proportions and transformed into chrysanthemum protoplasts. To further investigate the dependency relationship between Aa593 and CmNAC29, we independently transformed Fvuv-Aa593 or empty vector and pCmNCED3::LUC into wild-type and amiR-CmNAC29 chrysanthemum protoplasts. All transformed protoplasts were incubated in the dark at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 8 \mathrm { h }$ . LUC/REN values were determined with a dual luciferase reporter assay kit (Vazyme, Nanjing, China) using a GloMax 20/20 microplate reader (Promega, Madison, WI, USA). 

# 4.15 | Determination of ABA Content

The contents of ABA were determined using a plant ABA ELISA kit (Lengton Bioscience Co., Shanghai, China). The third fully expanded leaves were sampled. For different treatments, determination was performed for three biological replicates with three technical repetitions. 

# 4.16 | VIGS

To obtain amiR-CmNCED3 interference lines, we used primers (Table  S1) designed by the Web MicroRNA Designer (http:// wmd3.weigelworld.org/cgi-bin/webapp.cgi) for PCR amplification. The PCR products were inserted into a cabbage leaf curl virus (CaLCuV) vector (Tang et  al.  2010). The resulting construct was transformed into GV3101. A. tumefaciens suspension harbouring CaLCuV or CaLCuV-amiR-CmNCED3 and pCVB were mixed and transformed into ‘Jinba’ via vacuum penetration (Huang et al. 2022). To determine the dependency relationship between CmNAC29 and Aa593, we used the method described above to transiently interfere with CmNAC29 in the OE-Aa593 chrysanthemum lines. 

# 4.17 | EMSA

EMSA analysis was performed as described previously (Wang, Sun, et  al.  2020). Briefly, we purified GST-CmNAC29 protein induced in E. coli BL21 (DE3). Probes containing predicted binding sites and mutation sites in the promoter of CmNCED3 were labelled with biotin using an EMSA Probe Biotin Labeling Kit (Beyotime Biotechnology, Shanghai, China). 

To examine the effects of Aa593 on the binding of CmNAC29 to the CmNCED3 promoter, we purified the Aa593-His protein using Ni-NTA magnetic beads (Thermo Fisher Scientific), different amounts of which were added to GST-CmNAC29 and the biotin-labeled probes. 

# 4.18 | Confirmation of the Secreted SP of Aa593

Aa593-FL (containing SP), Aa593-ΔSP (without SP) and Aa593-SP (only containing SP) were severally inserted into the pSUC2 vector, transformed into the yeast strain YTK12, and coated on CMD/-W medium at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 days. The growing yeast cells were inoculated into CMD/-W and YPRAA media. In addition, transformants and non-transformants derived from YTK12 were cultured overnight in CMD/-W liquid medium and were resuspended in a solution containing $7 5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of sterile water, $2 5 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ acetic acid–sodium acetate buffer (pH 4.7), and $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ $1 0 \%$ sucrose solution (w/v). Thereafter, $0 . 1 \%$ TTC solution was added and incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . 

To observe the secretion of Aa593 in plant cells, the full length of Aa593 with its native promoter was fused with GFP in the pYF11 vector. Then the pAa593::Aa593-GFP vector was transformed into A. alternata protoplasts (Liu, Xin, Liu, Song, Liao, et  al.  2020). The leaves of C. seticuspe were injected with the hyphal suspension of the obtained strain, and the fluorescence signal was observed with a LSM900 confocal laser scanning microscope (Zeiss) at 24 hpi. 

# 4.19 | Analysis of the Ability to Induce Plant Cell Death and Suppress Plant Immunity

Vectors pICH31160-Aa593-FL-3HA and pICH31160-Aa593- ΔSP-3HA were constructed and transformed into GV3101 containing pJIC SA_Rep. Agrobacterium suspensions $( \mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } { = } 0 . 8 )$ were inoculated into N. benthamiana (six leaves in total). For analyzing the effect of overexpression of Aa593 on the immune genes, tobacco leaves were pretreated with sterile water or $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ fluridone, an inhibitor of ABA synthesis, for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ , respectively, and then injected with Agrobacterium suspension containing empty vector or p35S::Aa593. The leaves were sampled for RT-qPCR after $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ . 

In order to determine whether Aa593 can induce cell death in chrysanthemum, Ni-NTA magnetic beads were used (Thermo Fisher Scientific) to purify pET-Aa593-FL and pET-Aa593-ΔSP proteins. These proteins, along with the purified protein derived from the pET-32a empty vector, were then inoculated onto the same chrysanthemum leaves (three leaves in total). For analyzing the effects of Aa593 on immune gene expression, the above 

proteins were injected into chrysanthemum leaves, and samples were taken $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ later for RT-qPCR. 

# 4.20 | Generation of ΔAa593 Mutant and ΔAa593-C Complementation Strains

A gene recombination approach was adopted to replace the full length of Aa593 with the Hygromycin gene HPH. This recombinant gene fragment was transformed into A. alternata protoplasts via PEG-mediated transformation (Liu, Xin, Liu, Song, Liao, et  al.  2020). Gene sequencing and RT-qPCR were performed to identify the positive mutant strains. To construct the complementation strain, we amplified the native promoter and CDS of Aa593 using primers pYF11-Aa593-GFP-F/R (Table S1). The PCR product was then inserted into the pYF11-GFP vector via homologous recombination. After sequence verification, the recombinant plasmid was transformed into the protoplasts of the ΔAa593 mutant strain. Positive strains were subsequently verified through both RT-qPCR analysis and GFP fluorescence visualisation using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Zeiss). 

To assess the effects of the Aa593 mutation on the morphology and growth of A. alternata, each strain was inoculated with 5 potato dextrose agar (PDA) plates and then placed at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 7 days. Then we measured colony diameters and obtained photographic records of mycelial morphology. Additionally, we examined the morphology of hyphae using a DM1000 light microscope (Leica, Wetzlar, Germany). Furthermore, we inoculated chrysanthemum leaves with different strains as described previously, with at least 10 biological replicates for each treatment. 

# 4.21 | Inoculation Following Transient Overexpression of Aa593 in N. benthamiana

The left and right sides of the tobacco leaves were inoculated with Agrobacterium suspensions pICH31160-GFP and pICH31160- Aa593-FL. Then the plants were placed in a dark environment $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , relative humidity of $7 0 \%$ ) for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ . Subsequently, A. alternata was inoculated at the same sites, following which the plants were again placed in the dark $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , relative humidity of $9 0 \%$ ). In total, we treated five tobacco leaves, and after culturing for 2 days, the inoculated leaves were photographed. 

# 4.22 | ABA Treatment and Inoculation

Plants were sprayed with sterilised water (containing $0 . 5 \%$ ethanol) or $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ ABA (10 mL per plant) and cultured in the dark for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ . Thereafter, leaves were inoculated with different $A$ . alternata strains as described previously. Each treatment was performed with no $< 1 0$ biological replicates. The effectiveness of ABA was confirmed by RT-qPCR analysis of ABA-responsive marker genes. 

# 4.23 | Statistical Analysis

The lesion areas were calculated using Image J (NIH, Bethesda, MD, USA). 

All data were statistically analysed using IBM SPSS Statistics 20 (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA). Student's t-test or Duncan's multiple test was used to determine the significant differences.
