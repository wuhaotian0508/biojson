# ABSTRACT

Fruit stones play an important role in fruit development and are closely related to fruit quality. Lignification of the fruit stone (endocarp) is a typical feature of stone fruits. Stoneless jujube is a valuable jujube germplasm resource, and the degree of lignification of its fruit stone is low. However, the underlying mechanisms of lignification in the jujube endocarp remain unclear. In this study, we characterised a novel Class III peroxidase, PRX1, via comparative transcriptome analysis of the jujube endocarp and investigated its potential molecular mechanism in endocarp lignin synthesis. Subcellular localisation analysis revealed that ZjPRX1 was localised to the cytoplasm. According to the results of an in situ hybridisation analysis, the tissue-specific expression of ZjPRX1 was greatest in the endocarp. Moreover, ZjPRX1 overexpression in jujube fruits and calluses caused significant changes in the amount of lignin that accumulated. Scanning electron microscopy revealed that the cell walls of the stems of ZjPRX1-OE Arabidopsis lines were significantly thicker than those of WT Arabidopsis. Yeast one-hybrid, dual-luciferase reporter and electrophoretic mobility shift assays indicated that ZjbZIP33 activated ZjPRX1 expression by binding specifically to the G-box element in the ZjPRX1 promoter. IAA and $\mathrm { G A } _ { 3 }$ induced ZjbZIP33-ZjPRX1 expression and lignin formation. Overall, the results revealed a novel module, that is, ZjbZIP33-ZjPRX1, that positively regulates lignin formation in the jujube fruit stone. This study contributes to a better understanding of the molecular basis of lignin synthesis in the endocarp of jujube fruits and identifies candidate genes for optimising stone fruit breeding. 

# 1 | Introduction

Stone fruits with hardened endocarps are important fruits. Many stone fruit tree species, such as peach (Guo et  al.  2020), plum (Egea et al. 2022) and jujube (Liu et al. 2014), have significant economic value. In production, some cultivars with stoneless fruits are popular because stoneless fruits are very conducive to processing and are edible. Thus, the hardening of the endocarp is worthy of further study, and the developmental regulation of 

endocarp hardening has important theoretical value and industrial significance (Yu et al. 2023). The main components of the fruit endocarp are lignin and cellulose (Huss et al. 2023). The regulation of lignin synthesis in the fruit endocarp is the key to revealing the reason for fruit stone degradation, and the specific regulatory mechanisms have yet to be elucidated. 

Phenylpropane metabolism is the main pathway of lignin synthesis and involves the regulation of a variety of enzymes and a series 

of fine and complex regulatory processes (Zhang et  al.  2023a). Among these enzymes, peroxidases (PRXs) are located downstream of phenylpropane metabolism and catalyse the polymerisation of lignin monomers (Wei et al. 2024), having an important impact on lignin synthesis. Lignin deposition in Arabidopsis roots requires the regulation of AtPRX64, and mutations in this gene lead to changes in lignin content and composition (Morel et al. 2022). In pear, PbPRX2, PbPRX34, PbRX64 and PbPRX75 catalyse the synthesis of coniferol and sinapol, which participate in polymerisation to form lignin monomers and play key roles in the lignification of stone cells (Cao et al. 2016). In watermelon, ClPRX51 and ClPRX54 participate in lignin synthesis, thus promoting the formation of pericarp stone cells (Yang et al. 2022). At present, the mechanism of lignin polymerisation is poorly understood, but plant cells can control the location of lignin deposition by regulating the location of lignin-associated PRX and LAC (Hoffmann et al. 2020). 

Lignin is the main component of secondary cell walls, and the formation of secondary cell walls is a complex and finely regulated biological process. NAC and MYB are the most widely studied transcription factors for lignin synthesis (Qiang et al. 2022). In addition, other transcription factors, including bZIP, C3H, WRKY and bHLH, identified in pear fruits may regulate lignin synthesis (Wang et al. 2021). Regulatory networks of transcription factors and secondary cell wall metabolic genes have been constructed for Arabidopsis and poplar (Taylor-Teeples et al. 2015; Wei and Wei  2023). PagMYB128 is a positive regulator of lignin, cellulose and hemicellulose biosynthesis in poplar wood formation. Compared with AtMYB103, PagMYB128 has a wider regulatory role in secondary wall biosynthesis (Hao et al. 2024). Similarly, PbMYB61 binds to AC elements in the promoter of PbLAC1 and regulates the formation of lignin in stone cells (Zhu et al. 2023). AtSND5 can act as the first-tier switch to activate the expression of downstream secondary wall synthesis-related genes (Zhong et al. 2021). In rose, RhbZIP17 and RhWRKY30 regulate the expression of RhCAD, increase lignin biosynthesis and increase the resistance of rose petals (Li et al. 2024). 

Chinese jujube is a stone fruit tree species in Asia, and jujube fruits have good nutritional and economic value (Guo et  al.  2024). ‘Wuhexiaozao’ (‘Wuhe’) is an excellent stoneless fruit cultivar and is a bud mutation of the stone fruit cultivar ‘Jinsixiaozao’ (‘Jinsi’). The two cultivars are typical materials for studying fruit stone development. To date, the molecular mechanisms in the fruit stone of jujube remain largely unknown. Here, the fruit stones of the two aforementioned cultivars were used as materials. ZjPRX1 was found to positively regulate lignin formation, and ZjbZIP33 was screened as an upstream transcription factor. Moreover, IAA $\mathrm { G A } _ { 3 }$ induced the expression of ZjbZIP33 and ZjPRX1 in the jujube fruit stone. The results provide genetic resources for molecular breeding and a technical reference for the regulation of the production of stoneless jujube fruits. 

# 2 | Results

# 2.1 | ZjPRX1 Is Closely Related to the Hardening of Jujube Fruit Endocarps

To investigate the hardening process of the jujube endocarp during fruit stone development, we collected the fruits of the 

‘Jinsi’ and ‘Wuhe’ cultivars at different developmental stages. Histological observations revealed that the pigmented site and colour of the endocarp region of ‘Jinsi’ were significantly deeper than those of ‘Wuhe’ at the same developmental stage (Figure  1a), indicating that lignin deposition was greater in the endocarp of ‘Jinsi’. Moreover, the lignin content of their endocarps was measured (Figure  1b), and the results revealed that the lignin content in ‘Jinsi’ increased rapidly from Stage S2 and was significantly greater than that in ‘Wuhe’. Furthermore, paraffin sections were used to observe the changes in the fruit stone. Compared with that of ‘Wuhe’, the endocarp of ‘Jinsi’ had more cell layers and greater lignification (Figure 1c), suggesting that the development of the fruit stone is closely related to lignin. 

On the basis of the transcriptome data, most genes involved in lignin biosynthesis were significantly upregulated during the development of the fruit endocarp, of which PRXs presented the most significant changes (Figure  1d). Furthermore, the qRT-PCR results revealed that ZjPRX1 expression differed significantly between ‘Jinsi’ and ‘Wuhe’ and was positively correlated with lignin content (Figure 1e and Figure S1). ZjPRX1 was subsequently selected as a candidate gene for functional analyses. 

# 2.2 | ZjPRX1 Exhibits Tissue-Specific Expression in the Jujube Fruit Endocarp

ZjPRX1 encodes 333 amino acids, and the protein contains a conserved peroxidase domain and several haem-binding sites, active sites, substrate-binding sites and calcium-binding sites (Figure  2a). The phylogenetic analysis indicated that ZjPRX1 was most closely related to Rosaceae species (Figure  2b). Subcellular localisation and plasmolysis assay revealed that the 35S::ZjPRX1-GFP fusion protein localised to the cytoplasm (Figure 2c and Figure S2). 

To investigate the tissue-specific expression of ZjPRX1, five jujube tissues were used to assess ZjPRX1 expression via RT-PCR. ZjPRX1 was highly expressed in fruits (Figure 2d). Furthermore, the expression patterns of ZjPRX1 were explored in the endocarp and mesocarp of six ‘Jinsi’ and ‘Wuhe’ varieties via RTqPCR. The results revealed that the expression of ZjPRX1 in the endocarp was significantly greater than that in the mesocarp of all the varieties (Figure  2e). The spatial expression pattern of ZjPRX1 was subsequently verified via RNA in  situ hybridisation, and the results revealed that ZjPRX1 accumulated abundantly in the endocarp of ‘Jinsi’, with a weak signal observed in ‘Wuhe’ (Figure 2f), suggesting that this gene is actively involved in jujube endocarp formation.

# 2.5 | ZjbZIP33 Activates the Transcription of ZjPRX1 Through Direct G-Box Binding

A comprehensive analysis of the ZjPRX1 promoter was conducted to identify upstream regulators involved in lignin synthesis. Through PlantCARE analysis, a G-box and two MYB elements were identified in the 2000-bp promoter region of ZjPRX1 (Figure  5a and Table  S1). ZjPRX1 was used as a hub gene, and the coexpression network was comprehensively investigated via WGCNA (Figure S4). # 3 | Discussion

Among fruit trees, stone fruit trees are unique because their fruits contain a hardened endocarp, which provides a good safeguard for seeds and influences fruit quality. The development of a hardened endocarp is a complex and important scientific process that is closely related to lignin synthesis in the endocarp. The regulatory mechanism of lignin synthesis has been a research focus in woody plants (Tong et al. 2021; Allen et al. 2022; Song et al. 2023). However, why lignin synthesis occurs specifically in the endocarp of stone fruits is still unclear. As typical fruit trees, the fruits of most jujube cultivars have a hardened endocarp (stone fruit), and only a few cultivars have a soft endocarp (stoneless fruit). ‘Wuhe’ is a valuable type of stoneless fruit and is a bud mutation of ‘Jinsi’. These two cultivars have great research value for elucidating the regulatory mechanism of hardened endocarp development. In our study, the key genes in the lignin synthesis pathway were screened via transcriptomic analysis of the endocarp of the above two cultivars at different developmental stages (Figure 1c,d). 

Class III peroxidases (PRXs) are plant-specific oxidoreductases that participate in the last step of lignin synthesis and are key enzymes that catalyse the lignin monomer to generate lignin. However, owing to the large number of genes in this family, redundancy and extensive substrate specificity exist. The functions of the PRXs involved in lignin synthesis are species- and tissue-specific. In loquat, the expression of EjPRX12 is much greater in fruit than in other tissues, and EjPRX12 is also the most abundant PRX in fruit (Zhang et  al.  2022). In maize, ZmPRX1 overexpression increases the lignin content, and mutation of the gene reduces the lignin content in the roots (Zhai et al. 2024). MdPRX63 can induce lignin deposition in apple roots and increase resistance (Liu et  al.  2023). Therefore, the identification of PRXs related to lignification in fruit stone is key for revealing the regulatory mechanism of fruit stone development. In this study, ZjPRX1 was identified and was shown to be expressed specifically in jujube fruit stone (Figure 2f); additionally, its function in lignin synthesis was confirmed in jujube and Arabidopsis (Figures  3 and 4), suggesting that it is a crucial gene during lignification in jujube fruit stone. 

Previous studies have shown that bZIPs constitute a TF family involved in various biological and abiotic stresses (Zhang et  al.  2020). In grape, VIbZIP30 can directly activate the expression of the lignin synthesis gene VvPRXN1 and the drought stress gene VvNAC17 to increase drought resistance (Tu et al. 2020). PebZIP28667 can bind to the PePAL10 promoter to significantly inhibit its transcription, and PebZIP28667 ubiquitination weakens this inhibition (Yang et al. 2024). In pear, PbbZIP48 promotes the lignification of stone cells near the fruit core by activating the transcription of PbC3H1 and PbCCOMT2 (Gong et al. 2023). Thus, bZIPs can participate in lignin synthesis by activating various lignin synthesis genes 

in different species, but whether bZIPs are involved in the lignification of fruit stone has rarely been reported. In this study, the results of Y1H, EMSA and LUC assays indicated that ZjbZIP33 activated the expression of ZjPRX1 by binding specifically to the G-box element in the promoter (Figure 5d,f,h). The overexpression and silencing of ZjbZIP33 in jujube and Arabidopsis confirmed that ZjbZIP33 increased lignin synthesis by inducing the expression of ZjPRX1. Thus, a novel module, ZjbZIP33-ZjPRX1, was identified that positively regulates lignin formation in the jujube fruit stone. 

Studies have shown that lignin synthesis is also regulated by hormones and that exogenous auxin or gibberellin can directly or indirectly regulate the expression of genes related to lignin synthesis in plants (Yang et al. 2023; Xu et al. 2024). In pears, exogenous auxin application promotes the expression of PbrARF13 and further regulates the expression of the lignin synthesis gene PbrLAC5 through a cascade system of PbrARF13–PbrNSC– PbrMYB132, thereby reducing the lignin content in fruit stone cells (Xu et  al.  2023). In bamboo, the auxin response factors ARF3 and ARF6 directly bind to the promoter region of 4CL and CCoAOMT2 to activate their expression and thus affect lignin biosynthesis (Wang et al. 2024). In our study, we found that IAA and $\mathrm { G A } _ { 3 }$ treatment significantly promoted the expression of ZjbZIP33–ZjPRX1 to activate lignin synthesis (Figure 7). The above results showed that the function of exogenous auxin in lignin synthesis is distinct depending on the species. Compared with IAA, $\mathrm { G A } _ { 3 }$ may play a more noticeable role during lignin formation in jujube. Thus, $\mathrm { G A } _ { 3 }$ spraying could be applied during production to regulate fruit endocarp development. 

In conclusion, we revealed that ZjPRX1 is specifically expressed in the endocarp of jujube fruit and plays an important role in lignin synthesis. ZjbZIP33 was the upstream regulatory factor of ZjPRX1. After exogenous hormone treatment, IAA and $\mathrm { G A } _ { 3 }$ induced the expression of ZjbZIP33-ZjPRX1 in jujube; however, the effect of $\mathrm { G A } _ { 3 }$ was more significant. A model of how IAA $\mathrm { ^ G A } _ { 3 }$ modulates the expression of ZjbZIP33-ZjPRX1 and lignin formation in the jujube fruit endocarp is presented in Figure 8. These findings provide molecular evidence of the regulatory mechanism of jujube fruit stone development and provide a technical reference for the regulation of fruit stone production.

Structural changes in the fruit stone cells were observed via saffron solid green staining (Moreno-Sanz et al. 2020). The results were examined using a confocal microscope (TCS SP8, Leica, Germany). 

# 4.5 | Protein Structure Analysis and Phylogenetic Tree Construction

Conserved domains were searched using CD-Search (https:// www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/Structure/cdd/wrpsb.cgi). The PRX sequences of 23 species were downloaded from NCBI (https:// www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/) (Table  S2) and used to construct a phylogenetic tree. Phylogenetic analysis was performed on the basis of the conserved domains via the neighbour-joining method in MEGA11 with 1000 bootstrap iterations (Tamura et al. 2021). 

# 4.6 | Subcellular Localisation Analysis

The open reading frames (ORFs) of ZjPRX1 and ZjbZIP33 were amplified and cloned and then inserted into a pCAMBIA1300-35S-GFP vector in frame with the EGFP gene. The recombinant plasmids were subsequently introduced into Agrobacterium tumefaciens (strain GV3101), which was subsequently injected into tobacco leaves and onion epidermal cells. The pCAMBIA1300-35S-GFP vector was used as a control. The procedures were performed according to previous methods (Zhang et al. 2025). The infected tissues were evaluated $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ after infection. The tobacco plants were grown at $2 2 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ under a 16 h light/8 h dark cycle. The onions were cultured in the dark for $^ { 7 2 \mathrm { h } }$ at $2 6 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The GFP fluorescence was observed using a laser scanning confocal microscope (LSM900, Carl Zeiss AG, Germany) and fluorescence microscope (Axiom Imagery.M2, Carl Zeiss AG, Germany). 

# 4.7 | In Situ Hybridisation

The probe sequence was specific to the ZjPRX1 locus, as detailed in Table S3. Probe preparation was conducted in strict accordance with the guidelines provided by the DIG RNA Labelling Kit (Roche, Swiss Confederation). Segments of endocarp flesh of ‘Jinsi’ and ‘Wuhe’ at the S4 stage were fixed overnight in fixation solution. After decolorisation with tertbutanol, the samples were infiltrated with paraffin, embedded in wax, sliced, baked, dewaxed and dehydrated, followed by protease K digestion. A colour reaction was performed on the sections using a DIG nucleic acid detection kit (Roche, Swiss Confederation). 

# 4.8 | Scanning Electron Microscopy

Fresh tissue samples were cut into small pieces using a clean sterile scalpel and placed in a glass bottle containing fixative for $1 2 \mathrm { h }$ . Then, the fixed samples were put into different concentrations (concentrations of $3 0 \% - 5 0 \% - 7 0 \% - 8 0 \% - 9 5 \% - 1 0 0 \% - 1 0 0 \% )$ of ethanol solution for dehydration, and the samples were dried under vacuum for 30 min. The samples were put into a solution containing $1 0 0 \%$ tert-butanol and dried under vacuum for 30 min. Finally, the samples were put into a $1 0 \mathrm { m L }$ centrifuge tube for freeze-drying. The samples were sputter-coated with gold using an E-100 ion sputter (Mito City, Japan) and observed with a scanning electron microscope (Thermo Scientific Prisma E SEM, USA). 

# 4.9 | Transient Transformation of Jujube Fruit

The ZjPRX1 and ZjbZIP33 coding sequences were inserted into the pCAMBIA2301 vector and introduced into the GV3101 Agrobacterium strain. The bacteria were cultured in Luria-Bertani medium supplemented with kanamycin and rifampicin for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and then resuspended in infiltration buffer (MES, $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 } { \cdot } 6 \mathrm { H } _ { 2 } \mathrm { O }$ and acetosyringone, pH 5.6) to an $\mathrm { \Delta O D } _ { 6 0 0 }$ of 0.6–0.8 (Niu et al. 2023). This suspension was injected into jujube fruits at the young fruit stage, with an empty vector used as a control. The injected fruits were kept in the dark for $2 4 \mathrm { h }$ before 

sampling at 24, 72 and $9 6 \mathrm { h }$ for gene expression and lignin content analyses. The primer sequences are provided in Table S4. 

# 4.10 | Generation of Transgenic Jujube Fruit Calli

Jujube calli were generated from ‘Beiqi’ fruit pulp and subcultured every 2weeks in darkness for genetic transformation. The coding sequences (CDSs) of the genes were subsequently cloned and inserted into the pCAMBIA2301-GFP vector to achieve stable overexpression. The recombinant plasmids were subsequently introduced into the Agrobacterium GV3101 strain. After the wellcultivated bacterial cultures were centrifuged at $5 0 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ for 10min, the supernatants were carefully decanted. The pellet was then resuspended in resuspension buffer (MS, sugar, glucose and acetosyringone) to achieve an $\mathrm { { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } }$ value between 0.5 and 0.8, and the suspension was left to stand at room temperature for $^ { 2 - 3 \mathrm { h } }$ . Sterile forceps were used to select healthy calli, which were subsequently immersed in the resuspended culture for an infection period of 10–20min. Then, the residual bacterial culture on the calli was absorbed using sterile lint-free paper, and the calli were transferred to cocultivation medium (MS, maltose, agar, thidiazuron, NAA and acetosyringone, pH5.8) and incubated in the dark for 2days. Following the cocultivation period, the infected calli were rinsed in sterile water containing cephalothin. After thorough rinsing with sterile water, the calli were blotted dry with sterilised filter paper and lint-free paper to remove any residual moisture. They were then transferred to selection medium (MS, maltose, agar, thidiazuron, NAA, cephalothin, kanamycin, pH5.8) and incubated in the dark. 

# 4.11 | TRV-Based Virus-Induced Silencing of ZjPRX1 and ZjbZIP33

Approximately 300 bp of each target gene were successfully cloned and inserted into the pTRV2 vector. The pTRV2, pTRV2- ZjPRX1 and pTRV2-ZjbZIP33 plasmids were subsequently introduced into the Agrobacterium GV3101 strain. The cultured Agrobacterium cells were harvested and resuspended in a buffer solution containing $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ MES, 10mM $\mathrm { M g C l } _ { 2 }$ and $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ acetosyringone (AS) to achieve an $\mathrm { { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } }$ of 1.5. The cells were then incubated in the dark at room temperature for $^ { 2 - 3 \mathrm { h } }$ . Agrobacteria harbouring the recombinant plasmids were mixed at a 1:1 volume ratio and infiltrated into the cotyledons of jujube seedlings, followed by a 24-h incubation in the dark. The seedlings were then transferred to an incubator with a photoperiod of $1 6 \mathrm { h }$ of light and $^ { 8 \mathrm { h } }$ of darkness at $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . After 40 days of growth, the height of and relative lignin content in the transformed seedlings were assessed (Zhang et al. 2023b). The heights of the jujube seedlings above the cotyledons were measured and recorded, with 32 seedlings from each treatment group being measured. The pTRV1 + pTRV2 mixed vector served as a control in these experiments. The primers used are detailed in Table S4. 

# 4.12 | Arabidopsis Transformation

The transformation method was based on a previous study in our laboratory (Xue et al. 2024). A. tumefaciens strain GV3101 was transformed with the 35S::ZjPRX1 recombinant plasmid, 

facilitating the transformation of A. thaliana plants via the floral dip method. The progeny of the first generation from the 35S::ZjPRX1 transgenic plants were screened on Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium, which was enriched with hygromycin B at a concentration of $5 0 \mathrm { m g / L }$ . Transgenic plants were validated through RT-PCR analysis, and homozygous lines were identified and propagated for a minimum of three successive generations to ensure genetic stability. The primers used are detailed in Table S4. 

# 4.13 | Weighted Gene Coexpression Network Analysis

The RNA-seq data from ‘Jinsi’ and ‘Wuhe’ were subjected to weighted gene coexpression network analysis (WGCNA) using the R package to construct gene coexpression networks. This analysis was performed with default settings, employing a soft-threshold power of 15 and a correlation coefficient squared threshold of 0.8 to identify modules with similar gene expression patterns (Hu et al. 2023). Hierarchical clustering was used to classify genes into different modules (Chu et al. 2024; Zhang et al. 2021). Network visualisation, particularly for lignin biosynthesis-related modules, was achieved using Cytoscape v3.9.0, revealing the structural and transcription factor genes derived from the screened modules. 

# 4.14 | Transcriptional Activation and Yeast One-Hybrid Assays

These assays were performed as described in a previous study (Gao et al. 2021). In brief, full-length ZjbZIP33 was inserted into the pGADT7 vector to generate the pGADT7-ZjbZIP33 recombinant plasmid as the effector. A 600-bp promoter fragment of ZjPRX1 was inserted into the pABAi vector as a reporter. The effector and reporter vectors were cotransformed into the Y1H Gold yeast strain; then, cotransformed yeast were selected on SD medium that lacked leucine and uracil (SD/-Leu/-Ura). Aureobasidin A (AbA) was used to evaluate the interactions between the ZjbZIP33 and ZjPRX1 promoters. Three biological replicates were carried out for each combination. The primers used for vector construction are listed in Table S4. 

# 4.15 | Dual-Luciferase Assay

The sequences of the ZjPRX1 promoters were cloned and inserted into the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector; the vectors were subsequently expressed to determine LUC activity. The full-length CDS of ZjbZIP33 was cloned and inserted into a pGreenII 62-SK vector to construct an effector. All the vectors were subsequently transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101 (pSoup) competent cells. Luminescence images were obtained using a chemiluminescent imaging system (Vilber Fusion FX7 Spectra, France), and the luminescence intensity was measured with a multifunctional microplate reader (Tecan Infinite 200 PRO, Switzerland). Luciferase activity analysis was performed using a Luciferase Reporter Assay Kit $( \mathrm { A P E } \times \mathrm { B I O }$ , USA) per the manufacturer's instructions. LUC and REN activities were analysed in three independent experiments, with at least three biological 

replicates per assay. The primers used for vector construction are listed in Table S4. 

# 4.16 | Electrophoretic Mobility Shift Assay (EMSA)

The coding sequence of ZjbZIP33 was inserted into the pET-32a vector. The recombinant plasmid PET-ZjbZIP33 was introduced into E. coli BL21 to produce the recombinant 6His-ZjbZIP33 protein. The recombinant protein was purified as described previously (Gao et al. 2021). The DNA fragments and mutated sequences of the ZjPRX1 promoter were synthesised and labelled with biotin at the $5 ^ { \prime }$ DNA terminus to serve as biotin probes. Biotin-unlabelled fragments of the same sequences or mutated sequences were used as cold probes. The probes used for EMSAs were synthesised and biotinylated by Sangon Biotech (Shanghai, China). EMSAs were performed with a Chemiluminescent EMSA Kit (GS009; Beyotime) in accordance with the manufacturer's protocol. The primers and probes used are listed in Table S4. 

# 4.17 | Hormone Quantification

The endocarp of ‘Jinsi’ and ‘Wuhe’ at different developmental stages (S1, S2 and S3) was taken as materials. Samples $\mathrm { ( 5 0 m g ) }$ were ground to powder, followed by the addition of an appropriate amount of internal standard, and then, 1 mL of methanol/ water/formic acid (15:4:1, v/v/v) was used for extraction. The extraction mixture was concentrated and then redissolved in $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $8 0 \%$ methanol/water solution; then, the sample was passed through a $0 . 2 2 \mu \mathrm { m }$ filter membrane and placed in a sample bottle. The samples were analysed by LC–MS (Q Exactive Plus, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Germany). 

# 4.18 | Hormone Treatment

In these experiments, we utilised ‘Jinsi’ and ‘Wuhe’ jujube trees that exhibited consistent and robust growth in the field as experimental materials. These trees were subjected to treatments with water, IAA, $\mathrm { G A } _ { 3 }$ or CCC during the early fruit stage, with applications performed once every 10 days for a total of three applications. Additionally, healthy jujube seedlings were treated with various hormones (IAA, $\mathrm { G A } _ { 3 }$ or CCC), with water serving as the control. The seedlings were treated once every 7 days over a period of three applications. Each treatment consisted of three biological replicates. 

# 4.19 | RNA Isolation and Expression Analysis

Total RNA extraction, quantification and cDNA synthesis were conducted in accordance with established protocols (Meng et  al.  2024). The expression levels of related genes were assessed via both semiquantitative PCR and quantitative realtime PCR (qRT-PCR); the primer sequences are detailed in Table S4. The PCR setup and procedures adhered to the methodologies outlined in the literature (Bu et  al.  2016; Livak and Schmittgen 2001). Each experimental treatment was evaluated with three independent biological replicates. 

# 4.20 | Statistical Analysis

The data in this study are presented as means $\pm$ SDs of at least three independent experiments. Statistical analysis was conducted using SPSS statistics 22.0. Statistically significant differences are indicated either with $^ { * } p < 0 . 0 5 )$ or with $( ^ { \ast \ast } p < 0 . 0 1 )$ .
