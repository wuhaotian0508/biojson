# S T R U C T U R A L B I O LO G Y

# Structural assembly of maize CRY-GL2 photosignaling complex provides insights into its regulatory role in cuticular wax biosynthesis

Yaqi Liu1,2 †, Zhiwei Zhao2,3 †, Xue Zhang1,2 , Yahui Hao2 , Fan Feng2 , Yuan Chen1,2,4 , Ji Wang1,5 , Miaolian Ma1,2 , Jianxu Li6 , Fang $\mathsf { Y } \mathsf { u } ^ { 5 * }$ , Hongtao Liu2,3 *, Peng Zhang1,2 * 

Plant cryptochromes (CRYs) are blue-light photoreceptors regulating physiological processes via oligomerizationdependent interaction with effectors. However, the structural basis for photoactivated CRY-effector assembly remains elusive. Here, we report the crystal structure of an active maize CRY1c photolyase homology region in complex with GLOSSY2 (ZmGL2), a BAHD acyltransferase family protein that could form an enzyme complex with ECERIFERUM6 (ZmCER6) and direct very-long-chain fatty acid elongation in cuticular wax biosynthesis. Lightactivated CRY1c forms a homotetrameric scaffold. Each protomer binds one ZmGL2 molecule via conformational changes, forming a 4:4 hetero-octameric photosignaling complex. Structural alignment shows $78 \%$ overlap between the GL2-binding interfaces in the ZmCRY1c-ZmGL2 and ZmCER6-ZmGL2 complexes. Biochemically, CRY1c dose-dependently inhibits ZmCER6-ZmGL2 enzyme activity, unveiling a light-dependent regulatory switch modulating very-long-chain fatty acid elongation efficiency. Our work establishes the atomic model for light-activated CRY-effector assembly and uncovers spatial competition between photoreceptor and metabolic enzyme complexes as a photoregulatory paradigm in wax biosynthesis.

# INTRODUCTION

Cryptochromes (CRYs) are photoreceptors that mediate blue light perception in plant and nonplant species (1–3). In plants, CRYs play major roles in hypocotyl elongation and floral initiation, as well as regulating stomatal opening and circadian rhythms (4–7). CRY proteins contain two domains, the photolyase homologous region (PHR) domain that noncovalently binds the chromophore FAD (flavin adenine dinucleotide) and the variable C-terminal extension domain (CCE) (8, 9). In different plants, as well as Drosophila, zebrafish, and mammals, the similarity of PHR domain is high, and the differences are mainly attributed to the CCE domain $( l O , l l )$ . 

Previous structural data together with the functional analyses have suggested the light activation process of CRY, in which the “trp-triad” residues mediate the blue light–dependent photoreduction of FAD, which induces conformational changes and oligomerization through the PHR domain, and finally the activation of CRY (12–15). As an inhibitor of CRY, blue-light inhibitor of CRY could bind with CRY monomer and preclude the CRY oligomerization (16–18). The AtCRY2W374A mutant, although photochemically inactive in photoreduction, could mimic the photoactivated conformation to form oligomers independent of light and remain constitutively active (12, 19). The photoactivated or oligomerized CRY then bind with various downstream proteins to transmit light signal. To date, a large number of proteins have been identified to interact with plant CRYs in a light-dependent manner (20–22). However, the molecular mechanisms underlying how photoactivated CRYs interact with downstream proteins to transmit 

light signals in plants remain elusive because of the lack of atomic resolution structures between activated CRYs and downstream protein complexes. 

Recently, GLOSSY2 (GL2 afterward) was identified as a previously unknown CRY1-interacting protein specifically in Zea mays (Zm) through yeast two-hybrid screening, with their interaction being blue-light dependent (23). The maize genome encodes four CRY family members, which are phylogenetically classified as ZmCRY1s (ZmCRY1a, ZmCRY1b, and ZmCRY1c) and ZmCRY2. Notably, ZmCRY1c, which is enriched in the cytoplasm, specifically uses its N-terminal PHR domain, rather than its CCE domain, to interact with ZmGL2. Genetic evidence demonstrated that ZmCRY1c-ZmGL2 interaction modulates C32 aldehyde accumulation in maize leaves, implicating CRY’s role in very-long-chain fatty acid (VLCFA) biosynthesis. These findings further suggest a role for CRY in blue light–regulated epidermal wax biosynthesis, thereby potentially contributing to the enhanced ultraviolet B (UV-B) tolerance of Z. mays under high light intensity and elevated UV-B radiation. Maize GL2 locus was originally identified in a glossy leaf-surface mutant, where impaired gl2 function disrupted the synthesis of C32-chain cuticular lipids (24–26). Subsequent studies identified GL2 homologs in Arabidopsis, ECERIFERUM2 (cer2) (27–32). CER2 and its homolog GL2 belong to the BAHD (named for the first four enzymes described in this clade: benzylalcohol O-acetyltransferase; anthocyanin O-hydroxycinnamoyltransferase; N-hydroxycinnamoyl/benzoyltransferase; and deacetylvindoline 4-O-acetyltransferase) acyltransferase family members featuring a conserved HXXXD motif (33). However, unlike typical BAHD enzymes, CER2 and GL2 lack catalytic activity. GL2 forms an enzyme complex with the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) localized fatty acid elongase complex member ECERIFERUM6 [CER6 or $\beta$ -ketoacyl– coenzyme A (CoA) synthase KCS6] (CER6-GL2) and is responsible for the synthesis of C30-C34 VLCFAs (34–36), which are precursors of the plant cuticular wax.

# RESULTS

# Active ZmCRY1c forms a complex with GL2

Obtaining a stable protein complex of CRY with its downstream effectors is a prerequisite for structural analysis to elucidate how CRY transmits light signals through effector binding. To test whether maize ZmGL2 could form a stable complex with ZmCRY1c, the PHR domain of ZmCRY1c containing W368A $\mathrm { ( T r p } ^ { 3 6 8 }$ to Ala) mutation (ZmCRY1c-PHRW368A), which could enhance the oligomeric state mimicking the active conformation of ZmCRY1c (12, 19) was used; another FAD-binding failure mutant, ZmCRY1c-PHRD381A, was also generated on the basis of sequence alignment with AtCRY2D387A (fig. S1). The above proteins were recombinantly expressed, and the photoreduction characteristics were measured (Fig. 1A and fig. S2, A to C). The results show that ZmCRY1c-PHR wild type features typical photoreduction, while $\mathrm { Z m C R Y 1 c - P H R } ^ { \mathrm { W 3 6 8 A } }$ and ZmCRY1c-PHRD381A lost photoreduction under $5 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } } ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } ~ \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ −2 blue-light condition. When recombinantly expressed, ZmGL2 form a stable complex with $\mathrm { Z m C R Y 1 c - P H R } ^ { \mathrm { W 3 6 8 A } }$ in a 1:1 molar ratio (Fig. 1B and fig. S2D). The binding affinity of $\mathrm { Z m C R Y 1 c - P H R } ^ { \mathrm { W 3 6 8 A } }$ with ZmGL2 is about $0 . 6 \mu \mathrm { M }$ $( \mathrm { { \sim } 1 . 0 \mu M }$ for $\mathrm { \dot { Z } m C R Y 1 c } ^ { \mathrm { W 3 6 8 A } }$ full length), which is of several fold enhancement of wild-type ZmCRY1c-PHR [dissociation constant $( K _ { \mathrm { d } } \sim 2 . 8 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ ; ${ \sim } 2 . 7 \ \mu \mathrm { M }$ for ZmCRY1c full length] (Fig. 1C and fig. S3). Meanwhile, the FAD-binding failure mutant ZmCRY1c-PHRD381A, indicated by absence of photoreduction under $5 0 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m o l } }$ 

$\mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ s ^ { - 1 }$ blue-light condition, lost the binding ability to $\mathrm { Z m G L } 2$ (Fig. 1C). All these data confirm the light-dependent interaction between ZmGL2 and ZmCRY1c. 

# Architecture of ZmCRY1c-PHRW368A-ZmGL2 complex

The recombinant ZmCRY1c-PHRW368A-ZmGL2 protein complex was purified to homogeneity and concentrated to $1 0 \mathrm { m g / m l }$ for crystallization. The crystal structure of ZmCRY1c-PHRW368A-ZmGL2 complex was determined to 2.8-Å resolution by molecular replacement method (Fig. 2, A to F, and fig. S4, A and B). On the basis of the obtained structure information, we found that ZmCRY1c-PHRW368A forms a homologous tetramer (Fig. 2, A to C). As expected, the ZmCRY1c-PHRW368A tetrameric crystal structure is overall similar to the ZmCRY1c-PHRW368A cryo–electron microscopy (cryo-EM) structure [Protein Data Bank (PDB) 6LZ3] previously determined in active conformation (root mean square deviation of $1 8 5 3 \mathrm { ~ C o }$ atoms are 1.19 Å) (Fig. 2D). Two interaction interfaces INT1 (A/B or C/D) and INT2 (A/C or B/D) between ZmCRY1c-PHRW368A monomers defined before (12) were used here for consistency. INT1 is constituted by helices α6-α7, α12-α13, α15, and α18-α20 from monomers A/B or C/D, while INT2 is constituted by helices α2, α10, and α5-α6 connecting loop from monomers A/C or B/D (fig. S5, A and B). ZmGL2 adopts a typical BAHD conformation (fig. S4B) and binds symmetrically near the INT1 of ZmCRY1c-PHRW368A (Fig. 2, E and F), which forms a ZmCRY1c-PHRW368A-ZmGL2 hetero-octameric complex with 4:4 molar ratio.

# ZmCRY1c adjusts ZmCER6-ZmGL2 enzyme activity through competing with ZmCER6

ZmGL2 has been established as a central regulator of wax biosynthesis by mediating VLCFA elongation via interaction with ketoacyl-CoA synthase CER6 (24–26). Our parallel cryo-EM study (40) reveals the ZmCER6-ZmGL2 heterotetramer architecture, demonstrating how ZmGL2 binds ZmCER6 to expand the substrate-binding tunnel of ZmCER6 to accommodate ${ > } \mathrm { C } 2 8$ acyl chains and enable VLCFA elongation. Structural alignment revealed that ZmCRY1c and ZmCER6 share $7 8 \%$ interface overlap on ZmGL2’s N-terminal domain (Fig. 5, A to C), suggesting potential competitive binding. Since ZmCER6- ZmGL2 complex integrity is essential for enzymatic activity (40), we proposed that light-activated ZmCRY1c modulates VLCFA elongation through competitive binding with ZmCER6. 

Enzymatic assays showed that both $\mathrm { Z m C R Y 1 c } ^ { \mathrm { W 3 6 8 A } }$ and ZmCRY1c inhibited $\mathrm { Z m C E R 6 - Z m G L 2 }$ activity in a dose-dependent manner, with more than $9 0 \%$ suppression achieved at 20:1 molar ratio. Compared to $\mathrm { Z m C R Y 1 c } ^ { \mathrm { W 3 6 \dot { 8 } \dot { A } } }$ , ZmCRY1c exhibited a weaker inhibitory effect on the ZmCER6-ZmGL2 activity (Fig. 5D). This competitive inhibition mechanism suggests that light-activated ZmCRY1c acts as a molecular switch displacing ZmCER6 from ZmGL2, thereby modulating VLCFA elongation reaction. While in vivo plant validation studies are awaited, these findings suggest that ZmCRY1c may function as a light-dependent switch in regulating cuticular wax biosynthesis through adjusting the VLCFA elongation efficiency in maize. 

# DISCUSSION

While more than 30 CRY-interacting proteins have been identified through genetic and biochemical studies (20–22), the structural basis for photoactivated CRY-effector complex assembly has remained poorly characterized. Our work bridges this critical gap by establishing the atomic-resolution structure of a plant CRY bound to its downstream effector ZmGL2, a BAHD protein–forming complex with the ketoacyl-CoA synthase ZmCER6 to exert condensation activity during VLCFA elongation and cuticular wax biosynthesis. Moreover, we establish that light-activated ZmCRY1c directly competes with the ZmCER6 for ZmGL2 binding, creating a dose-dependent regulatory circuit that gates VLCFA elongation efficiency, a biochemical mechanism reconciling CRY’s dual roles in photoperception and metabolic regulation. 

Since the elucidation of the first Arabidopsis CRY1 crystal structure, structural studies on CRYs and their associated complexes have markedly expanded in both mammals and plants, yet most solved structures to date have been captured as monomers despite their proposed oligomerization during activation of plant CRYs (41–43). More recently, the structure of the dynamic CRY2 complex with its downstream effector CIB1 was determined, although its resolution was insufficient to fully reveal the molecular mechanism of photosignal transduction (37). This study addresses this key knowledge gap by determining the atomic-resolution structure of an active plant CRY in complex with its downstream effector ZmGL2 through N-terminal PHR domain, uncovering the molecular basis of photosignaling complex assembly. 

Compared the fully activated ZmCRY1c-PHRW368A structure present in this study to the inactive structures of Arabidopsis CRY1 and CRY2, substantial conformational changes are observed. These changes primarily manifest in the α6-α7 region of the A1 subdomain (fig. S10, A and B), the α18-α20 region of the A3 subdomain (fig. S10C), and the L11 loop and α21 of the A2 subdomain (fig. S10D). In addition, subtle conformational changes occur in FAD and the trptriad residue $\mathrm { T r p } ^ { 3 9 1 }$ (fig. S10E). While most of these conformational 

changes align well with our previous report (12), distinct conformational alterations are observed in the α17 and α16-α17 loop (Fig. 3C and fig. S10F), suggesting that further conformational rearrangement in CRYs could be induced by downstream effector binding. In addition, the ZmCRY1c-ZmGL2 structure delineates a spatial linkage between the ZmGL2-binding interface and the conserved tryptophan triad (W315/W368/W391), which forms an electron-transfer chain extending from the FAD pocket to INT1 (fig. S11A). Integrating these structural insights, we have refined the mechanistic model for plant CRYs photosignaling. Stepwise photoactivation initiates with blue light photoreception, triggering flavin semiquinone formation (FADH•). This induces conformational changes propagated through the Trp triad, leading to dissociation of the CCE domain from the PHR domain. Subsequent structural rearrangement of INT1 drives CRY oligomerization. This oligomerization enables the positioning of the α16-α17 structural elements to establish the ZmGL2- binding interface (fig. S11B). Last, the binding of ZmGL2 stabilizes 

this active conformation through residue-level adjustments, completing the photocycle. 

Structural and biochemical analyses reveal that ZmCRY1c oligomerization generates a ZmGL2-binding interface involving the α17 and α16-α17 loop proximal to the INT1 region (Fig. 3, A to D), corroborating INT1’s predicted role as a functional interaction hub conserved among plants (12). Intriguingly, the ZmGL2-binding region on ZmCRY1c spatially align with the C-terminal lid of Drosophila CRY (dCRY) (44–46), a region mediating monomeric dCRY-TIM interactions in circadian regulation (fig. S12), and share topological parallels with photolyase DNA binding modules (47), suggesting an evolutionary conservation of this structural module across different photoactivated CRYs. The mechanistic convergence extends to Arabidopsis CRY2, where blue light–induced oligomerization exposes the INT2 surface for CIB1 effector recruitment (37). Collectively, these observations support a paradigm wherein activated CRYs could dynamically generate distinct interaction surfaces, enabling contextspecific recruitment of diverse effectors—a mechanistic rationale for CRY’s pleiotropic signaling roles. 

Although CRY and CER2/GL2 family members are present in many plants, including Arabidopsis and maize, we observed no interaction between Arabidopsis CRY2 and CER2 (fig. S9). This specificity correlates with the lack of conservation in key interaction residues: Residues critical for ZmGL2 binding (Phe71, $\dot { \mathrm { S e r } } ^ { 2 7 }$ , Val25, and $\mathrm { V a l } ^ { 2 9 }$ ) were not conserved among GL2 homologs across species (fig. S8), and similarly, key ZmCRY1 residues (Phe412 and Tyr415) showed poor conservation among CRYs (fig. S7). Therefore, divergence at these key residues likely accounts for the specificity of this signaling module in maize, which has been validated and used in a yeast optical design (Fig. 4 and fig. S6). 

The CRY1 family in maize is composed of three members: ZmCRY1a, ZmCRY1b, and ZmCRY1c (23). Subcellular localization analysis revealed that ZmCRY1a and ZmCRY1b are primarily nuclear, whereas ZmCRY1c exhibits dual localization in both the nucleus and cytoplasm. For ZmGL2, it localized in the nucleus and cytoplasm. Notably, ZmCRY1c and ZmGL2 colocalize in the cytoplasm of Nicotiana benthamiana leaves and maize leaf protoplasts, suggesting that the ZmCRY1c-ZmGL2 signaling module function in the cytoplasmic compartment. According to literature, CER6-CER2 (GL2) complex was thought to play the crucial role in extension of VLCFAs in ER (24–26, 29, 48, 49). Given that ZmCRY1c binds competitively to ZmGL2 in place of ZmCER6, and that the ZmCER6-ZmGL2 enzyme complex is essential for VLCFA elongation activity during cuticular wax biosynthesis, we deduce that the ZmCRY1c-ZmGL2 photosignaling module functions as a photoregulatory node, serving to modulate maize wax deposition. 

In a word, the present study provides the molecular-level elucidation of how photoactivated CRY interacts with its downstream effector GL2 to transduce the light signal. While in planta validation remains necessary, these findings propose a paradigm wherein light quality directly modulates cuticular wax biosynthesis through CRY-mediated competition within a metabolic complex. This “optoregulatory” mechanism may be critical for plant environmental adaptation. 

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Gene cloning and protein purification

The genes encoding ZmCRY1c, ZmGL2 were cloned from maize B73 cDNA by polymerase chain reaction (PCR). For protein expression in 

Escherichia coli, genes were subcloned into pET-duet vector, with a 6×His tag at the N terminus of ZmGL2. The point mutation was generated by one-step PCR. The AtCRY2-ZmCRY1c chimera was generated by overlap PCR. E. coli BL21 (DE3) strain was used for protein expression. The bacteria were cultured at ${ } ^ { 3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } , }$ , 200 rpm and induced by $0 . 2 5 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ isopropyl $\beta$ -d-thiogalactopyranoside for 12 hours. The bacteria were collected and resuspended in buffer A [20 mM tris-HCl $\mathrm { ( p H 8 . 0 ) }$ , 100 mM NaCl, and $1 \mathrm { m M }$ TCEP] supplemented with $1 \mathrm { m M }$ phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride, and lysed by a high-pressure cell disruptor at 700 bar and centrifuged at ${ 2 0 , 0 0 0 g }$ for 1 hour. The supernatant was incubated with Ni–nitrilotriacetic acid (NTA) beads (Qiagen) for 1 hour at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . After that, Ni-NTA beads with the bound protein was washed by buffer A supplemented with $2 5 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ imidazole. The protein was eluted with buffer A supplemented with $2 5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ imidazole and concentrated for further purification on a Superdex-200 10/300 column. 

# Crystallization, data collection, and structure determination

The purified ZmCRY1c-PHRW368A-ZmGL2 complex was concentrated to $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m g / m l }$ for crystallization. Initial protein crystals were obtained at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in condition of $2 0 \%$ (w/v) polyethylene glycol 3350, $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ bis-tris propane/hydrochloric acid (pH 6.5), and $2 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ sodium fluoride. The protein to reservoir ratios is 1:1 in three-well sitting-drop crystallization plates. For crystal optimization, $1 . 5 \mu \mathrm { l }$ of protein mix with $1 . 5 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ of reservoir was set up in 24-well hanging drops plates. The crystals were cryoprotected by reservoir solutions supplemented with $3 0 \%$ glycerol and flash-cooled in liquid nitrogen. Data were collected at BL19U1 beamline of the Shanghai Synchrotron Radiation Facility under $1 0 0 \mathrm { K }$ of liquid nitrogen stream. The data were processed with HKL3000 package (50), and the initial phase was determined by molecular replacement with Phenix, using the cryo-EM structure of ZmCRY1cW368A $\mathrm { Z m C R Y 1 } \dot { \mathrm { c } } ^ { \mathrm { W 3 6 8 A } }$ (PDB ID: 6LZ3) and AlphaFold (51) predicted structure of ZmGL2 (AlphaFold ID: AF-A0A804MAL4-F1) as templates. The ZmCRY1c-PHRW368A-ZmGL2 complex structure model was manually built with Coot (52). The complex structure was refined by manual adjustment in coot and refinement with Phenix (53). Data collection and refinement statistics are reported in table S1. 

# Isothermal titration calorimetry

ITC was performed with Microcal ITC 200 (Malvern) at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The cell of the machine was filled with $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { l }$ of ZmCRY1c-PHR and their mutants, respectively. The syringe was filled with $4 0 \mu \mathrm { l }$ of ZmGL2 and their mutants, respectively. All samples were centrifuged at $1 4 { , } 0 0 0 g$ for $1 0 \mathrm { m i n }$ before titration. The titration was performed by injections of $2 \mu \mathrm { l }$ of aliquots followed by 120 s of equilibration; the reaction was conducted 20 times injections. The reaction heat change was measured after individually injection. The data were treated and analyzed by Origin. 

# UV-Vis absorption spectroscopy

UV-Vis absorption spectra of the recombinantly expressed protein ZmCRY1c-PHR, ZmCRY1c-PHRW368A, and ZmCRY1c-PHRD381A were measured with UV-Vis spectrophotometer (UV-2600, Shimadzu, Japan). Six hundred microliters of protein samples $\mathrm { ( 6 m g / m l ) }$ ) in buffer containing 20 mM tris-HCl (pH 8.0), 100 mM NaCl, and 5 mM dithiothreitol was kept at quartz cuvettes with an optical path length of $1 \mathrm { c m }$ . Samples were illuminated using light-emitting diode lamps with wavelengths of 450 to $4 7 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ and power of 8.3 W to irradiate 

protein samples. The vertical distance between the light source and the sample is ${ \sim } 2 4 ~ \mathrm { c m }$ . The absorption spectra (300 to $7 0 0 ~ \mathrm { n m }$ ) of samples were recorded at indicated times (0, 1, 3, and 5 min) after blue-light illumination $( 5 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 } )$ under aerobic conditions at $2 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The absorption curves were plotted using GraphPad. 

# Yeast two-hybrid assay

The cDNA fragments of ZmGL2 and mutants were cloned into the pGADT7 vector, and the fragments of ZmCRY1c-PHR and mutants were cloned into the pBridge vector. Recombinant vectors were cotransformed into yeast Y190 (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) strain and plated on the Trp-Leu dropout solid medium. The plates were incubated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 3 to 4 days. Yeast colonies were then selected and suspended on Trp-Leu dropout liquid medium supplement with $0 . 0 2 \%$ adenine and $2 \%$ glucose. Selected colonies were grown in dark conditions overnight before transfer to yeast extract, peptone, and dextrose (YPD) media. Cells in YPD media were divided into dark and blue light groups and then cultured in $2 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 200 rpm for around 2 hours until optical density at $6 0 0 \ \mathrm { n m }$ absorption reached 0.5. $\beta$ -Galactosidase activity was measured using chlorophenol red-$\beta$ -d-galactopyranoside as substrate. Three biological replicates were used for each combination. 

# In vitro enzyme activity assays

The in vitro enzymatic assays for inhibitory effect of ZmCER6- $\mathrm { Z m G L } 2$ complex by $\mathrm { Z m C R Y i c } ^ { \mathrm { W 3 6 8 A } } .$ -PHR protein were performed in a $5 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ of total volume. The reaction system containing $4 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ 30:0-CoA, $4 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ malonyl-CoA substrates, $1 \mu \mathrm { M }$ purified ZmCER6- ZmGL2 protein, $\mathrm { Z m C R Y 1 c } ^ { \mathrm { W 3 6 8 A } }$ -PHR protein (2, 4, 8, and $2 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ protein was added, respectively), and $1 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ tris-HCl buffer (pH 7.5). Enzyme reaction and analysis methods follow a same protocol as described in a companion paper (40). 

# In vitro pull-down assays

To analyze the specific interaction between ZmCRY1c and ZmGL2, ZmCRY1c-PHRW368A and ZmGL2 (N-terminal fused with $6 { \times } \mathrm { H i s } )$ ) proteins (or mutations) were coexpressed in E. coli BL21 (DE3) strain. After cell disruption and centrifugation, the supernatants were loaded onto Ni-NTA beads (Qiagen). The nonspecific bound protein was washed off using buffer A supplemented with $2 5 \mathrm { m M }$ imidazole. Target protein was eluted from the beads by buffer A supplemented with $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ imidazole. After that, protein samples subjected to SDS–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis analysis and stained with Coomassie blue. The amounts of input were aligned with anti-GL2 (ORIZYMES, Shanghai) or anti-flag (Smart-Lifesciences) antibodies.
