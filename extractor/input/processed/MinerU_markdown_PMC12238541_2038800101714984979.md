# Abstract

Phosphatidylcholine (PC) is a crucial membrane phospholipid involved in both cellular processes and stress responses. CTP: phosphocholine cytidylyltransferase 1 (CCT1) is considered to catalyze a key regulatory step in primary PC de novo biosynthesis, but its functions and regulation are yet to be well elucidated. This study explored the physiological functions of CCT1 in Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana) (AthCCT1) in PC biosynthesis under normal growth conditions and in root development under osmotic stress, as well as its regulation by phosphorylation. Arabidopsis cct1 knockdown cct2 knockout lines exhibited significantly decreased PC intensities under normal growth conditions and impaired root growth under osmotic stress, which was rescued by AthCCT1 overexpression. Moreover, based on our previous findings that AthCCT1 is phosphorylated at Serine 187 (S187), we further investigated how this phosphorylation affects its biochemical and biological functions. The S187D phosphomimetic protein mutant of AthCCT1 exhibited reduced lipid-induced conformational changes and decreased enzymatic activity compared to the native protein. Molecular dynamics simulations of the S187D protein mutant revealed that the auto-inhibitory region, a conserved regulatory domain across CCT enzymes, remained closer to the αE helix, maintaining a constrained interaction between them. Consistent with the results of the in vitro analyses, overexpression of AthCCT1-S187D did not rescue stress-induced short-root phenotypes in cct1 knockdown cct2 knockout Arabidopsis lines. Taken together, the results revealed that AthCCT1 regulates PC biosynthesis under normal conditions and root development under osmotic stress, with its phosphorylation state at S187 playing an important role in modulating its enzymatic activity and physiological functions. 

# Introduction

In eukaryotic cells, most biological membranes are composed of a lipid bilayer primarily consisting of phospholipids (PLs) with embedded proteins, as exemplified by the plasma membrane, which serves as a barrier separating the cell from its external environment (Mamode Cassim et al. 2019). In plants, phosphatidylcholine (PC) is typically the predominant PL class in extraplastidial membranes, accounting for $2 5 \%$ to $6 0 \%$ of total glycerolipids within nonplastidial membranes (Moreau et al. 1998). Beyond its structural role, PC is also essential in several cellular processes including lipid biosynthesis, where PC serves as a hub for acyl-editing and an acyl donor for acyl-CoA-independent biosynthesis of triacylglycerol (TAG) (Bates et al. 2013; Chen et al. 2015; Wang et al. 2024). PC also plays an important role in plant growth and development. For instance, insufficient PC levels could result in dwarfism and abnormal plant development (Chen et al. 2019; Liu et al. 2019). Similarly, a study found that transgenic Arabidopsis 

(Arabidopsis thaliana) lines with enhanced PC levels in the shoot meristem showed early flowering, whereas those with diminished PC levels exhibited delayed flowering (Nakamura et al. 2014). 

Given the broad importance of PC, it is crucial to thoroughly understand its biosynthesis. The primary route for the de novo PC biosynthesis was initially described in rat liver and yeast, and is commonly known as the CDP-choline pathway or the Kennedy pathway (Kennedy and Weiss 1956). This pathway involves three sequential steps catalyzed by choline kinase, CTP: phosphocholine cytidylyltransferase (CCT), and choline phosphotransferase, respectively (Kennedy and Weiss 1956; Gibellini and Smith 2010). In Arabidopsis, the nomenclature for these enzymes exhibits certain differences (Nakamura 2021). First, the phosphorylation of choline is mediated by choline/ethanolamine kinase (CEK), which reacts with ATP to form phosphocholine and ADP (Tasseva et al. 2004; Lin et al. 2015). Subsequently, CCT facilitates the transfer of cytidyl group to phosphocholine in a 

CTP-dependent manner to produce CDP-choline and diphosphate (Choi et al. 1997). The final step is carried out by aminoalcohol aminophosphotransferase, which transfers a phosphocholine group from CDP-choline to a DAG backbone to synthesize PC (Goode and Dewey 1999). In addition to the de novo biosynthesis, PC can also be synthesized by three methylation steps of phosphatidylethanolamine (PE) by PL N-methyltransferases (Keogh et al. 2009; Tan and Nakamura 2022). 

Apart from the variations in nomenclature for these enzymes, there are also differences in the sources of choline and the synthesis of phosphocholine. In animals, choline can be sourced from dietary intake and directly influences PC content via choline kinases (Nakamura 2021). By contrast, plants lack a substantial exogenous choline supply and the role of CEK in PC production appears to be minor (Lin et al. 2019). For instance, Arabidopsis CEK1, a primary choline kinase localized at the ER, phosphorylates free choline to phosphocholine and plays a role in ER stress tolerance, whereas its contribution to PC biosynthesis is limited (Lin et al. 2019). Instead, plant phosphocholine is primarily derived from serine, which is decarboxylated to ethanolamine by serine decarboxylase, followed by phosphorylation to phosphoethanolamine by CEK4 (Lin et al. 2015; Nakamura 2021). This phosphoethanolamine is then converted to phosphocholine via three methylation steps catalyzed by phospho-base N-methyltransferases (PMT1, PMT2, PMT3) (Chen et al. 2019; Liu et al. 2019). Therefore, PMTs constitute the primary pathway providing phosphocholine for CCT-mediated PC synthesis, distinguishing plant metabolism from animal systems. 

Similar to that in mammals (Vance et al. 1980), CCT has been reported as a key rate-limiting enzyme in the de novo synthesis of PC in plants, including rye (Secale cereale) and pea (Pisum sativum L.) (Price-Jones and Harwood 1983; Kinney et al. 1987). Research on CCT across a wide range of species has provided valuable insights into its function and regulation. In humans, disruption of CCTα, the homolog of plant CCT1, can cause abnormal bone development and dwarfism (Hoover-Fong et al. 2014). The physiological functions and regulatory mechanisms of CCTs have also been reported in plants (Nishida et al. 1996; Choi et al. 1997; Craddock et al. 2015). For instance, A. thaliana CCT1 (hereafter AthCCT1) is activated by phosphatidic acid (PA), and this activation plays an essential role in ensuring PC levels maintain membrane homeostasis in roots (Craddock et al. 2015). Moreover, AthCCT2 has an important role in basal innate immunity, as evidenced by the increased susceptibility of a cct2 T-DNA insertion mutant to the pathogen Hyaloperonospora arabidopsidis (Meteignier et al. 2017). In addition, a recent study has revealed that CCT2 positively influences root gravitropism in both Arabidopsis and maize (Yoshihara et al. 2022). Nevertheless, the in vivo functional roles of AthCCT1, especially in PC biosynthesis and plant development, have yet to be fully elucidated, as AthCCT1 and AthCCT2 may have overlapping functions and the lipid profiles of the Arabidopsis cct1 cct2 mutant remain unclear (Nakamura 2021). 

Similar to the investigations of their physiological functions, indepth biochemical studies of plant CCTs, particularly regarding their functional domains and regulation, are still in the early stages (Caldo et al. 2019). We recently reported that AthCCT1 consists of an N-terminus, catalytic domain (domain C), membranebinding domain (domain M), and C-terminus (Caldo et al. 2019), showing structural similarity to the functional domains of rat CCTα (Cornell 2020), yet with specific differences. AthCCT1 has two phosphorylation sites for the serine/threonine kinase sucrose nonfermenting 1-related protein kinase 1, SnRK1, located within 

the catalytic domain (Caldo et al. 2019). The SnRK1 consensus sequence consists of a 10-residue motif characterized by hydrophobic residues at positions −5 and $+ 4$ , and a basic residue, either arginine or lysine, at position $^ { - 3 }$ or −4 relative to the phosphorylated serine or threonine (Halford et al. 2003). The phosphorylation of AthCCT1 at serine 187 aligns with this sequence (Caldo et al. 2019). Serine-187 (S187) is the major phosphorylation site, as its phosphorylation led to a $6 7 \%$ enzyme activity reduction and decreased substrate affinity for CTP (Caldo et al. 2019). Moreover, rat CCTα has a joining segment (J segment) between domains C and M, which is an allosteric linker and functions to stabilize the amphipathic αE helix, playing a crucial role in regulating the reversible membrane binding of rat CCTα in domain M (Knowles et al. 2019; Taneva et al. 2019). Based on the sequence alignment analysis, S187 resided within the J segment, suggesting its potentially conserved regulatory role in AthCCT1, though the mechanism remains to be investigated. 

The aim of this study, therefore, was to investigate the physiological functions of AthCCT1 and its regulation by phosphorylation, with a focus on its S187 site. We initially developed a cct1 knockdown (kd) line in a cct2 knockout (ko) mutant background (hereafter referred to as cct1 kd cct2 ko) to examine the impacts of AthCCTs in PL homeostasis and plant development. In terms of PL homeostasis, lipidomic analysis revealed that the MS signal intensities of PC (hereafter referred to as PC intensity; similarly, the MS signal intensity of other lipid classes is presented in the format of [lipid class name] intensity or intensity of [lipid class name]) in cct1 kd cct2 ko, relative to the wild type (WT), were significantly lower in developing siliques, which was complemented by the seed-specific overexpression of AthCCT1. Moreover, cct1 kd cct2 ko mutants showed a marked reduction in root length compared to WT under mannitol treatment, a phenotype that was able to be recovered by constitutive AthCCT1 overexpression. Furthermore, phosphomimetic AthCCT1 variants were used to explore the effects of phosphorylation at the S187 site, with a focus on how it influenced the enzyme dynamics within the secondary and tertiary structures and biological functions. The results indicated that the phosphomimetic variants exhibit enzyme activities and substrate affinity comparable to those of SnRK1-phosphorylated AthCCT1. The phosphomimetic mutation at S187 to aspartic acid (S187D) resulted in diminished lipid-induced conformational changes, particularly in the alpha-helical content. Moreover, the constitutive expression of AthCCT1-S187D gene was unable to recover the reduced root length under osmotic stress, indicating the phosphomimetic mutation potentially impacted its physiological functions. Overall, our results revealed the function of AthCCT1 in contributing to PC homeostasis under normal conditions and root development under osmotic stress, as well as its modulation by phosphorylation at S187.

# The cct1 kd cct2 ko mutant showed a significant reduction in PC intensity

Subsequently, we carried out the lipidomic analysis of developing siliques at the same stage from WT, cct2 ko, ProPha:hpCCT1 cct2, and ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 lines for in-depth analysis of their lipid profiles. As shown in Fig. 1A, while TAG is the major lipid class in Arabidopsis developing siliques, the accumulated intensity of neutral lipid was not substantially affected by the downregulation of AthCCT1 in the cct2 ko background. Similarly, the total intensity of galactolipids (GL) showed no significant change (Fig. 1A). In contrast, significant decreases in the relative abundance of total PLs were observed in both ProPha:hpCCT1 cct2 and ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 lines (Fig. 1A). Moreover, cct2 ko did not lead to a reduction in the intensity of PC, the major PL class, compared to WT, whereas PC intensities in both cct1 kd cct2 ko lines were notably decreased relative to the wild-type or cct2 ko lines (Fig. 1B). In addition, although all samples contain a variety of other PL species, including PE, phosphatidylglycerol (PG), phosphatidylinositol (PI), Lysophosphatidylcholines (LPC), and lysophosphatidylethanolamine (LPE), the MS signal intensities of lysophospholipids (LPLs including LPC and LPE), PG, or PI showed no notable changes compared to WT for each respective lipid class (Fig. 1C). The relative abundance of high-intensity PC species with detailed chain information was shown in a color-coded heatmap (Fig. 1D). In all samples, the most abundant PC species are PC36:5, PC36:4, and PC34:2. A clear demarcation is visible in the heatmap between WT/cct2 groups and the group of two cct1 kd cct2 ko lines, demonstrating that knocking down cct1 on top of cct2 ko led to an obvious reduction in signal intensity across major PC species (Fig. 1D). On the contrary, the heatmap of other PLs did not show a clear pattern, though the intensity levels of several other PL species, such as PE 18:2_18:3, PE 18:3_18:3, decreased in the group of the two cct1 kd cct2 ko mutant lines (Fig. 1E). 

# The overexpression of AthCCT1 in cct1 kd cct2 ko mutant recovered PC intensity

To further investigate the role of AthCCT1 in lipid biosynthesis in developing siliques, we generated transgenic lines overexpressing AthCCT1 driven by a seed-specific glycinin promoter (ProGly:CCT1) in the ProPha:hpCCT1 cct2 background (Supplementary Fig. S2). The resulting lines, designated as ProGly:CCT1 ProPha:hpCCT1 cct2 (seed-specific CCT1 overexpression in cct1 kd cct2 ko), were analyzed for their lipid profiles to see if the observed lipidomic phenotype in Fig. 1 can be rescued by AthCCT1 overexpression. To avoid batch-to-batch variability, we grew the newly generated lines with control together under identical conditions for direct comparison. As shown in Fig. 2A, the collected total TAG intensity was still not affected by the overexpression of AthCCT1. However, compared to the reduction in total PL intensity observed in the cct1 kd cct2 ko line (Fig. 1A), the overexpression of AthCCT1 restored the total PL intensity (Fig. 2A). Accordingly, the intensity of total PC in developing siliques harvested from the AthCCT1 overexpression lines was also restored to levels comparable to those of the WT 

(Fig. 2B). No obvious changes were observed in the MS signal intensities of LPL or PI (Fig. 2C). Heatmaps were developed to display the relative abundance of high-intensity PL species in the AthCCT1 overexpression lines (Fig. 2, D and E). In contrast to the clear separation between high and low intensity observed in WT and mutant lines (Fig. 1D), upon the reintroduction of AthCCT1 in the mutant lines, this distinct demarcation was not observed (Fig. 2D). 

# AthCCTs affected the seedling root length in response to osmotic stress

Similar to the exact role of AthCCT1 in lipid metabolism, its involvement in plant development and stress response remains largely unexplored. To investigate the role of AthCCTs on vegetative growth, we cultivated ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 lines on 1/2 Murashige and Skoog (MS) medium and observed leaf and root growth. The ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 (constitutive cct1 kd in cct2 ko) mutant displayed no obvious developmental abnormalities when grown under continuous light and long-day conditions on 1/2 MS medium (Fig. 3A). The mutant exhibited a slight but not statistically significant decrease in root length compared to the WT (Fig. 3, A and B). Considering the established role of PC in maintaining membrane functionality under abiotic stress (Tasseva et al. 2004), we subsequently investigated the response of cct1 kd cct2 ko lines to osmotic stress, and in those experiments, the seedlings of ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 mutants were cultured under 200 mM mannitol for 10 days. As shown in Fig. 3C, seedlings of ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 lines exhibited shorter primary roots compared to WT plants when exposed to mannitol. The seedling primary root length of ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 mutants was significantly shorter than that of the WT (Fig. 3D), indicating that the downregulation of AthCCTs affected root growth under mannitol-induced osmotic stress conditions. 

# The complementation of AthCCT1 in root development under osmotic stress

To assess whether the observed short-root phenotype under osmotic stress in cct1 kd cct2 ko was due to the reduced CCT expression in our mutant lines, we generated transgenic lines overexpressing native AthCCT1 driven by a constitutive promoter (Pro35s:CCT1) in the ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 background. This also allowed us to determine if AthCCT1 complementation could rescue the stress-sensitive phenotype in the mutant lines. We examined the root development of Pro35s:CCT1 ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 (constitutive CCT1 overexpression in cct1 kd cct2 ko) lines under normal and osmotic conditions. Under normal conditions, similar root phenotypes were observed between the WT and transgenic lines (Fig. 4, A and B). With the application of mannitol, the shorter root phenotype of Pro35s:hpCCT1 cct2 was complemented in AthCCT1 overexpressing lines (Fig. 4, C and D), suggesting that overexpression of AthCCT1 could restore the abnormal root growth under osmotic stress.

# Discussion

AthCCT1’s roles in plant lipid biosynthesis, plant development, and posttranslational regulation are yet to be fully elucidated. To address these gaps, we generated cct1 kd cct2 ko mutants, as single mutants showed no obvious phenotypes in lipid profiles (Inatsugi et al. 2009). The cct1 kd cct2 ko mutants exhibited a 

marked reduction in PC intensity relative to the WT (Fig. 1), restored by AthCCT1 expression (Fig. 2), suggesting its important role in PC biosynthesis via the CDP-choline pathway. Previous studies have reported that PC deficiency caused by knocking out certain genes in PC biosynthesis impairs root development in Arabidopsis (Cruz-Ramírez et al. 2004; Liu et al. 2019). Specifically, pmt1-1 pmt2-1 mutants, with substantially decreased PC content due to disruptions in the PMT pathway of PC synthesis, exhibited only $2 5 \%$ of WT root length and reduced root cell viability (Liu et al. 2019). More severe developmental abnormalities were observed in pmt1 pmt2 pmt3 triple mutants, which completely lacked de novo PC biosynthesis and showed lethality at the postembryonic seedling stage (Liu et al. 2019). All these results highlighted the key role of PC in plant growth regulation.

During cold acclimation in Arabidopsis rosettes, the overall CCT activity increased and resulted in elevated PC levels (Inatsugi et al. 2002). In peaches, exogenous PC could enhance drought and salt resilience (Sun et al. 2020, 2022). Specifically, PC treatment improved drought tolerance in seedlings by enhancing physiological parameters such as proline accumulation and photosynthesis and reducing lipid peroxidation and membrane damage (Sun et al. 2020). Additionally, PC supplementation preserved root integrity and viability, thereby improving osmotic adjustment capacity under drought stress. On the other hand, PC treatment had an important effect on root physiology, promoting increased root growth and preserving root cell membrane integrity of peach seedlings under salinity stress (Sun et al. 2022). Cold, drought, and salt stress share a common factor, osmotic 

stress, which is characterized by a reduction in cellular water potential (Xiong and Zhu 2002; Askari-Khorasgani et al. 2021). Therefore, PC might also play an important role in osmotic stress response. 

Beyond osmotic stress, enzymes in the PC biosynthesis pathway are involved in responses to several other stress conditions. For instance, CEK1 is needed for tolerance to endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress (Lin et al. 2019), while silencing of PMT increases sensitivity to salt and temperature stresses. These findings highlight the contributions of CEK and PMT pathways under various stress conditions through choline metabolism. Considering that PMTs serve as the primary pathway providing phosphocholine for PC synthesis (Chen et al. 2019; Liu et al. 2019), the contributions of PMT and CEK to CCT function may vary under different growth conditions. In the future, it might be necessary to further explore 

how CCTs respond to these stress conditions and how the corresponding PMT and CEK pathways change, which could systematically reveal how PC metabolism contributes to stress responses. 

In our study, suppression of AthCCTs resulted in reduced root length specifically under osmotic stress (Fig. 3). In contrast, the pmt1/xpl1 mutant displayed a short primary root and epidermal cell death under normal growth conditions (Cruz-Ramírez et al. 2004). This difference could possibly be due to their positions in the PC biosynthesis pathway. PMT1 functions upstream in PC biosynthesis by converting phosphoethanolamine to phosphocholine, and its mutant phenotype can be complemented by exogenous supplementation of phosphocholine (Cruz-Ramírez et al. 2004). Conversely, AthCCTs act downstream, therefore, partial suppression in our transgenic lines may allow for normal growth. Furthermore, the increased epidermal cell death observed in the roots of the pmt1/xpl1 mutant was alleviated by PA treatment, indicating that broader disruptions in lipid signaling may contribute to this phenotype (Cruz-Ramírez et al. 2004). However, detailed studies of the regulatory mechanisms and stress-responsive signaling pathways associated with these enzymes are needed to fully elucidate their differential impacts on root development. 

Building upon the physiological roles of AthCCT1 identified in our experiments, we further explored its regulation via phosphorylation and investigated how phosphorylation is related to its physiological roles. AthCCT1-S187D had a comparable reduction of enzymatic activities to that observed with SnRK1-mediated phosphorylation at S187 (Fig. 5B) (Caldo et al. 2019), and thus was a reliable phosphomimetic variant for subsequent biochemical and biological studies. 

The CD analysis revealed the presence of lipid vesicles increased AthCCT1’s helical content (Fig. 6D), which aligned well with previous findings on rat CCTα, indicating that in a lipid environment, the helical content of full-length CCT increased at the expense of other secondary structures (Taneva et al. 2003). In the absence of lipids, rat CCTα exhibited a helical content of $2 5 \%$ (Taneva et al. 2003), which closely matched the $2 7 . 1 \%$ helical content observed in AthCCT1 (Fig. 6C). Upon the addition of lipid vesicles in the buffer, the full-length rat CCTα experienced a significant increase in helical content (Taneva et al. 2003). This increase was primarily attributed to the domain M of rat CCTα, which shifted from a mixed structure to a helical-dominated conformation, assisting in the formation of a hydrophobic face for membrane binding. The increase of lipid-induced helical content in the secondary structure of AthCCT1-S187D was less pronounced (Fig. 6D), with reduced stability in its conformation, particularly in the AI region of the domain M, and fewer contact sites between the AI region and membrane (Fig. 7). 

Moreover, the protein sequence alignment of AthCCT1 and rat CCTα showed that S187 is located in the J segment in AthCCT1, which is a short joining segment between the C-terminus of αE (αEc) and leash region of the domain M (Fig. 5A). In rat CCTα, the J segment plays an essential role in enzyme regulation and activation, which, along with αE helices, serve more than just as a linker between domain C and domain M (Knowles et al. 2019). They undergo structural remodeling into a more compact form upon membrane binding of domain M, which is necessary for enzyme activation (Cornell 2020). Also, the J segment aids in facilitating effective cytidyl transfer in a membrane environment by positioning the active site close to the membrane surface (Knowles et al. 2019). Given the amphitropic nature of CCT1, the phosphomimetic mutation at S187 in the J segment might affect AthCCT1’s interaction with membranes and its structural transition. This is 

supported by our MDS results, which indicated that AthCCT1-S187D has fewer AI-membrane contact sites and reduced conformational stability, suggesting that the S187D mutation may impair the AI region’s ability to effectively engage with the membrane. This finding aligns with our CD analysis, which showed a less pronounced increase in lipid-induced helical content for AthCCT1-S187D, supporting the idea of defective membrane-induced structural transitions. 

Indeed, phosphorylation has been shown to influence the activities of mammalian CCT by modulating its translocation to membranes (Yue et al. 2020). In mammalian systems, the phosphorylation site S319 within the C-terminal phosphorylation domain of CCTα has been identified as a critical regulatory site for PC biosynthesis (Yue et al. 2020). This proximal phosphorylation site to domain M has a negative regulatory effect on membrane association and catalytic activity of CCTα when phosphorylated. Conversely, CCTα translocation to nuclear membranes is accompanied by S319 dephosphorylation, which disturbed its inhibitory interactions with the domain M. These changes in conjunction with membranederived signals such as PC depletion, synergistically enhanced CCT’s membrane association and subsequent activation (Yue et al. 2020). The presence of phosphorylation site S187 in AthCCT1 suggested the possibility of similar regulatory mechanisms, but the functional significance of these phosphorylation-mediated controls in plant systems remains to be further determined. 

It is interesting to note that despite maintaining approximately $40 \%$ of native AthCCT1 activity in vitro, the S187D exhibited limited capacity to fully restore native gene function in vivo in contrast to the complete complementation achieved by AthCCT1 overexpression (Fig. 8). This may indicate that the phosphorylation state at S187 plays a subtle but important role in the in vivo functions of AthCCT1 during osmotic stress responses and suggest a complex regulation of CCT enzymes in the cellular environment. Further phosphoproteomic analysis may be helpful to fully elucidate the complex interplay between phosphorylation status of AthCCT1 and its function in plant osmotic stress responses. 

Considering the challenges associated with obtaining a true double knockout line, we adopted an alternative approach in this study by generating an RNAi-mediated knockdown of AthCCT1 in a cct2 knockout background (cct1 kd cct2 ko line). This strategy allowed a partial reduction of AthCCT1 activity while maintaining plant viability. Using this line as the target, we investigated the impact of this genetic modification on lipid metabolism by analyzing the lipid profile of siliques (Fig. 1). In Arabidopsis, siliques are the primary reproductive organs for lipid accumulation, making them a commonly used and ideal tissue for lipid profiling in numerous studies. Additionally, the decision to analyze lipid profiles in siliques was supported by previous studies reporting higher PC abundance in siliques (Watanabe et al. 2013). Consequently, we prioritized siliques as the main tissue for lipid analysis in this study. Subsequently, we extended our investigation to explore the role of AthCCT1 in osmotic stress responses in seedlings, which revealed the observed root growth phenotype (Fig. 3). Although the root tissue sample quantity in this study was insufficient for detailed lipid profiling, the observed root phenotype indicates that future research may gain valuable insights from lipidomic analysis of root tissue under osmotic stress. Moreover, given the potentially important roles of PC in other plant tissues, lipidomic profiling across various tissues could further enhance our understanding of the biological functions of AthCCT1 and its phosphorylation across different developmental stages. 

On the other hand, although AthCCT1 is presumed to contribute to the short-root phenotype under mannitol-induced osmotic 

stress by regulating PC homeostasis, this phenotype may also arise from a potential moonlighting function of AthCCT1. Previous studies have shown that CCT1 localizes to both the cytoplasm and nucleus (Sauer and Robinson 1985; Liu et al. 2021), suggesting more complex intracellular behavior and functions. For instance, CCT1 may physically interact with other proteins, and changes in its abundance could influence these interactions, contributing to the observed phenotype. To gain deeper insights into the role of CCT1, future studies could extend beyond measuring PC levels in AthCCT1 mutants by incorporating protein interaction analyses, such as proximity labeling or coimmunoprecipitation, to identify AthCCT1 interacting partners involved in stress responses. Moreover, transcriptomic analysis of AthCCT1 mutants may also uncover changes in the expression of stressresponsive genes, shedding light on the possible moonlighting functions of AthCCT1. Furthermore, generating a mutant library in the Atcct1 background and identifying lines with a more severe root development phenotype than the Atcct1 mutant under mannitol-induced osmotic stress, followed by gene identification and characterization, may further explore the functional roles of CCT1. 

Collectively, with integrated genetic, biochemical, and molecular approaches, our study revealed the roles of AthCCT1 in regulating PC biosynthesis under normal conditions and root development under osmotic stress. We also revealed that phosphorylation status at the S187 site played an important role in modulating AthCCT1 activity, possibly through alterations in its structural conformation and stability. The inability of S187D overexpression to rescue the short-root phenotype in cct1 kd cct2 ko mutants under osmotic stress further indicated the influence of the S187 site on AthCCT1’s biological functions, specifically in promoting root growth and ensuring developmental resilience during environmental challenges. 

# Materials and methods

# Plant materials and growth conditions

All Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana) lines were derived from the Columbia-0 ecotype background. Arabidopsis Col-0 and mutant lines were used to investigate the role of Arabidopsis CTP:phosphocholine cytidylyltransferase 1(AthCCT1) in PC biosynthesis and stress responses. The cct2 ko mutant (SALK_200207C, confirmed and kindly provided by Dr. Ikuo Nishida from Saitama University, Japan) served as a genetic background to study CCT1 function. To clarify the nomenclature of mutant and transgenic lines, their details were summarized in Table 1, following the convention [Promoter]:[Construct] [Background]. 

All seeds were sowed in Sunshine LA4 potting mix (SunGro Horticulture, Vancouver, BC, Canada), stratified and cultivated in a growth chamber under controlled conditions. The chamber maintained a 16-hour light/8-hour dark cycle at temperatures of $2 2 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ during the light period and $1 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ in the dark, with a white light at an intensity of $2 5 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ as previously described (Singer et al. 2016). Plants were supplemented once a week with N:P:K 20:20:20 fertilizer (Chissa-Asahi Fertilizer Co., Ltd, Tokyo, Japan). For seedling growth, homozygous seeds were surface-sterilized, stratified, and then grown on half-strength MS media in square plates $( 2 4 5 \mathrm { m m } \times 2 4 5 \mathrm { m m } )$ ) (Murashige and Skoog 1962). The plates were positioned vertically and maintained under the same growth conditions as mentioned above. The root length of the seedlings was recorded and measured using the Image J software. 

# RNA extraction and cDNA synthesis

Genomic DNA was extracted using the DNeasy Plant Mini kit (Qiagen, Toronto, ON, Canada). Total RNA was isolated from Arabidopsis homozygous developing siliques (14 days after flowering) using the Spectrum Plant Total RNA Kit (Sigma $=$ Aldrich Canada Co., Oakville, ON), and DNA contamination was eliminated using the on-column DNase set provided by the manufacturer (Sigma-Aldrich). The cDNA synthesis was conducted using the SuperScript IV First-Strand Synthesis System (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Inc., Waltham, MA). 

# Construct design

To explore tissue-specific and systemic effects of AthCCT1, we employed seed-specific promoters (Phaseolin, ProPha; Glycinin, ProGly) and constitutive promoters (tCUP3, ProtCUP3; 35s, Pro35s), respectively. Seed-specific promoters were employed to target AthCCT1 kd via RNAi (hpCCT1) and overexpression in developing siliques, an important stage for lipid accumulation, whereas constitutive promoters were used to study AthCCT1’s broader physiological roles, such as in root development. Constructs included ProPha: hpCCT1 cct2 and ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 for AthCCT1 kd in the cct2 ko background, and ProGly:CCT1 ProPha:hpCCT1 cct2 and Pro35s: CCT1 ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 for AthCCT1 overexpression in the cct1 kd cct2 ko lines (Table 1). An additional line, Pro35s:S187D ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2, was generated to evaluate the functional effect of the S187D mutation. 

# Generation of AthCCT1 RNAi constructs

The RNAi vector construction process generally followed the method in our previous study with minor modifications (Singer et al. 2016). The synthesized cDNA was utilized as the template to amplify the AthCCT1 fragment. To generate the seed-specific RNAi vector, a 367-bp segment in the $3 ^ { \prime }$ untranslated region (UTR) region of AthCCT1 cDNA was amplified using primers ECORI 73-1 and HindIII 73-1, PstI 73-1 and NcoI 73-1 to create sense and antisense copies (Supplementary Table S1). The RNAi cassette was constructed with a seed-specific $\beta$ -phaseolin promoter, derived from the common bean phaseolin gene (Slightom et al. 1983), driving a sense copy of the AthCCT1 3′UTR fragment (Frisch et al. 1995), followed by an intronic spacer, an antisense copy of the AthCCT1 3′UTR fragment, and a $\beta$ -phaseolin terminator. This cassette was inserted into the pPZP-RCS1 vector, resulting in the final seed-specific AthCCT1 RNAi vector. 

For the constitutive AthCCT1 RNAi vector, the 367-bp segment in the 3′UTR region of AthCCT1 cDNA was amplified using primers XbaI 20-1 and BamHI 20-1, EcoRI 20-1 and SalI 20-1 to create sense and antisense copies (Supplementary Table S1). To construct the constitutive AthCCT1 RNAi vector, the sense AthCCT1 3′UTR fragment was positioned between the tCUP3 promoter and an intronic spacer, and the antisense copy was inserted into between the spacer and the Pisum sativum Ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase transcriptional terminator in the background of pPZP-RCS1 vector. The potential off-target effects were evaluated and checked to be absent using the dsCheck software (https:// dscheck.rnai.jp/). 

# Generation of ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 and ProPha: hpCCT1 cct2 lines

The AthCCT1 RNAi vectors were transformed into Agrobacterium tumefaciens GV3101 through electroporation, and single colonies were verified through sequencing. The resulting single colonies were cultured in a Luria broth (LB) liquid medium with antibiotics 

and subsequently used to transform Arabidopsis cct2 via the floral dipping method (Clough and Bent 1998), resulting in the generation of ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 and ProPha:hpCCT1 cct2 lines. Seeds from the transformed plants were harvested and sown on kanamycin-resistant agar plates. Positive transformants were identified and further cultivated until T3 homozygous lines were established. The homozygous T3 seeds were then used for subsequent analysis and experiments. 

# Generation of AthCCT1 overexpression vectors and lines

Overexpression vectors for seed-specific and constitutive expression of AthCCT1, as well as constitutive expression of S187D, were constructed and verified through sequencing. To obtain the AthCCT1 CDS, cDNA of the Col-0 plant was amplified with primers AthCCT1-OE-f and AthCCT1-OE-r (Supplementary Table S1). The resulting AthCCT1 CDS fragment was inserted into the pBinGlyRed and pBin35SRed vectors, respectively. The pBinGlyRed vector contains the glycinin promoter (ProGly) (Kim et al. 2015), a seed-specific promoter derived from the soybean glycinin gene, while the pBin35SRed vector uses the constitutive CaMV 35S promoter (Huai et al. 2015; Nguyen et al. 2015). A previous study comparing seed-specific promoters indicated that the glycinin promoter exhibits expression levels statistically indistinguishable from the phaseolin promoter in Arabidopsis (Shockey et al. 2015). The seed-specific AthCCT1 overexpression vector (pBinGlyRed) was transformed into ProPha: hpCCT1 cct2 line, leading to the production of ProGly:CCT1 ProPha:hpCCT1 cct2 line (Table 1). The constitutive AthCCT1 overexpression vector (pBin35SRed) was transformed into ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 line, resulting in the production of Pro35s:CCT1 ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 lines (Table 1). The homozygous T3 seeds were used for subsequent analysis and experiments. The pBinGlyRed and pBin35SRed vectors were generously provided by Dr. Edgar Cahoon’s laboratory at the University of Nebraska-Lincoln (Huai et al. 2015; Kim et al. 2015; Nguyen et al. 2015). 

Similarly, the S187D CDS was amplified with primers AthCCT1-OE-f, AthCCT1-OE-r, s187d-OE-f, and s187d-OE-r (Supplementary Table S1) using AthCCT1 CDS as the template via a two-step overlap extension PCR (Ho et al. 1989). The resulting S187D CDS was inserted into the pBin35SRed vector. The constitutive S187D overexpression vector was transformed into ProtCUP3: hpCCT1 cct2 line, resulting in the production of Pro35s:S187D ProtCUP3:hpCCT1 cct2 lines. 

# Reverse transcription quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR)

The resultant cDNA was diluted and $1 \mu ]$ was used as the template for the reverse transcription quantitative RT-PCR (RT-qPCR), which was performed with triplicate in SYBR Green PCR Master Mix at a final volume of $1 0 \mu 1$ . The expression levels of CCT1 were quantified in WT, cct1 kd cct2 ko, and CCT1 overexpression lines with gene-specific primers CCT1-F and CCT1-R (Supplementary Table S1). For the CCT2 expression levels, primers CCT2-F and CCT2-R were used. The protein phosphatase 2A subunit A3 (PP2AA3) gene is stalely expressed in tissue in Arabidopsis and was used as the internal control with primer AtPP2AAF1 and AtPP2AAR1 (Supplementary Table S1) (Czechowski et al. 2005; Singer et al. 2016). Gene expression levels were quantified relative to the internal control using the comparative Ct method (Schmittgen and Livak 2008). 

# Production and purification of recombinant AthCCT1, S187D, and S187E

The CDS of the candidate genes was amplified using Platinum SuperFi II DNA polymerase (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Mutations of CCT1 were introduced using a two-step overlap extension PCR (Ho et al. 1989). To generate constructs for protein expression, the codon-optimized CDS of AthCCT1, S187D, and S187E were amplified from existing vectors in our lab using the primer sets CCTecoliNdeI/CCTecoliBamHI, CCTmutD-F/R, and CCTmutE-F/ R, respectively (Caldo et al. 2019) (Supplementary Table S1). The amplified fragments were then inserted into the BamHI and NdeI restriction sites of the existing pET16b vector. The resulting vectors were verified through sequencing and subsequently transformed into BL21(DE3) competent cells for protein expression (New England Biolabs, Ipswich, MA, USA). Protein expression, purification, and enzyme assay processes in this study were performed following previously described methods (Caldo et al. 2019; Xiao et al. 2023). The protein concentration was determined by Bradford assay with bovine serum albumin as the protein standard (Bradford 1976). 

# Liposome preparation and CCT enzyme activity assay

For the protein in vitro enzyme assay, we conducted the liposome preparation and enzyme activity assay generally following previous studies with minor modifications (Brault and Friesen 2016; Caldo et al. 2019). The reaction system consisted of a $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { l }$ mixture containing 4 mM phosphocholine, $\mathsf { 1 0 } \mathrm { m m }$ CTP, sonicated $2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M } \ P C { : 2 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M } }$ PA vesicles, ${ 1 \times }$ CCT buffer (50 mM imidazole, $2 5 \mathrm { m M }$ magnesium acetate, pH 7.0). All these chemicals except the lipids were bought from Sigma-Aldrich. CCT proteins were added to the indicated mixture and incubated for 15 min at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } .$ . At the end of the reaction, samples were transferred to boiling water for 5 min to terminate the reaction. Then, samples were centrifuged at ${ 1 6 , 0 0 0 } \mathrm { g }$ for 10 min and the supernatant was diluted ten times with distilled water and transferred to clear ${ 1 2 \times }$ 32 mm glass vials with a final volume of $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ . The final product of the reaction, CDP-Choline, was quantified using an Agilent 1260 Infinity HPLC system equipped with a Zorbax C18 (Aq) column $( 4 . 6 \ : \mathrm { m m } \times 1 5 \ : \mathrm { c m }$ ). A 5-μl aliquot of the diluted supernatant was injected into the column, and CDP-choline was detected by UV absorbance at $2 5 4 \mathrm { n m }$ , corresponding to the absorbance of the cytosine ring. To quantify the CDP-choline in the test samples, a standard curve was generated by gradient dilution of the CDP-choline standard (0.001 mM to 0.1 mM), which is based on the relationship between CDP-choline concentration and the corresponding peak areas (Brault and Friesen 2016). 

# CD sample preparation and analysis of the secondary structure of AthCCT1s

The CD analysis was conducted for AthCCT1 and S187D in the presence/absence of lipid vesicles PC/PA. The CD samples were prepared in a $5 0 \mathrm { m m }$ phosphate buffer at pH 7.0 (Taneva et al. 2003). The CD spectra of AthCCT1 and S187D in solutions with or without sonicated lipid vesicles were recorded. A $5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ phosphate buffer was used as the reference solution, with PA and PC serving as lipid activators (Taneva et al. 2003; Caldo et al. 2019). CD measurements were carried out using a DSM-1000 CD spectrophotometer (OLIS Instruments, Bogart GA, USA) equipped with a Peltier system to maintain the cell temperature at $2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The experiments were conducted in a cylindrical quartz cuvette cell of $0 . 2 \mathrm { m m }$ path length with a bandwidth setting of 1 nm. 

The wavelength range for the measurements was set between 260 and 190 nm. Five measurements under $\mathrm { N } _ { 2 }$ were averaged for each spectrum. The spectra of the buffer with or without lipids were used as the baseline and subtracted from the corresponding sample spectra before analysis. The data of subtracted and smoothed spectra were submitted to DichroWeb to estimate the secondary structure of sample proteins (Miles et al. 2022). The SMP180t dataset was chosen as the reference dataset (Abdul-Gader et al. 2011). The CDSSTR and CONTINLL algorithms were used for the analysis (Sreerama and Woody 2000). 

# Protein modeling, molecular docking, and in silico mutagenesis

The amino acid sequence of CCT1 from Arabidopsis was obtained from the UniProt database (The UniProt Consortium 2023) (ID: Q9ZV56). To generate a 3D model of AthCCT1, multiple approaches were tested, including the AlphaFold Protein Structure Database (AF) (Jumper et al. 2021), ModBase (Pieper et al. 2006), and Swiss-Model (Waterhouse et al. 2018), representing the primary methods of 3D structure prediction: Artificial Intelligence and Homology Modeling (HM), respectively. The top three models from each program demonstrated a high degree of agreement in terms of the enzyme’s overall structure, except for the region encompassing Residues 184 to 197, where the residue S187 under investigation in this study is located. This region was predicted as an α-helix by the programs utilizing HM, while AF predicted it as a helix–loop–helix. To verify these predictions, the secondary structure of AthCCT1 was predicted using JPred4 (Drozdetskiy et al. 2015), RaptoX (SS3 & SS8) (Wang et al. 2016), and PSIPRED (McGuffin et al. 2000). These predictions, along with a comparison to the rat CCTα deposited in the Protein Data Bank (PDB ID: 4MVD) (Lee et al. 2014), aligned with the model generated by AF, which was subsequently employed in all further protocols. 

The initial 3D structures of CTP and phosphocholine were retrieved from the PDB (IDs: 1UDW (Suzuki et al. 2004) and 4BES (Campanacci et al. 2013), respectively). The pKa values of both ligands were evaluated using Epik (Shelley et al. 2007) and PropKa3.1 (Olsson et al. 2011), resulting in a total charge of −1 for phosphocholine and −4 for CTP at pH 7.4. Subsequently, the ligands and the AthCCT1 model were prepared and energyminimized using the OPLS3e force field (Roos et al. 2019) within the Schrödinger-Maestro suite (Maestro Schrödinger 2020). Up to 16 stereoisomers were generated for each ligand. The molecular docking grid covered the entire globular region of AthCCT1 with dimensions of $3 6 \mathring { \mathrm { A } } \times 3 6 \mathring { \mathrm { A } } \times 3 6 \mathring { \mathrm { A } }$ . The docking process was conducted in two stages: initially, CTP was docked using the Glide (Halgren et al. 2004) extra precision (XP) (Friesner et al. 2006) scoring function, yielding 10 poses per stereoisomer. The best docking pose was selected based on energetic criteria (docking score) and geometric criteria (RMSD) against the analogous region of CTP in the co-crystallized CDP-choline ligand from rat CCTα. CDP-choline is one of the products of the enzymatic reaction of the CCT1 family. Of the 89 poses generated for CTP, the best was selected based on the aforementioned criteria and used in conjunction with AthCCT1 for the second round of docking, this time with phosphocholine. The same parameters and criteria were applied in this stage, with geometric comparison performed by calculating the RMSD of each pose against phosphocholine from the crystallographic structure (PDB ID: 4ZCR (Guca et al. 2018)). Subsequently, an AthCCT1-CTP-phosphocholine complex was assembled and dimerized through structural alignment with the rat CCTα structure. Following assembly of the AthCCT1 

dimer with its ligands in the catalytic site, in silico mutagenesis of S187D was performed for each monomer. 

# Molecular dynamics simulations

To analyze the impact of the S187D mutation on AthCCT1 activity at the molecular level, MDS was conducted. Two systems were simulated: native AthCCT1 in the presence of a membrane (AthCCT1_MEM) and AthCCT1 with the S187D mutation in the presence of a membrane (S187D_MEM). The AthCCT1_MEM and S187D_MEM systems were embedded in a 1-palmitoyl-2-oleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine (POPC) membrane patch with a surface area of $5 0 \mathring { \mathrm { A } } ^ { 2 }$ . All systems were solvated in an orthorhombic box with periodic boundary conditions, filled with single-point-charge (SPC) water molecules, and neutralized with $\mathrm { N a ^ { + } }$ and ${ \mathsf { C l } } ^ { - }$ ions. The systems were simulated in triplicate for 500 ns under NPT ensemble conditions (at standard temperature and pressure). The MDS were performed using the OPLS3e force field and the Schrödinger-Maestro suite. Upon completion of the MDS, the frequency of interaction of each of Residues 195 to 250 (leash and AI region) with the lipid membrane was determined throughout the simulation trajectory. Finally, the distance between residue M168 of the αE region and residue A239 of the AI region was calculated to characterize the extensibility/compaction of the AI region toward the protein core. 

# Lipid extraction and lipidomic analysis

Arabidopsis developing siliques (14 days after flowering) were harvested and fast frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at $- 8 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Approximately $1 0 \mathrm { m g }$ plant samples were immersed in $3 3 3 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $90 \%$ ice-cold methanol in water and homogenized for a total of 20 s with 1-mm beads in a bead beater (Mini-beadbeater-16, BioSpec, Bartlesville, OK, USA). The fragments were then kept at $- 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $4 \mathrm { { h } }$ to allow complete protein precipitation. Lipid extraction from each sample was performed by adding 1 mL of methyl tert-butyl ether and shaking for 5 min. To induce phase separation, $3 1 7 \mu \mathrm { L }$ water was added, followed by centrifugation at $1 4 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { r p m }$ for 10 min at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Lipids on the upper layer were transferred to a new 2-mL tube with a snap cap and dried. The dried lipids were resuspended in a 1:1 (v/v) solution of acetonitrile and isopropyl alcohol and centrifuged for 15 min at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ at 14,000 rpm. The supernatant was transferred to a clean glass insert for liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS) analysis. Ten µL from each sample was combined to use as a quality control (QC) sample which was tested together with the samples. 

An Agilent 1290 Infinity II ultra-high performance liquid chromatography (UHPLC) system connected to an ultra-high resolution Qq-Time-Of-Flight (UHR-QqTOF) mass spectrometer (Impact II; Bruker Corp., Billerica, MA, USA) was used to examine the samples. A reversed-phase separation of the analytes was performed using a Waters Acquity BEH C18 column $( 1 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ , $1 . 0 \mathrm { m m } \times$ 100 mm, 130 Å) (Milford, MA, USA) at $3 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The LC mobile phase A for positive ionization consisted of water/acetonitrile (40:60, v/v) with $2 \mathrm { m u }$ ammonium formate and $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid, with a pH of 4.8. The LC mobile phase A for negative ionization consisted of water/acetonitrile (40:60, v/v) with $5 \mathrm { m M }$ ammonium acetate, with the pH set to 9.8 by ammonium hydroxide. $90 \%$ isopropyl alcohol in acetonitrile was used as mobile phase B for both positive and negative ionization. The LC was set to a consistent flow rate of 0.1 mL/min. The gradient of mobile phase B was as follows: 0 min, $5 \%$ ; 8 min, $40 \%$ ; 14 min, $70 \%$ ; 20 min, $9 5 \%$ ; 23 min, $9 5 \%$ ; 33 min, $5 \%$ . During the equilibration phase, $2 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $2 5 0 \mathrm { m M }$ sodium formate was injected for mass spectrometer calibration. The mass 

spectrometer settings were as follows: dry gas flow rate of $6 \ : \mathrm { L } / \mathrm { m i n }$ at $2 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , 1.0 bar nebulizer pressure, spectra rate of $8 \ : \mathrm { H z }$ , mass range of 70 to $2 { , } 5 0 0 \mathrm { m } / \mathrm { z }$ , and cycle time of 2 s with a 500 V end plate offset. The capillary voltage was $- 3 . 6 \mathrm { k V }$ and $+ 4 . 5 \mathrm { k V }$ for negative and positive ionization, respectively. The system operated in Auto MS/MS mode with collision energies from 20 to $5 0 \mathrm { e V }$ , with an absolute threshold of 250 counts, and a charge state of 1. 

For the LC-MS data analysis, raw data was calibrated with an internal sodium formate standard to ensure mass accuracy, before being converted to Analysis Base File (.abf) format using Reifycs Abf Converter (v4.0.0) (https://www.reifycs.com/ AbfConverter/). The converted data was then processed with MS-DIAL (v4.80) (Tsugawa et al. 2015), employing the following parameters: MS1 tolerance of 0.01 Da, MS2 tolerance of 0.05 Da, minimum peak height of 1,000, and a mass slice width of 0.05 Da. An alignment retention time (RT) tolerance of 0.3 min was used. In this study, we only focused on the changes in levels of individual lipid classes across different genotypes caused by the modification of CCTs in plants. The lipidomic data presented in this study were acquired in positive ionization mode. 

# Data analysis and visualization

GraphPad Prism software (v8.0) was used for the data analysis and plotting in the root phenotypic analysis and enzyme assay. DOG (v2.0) software was used to prepare the diagram of the functional domain organization of AthCCT1 (Ren et al. 2009). The secondary structure of AthCCT1 was visualized using Esprit 3.0 (Robert and Gouet 2014). To generate the heatmaps in Figs. 1 and 2, the data of major lipid species were processed and generated using OriginLab Pro v2023 software (OriginLab Corporation Inc., USA), with color gradients reflecting relative abundance trends across genotypes. Pymol software was used to visualize the 3D structure of AthCCT1 and mutant S187D.
