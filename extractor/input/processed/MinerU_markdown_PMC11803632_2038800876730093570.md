# Abstract

MYB family transcription factors (TFs) play crucial roles in plant development, metabolism, and responses to various stresses. However, whether MYB TFs are involved in regulating fatty acid biosynthesis in seeds remains largely elusive. Here, we demonstrated that transgenic Arabidopsis (Arabidopsis thaliana) plants overexpressing MYB73 exhibit altered FATTY ACID ELONGATION1 (FAE1) expression, seed oil content, and seed fatty acid composition. Electrophoretic mobility shift assays showed that FAE1 is a direct target of MYB73, and functional assays revealed that MYB73 represses FAE1 promoter activity. Transcriptomic analysis of the MYB73-overexpressing plants detected significant changes in the expression of genes involved in fatty acid biosynthesis and triacylglycerol assembly. Furthermore, MYB73 expression was responsive to abscisic acid (ABA), and ABA-responsive element binding factor 2 directly bound to the ABA-responsive element in the MYB73 promoter to activate its expression. Additionally, we determined that MYB73 exhibits the hallmarks of an intrinsically disordered protein and forms phase-separated condensates with liquid-like characteristics, which are important in regulating target gene expression. Together, our findings suggest that MYB73 condensate formation likely fine-tunes seed oil biosynthesis. 

# Introduction

Most plant species synthesize and store triacylglycerol (TAG) in their seeds as crucial reservoirs of energy and carbon for the subsequent germination and growth. Seed oils hold significant importance not only in human nutrition but also as sustainable raw materials for a myriad of industrial applications (Chapman and Ohlrogge 2012; Kong et al. 2020b; Song et al. 2022; Yang et al. 2022a). The biosynthesis of TAG is a complex biological process (BP), intricately controlled by balanced transcriptional regulation across multiple branch pathways (Chapman and Ohlrogge 2012; Kong et al. 2020b; Yang et al. 2022a). Over the past 2 decades, extensive investigations have unveiled the intricacies of the transcriptional network governing plant oil production. Within this network, ample evidence underscores the essential role of the transcription factor (TF) WRINKLED1 (WRI1) in plant oil biosynthesis (Ruuska et al. 2002; Cernac and Benning 2004; Baud et al. 2007; Ma et al. 2015; Zhai et al. 2017; Kong et al. 2020a, 2020b; Qiao et al. 2022; Yang et al. 2022a, 2023; Lim et al. 2023). Recent studies have also illustrated the integral roles of other transcriptional regulators, such as LEAFY COTYLEDON1 (LEC1), LEAFY COTYLEDON2 (LEC2), FUSCA3 (FUS3), and ABSCISIC ACID INSENSITIVE3 (ABI3), in the transcriptional regulation of seed oil accumulation that is crucial for seed development and maturation (Baud et al. 2007; Mu et al. 2008; Zhang et al. 2016; Yang 

et al. 2022b). Beyond the well-established roles of LEC1, LEC2, FUS3, ABI3, and WRI1, considerable advancements have been made in uncovering the roles of other transcriptional regulators in seed oil biosynthesis. For instance, in Arabidopsis (A. thaliana) seeds, BASIC LEUCINE ZIPPER TRANSCRIPTION FACTOR 67 (bZIP67) interacts with LEC1-LIKE (L1L) and NUCLEAR FACTOR-YC2 (NF-YC2) and binds to the promoter of FATTY ACID DESATURASE 3 (FAD3), facilitating the biosynthesis of $\mathfrak { a }$ -linolenic acid (Mendes et al. 2013). GLABRA2 (GL2) encodes a homeobox protein, and its knockout mutant exhibits increased seed oil content (Shen et al. 2006). Notably, the gl2 mutant increases oil production by reallocating carbon resources to the embryo, consequently reducing seed coat mucilage production (Shi et al. 2012). Recent investigations have further revealed the involvement of additional transcriptional regulators, such as WRKY, DOF (DNA binding one finger), and tandem CCCH zinc finger proteins, in the regulation of seed oil accumulation (Wang et al. 2007; Fatihi et al. 2013; Song et al. 2020; Lu et al. 2021; Yang et al. 2022a). 

MYB TFs serve as pivotal transcriptional regulators in various plant processes, including development, metabolism, and stresses response (Stracke et al. 2001; Dubos et al. 2010; Liu et al. 2015; Roy 2016). Distinguished by a highly conserved DNA-binding domain comprising up to 4 imperfect amino acid sequence repeats (R), MYB proteins are categorized into 4 subfamilies based on the number of adjacent repeats, namely, R1-MYB, R2R3-MYB, 

R1R2R3-MYB, and atypical-MYB (Dubos et al. 2010). The prevalent R2R3-MYBs, characterized by 2 repeats, undergo further subdivision based on the conservation of DNA-binding domains and amino acid motifs within the highly variable C-terminal regions (Dubos et al. 2010). However, the functions of many R2R3-MYBs remain poorly understood. Recent studies have shed light on the involvement of certain R2R3-MYBs as activators or repressor of fatty acid biosynthesis. For instance, the subgroup 1, MYB96 positively regulates TAG biosynthesis through activating the expression of Acyl-CoA:diacylglycerol acyltransferase 1 (DGAT1) and phospholipid:diacylglycerol acyltransferase 1 (Lee et al. 2018). MYB30, also belonging to subgroup 1, governs the biosynthesis of very-long-chain fatty acids (VLCFAs) by regulating genes encoding the 4 enzymes of the acyl-CoA elongase complex (Raffaele et al. 2008). The subgroup 24 MYB92 directly binds to the promoter of biotin carboxyl carrier protein 2 (BCCP2), promoting fatty acid biosynthesis while simultaneously activating the expression of suberin biosynthetic genes (To et al. 2020). On the contrary, MYB76, in subgroup 12, functions as a negative regulator of seed oil accumulation, influencing the expression of genes involved in photosynthesis and the biosynthesis, modification, and degradation of fatty acids, as well as lipid droplet formation in Arabidopsis (Duan et al. 2017). Similarly, MYB89 has been identified as a repressor of fatty acid biosynthesis; it targets WRI1 and key genes such as phosphoenolpyruvate enolase 1 (ENO1), BCCP1, 3-ketacyl ACP synthase I (KASI), and 3-ketoacyl-CoA synthase 11 (KCS11) to inhibit fatty acid biosynthesis in Arabidopsis (Li et al. 2017). In addition, MYB9 and MYB107, members of subgroup 10, are critical for regulating suberin-related gene expression during Arabidopsis seed development. Mutations of both genes result in decreased aliphatic and aromatic suberin constituents in seeds (Lashbrooke et al. 2016). The subgroup 11 MYB41 activates the genes involved in suberin biosynthesis, and its overexpression leads to the formation of suberin-like lamellae in leaves (Kosma et al. 2014). 

Recent advances have provided compelling evidence that membraneless biomolecular condensates, often resulting from phase separation, play indispensable roles in diverse BPs within plant cells, underlying their comparable significance across various organisms (Banani et al. 2017; Cuevas-Velazquez and Dinneny 2018; Bienz 2020; Wunder and Mueller-Cajar 2020; Field et al. 2023; Miao et al. 2023; Liu et al. 2024). These condensates efficiently amplify local concentrations of biomolecules, thereby facilitating precise coordination of biochemical reactions across both spatial and temporal dimensions. The formation of various biomolecular condensates is orchestrated through a phenomenon known as liquid–liquid phase separation (LLPS), a process driven by multivalent interactions, particularly those mediated by intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs) of proteins (Mitrea and Kriwacki 2016; Banani et al. 2017; Borcherds et al. 2021; Miao et al. 2023). IDRs, characterized by their lack of a stable 3D structure yet influencing vital biological activities, play an important role in LLPS. The formation of protein condensates has been demonstrated to be involved in a myriad of BPs in plants, including phytohormone signaling (Powers et al. 2019), plant immune response (Zavaliev et al. 2020; Huang et al. 2021a), flowering (Fang et al. 2019; Huang et al. 2021b; Zhang et al. 2022), intrachloroplast cargo sorting (Ouyang et al. 2020), shoot meristem maintenance (Cao et al. 2023), microRNA production (Xie et al. 2021), circadian clock rhythm (Wang et al. 2021b), leaf senescence (Cheng et al. 2022), and temperature responsiveness (Jung et al. 2020; Huai et al. 2024). This intricate orchestration underscores the versatile and indispensable roles of protein condensates in shaping the physiological landscape of plant biology. However, 

little is known about the influences of phase separation on the functions of TFs involved in fatty acid biosynthesis. 

While numerous TFs from different families, including R2R3 MYBs, have been identified as positive regulators of fatty acid biosynthesis, only a few negative regulators have been characterized (Duan et al. 2017; Li et al. 2017). Recently, we demonstrated that other TFs, such as TCP4 (Kong et al. 2020a) and bZIP52 (Yang et al. 2023), negatively regulate fatty acid biosynthesis. In our efforts to explore MYB TFs involved in seed oil production, we discovered that the expression of Arabidopsis MYB73 is closely associated with seed development. Previous studies have shown that MYB73 is involved in auxin signaling, lateral root development, wounding responses, salt stress, and flowering (Kim et al. 2013; Zhao et al. 2014; Yang et al. 2020; Wang et al. 2021a; Chae et al. 2024). Homologs of MYB73 in other plants, such as rose, function as transcriptional repressor (Jing et al. 2023). However, the connection between MYB73 and fatty acid biosynthesis has not been established. Additionally, formation of nuclear condensates by MYB TFs and their function in FA biosynthesis have not been explored. In this study, we uncovered the role of MYB73 in modulating fatty acid biosynthesis and composition in seeds. Arabidopsis transgenic lines overexpressing MYB73 (MYB73-OE) exhibited decreased seed oil content with altered fatty acid composition. MYB73 functions as a transcriptional repressor that represses the expression of various genes involved in de novo fatty acid biosynthesis and TAG assembly. We also found that MYB73 is an intrinsically disordered protein capable of undergoing phase separation both in vitro and in planta, a property that is important for repressing the expression of target genes. Our findings suggest that the formation of MYB73 biomolecular condensate represents a potential molecular mechanism for fine-tuning plant oil biosynthesis. 

# Results

# Profiling MYB73 expression during seed development

We used reverse transcription quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) to examine MYB73 expression in developing seeds. Our results showed that the MYB73 expression was increased during seed development, reaching a peak at 10 days after flowering (DAF) with a slight reduction after, then increasing again at 16 DAF (Supplementary Fig. S1). The expression profile of MYB73 was possibly correlated to the seed development and traits, prompting us to speculate that MYB73 plays a potential role in regulating seed oil accumulation during seed maturation. 

# Overexpression of MYB73 affects seed oil content and fatty acid composition

To investigate the role of MYB73 in seed oil biosynthesis, we generated stable transgenic Arabidopsis lines overexpressing FLAG-MYB73 (MYB73-OE; driven by the CaMV 35S promoter). We obtained multiple homozygous MYB73-OE lines that showed substantially elevated MYB73 expression (Supplementary Fig. S2). Fatty acid analysis of the MYB73-OE lines showed reduced seed oil accumulation compared to wild-type (WT) (Fig. 1A). In addition, we found that seeds of MYB73-OE lines were smaller in size, with decreased seed weight, length, width, total area, and perimeter (Fig. 1B to D). 

Fatty acid composition analysis revealed that MYB73 overexpression led to higher relative contents of C18:1 and C18:2, but lower contents of C20:1 and C22:1 (Supplementary Fig. S3).

The fatty acid composition alterations in MYB73-OE closely resembled those of FATTY ACID ELONGASE 1 (FAE1) loss-offunction mutants. FAE1 (also known as KCS18) encodes a member of the KCS family involved in the biosynthesis of VLCFA (Baud and Lepiniec 2009). Previous studies have shown that FAE1 is seed-specific and predominantly responsible for the elongation of fatty acyl chains from 18 to 22 carbons (Lemieux et al. 1990; Rossak et al. 2001; Baud and Lepiniec 2009). The fae1 knockout mutants have impaired function in mediating elongation of C18:0 and C18:1, causing reduced levels of C20:0, C20:1, and C22:1. Therefore, the fatty acid composition changes in MYB73-OE prompted us to speculate that MYB73 is a transcriptional repressor of FAE1. 

# Overexpression of MYB73 downregulates FAE1 expression

We subsequently examined the expression of FAE1 in developing seeds of MYB73-OE using RT-qPCR. As shown in Fig. 2A, the expression of FAE1 was significantly reduced in MYB73-OE compared to WT. To further investigate the transcriptional regulation of MYB73 on FAE1, we conducted a dual-luciferase (dual-LUC) assay in Nicotiana benthamiana leaves (Liu et al. 2008; Kong et al. 2022; Yang et al. 2023). A reporter construct, in which the promoter of FAE1 (proFAE1) drives the expression of the firefly LUC reporter gene, and an effector construct, in which the CaMV 35S promoter drives MYB73 expression, were generated (Fig. 2B), and the 2 vectors were introduced into N. benthamiana leaves. LUC activity was significantly reduced upon transient expression of MYB73, suggesting the repression of proFAE1 activity by MYB73 (Fig. 2C). 

# MYB73 directly binds to proFAE1

To test whether FAE1 is a direct target of MYB73, we examined the potential MYB binding cis-elements in proFAE1 and identified MBSI (TAACGG) as the MYB73 binding cis-element (Fig. 2D). To 

test the direct binding of MYB73 to proFAE1, we generated a biotin-labeled DNA probe based on the sequence of proFAE1 (Fig. 2D). Electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA) was subsequently carried out, using purified recombinant GST-His-MYB73. We found that GST-His-MYB73 bound to the proFAE1 probe (Fig. 2E, left panel). As a control, EMSA was also conducted using a probe with a mutation in MBSI (probe M; Fig. 2D). No binding of MYB73 to the probe M was detected (Fig. 2E, left panel). In addition, with an increasing concentration of unlabeled probe $( 2 0 \times$ to ${ 1 0 0 \times }$ of the cold probe) in the reaction mixes, we observed reduced signal of protein–DNA complex in EMSA, suggesting a high specificity of MYB73 binding to the labeled probe (Fig. 2E, right panel). 

# MYB73 negatively regulates the expression of various genes involved in plant oil biosynthesis

Significant differences were found in the global transcriptomes of WT and MYB73-OE, as indicated by principal component analysis (PCA) (Supplementary Fig. S4A). Our results indicated that the expression of 1,921 genes was significantly altered in MYB73-OE compared to WT (Supplementary Fig. S4B). Among these significantly differentially expressed genes (DEGs), $3 4 . 4 \%$ were downregulated in MYB73-OE. # MYB73 forms liquid-like biomolecular condensates in plant cells

In silico analysis of MYB73 protein revealed structural intrinsic disorder, characterized by 2 IDRs (Supplementary Fig. S8A). Accumulating evidence suggests that proteins capable of undergoing phase separation often contain IDRs and/or low complexity sequences (Molliex et al. 2015; Patel et al. 2015; Mitrea and Kriwacki 2016; Banani et al. 2017; Zhou et al. 2019; Borcherds et al. 2021; Huang et al. 2021b; Wang et al. 2022; Miao et al. 2023). Many of the proteins that undergo phase separation display 

an uneven distribution of charges along the peptide backbone, resulting in the multivalency of these polypeptides and the formation of fragmented regions with highly positive or negative charge densities (Toretsky and Wright 2014), as observed in MYB73 via CIDER analysis (Supplementary Fig. S8B). We next evaluated the probability of MYB73 to undergo phase separation using FuzDrop analysis and found high potential for MYB73 to undergo phase separation (Supplementary Fig. S8C). These identified features prompted us to investigate experimentally whether MYB73 indeed undergoes phase separation. 

We transiently expressed YFP-MYB73 in N. benthamiana leaves, followed by confocal microscopy imaging. We observed the formation of puncta in nucleus, reminiscent of biomolecular condensates mediated by protein phase separation (Fig. 4A). To determine whether MYB73 nuclear condensates exhibit liquidlike properties, we carried out fluorescence recovery after photobleaching (FRAP) assay on the YFP-MYB73 nuclear bodies. As shown in Fig. 4B and C, YFP-MYB73 rapidly redistributed from the nonbleached region to the bleached region, indicative of the liquid-like property of YFP-MYB73 condensate. Collectively, these findings suggest that MYB73 protein forms nuclear bodies with liquid-like properties via phase separation in planta. 

Next, we assessed the capability of MYB73 to undergo phase separation in vitro. We produced and purified the recombinant His-MYB73-YFP protein. In the presence of the molecular crowding agent polyethylene glycol 8000 (PEG8000), we observed the formation of spherical droplets of His-MYB73-YFP, but not His-YFP (Fig. 4D). The size of the resulting condensates gradually enlarged with increasing concentration of His-MYB73-YFP, displaying a dose-dependent manner (Fig. 4E). FRAP experiment showed that MYB73 condensates recovered after photobleaching (Fig. 4F and G). Together, these results indicate that MYB73 can undergo phase separation in vitro, as well as in vivo. 

The N- and C-termini of MYB73 consist of 2 IDRs, predicted in silico and named IDR1 and IDR2, respectively (Supplementary Fig. S9A). To determine which IDR(s) promote MYB73 condensate formation, we performed deletion analysis. Interestingly, deletion of either IDR1 or IDR2 impaired MYB73 puncta formation in the nucleus (Supplementary Fig. S9B), suggesting that both IDR1 and IDR2 are required for MYB73 condensation. Furthermore, removal of IDR1 or IDR2 significantly reduced the ability of MYB73 to repress the promoter activity of its target in planta (Supplementary Fig. S9C and D). Our findings suggest that MYB73 condensate formation is crucial for its transcriptional repression activity. 

# Discussion

This study highlights the function of MYB73 in regulation of fatty acid biosynthesis and provides evidence of the formation of biomolecular condensates by a MYB TF. The biosynthesis of seed oil in vascular plants is governed by complex regulatory networks that synchronize a range of environmental and developmental cues, alongside multilayered regulatory mechanisms. Among these, transcriptional regulation plays a pivotal role in determining the seed oil biosynthesis. However, the precise mechanism by which TFs and the regulatory network influence the seed oil content in plants remains largely elusive. In our study, we first established that MYB73 played a role in repressing fatty acid biosynthesis (Fig. 1), which is a previously unknown function for MYB73. We also established that MYB73 significantly repressed the expression of FAE1 by binding to its promoter (Fig. 2). FAE1 plays crucial role in VLCFAs biosynthesis (Baud and Lepiniec 2009). 

Previous studies have shown that the fae1 knockout mutants are impaired in mediating elongation of C18:0 and C18:1, causing reduced levels of C20:0, C20:1, and C22:1 (Lemieux et al. 1990; Baud and Lepiniec 2009). Fatty acid composition in MYB73-OE closely resemble to that of fae1 knockout mutants, with reduced levels of C20:0, C20:1, and C22:1 (Supplementary Fig. S3). Analysis of the MYB73-OE transcriptome and RT-qPCR analysis revealed downregulation of BCCP2, PKP-β1, WRI1, and DGAT1, suggesting that MYB73 targets other genes involved in fatty acid biosynthesis (Supplementary Fig. S4 and Fig. 3A). The seeds of MYB73-OE lines showed variations in size, weight, length, and perimeter. Although the seed fatty acid composition of MYB73-OE resembles that of the fae1 mutant, the mutant does not exhibit variations in seed size. A TF regulates diverse BPs in plants by affecting gene expression in response to various cues. MYB73 is known to regulate many developmental and physiological processes, including auxin signaling, lateral root development, wound response, seed germination, and flowering. A recent study has shown that the MYB73 homolog in rose acts as a repressor of petal size (Jing et al. 2023). The involvement of MYB73 in development and phytohormone signaling suggests that it likely affects seed size by altering the expression of genes related to cell size and cell fate determination. This is supported by our MYB73-OE RNA-seq data, which reveal that 18 of the 30 GO terms enriched in negatively regulated genes are related to cell fate/specification and development (Supplementary Fig. S4). 

While many transcriptional activators have been reported in regulation of seed oil production, relatively fewer repressors related to fatty acid biosynthesis have been characterized. Repressors are critical in fine-tuning transcriptional regulation (Mitsuda and Ohme-Takagi 2009; Reynolds et al. 2013; Strader et al. 2022). A regulatory network often compresses multiple activators and repressors, and each factor responds to specific environmental signals and contributes to the combinatorial transcriptional control. MYB96 functions as a positive regulator in oil biosynthesis, and overexpression of MYB96 upregulates DGAT1 (Lee et al. 2018). Interestingly, MYB96 does not bind to proDGAT1 directly, rather, MYB96 regulates DGAT1 expression by targeting the promoter of ABSCISIC ACID INSENSITIVE4 (ABI4) (Lee et al. 2018). Different from MYB96, we found that MYB73 acted as a transcriptional repressor of DGAT1, and directly bound to proDGAT1 (Fig. 3). Furthermore, 4 key regulators of seed development, LEC1, LEC2, ABI3, and FUS3, play crucial roles in positively regulating the expression of WRI1 (Baud et al. 2007; Mu et al. 2008; Zhang et al. 2016; Pelletier et al. 2017; Tian et al. 2020; Yang et al. 2022a). However, our understanding of the transcriptional repression mechanism underlying WRI1 expression is sparse. Here, we discovered that WRI1 expression was directly repressed by MYB73 (Fig. 3A and B). The result further enhanced our understanding of the multilayered regulation of WRI1 expression. 

MYB73 is responsive to ABA, a phytohormone well known for the roles in seed maturation, dormancy, and germination (Chen et al. 2020; Ali et al. 2021). ABA orchestrates the expression of many genes involved in various abiotic stress responses (Fujita et al. 2011; Chen et al. 2020). Among these genes, ABFs emerge as critical TFs responsive to ABA signaling under abiotic stress conditions (Choi et al. 2000; Kim et al. 2004; Ng et al. 2014; Yoshida et al. 2015). ABRE has been extensively studied as a cis-element crucial for ABA-induced gene expression (Choi et al. 2000; Hattori et al. 2002; Fujita et al. 2011; Ng et al. 2014; Yoshida et al. 2015). While the involvement of ABFs in stress tolerance is well established (Choi et al. 2000; Kim et al. 2004; Yoshida et al. 2015), less is known about its role in controlling seed oil biosynthesis. # Materials and methods

# Plant materials

This study utilized Arabidopsis (A. thaliana) WT plants of the Columbia ecotype. Arabidopsis, N. benthamiana, and transgenic N. benthamiana expressing H2B-RFP (Zhang et al. 2022) plants were maintained in the growth chambers under conditions of $2 3 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and a photoperiod of $1 6 \mathrm { { h } }$ light $( 1 0 0 - 1 5 0 \mu \mathrm { m o l } \ \mathrm { m } ^ { - 2 } \ \mathrm { s } ^ { - 1 }$ illumination)/8 h dark. The methods for Arabidopsis transformation, seed surface sterilization, plant growth on plates, and the selection of transgenic overexpression lines were carried out according to previously established protocols (Ma et al. 2013). 

# Plasmid construction

Entry constructs containing the coding sequence (CDS) of various genes used in this study, including the truncated variants, were used for recombination into diverse pEarleyGate binary vectors through Gateway LR reactions (Life Technologies). The proBCCP2, proPKP-β1, and proWRI1 reporter constructs (pGreenII 0800-LUC vector) were described previously (Lim et al. 2023; Yang et al. 2023). PCR amplified $0 . 5 \mathrm { k b }$ promoter of FAE1 (proFAE1) and $1 . 7 \mathrm { k b }$ promoter of DGAT1 (proDGAT1) were subcloned into the pGreenII 0800-LUC vector (Hellens et al. 2005). For epitope-tagged recombinant protein production in Escherichia coli, full-length cDNA of ABF2 was subcloned into pET41a-6×His vector (Kong et al. 2012) for 6-histidine-tagged fusion protein expression. Full-length MYB73 was subcloned into pNIC-GST-His vector (Protein Production Platform, Nanyang Technological University) to generate an N-terminal 6-histidine-tagged protein. Full-length MYB73-YFP was subcloned into pNIC28-Bsa4 vector (Protein Production Platform, Nanyang Technological University) to generate an N-terminal 6-histidine-tagged MYB73-YFP protein. A list of the primers used in this study is provided in Supplementary Table S1. 

# Bioinformatic analysis

IDRs were analyzed using PONDR-VL3 (Peng et al. 2005). Analysis of protein charge distribution was performed using CIDER (Holehouse et al. 2017). Analysis of protein phase separation was performed using FuzDrop server (Hatos et al. 2022). 

# Measurement of seed weight and seed size

Arabidopsis seed weight and size were assessed following the protocol described previously (Fatihi et al. 2013; Yang et al. 2023). 

# Transient expression in N. benthamiana and confocal microscopy

Agrobacterium tumefaciens-mediated transient expression assays in N. benthamiana leaves, and confocal microscopy were performed as described previously (Kong et al. 2020a; Kong et al. 2022). The excitation and emission wavelengths for YFP and RFP are 488 and $5 6 1 \mathrm { n m }$ , and 505 to $5 4 5 \mathrm { n m }$ , and 589 to $6 2 5 \mathrm { n m }$ , respectively. 

# Recombinant protein production and purification

Recombinant proteins for GST-His-MYB73, His-ABF2, His-YFP, and His-MYB73-YFP were produced in E. coli strain BL21 (DE3). Cell cultures were induced for $2 \mathrm { { h } }$ at $2 4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (for GST-His-MYB73 and His-ABF2) or $1 6 \mathrm { { h } }$ at $1 6 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (for His-YFP and His-MYB73-YFP) by adding isopropyl- $\cdot \beta$ -D-thio-galactoside (IPTG) to a final concentration of 1 mM (for GST-His-MYB73 and His-ABF2) or $0 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ (for His-YFP and His-MYB73-YFP). Cells were lysed by LM20 microfluidizer (20,000 psi) in binding buffer with 1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride and Pierce protease inhibitor tablet (Thermo Scientific). Binding buffer for GST-His-MYB73 and His-ABF2 contained 50 mM sodium phosphate buffer $\mathrm { ( p H ~ \ 8 . 0 ) }$ , $3 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ NaCl, $1 0 \mathrm { m M }$ imidazole. Binding buffer for His-YFP and His-MYB73-YFP contained $2 0 \mathrm { m M }$ HEPES buffer (pH 7.5), 500 mM NaCl, 10 mM imidazole. Lysates from microfluidizer were centrifuged for 1 h at $2 0 , 0 0 0 \times g$ , $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ (JA 25.5 rotor, Beckman Coulter) and the supernatants were filtered with a $0 . 4 5 \mu \mathrm { m }$ syringe filter (Pall Corporation). For GST-His-MYB73 and His-ABF2, the filtered supernatants were passed through HisPur Ni-NTA Resin (Thermo Scientific) for binding, washing and elution according to the manufacturer’s standard protocol. Eluted fractions with good yield were dialyzed to remove imidazole. For His-YFP and His-MYB73-YFP, filtered supernatants were loaded onto a 5 mL Histrap column (Cytiva) connected to an FPLC AKTAxpress system (GE Healthcare). After affinity binding, the column was washed followed by elution using a gradient of 40 to 500 mM imidazole in elution buffer [20 mM HEPES (pH7.5), 500 mM NaCl, and $5 0 0 \mathrm { m M }$ imidazole]. Eluted fractions with good yield were dialyzed to remove imidazole. 

# Electrophoretic mobility shift assay

EMSA was performed as described previously (Kong et al. 2012, 2022). 5′ end biotin-labelled probes were used for EMSA. Each standard binding reaction of $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ solution contained 0.05 $\mu \mathrm { g / \mu L }$ poly(deoxyinosinic-deoxycytidylic) acid (polydI:dC), 15 mM HEPES-KOH (pH 7.5), 7.5 mM KCl, $0 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ EDTA, $5 \%$ (v/v) glycerol, 2 mM DTT, $1 ~ \mu \mathrm { g } / \mu \mathrm { L }$ BSA, $2 \ \mathrm { f m o l / \mu L }$ of the biotin probes, and varied amount of purified proteins for each assay ( $_ { \sim 2 0 }$ pmol GST-His-MYB73 for proFAE1, ${ \sim } 5 0$ pmol GST-His-MYB73 for proWRI1, ${ \sim } 1 0$ pmol GST-His-MYB73 for proBCCP2, ${ \sim } 1$ pmol His-ABF2 for proMYB73). The reaction mixes were incubated on ice for 30 min. The DNA–protein complexes were resolved on $5 \%$ 

(w/v) nondenaturing polyacrylamide gels and then were transferred to nylon membranes. The band shifts were detected by a chemiluminescent nucleic acid detection module (Thermo Scientific). 

# In vitro phase separation assay

For in vitro phase separation assay, purified His-MYB73-YFP and His-YFP recombinant proteins were used. Droplet assembly was performed with an addition of PEG8000 (Sigma-Aldrich) at a final concentration of $1 \%$ (w/v). The fluorescence was observed and imaged using Leica DMi8 microscopy (Leica Microsystems) equipped with a HCX PL APO $1 0 0 \times / 1 . 4$ oil objective, ORCA-Flash4.0 LT (Hamamatsu, Japan), and a solid-state Spectra-X light engine (Lumencor). The images were acquired using Metamorph software (Molecular Devices) and processed using ImageJ. 

# Fluorescence recovery after photobleaching

FRAP assay was conducted on a Nikon ECLIPSE Ti2-E inverted microscope (Nikon Instruments) equipped with a CSU-W1 confocal spinning unit (Yokogawa) and a $1 0 0 \times 1 . 4 9$ NA Plan-Apo objective lens (Nikon Instruments). The YFP signal was excited at 488 nm (Vortran) and the emission was collected at 589–625 nm through an ORCA-Fusion sCMOS camera (Hamamatsu). Image acquisition was controlled by MetaMorph (Molecular Devices) software. Photobleaching was carried out at 488 nm, which is controlled by iLAS 2 platform (Gataca systems), for a circle region. The signal recovery post bleaching was recorded by time-series image till the fluorescence emission reached a plateau. The fluorescence intensity of the bleached region in each frame of the time-series images was determined in Image J. 

# Gene expression analysis (RNA extraction, RT-PCR, and RT-qPCR)

Arabidopsis pooled siliques or seedlings were harvested and ground into fine powder in liquid nitrogen. Total RNA extraction was carried out using the Monarch Total RNA Miniprep Kit (New England Biolabs), following the manufacturer’s protocols. Subsequently, DNase I (New England Biolabs) was employed to remove genomic DNA, according to the manufacturer’s instructions. cDNA synthesis was conducted using the qScript cDNA SuperMix (Quantabio). 

In the RT-PCR, experiments were conducted utilizing 1 μL of cDNA as a template for PCRs (30 cycles) in $2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ . For agarose gel loading, $8 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of MYB73 and $3 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of ACTIN2 samples were used. ACTIN2 was used as an internal standard with following gene-specific primers [forward (FW), $5 ^ { \prime }$ -GCTCCCAGGGCTGTTT TTCCCA-3′, and reverse (RV), $5 ^ { \prime }$ -CCCGCTCTGCTGTTGTGGTGA-3′] as described by Yang et al. (2023). Primers for MYB73 (FW, $5 ^ { \prime }$ -AAAAAGATCCGGCGATGTCAAACCCGACCCGT-3′, and RV, $5 ^ { \prime }$ -GTTCTTATCACTCTACTCCATCTTCCCAATTCCG-3′). 

Reverse transcription quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) was conducted using Luna Universal qPCR Master Mix (New England Biolabs) according to the supplier’s protocols. The transcript accumulation level of the target gene was normalized to an internal standard isopentenyl pyrophosphate:dimethylallyl pyrophosphate isomerase 2 (IPP2) gene. Primers used for RT-qPCR were listed in Supplementary Table S2. 

# RNA-seq analysis

Total RNA extracted from pooled Arabidopsis siliques 12 DAF were used, with 3 biological replicates each for WT and MYB73-OE line (#3). RNA-seq libraries were generated using NEBNext Ultra II 

Directional RNA Library Prep Kit (New England Biolabs) for Illumina according to the manufacturer’s instruction and sequenced with Novaseq-6000 (Illumina) using paired-end sequencing with read lengths of 150 base pairs at sequencing depths of ${ \sim } 2$ million reads per sample. CDSs of the Arabidopsis nuclear genome (Araport 11) from The Arabidopsis Information Resource database (https://www.arabidopsis.org) were used as read pseudo alignment references using Kallisto (Bray et al. 2016) to estimate gene expression. Transcript per million-normalized abundance values of genes calculated from Kallisto were mean-centered, standardized to unit variance, used as features for PCA to visualize global transcriptome similarity between samples. Un-normalized gene counts estimated by Kallisto were used to detect gene differential expression in MYB73-OE siliques as compared to WT, using the R package DESeq2 (Love et al. 2014), with a Benjamini–Hochberg (Benjamini and Hochberg 1995) corrected P-value of $\le 0 . 0 5$ from DEseq2’s Wald tests, and a Log2FC of ${ \ge } 1$ or ≤ −1 being considered as significantly upregulated and downregulated DEGs, respectively (Supplementary Table S3). Functional annotations of Arabidopsis genes in the form of BP GO terms (The Gene Ontology 2019) were imported from the org.At.tair.db R package (https://bioconductor.org/packages/release/data/annotation/html/ org.At.tair.db.html). Using hypergeometric tests (Falcon and Gentleman 2008) onboard the R package ClusterProfiler, BP GO terms that are statistically overrepresented (enriched) in upregulated and downregulated DEGs were identified using a BH corrected P-value threshold $\mathrm { \Theta } \mathrm { o f } \le 0 . 0 5$ (Supplementary Tables S4 and S5). 

# The dual-LUC assay

Transient dual-LUC assay in N. benthamiana leaves was performed as previously described (Kong et al. 2022; Lim et al. 2022). After agroinfiltration, N. benthamiana plants were put in a plant growth chamber. Leaf discs were collected 3 d after agroinfiltration for the dual-LUC experiment using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter 1000 Assay System (Promega). In brief, 3 leaf discs (5 to 6 mm in diameter) from the agroinfiltration areas were excised, and subsequently ground to fine powder in liquid nitrogen. The fine powder was then homogenized in $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ Passive Lysis buffer (Promega). Next, 5 µL of the extract was mixed with $4 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of Luciferase Assay Buffer, and the firefly LUC activity was quantified by a cell imaging multimode plate reader (BioTek Cytation 5). The reaction was stopped by the addition of 40 µL Stop and Glo Buffer (Promega), and the Renilla (REN) LUC activity was quantified. The firefly LUC activity was normalized to the REN LUC activity. 

# Abscisic acid treatment of Arabidopsis

7-day-old seedlings were taken out from agar and placed in 6 well plates. Each well contained water with or without abscisic acid (ABA). $1 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ ABA was final concentration in solution. ABA stock solution was made up in $1 0 \%$ ethanol $( \mathrm { v / v ) }$ and mock control included equivalent amount of ethanol without ABA [the final concentration of ethanol was $0 . 0 1 \%$ (v/v)]. Samples were harvested $3 \mathrm { h }$ after the treatment for RNA extraction. 

# Fatty acid analysis

Lipid analysis in Arabidopsis seeds was performed as previously described (Ma et al. 2013). Briefly, 20 dried Arabidopsis seeds per sample were counted and placed in glass tubes, each tube adding 1 mL freshly prepared sulfuric acid in methanol $[ 5 \%$ (v/v)], $3 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of toluene, $2 5 \mu \mathrm { L }$ BHT solution $[ 0 . 2 \%$ (w/v) butylated hydroxy toluene in methanol)], and $2 5 ~ \mu \ g$ of triheptadecanoin internal standard. Samples were then incubated at $8 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 90 min. After the reaction, 

the fatty acid methyl ester extracts were extracted by adding 1 mL of $0 . 9 \%$ (w/v) NaCl and 1 mL of hexane before centrifugation. Upper organic phase was collected then dried under nitrogen gas and redissolved in $3 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ hexane. The samples were transferred into vials and analyzed by gas chromatography-flame ionization detection.
