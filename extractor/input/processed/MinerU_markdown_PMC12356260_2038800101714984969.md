# Insights into angiosperm evolution and lineage-specialized lignan biosynthesis from the early-diverging Schisandra genome

Jiushi Liu1,2 †*, Ruilin Xiong1,2 †, Leijiao Liu3 †, Tai-Ping Zhou4 †, Jiayu Xue5 , Dan Sun6 , Ranran Gao7 , Shanshan Chen3 , Rui Xu1 , Yuanyuan Xing1 , Shuang Peng1 , Bin Li1,2 , Xueping Wei1 , Wei Sun7 , Xiwen Li7 , Jun $\mathsf { \pmb { A } } \mathsf { \pmb { i } } ^ { \mathsf { \pmb { 6 } } }$ , Hongmei Luo1 , Bengang Zhang1 , Binju Wang4 , Zhichao $\bar { \mathbf { x } } \mathbf { u } ^ { 3 * }$ , Haitao Liu1,2 * 

Schisandraceae, an early-diverging angiosperm lineage, produces dibenzocyclooctadiene (DBCOD) lignans, unique bioactive compounds with liver-protecting properties. Although DBCOD lignan chemodiversity is well documented, their biosynthesis and evolution remain unclear. Here, we present a high-quality Schisandra chinensis genome, completing genomic representation of early angiosperms. Phylogenetic analysis confirms Austrobaileyales as sister to monocots, magnoliids, and eudicots, clarifying their evolutionary position. We identified an Austrobaileyalesspecific whole-genome duplication postdiversification from Amborellales and Nymphaeales. Integrating coexpression networks and biochemical assays, we delineated five key steps in DBCOD lignan biosynthesis, including dimerization, hydroxylation, methylation, and C─C phenol coupling. Notably, we found SchCYP719G1b, a previously unidentified enzyme catalyzing C─C linkage to form the signature DBCOD scaffold. Substrate selectivity assays and quantum mechanical/molecular mechanics calculations suggested that the para-hydroxyl diradical electronic properties preferentially drive selective C2─C2′ coupling over alternative pathways. Our findings illuminate early angiosperm evolution and the molecular basis of specialized lignan diversity.

# INTRODUCTION

Angiosperms, commonly known as flowering plants, represent the most diverse group of land plants, comprising over 350,000 extant species (http://theplantlist.org/). Basal angiosperms, representing earlydiverging lineages within this group, include the Amborellales, Nymphaeales, and Austrobaileyales (ANA) (1–4). Despite extensive molecular phylogenetic analyses, the evolutionary relationships between these early-diverging clades and the Mesangiospermae remain contentious (5–7). Charles Darwin famously referred to the rapid emergence and initial diversification of angiosperms as an “abominable mystery” (8), highlighting the enduring challenges in understanding their evolutionary history. Notably, early controversies surrounding the Austrobaileyales arose due to their unique morphological features. This was recognized in the updated Angiosperm Phylogeny Group II as a primitive angiosperm clade (9). This lineage is considered sister to a group comprising monocots, magnoliids, and eudicots and holds a critical position for studying the early evolution of these plants (10). Schisandra chinensis, a renowned traditional Chinese medicine, belongs to the Schisandraceae family within the order Austrobaileyales (11). S. chinensis and other Schisandraceae species uniquely accumulate dibenzocyclooctadiene (DBCOD) lignans, which have shown promise in clinical trials of drug-induced liver toxicity (12–16). To date, 358 DBCOD lignans have been reported, many of which exhibit notable biological activities, with 37 demonstrating hepatoprotective effects (16). DBCOD lignans consist of two C6─C3 unit lignans linked via a ${ \mathrm { C } } 2 { \mathrm { - } } { \mathrm { C } } 2 ^ { \prime }$ bond oxidative cyclization, forming the characteristic octacyclic ring structure (Fig. 1). The octacyclic ring serves as a crucial pharmacophore for the activity of natural DBCOD lignan products in Schisandraceae species (16, 17). Although the asymmetric total syntheses of DBCOD lignans have been reported (18), the biosynthetic enzymes responsible for C─C phenol coupling in octacyclic ring formation remain elusive. Existing evidence suggests that DBCOD lignan biosynthesis results from the C─C phenol coupling of dibenzylbutane lignans, which are derived from the coupling of propenylphenols (Fig. 1) (12). The S. chinensis genome advances our understanding of the evolutionary mechanisms underlying lineagespecific DBCOD lignans biosynthesis. 

In this study, we assembled a high-quality chromosome-scale genome for the early-diverging angiosperm S. chinensis. On the basis of genome and colinearity analyses, whole-genome duplication (WGD) events and ancestral angiosperm karyotypes (AAKs) were further inferred to understand species speciation and lineage phylogeny of early-diverging angiosperms. Furthermore, through coexpression matrix selection and biochemical assays, we elucidated the biosynthetic pathway of DBCOD lignans in S. chinensis, involving three types of enzymes: laccases (LACs), methyltransferases (OMTs), and cytochrome P450 monooxygenases (CYP450s). # RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

# S. chinensis genome fills the missing genomic gap of the ANA grade angiosperms

S. chinensis has an estimated genome size of 5.05 Gb with a high heterozygosity $( \approx 0 . 6 1 \% )$ based on the $2 7 k$ -mer frequency distribution analysis (fig. S1). The genome of S. chinensis was sequenced using a third-generation sequencing platform, generating 146.46 Gb $_ { \mathrm { \sim } 4 0 \times }$ coverage) of HiFi reads. The filtered HiFi reads were directly assembled using Hifiasm, resulting in a primary genome assembly of 5.03 Gb, containing 19,030 contigs, with a contig N50 length of $7 5 5 . 6 8 \mathrm { k b }$ (table S1). This draft genome assembly covered $9 5 . 4 \%$ of the estimated nuclear genome size, with a GC content of $4 0 . 9 6 \%$ (fig. S1 and table S1). Chromosome conformation capture methodology (i.e., Hi-C sequencing) was used for scaffolding, anchoring 4.95 Gb $( 9 8 . 4 1 \% )$ ) of the preliminary assembly onto 14 pseudochromosomes $2 n = 2 8$ ), with a contig N50 length of 386.48 Mb (Fig. 2A, fig. S2, and tables S1 and S2). The genome assembly was evaluated using Benchmarking Universal Single-Copy Orthologs (BUSCO), with $9 6 . 7 0 \%$ of the 1614 embryophyta single-copy orthologs identified as complete (table S3), suggesting that the S. chinensis genome assembly was of high quality. 

Approximately $8 3 . 4 4 \%$ (4.50 Gb) of the genome was annotated as transposable elements (TEs), with $4 2 . 3 2 \%$ as long terminal repeat (LTR) retrotransposons. In total, 970,270 LTR elements were identified in the S. chinensis genome, including 349,691 $( 9 . 8 2 \% )$ from the Copia superfamily and 610,407 $\left( 3 2 . 3 5 \% \right)$ ) from the Gypsy superfamily (table S4). A total of 35,468 protein-coding genes were predicted using a combination of ab initio gene prediction and RNA sequencing (RNA-seq)–assisted annotation. 

# Well-resolved phylogenetic position and specific polyploidization event in Austrobaileyales

The phylogenetic relationships of early-diverging angiosperms are a long-debated issue in plant evolution (19). Now, the S. chinensis genome provides us an opportunity to revisit this “abominable mystery.” We constructed an angiosperm species tree using sequences from 27 chromosome-level plant genomes and six transcriptomes, including representatives of the ANA-grade, all five lineages of Mesangiospermae, and gymnosperm outgroups (Ginkgo biloba and Cycas panzhihuaensis) (table S5). We constructed a series of phylogenetic trees using five different low-copy nuclear (LCN) gene sets comprising 102, 519, 1157, 1726, and 2291 orthologs. Analyses of these five datasets yielded similar and robust tree topologies for the phylogenetic relationships of Amborellales, Nymphaeales, Austrobaileyales, and Mesangiospermae, with the former three being successive sisters to all the remaining angiosperms, despite the variation in the relationships of the five mesangiosperm lineages (Fig. 2B and fig. S3). Therefore, Austrobaileyales, represented by species from Schisandra and Illicium, was consistently resolved as the last diverging lineage of the ANA grade and a sister group of Mesangiospermae. The phylogenetic position maintained sufficient stability and was consistent with the phylogenetic histories of nuclear genes and the plastid genome (7, 9, 19). 

The stable phylogenetic position is likely due to the fact that this lineage emerged long enough ago from the taxa that differentiated both before and after it, such as Nymphaeales and Mesangiospermae (20). Molecular dating, on the basis of fossil age calibrations (table S6), estimated that Nymphaeales diverged from the common ancestor of the Austrobaileyales-Mesangiospermae lineage ~187.49 million years ago (Ma) (Fig. 2B); the divergence of Austrobaileyales and Mesangiospermae was inferred to be ${ \sim } 1 7 4 \ \mathrm { M a }$ , whereas the start of withinlineage differentiation was inferred to occur at ${ \sim } 1 1 4$ and 141 Ma, respectively. Hence, there were long time intervals between the splits of the three lineages, which allowed for the accumulation of adequate mutations to serve as phylogenetic signals and to recover a robust phylogenetic relationship for the three lineages. In contrast, the five lineages of Mesangiospermae—eudicots, monocots, magnoliids, Ceratophyllales, and Chloranthales—remained controversial according to the results of the five datasets (fig. S3), reflecting a puzzle caused by the early rapid radiation of the Mesangiospermae (21–23). ANA-grade plants seem to have undergone rare polyploidization events compared with later differentiated lineages, according to previous studies (20, 24); however, whether the phenomenon applies to Austrobaileyales remains poorly understood.

# Selection of candidate genes related to DBCOD lignan biosynthesis in S. chinensis

Schisandraceae species, which are early-diverging angiosperms, have been reported to uniquely produce DBCOD lignans before the radiation of mesangiosperms (12). To investigate DBCOD lignan biosynthesis, methanolic extracts of the roots, stems, leaves, and fruits of S. chinensis were analyzed using liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry (LC-MS) to determine their tissue differential distribution (fig. S22 and table S8). Our results indicate that DBCOD lignans are enriched in fruits and roots compared to stems and leaves. Gomisin J, gomisin K1, and schisandrol A predominantly accumulated in the fruit, whereas schisandrol B, gomisin D, and $( + )$ -gomisin M2 were primarily concentrated in the root. These compounds are likely to serve as biosynthetic precursors for various lignans, suggesting that key enzymes are highly expressed in these tissues. However, the precursors of DBCOD lignans, dibenzylbutane lignans, such as pregomisin and schineolignin B, mainly accumulated in the stems (Fig. 4A). Distribution patterns in different tissues were used to select biosynthetic enzymes related to DBCOD lignan biosynthesis (Fig. 4A). 

Coexpression patterns between biosynthetic genes and metabolite accumulation have been used to accurately identify unknown enzymatic genes, such as those involved in the biosynthesis of colchicine, lycopodium alkaloids, and limonin (26–28). To identify the genes involved in DBCOD lignan biosynthesis in S. chinensis, transcriptomes from the fruits, roots, stems, and leaves were combined with the accumulation patterns of representative lignans for Pearson’s correlation coefficient $( r )$ analysis to select candidate biosynthetic genes. First, the biosynthesis of dibenzylbutane lignans was proposed to involve oxidative dimerization, hydroxylation, and O-methylation, starting with isoeugenol (Fig. 5A). Genome-wide identification of the related gene families annotated 19 LAC, 525 CYP450, and 50 OMT members. To prioritize candidate biosynthetic genes, we systematically calculated Pearson’s correlation coefficients between these genes and lignan metabolites, as well as among the genes themselves, thereby integrating transcriptional and metabolic to refine our biosynthetic pathway predictions (Fig. 4B). 

# Functional elucidation of the DBCOD lignan biosynthetic genes in S. chinensis

Previous isotope-labeling studies have indicated that isoeugenol (1) is the precursor of verrucosin (2) through oxidative coupling (29) (Fig. 5A), which might be catalyzed by LAC members. For instance, LACs have been shown to catalyze the oxidative dimerization of rosmarinic acid to synthesize salvianolic acid B (30, 31). Candidate LACs 

were selected based on their expression patterns across root, stem, leaf, and fruit tissues and the tissue-specific accumulation trends of pregomisin. Pearson correlation coefficients $( r )$ were calculated, revealing that nine LACs exhibited significant correlations $( r > 0 . 7 0 )$ 1 with high accumulation of pregomisin in stems (Fig. 4B). These candidate LAC genes were cloned from S. chinensis and heterologously expressed in Pichia pastoris, and crude protein lysates were assayed for in vitro oxidative activity with isoeugenol as the substrate (32). LC-MS analysis revealed that four candidate LACs, SchLAC14, SchLAC15, SchLAC16, and SchLAC17, catalyzed the dimerization of isoeugenol (1) to form 2 $( \mathrm { [ M - H ] } ^ { - } = 3 4 3 . 1 5 6 7 \mathrm { D a } )$ . Furthermore, when compared to the standards, product 2 was identified as verrucosin (2), which was further hypothesized to be converted to dibenzylbutane lignan dihydroguaiaretic acid (3) after the ring opening of the oxygen-containing heterocycles (Fig. 5B) (33). 

Theoretically, pregomisin (7) could be produced under the symmetrical hydroxylation (C5 and $\mathrm { C } 5 ^ { \prime }$ ) and O-methylation (C4 and $\mathrm { C } 4 ^ { \prime }$ ) of the benzene ring on dhydroguaiaretic acid (3) (Fig. 5A). To elucidate the catalytic steps, four CYP450 genes—comprising two CYP719Cs, one CYP73A, and one CYP76A—were prioritized based on their Pearson correlation coefficients $( r > 0 . 7 0 )$ with pregomisin. These four candidate SchCYP450s were heterologously expressed in Saccharomyces cerevisiae. The resulting microsomes were subsequently assayed for biochemical activity using dihydroguaiaretic acid (3) as the substrate (Fig. 4B). Liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/ MS) analysis indicated that SchCYP719C7 converted 3 to produce two peaks with mono- or polyhydroxylated modifications, resulting in products 4 $( \mathrm { [ M + H ] ^ { + } } = 3 4 7 . 1 8 6 4 \mathrm { D a } )$ and 5 $( \mathrm { [ M + H ] ^ { + } } = 3 6 3 . 1 8 1 9 \mathrm { \bar { D a } } )$ (Fig. 5C and fig. S23). 

Furthermore, five OMT candidates were selected based on their strong correlation $( r > 0 . 9 0 )$ with SchCYP719C7 (Fig. 4B). These SchOMTs were heterologously expressed in Escherichia coli, and the purified recombinant proteins were assayed for catalytic activity using a substrate mixture containing compounds 4 and 5—derived from dihydroguaiaretic acid (3) via SchCYP719C7 catalysis. LC-MS analysis indicated that the methylated lignans 6 $( \mathrm { [ M + H ] ^ { + } } = 3 7 5 . 2 1 6 8 \mathrm { D a } )$ and 7 $\left( \left[ \mathrm { M + H } \right] ^ { + } = 3 9 1 . 2 1 0 \dot { 8 } \mathrm { D a } \right)$ were detected as catalytic products of SchOMT02 (fig. S24). Furthermore, products 6 and 7 were identified as schineolignin B (6) and pregomisin (7), respectively, by comparison with standards (Fig. 5D and fig. S24). These results suggest that SchCYP719C7 can catalyze the hydroxylation modification (C5 and $C 5 ^ { \prime }$ ) on the benzene ring of dihydroguaiaretic acid (3), and SchOMT02 could methylated the hydroxylation groups (C4 and C4′) to produce schineolignin B (6) and pregomisin (7). 

Considering the structural relationship between pregomisin (7) and gomisin J (8), which feature the backbones of dibenzylbutane lignans and DBCOD lignans, respectively, we hypothesized that DBCOD lignan biosynthesis may involve $\mathrm { C - C }$ coupling of the benzyl groups of pregomisin (7). Guided by strong correlations $( r > 0 . 9 0 ) $ ) with gomisin J accumulation, seven SchCYP450 candidates—comprising one CYP719C, one CYP77A, two CYP708Bs, and three CYP71CUs— were prioritized for functional characterization (Fig. 4B). These enzymes were heterologously expressed in S. cerevisiae and assayed for catalytic activity using pregomisin (7) as the substrate. # Functional divergence of SchCYP719 genes driven by gene duplication and expansion

To understand the mechanism underlying the origin and functional evolution of SchCYP719 in S. chinensis, we performed a systematic survey of the P450 gene superfamily in 326 plants, including the genomes of eight Chlorophyta, one Rhodophyta, and 310 land plants, and transcriptomes of seven green plants (table S9). Homologous identification and phylogenetic relationships of candidate CYP450 genes indicated that CYP719 genes were exclusively found in the genomes and transcriptomes of angiosperms and were absent in other land plants and algae (Fig. 6A), suggesting a seed plant origin for the CYP719 family. A detailed phylogeny of the CYP719 family was constructed using CYP701 as an outgroup (Fig. 6B and fig. S27), from which we observed early angiosperm divergence dividing the CYP719s into two branches. CYP719A/B subfamily members, which have been reported to be responsible for the formation of methylene dioxygen bridge and coupling of the benzene ring of BIAs, cluster into one independent branch (Fig. 6, B and E) (37, 38). # Catalytic mechanism and effective mutation of SchCYP719G1b

To elucidate the mechanism of aromatic C─C coupling catalyzed by SchCYP719G1b from pregomisin, combined molecular docking, molecular dynamics (MD), and QM/MM calculations were conducted (39, 40). Most P450-catalyzed reactions are mediated by the highvalent oxo-iron(IV) porphyrin radical cation active species, Compound I (Cpd I) (41). Previous studies have suggested that Cpd I is also responsible for aromatic C─C coupling (42, 43). MD simulations showed that the proximal phenolic hydroxyl group of the substrate formed an H-bond interaction with the oxygen atom of Cpd I, whereas the other hydroxyl group remained significantly distant from Cpd I (fig. S30). Furthermore, the substrate engaged in H-bond interactions with $\mathrm { A s p } ^ { 2 9 2 }$ and π-π interactions with Phe471. To evaluate the computational findings, we performed targeted mutagenesis to probe the role of the active site residues in the catalytic reaction. The results showed that all amino acid mutations except T109L 

within the 4.00-Å range near the substrate, including key residues Asp292 and Phe471, significantly reduced the activity, suggesting that these residues play a key role in substrate binding and localization (figs. S31 and S32). In the QM/MM optimized structure of the reactant complex (RC), the O1─H5 distance is 1.98 Å, whereas the O1─H5′ distance is $7 . 5 4 \mathrm { \AA }$ (Fig. 7). Starting from the RC, Cpd I readily abstracts a hydrogen atom from the proximal phenolic hydroxyl group of pregomisin, forming a radical intermediate and Cpd II species with an energy barrier of $6 . 2 \mathrm { k c a l / m o l } .$ In the initial conformation of Int1, the distal hydroxyl group did not establish a favorable H-bond network with the Cpd II species, hindering the formation of diradical species through a second hydrogen atom transfer (44) (fig. S33). During the additional MD simulations of Int1, the H-bond network could readily rearrange with the aid of water molecules, establishing a robust hydrogen bond network between the distal hydroxyl of the substrate radical and the Cpd II species (labeled as Int1′) (fig. S34). Starting from Int1, QM/MM calculations revealed that water-assisted proton transfer from the distal hydroxyl group of the radical intermediate to Cpd II drives a typical proton-coupled electron transfer (PCET) process, resulting in the formation of diradical species and new water molecules in Int2 (Fig. 7). This water-mediated PCET process has been proposed for many P450 reactions (45–47). This process has an energy barrier of 17.8 kcal/ mol $( \mathrm { I n t 1 } ^ { \prime }  \mathrm { T S } 2 )$ ). Int2 represents a stable diradical species, which is $3 . 9 \mathrm { k c a l / m o l }$ lower than that of Int1′. 

For comparison, we explored the intramolecular hydrogen atom transfer from the distal phenolic hydroxyl group to the proximal oxygen radical. Our calculations showed that the intramolecular hydrogen atom transfer in Int1′ involves an energy barrier of $2 0 . 5 \mathrm { k c a l / m o l } .$ producing the distal oxygen radical intermediate $\mathrm { I n t } 2 ^ { \prime }$ (fig. S35). The calculated energy barrier suggests that this intramolecular hydrogen atom transfer is energetically unfavorable, which is in agreement with a previous study (43). Spin population analysis of Int2 confirms the formation of diradical species, with the spin populations at C2, $\mathbf { \mathrm { C } 2 ^ { \prime } }$ , and C6 being −0.23, 0.23, and −0.11, respectively. Our calculations indicate that the ${ \mathrm { C } } 2 { \mathrm { - } } { \mathrm { C } } 2 ^ { \prime }$ coupling involves an energy barrier of $1 3 . 3 \mathrm { k c a l / m o l }$ $( \mathrm { I n t } 2  \mathrm { T S } 2$ , as shown in Fig. 7), which is favored over that of the $C 6 { - } C 2 ^ { \prime }$ coupling (with a barrier of $1 6 . 2 \mathrm { k c a l / m o l } )$ (48) (fig. S36). The key residues Asp292 and $\mathrm { P h e } ^ { 4 7 1 }$ are involved in substrate binding and localization but do not regulate selective $\mathrm { C - C }$ coupling. Last, water-assisted proton transfer leads to the production of gomisin J (8) (fig. S37). Thus, selective C─C coupling is mostly governed by the electronic properties of the diradical species. Specifically, the computational model predicts greater thermodynamic feasibility for the formation of the ${ \mathrm { C } } 2 { \mathrm { - } } { \mathrm { C } } 2 ^ { \prime }$ coupled product, gomisin J (8), compared to the alternative ${ \mathrm { C } } 6 { \mathrm { - } } C 2 ^ { \prime }$ coupling pathway. This preference is supported by the lower activation energy barrier and the more stabilized transition state associated with the ${ \mathrm { C } } 2 { \mathrm { - } } { \mathrm { C } } 2 ^ { \prime }$ coupling route.

# MATERIALS AND METHODS

# Materials

Plant materials and chemical standards: S. chinensis plants were observed from the Institute of Medicinal Plant Development, the Chinese Academy of Medical Sciences, Beijing, China. The species was identified and confirmed as S. chinensis using DNA barcoding technology (49). Various tissues, including leaves (L), stems (S), fruits (F), and roots (R), were collected for transcriptome sequencing and metabolome analysis. The standards of schisandrol A, pregomisin, angeloylgomisin H, schisantherin A, schisantherin D, schisandrin B, benzoylgomisin O, interiotherin A, angeloylgomisin O, gomisin D, angeloylisogomisin O, schisandrol B, gomisin J, gomisin O, gomisin G, gomisin K2, gomisin K3, schisandrin C, schisandrin A, and gomisin N were isolated and stored in our laboratory. 

# Genome sequencing and assembly

High-quality total genomic DNA was extracted from fresh leaves of an individual S. chinensis plant, which was subsequently used to construct different libraries for genome sequencing. Short insert fragments for Illumina sequencing and long fragments for SMRT (Single Molecule Real-Time) sequencing were used for genome sequencing. The short-read libraries were constructed and sequenced using the Illumina HiSeq X Ten platform. For long-read sequencing, the libraries were constructed and sequenced using two cells of the PacBio Sequel platform (https://pacb.com/). Raw data were filtered to remove adapters and low-quality reads using SMRT Portal analysis. A Hi-C library of young S. chinensis leaves was also prepared following the standard procedure (50) and was subjected to paired-end sequencing. The cross-linked and lysed cells for the Hi-C library were digested using Hind III restriction enzyme. 

The genome size of S. chinensis was estimated using the $2 7 k$ -mer distribution with the short-read data. Draft genome assembly was performed using Hifiasm (v0.16.0-r369) (51). The completeness of the S. chinensis genome assembly was estimated using the Embryophyta odb 10 dataset with BUSCO (v5.2.2) (52). The paired-end Hi-C reads 

were mapped to the draft assembly to improve the genome assembly, and the contigs were anchored into pseudochromosomes using the ALLHiC pipeline (53). 

# Genome annotation and gene expression analysis

To predict and classify repeat elements in the S. chinensis genome, we used the Repeat-Modeler (v2.0.2) package, which includes two de novo repeat-finding programs, RECON (v1.08) and RepeatScout (v1.0.6). In addition, Genome-tools (v1.6.2) and LTR_retriever (v2.9.0) were used to identify and classify LTR retrotransposons, and RepeatMasker (v4.0.6) was used to mask total TE sequences (54). The transcriptome data from various tissues were assembled de novo using Trinity (v2.8.5) with default parameters (55). The MAKER (v3.1.3) annotation pipeline was used to predict putative proteincoding genes with parameters as described previously (56). Gene prediction species models were trained using Augustus (v3.4.0) (57). The unigenes and predicted protein sequences from RNA-seq of S. chinensis, and the annotated protein sequences from A. trichopoda and N. colorata were combined as expressed sequence tags and protein homology evidence for BLAST and Exonerate alignments. The completeness of the genome annotation was evaluated using BUSCO (v5.2.2). The RNA-seq data from different tissues of S. chinensis were aligned to the genome using HISAT2 (v2.2.1) (58) and the read counts and transcripts per million (TPM) values were calculated and normalized using R packages. 

# Phylogenetic reconstruction

To identify probable orthologous genes for phylogenetic analysis, we first downloaded the sequenced genomes from public resources (table S5). OrthoFinder (v2.5.5) (59) was used to identify orthogroups with the default parameters. The orthologs were obtained from 31 angiosperms and two early-diverging gymnosperms G. biloba (60) and C. panzhihuaensis (61). The LCN gene sets were identified based on OrthoFinder results. LCN genes represent the low-copy genes, which ranged between one and six gene copies per species in each cluster and were identified among 33 species by OrthoMCL. We carefully selected five LCN gene sets (2291, 1726, 1157, 519, and 102) from 33 species and applied both a supermatrix method and the multispecies coalescent model to infer the phylogeny of angiosperms. The LCN genes were aligned and trimmed the sites with less than $9 0 \%$ coverage using MAFFT (v7.525) (62) and trimAI (v1.4) (63). IQTREE (v2.3.3) (64) was used for model selection and subsequent reconstruction of the maximum-likelihood phylogenetic tree for protein alignments, consensus tree was obtained after 1000 times ultrafast bootstrap. The ML tree of each LCN gene was reconstructed by IQTREE as mentioned below. The coalescence tree was reconstructed by ASTRAL (v5.7.1) (65) based on gene trees from LCN genes. FigTree (v1.4.4) was used to summarize and visualize species trees. The evolutionary timescale was analyzed using the MCMCtree of the PAML (v4.10.7) (66) package with 50,000 iterations and a sample frequency of 16 after 200,000 iterations as the burn-in. The divergence times of the species were estimated based on the fossil-based age constraints (table S6). CAFE (v5.0) (67) predicted gene family evolution, including gene expansion and contraction. 

# Identification of WGD events

WGDI was used to identify the polyploidy events that occurred in the selected seven angiosperm species by inferring the colinear regions and calculating the $K _ { S }$ values of colinear genes (68). First, 

BLASTP with a cutoff e value of $1 \times 1 0 ^ { - 5 }$ was used to identify the similarity of genes within or between each genome (69). We used the “-icl” parameter in WGDI to find colinear genes within each genome. $K _ { S }$ values between colinear genes were estimated by the Nei-Gojobori approach in PAML with the parameter “-KS.” Then, dotplots of colinear genes were plotted with the parameter “-bk.” The polyploidy level of each species was confirmed by counting the syntenic ratios between different species. Last, $K _ { S }$ peaks were fitted using the “-pf ” parameter in WGDI. Genome-wide comparison of syntenic depth was also used to reveal the shared or specific occurrences of polyploidization. For example, A. trichopoda and S. chinensis show a 1:2 syntenic pattern, with one paralogous region in A. trichopoda matching two regions in S. chinensis. N. colorata and S. chinensis show a 2:2 syntenic pattern, whereas V. vinifera and S. chinensis show a 3:2 syntenic pattern. To address potential inaccuracies in WGD event detection stemming from differing substitution rates among candidate species, ksrates (v1.1.3) (70) was used to position adjusted WGD events via rate-adjusted mixed paralog-ortholog $K _ { S }$ distributions for S. chinensis, A. trichopoda, N. colorata, I. anisatum, and Liriodendron tulipifera. 

# Chromosomal rearrangement and ancestral karyotype reconstruction

The identification of colinear genomic blocks among the genomes of S. chinensis, A. trichopoda, N. colorata, E. ferox, and B. schreberi, using the WGDI pipeline (v0.6.4) (37) facilitated the reconstruction of the AAK. One WGD event of the S. chinensis genome was used to infer the chromosomes of the SAKs. Similarly, we inferred that the 36 B. schreberi chromosomes arose from 12 BAKs. In addition, we inferred that they arose from 14 NAKs based on genome colinearity analysis of N. colorata and E. ferox. Subsequently, it was inferred that they were all derived from 13 NSAKs based on the colinearity analysis of SAKs with NAKs and SAKs with BAKs, respectively. Last, the AAK, which consists of 15 chromosomes, was inferred based on the colinearity analysis of the NSAK ancestral chromosomes with A. trichopoda. Then, homologous genes were identified using BLASTP with an e value cutoff of $1 \times \bar { 1 } 0 ^ { - 5 }$ . The genome colinearities were then plotted with the “-d” parameter of WGDI, and the grouping of chromosomes was optimized according to the colinear blocks. To further confirm the ancestral chromosomes karyotypes of ancestral, the “-pc” parameter of WGDI was used to classify the three species into subgenomes and mapped these subgenomes to the AAK chromosomes using the “-a” parameter to obtain colinear genes. Last, the AAK was used to map the extant genomes to calculate the fusion events. 

# Identification of candidate genes and co-relation analysis

The different tissues (fruits, roots, stems, and leaves) of S. chinensis were collected, and the metabolites were extracted by methanol immersion. The supernatant was analyzed by LC-MS/MS. LC-MS/MS analysis was performed on an UPLC-QTOF-MS (ultraperformance liquid chromatography coupled with quadrupole time-of-flight mass spectrometry) system (Waters Xevo G2 Qtof) equipped with an Acquity UPLC BEH C18 column $2 . 1 \mathrm { m m }$ by $1 0 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , $1 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m } $ ). The mobile phase consisted of $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid in water (A) and acetonitrile (B), and the linear gradient elution program was as follows: 0 to 2 min, $4 9 \%$ B; 2 to 4 min, 49 to $5 2 \%$ B; 4 to 6 min, 52 to $6 0 \%$ B; 6 to 9 min, 60 to $6 2 \%$ B; 9 to 10 min, $6 2 \%$ B; 10 to 13 min, 66 to $7 0 \%$ B; 13 to 15 min, 66 to $7 0 \%$ B; 15 to 17 min, 70 to $9 5 \%$ B (flow rate, 

$0 . 3 \mathrm { m l } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ ; column temperature, $4 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ). The mass spectrometer equipped with electrospray ionization (ESI) source operating in positive ion mode, with a mass/charge ratio $\left( m / z \right)$ range of 100 to 1200. The conditions of the MS detector were as follows: capillary of 3 kV, sampling cone of 30 V, source temperature of $1 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } _ { : }$ , desolvation temperature of $3 0 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , cone gas flow of 50 liters/hour, desolvation gas flow of 600 liters/hour, and collision energy of $6 \mathrm { k V . }$ The identification of lignans in S. chinensis extracts was mainly determined by comparison with standards. Only a few were resolved on the basis of their spectral characteristics. 

To identify candidate genes involved in lignan biosynthesis, we first performed domain annotation (Pfam) to screen all CYP450 and LAC genes in the S. chinensis genome. Genes with $\mathrm { T P M } < 5 0 $ across all tissues (roots, stems, leaves, and fruits) were excluded to focus on highly expressed candidates. Next, Pearson’s correlation coefficients $( r )$ were calculated by integrating the normalized expression levels (TPM) of these genes with the tissue-specific accumulation profiles of lignans (pregomisin and gomisin J). Candidates showing strong correlations $( r > 0 . 7 0 )$ between gene expression and lignan content were prioritized for functional assays. Subsequently, the validated functional gene SchCYP719G1b was used as a bait gene for coexpression analysis, leading to the selection of OMT candidates with stringent correlation thresholds $( r > 0 . 9 )$ ) for further characterization. 

# Functional characterization of SchLAC genes

The open reading frames of candidate genes (listed in table S10) were cloned by polymerase chain reaction with reverse transcription from the total RNA of S. chinensis. All primers used for cloning are listed in table S11. The DNA sequence was inserted into the pPIC9k plasmid, a secretory expression vector containing a N-terminal $\alpha$ -factor secretion signal for recombinant protein production in P. pastoris. The recombinant vector was transformed into the GS115 strain (P. pastoris). The positive transformant were screening in both MD ( $\mathrm { 1 0 \times Y N B }$ , $5 0 0 \times$ biotin, and $2 0 \%$ glucose) and MM $\mathrm { \Delta } ^ { \prime } 1 0 \times \mathrm { Y N B }$ $1 0 \times$ , $5 0 0 \times$ biotin, and $5 \%$ methanol) plates. 

Positive transformants were inoculated into $5 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of BMGY medium [yeast extract (10 g/liter), peptone (20 g/liter), 0.1 M phosphatebuffered saline (PBS) $\left( \mathrm { p H } 6 . 0 \right)$ , $1 0 \times$ YNB, $5 0 0 \times$ biotin, $1 0 \%$ glycerol, and G418 $\mathrm { ( 1 ~ m g / m l ) }$ ], shaken at $2 2 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until the optical density (OD) reached 2. Bacteria were collected by centrifugation and resuspended in $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of BMMY medium [yeast extract (10 g/liter), peptone (20 g/liter), 0.1 M PBS (pH 6.0), $1 0 \times$ YNB, $5 0 0 \times$ biotin, $5 \%$ methanol, and G418 $\mathrm { ( 1 ~ m g / m l ) }$ ], supplemented with methanol every 24 hours to a final concentration of $0 . 5 \%$ , and incubated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 days. The bacteria were collected by centrifuge and resuspended with $5 0 \mathrm { m l }$ of PBS (0.02 M, pH 6.0) sulfate buffer and then crushed by a high-pressure cell crusher and centrifuged at 8000 rpm for $1 5 \mathrm { { m i n } }$ . The protein is present in the supernatant. 

LAC activity was detected using a $3 0 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { l }$ reaction system containing $3 4 \mathrm { m M }$ MES buffer (pH 6.0), 0.3 mM ABTS [2, 2ʹ-azino-bis(3- ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid)], $0 . 3 \mathrm { m M }$ isoeugenol, and $2 5 \mu \mathrm { l }$ of crude enzyme and then reacted for 8 hours in a shaker at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . The reaction solution was extracted with ethyl acetate, and the supernatants were evaporated and reconstituted in methanol. The products were analyzed by UPLC-MS/MS using an Acquity UPLC BEH C18 column ( $2 . 1 \ \mathrm { m m }$ by $1 0 0 ~ \mathrm { { m m } }$ , $1 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ ; column temperature: $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ). The mobile phase consisted of (A) $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid in water and (B) acetonitrile, with the following gradient program: 0 to 3 min, $5 \%$ B; 3 to 7 min, 5 to $3 0 \%$ B; 7 to 10 min, 30 to $7 5 \%$ B; 10 to $1 5 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ , 

75 to $9 5 \%$ B; 15 to $2 0 \mathrm { m i n }$ , $9 5 \%$ B (flow rate: $0 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m l } \mathrm { m i n } ^ { - 1 }$ ). MS detection was performed on a Waters Xevo G2 Qtof in negative ion mode with the following parameters: full scan range $^ { * } m / z ^ { * } 1 0 0$ to 1200, MS/MS acquisition using collision energy ramp (20 to $4 0 \mathrm { e V }$ ). 

# Expression, purification, and functional characterization of SchOMTs

Full-length open reading frames of SchOMTs were amplified and cloned into the pET28a vector, and pET28a-OMT constructs were transformed into E. coli strain BL21(DE3). 

To obtain purified recombinant proteins, a primary culture was incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ on a shaker at $2 2 0 ~ \mathrm { r p m }$ overnight in $5 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of LB medium. The overnight culture was transferred into $5 0 0 \mathrm { m l }$ of fresh LB containing kanamycin ( $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g } /$ liter) and incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and 220 rpm to an OD600 between 0.4 and 0.6. To induce recombinant protein expression, $0 . 5 \mathrm { m M }$ IPTG (isopropyl- $\cdot \beta$ -d-thiogalactopyranoside) was added and the culture was further incubated 16 hours at $1 8 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ . Cells were harvested by centrifugation $( 8 0 0 0 g , 5 \mathrm { m i n } )$ ), resuspended in $4 0 \mathrm { m l }$ of lysis buffer $[ 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ sodium phosphate $\left( \mathrm { p H } 7 . 5 \right)$ , 300 mM NaCl, $1 0 \%$ (v/v) glycerol, and $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ $\beta$ -mercaptoethanol], and sonicated for $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m i n }$ (3-s on; 3-s off). After centrifugation (12,000g, 20 min, $4 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } ,$ ), the cleared lysate was loaded onto a Ni-NTA, the resin was washed with 30 column volumes of binding buffer $[ 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ sodium phosphate $\mathrm { ( p H } ~ 7 . 5 )$ , 300 mM NaCl, $1 0 \%$ (v/v) glycerol, and $1 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ $\beta$ -mercaptoethanol] and eluted with the binding buffer containing $2 5 0 ~ \mathrm { m M }$ imidazole to obtain $6 \times$ His-tagged proteins. Concentrating and desalting of purified proteins were performed using an Amicon centrifugal filter-30K (Millipore, Billerica, MA, USA) and stored in a buffered solution $\mathrm { [ 5 0 ~ m M }$ sodium phosphate $\mathrm { ( p H } 7 . 5 )$ , $1 0 0 \ : \mathrm { m M N a C l }$ , $1 0 \%$ (v/v) glycerol, and 1 mM dithiothreitol (DTT)]. The SDS–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis of SchOMT02 was shown in fig. S38. 

The enzyme assays were performed in a final volume of a $1 0 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { l }$ reaction system with Gly buffer $\left( \mathrm { p H 9 . 0 } \right)$ containing $1 0 ~ \mu \ g$ of purified proteins, 2 mM S-adenosylmethionine (SAM), and the enzymatically generated product from SchCYP719C7-catalyzed conversion of substrate 3. The reaction was quenched by adding methanol $( 5 0 \%$ , v/v). The empty vector was negative controls. Catalytic products were analyzed using a Waters Acquity UPLC system (Waters, Milford, MA, USA) equipped with a C18 column (2.1 mm by $1 0 0 \mathrm { m m }$ , $1 . 7 \mu \mathrm { m }$ ; column temperature: $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ ). The mobile phase consisted of (A) $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid in water and (B) acetonitrile, with the following gradient program: 0 to 2 min, $1 5 \%$ B; 2 to 4 min, $1 5 \%$ B to $2 0 \%$ B; 4 to 6 min, $2 0 \%$ B to $3 0 \%$ B; 6 to 9 min, $3 0 \%$ B to $4 5 \%$ B; 9 to 10 min, $4 5 \%$ B  to $5 5 \%$ B; 10 to $1 3 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , $5 5 \%$ B  to $6 6 \%$ B; 13 to $1 5 \ \mathrm { m i n }$ , $6 6 \%$ B to $7 0 \%$ B; 15 to 17 min, $7 0 \%$ B to $9 5 \%$ B; 17 to 18 min, $9 5 \%$ B (flow rate: $0 . 3 ~ \mathrm { m l ~ m i n ^ { - 1 } }$ ). MS analysis was conducted on a Waters Xevo G2-S QTOF mass spectrometer in positive ion mode, with a full scan range of $^ { * } m / z ^ { * } 1 0 0$ to 1200 and MS/MS acquisition parameters set to collision energy ramp (20 to $4 0 \mathrm { e V }$ ). Data acquisition was controlled by Waters MassLynx software (v4.2). 

# Heterologous expression of SchCYP450 in S. cerevisiae and in vitro activity assays

Each CYP450 gene was inserted into the pESC-His vector by Gibson assembly. The pESC-His::CYP450 construct was transformed into the yeast strain WAT11. After being cultured and induced, yeast cells were harvested, crushed, and differentially centrifuged to obtain microsomes as described in previous study (61). In vitro enzymatic 

activity assays were carried out at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 12 hours in a $5 0 0 \mathrm { - } \mu \mathrm { l }$ reaction system, containing $2 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { l }$ of total microsomal proteins and $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ NADPH (reduced form of nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate), along with a regenerating system [consisting of $5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ FAD (flavin adenine dinucleotide), $5 \mu \mathrm { M }$ FMN (riboflavin $5 ^ { \prime }$ -phosphate), 5 mM glucose-6-phosphate, and glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase $( 1 \ \mathrm { U } \ \mathrm { \bar { m l } } ^ { - 1 } )$ ], and $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ substrates. The reaction product was extracted with ethyl acetate, and the solvent was evaporated and redissolved in methanol for LC-MS analysis. Negative control reactions were carried out with microsomal preparations from recombinant yeast transformed with empty pESC-His. The products were determined by LC-MS, and the relevant parameters are consistent with the analysis of SchOMTs. 

# Transient expression of SchCYP719G1b in Nicotiana benthamiana

Target SchCYP450 genes were cloned into the pEAQ vector and transformed into the Agrobacterium tumefaciens strain GV3101. Bacteria were cultivated at $3 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ to an OD600 of 1 in $2 0 ~ \mathrm { m l }$ of LB medium [yeast extract $( 5 \ { \mathrm { g } } \ { \mathrm { l i t e r } } ^ { - 1 } )$ ), tryptone $( 1 0 \mathrm { ~ g ~ l i t e r } ^ { - 1 }$ ), NaCl $( \mathrm { 1 0 ~ g ~ l i t e r } ^ { - 1 } )$ , kanamycin $( 5 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { m l } ^ { - 1 } )$ ), and rifampicin $( 1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { g } \mathrm { m l } ^ { - 1 } ) ]$ ], resuspended in MMA buffer [10 mM MES (pH 5.6), $1 0 \ \mathrm { m M } \ \mathrm { M g } { \mathrm { C l } _ { 2 } } .$ , and $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { M }$ acetosyringone] to an OD600 of 1. The final culture was used to infiltrate the underside of 4- to 5-week-old N. benthamiana leaves. For substrate-feeding experiments, tobacco leaves were infiltrated with $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { M }$ substrates (dissolved in an aqueous solution containing $0 . 0 0 2 \%$ dimethyl sulfoxide) on the fourth day after strain infiltration. After 24 hours, leaves were harvested, lyophilized, and extracted for LC-MS analysis under identical chromatographic and mass spectrometric conditions as those used for SchOMTs characterization. N. benthamiana were grown in a greenhouse under a 16-hour light/8-hour dark photoperiod at $2 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ and $6 0 \%$ relative humidity. 

# Origin and evolution of the plant CYP719 family

Plant genomes (genomic assembly, protein sequence, and genome annotation) and transcriptomes were downloaded from the NCBI, Ensembl Plants, or GigaDB, among others. The CYP719 genes were identified in various plant species using reported CYP719s as seeds via BLASTp with an e value of $1 \times { 1 0 } ^ { - 1 0 }$ . We downloaded the CjCY-P719A1 from C. japonica; PsCYP719A21 and PsCYP719B1 from P. somniferum; TfCYP719A from Thalictrum flavum; AmCYP719A13 and AmCYP719A14 from Argemone mexicana; Ec-CYP719A2, EcCY-P719A3, and EcCYP719A5(B5UAQ8.1) from Eschscholzia californica (38); and StCYP719C4 and StCYP719C3 from Stephania tetrandra (71) for BLASTP analysis. We followed the CYP450 nomenclature system and retained those BLAST results with ${ > } 4 0 \%$ amino acid sequence identity to relevant CYP719 genes. Then, the annotated CYP719 genes from S. chinensis, L. tulipifera, Cinnamomum kanehirae, M. dauricum, Aquilegia fimbriata, Nelumbo nucifera, Saururus chinensis, C. sessilifolius, and Aquilegia coerulea were used respectively to construct the ML phylogenetic tree using MAFFT (v7.525) (62), trimAI (v1.4) (63), and IQ-TREE (v2.3.3) (64) analysis. Detection of S. chinensis, C. panzhihuaensis, A. trichopoda, C. kanehirae, M. dauricum, A. fimbriata, and A. coerulea synthetic blocks and visualization of microsynchronization results using JCVI (v1.4.11) (72). 

# Ancestral sequence reconstruction

Ancestral sequence reconstruction is an emerging protein engineering strategy that infers ancestral protein sequences from extant homologs, 

offering solutions to critical biological and evolutionary questions. Ancestral sequences were reconstructed using the FastCodeML program within the PAML software package, under the LG substitution model, by inputting a phylogenetic tree and a multiple sequence alignment (MSA) file. The resulting ancestral node protein sequences were extracted from the output files. Phylogenetic tree visualization and topology analysis were performed using FigTree, enabling the identification of key ancestral nodes Anc26, Anc40, and Anc47 within the reconstructed sequences for downstream functional studies. 

# Protein structure prediction, docking, and site-directed mutagenesis

The SchCYP719G1b structure model was generated by AlphaFold3. Determination of active-site pocket and ligand docking calculations were performed with AutoDock Vina 1.5.6. The complex was displayed by PyMOL, and the residues within 5 Å of the substrates and those comprising the entrance channel were marked. Site-directed mutagenesis was designed based on these residues. Mutants were constructed by cloning from recombinant plasmid pESC-His::CYP450 using primers containing mutation sites. Protein expression levels of all mutants were routinely monitored by Western blot (WB) analysis with anti-Myc tag antibodies to confirm comparable enzyme amounts across catalytic assays. Molecular graphics were also rendered with PyMOL.
