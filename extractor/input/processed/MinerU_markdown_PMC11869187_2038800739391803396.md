# Summary

Salinity significantly inhibits plant growth and development. While the recretohalophyte Limonium bicolor can reduce its ion content by secreting salt, the metabolic pathways it employs to adapt to high salt stress remain unclear. This study aims to unravel this enigma through integrated transcriptomic and metabolomic analyses of L. bicolor under salt stress conditions. The results showed that compared to the control (S0), low salt treatment (S1) led to a significant increase in plant growth, photosynthesis efficiency and antioxidant enzyme activity but caused no significant changes in organic soluble substance and ROS contents. However, high salt treatments (S3 and S4) led to a significant decrease in plant growth, photosynthesis efficiency and antioxidant enzyme activity, accompanied by a significant increase in organic soluble substance and ROS contents. A significant increase in phenolic compounds, such as caffeoyl shikimic acid and coniferin, upon the treatments of S1, S3 and S4, and a decrease and increase in flavonoids upon the treatments of S1 and S3 were also observed, respectively. This study also demonstrated that the expression patterns of key genes responsible for the biosynthesis of these metabolites are consistent with the observed trends in their accumulation levels. These results suggest that under low salt stress conditions, the halophyte L. bicolor experiences minimal osmotic and oxidative stress. However, under high salt stress conditions, it suffers severe osmotic and oxidative stress, and the increase in organic soluble substances and flavonoids serves as a key response to these stresses and also represents a good strategy for the alleviation of them. 

# Introduction

Soil salinization has become increasingly severe and has emerged as a significant limiting factor constraining global agricultural production (Munns et al., 2020; Shabala et al., 2014). According to the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations, approximately 960 million ha of land worldwide are currently affected by salinization (Grigore and Toma, 2020; Munns et al., 2020). Most salinized soil is unsuitable for common crops (Gong et al., 2020). However, it is an important land reserve and also contains abundant salt-resistant species. Halophytes are plants that can grow and develop normally in soil with NaCl concentrations above $2 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ (Grigore and Vicente, 2023). They are the dominant species that thrive in salt-alkaline soils and are classified into three types: euhalophytes, salt-exclusion halophytes and recretohalophytes (Casu, 1905; Grigore and Vicente, 2023). As a result of long-term adaptation to saline habitats, halophytes have evolved salt tolerance mechanisms to cope with salt stress. These mechanisms include reducing ion levels in functional organs or the cytoplasm, employing efficient reactive oxygen species (ROS) scavenging strategies and possessing osmotic adjustment capabilities (Chen et al., 2018; Gong et al., 2020; Zhu, 2003). Halophytes are considered the 

dominant species for studying salt-resistant mechanisms. Understanding the salt tolerance mechanism of halophytes is of great importance for accelerating the cultivation of new varieties of salt-resistant crops and the development and utilization of saline-alkali lands (Ahmad et al., 2022; Grigore and Vicente, 2023). 

Limonium bicolor is a dicotyledonous, perennial herb belonging to the Plumbaginaceae family and is a typical recretohalophyte characterized by multicellular salt glands located on its leaves and stem (Yuan et al., 2014, 2022). Additionally, the root or entire herb of L. bicolor can be utilized as traditional Chinese medicine, known for its blood-enriching properties. The seedlings can also serve as forage grass, and its flowers retain their freshness, making them an ideal material for dry flower arrangements (Chen et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 1998). This species exhibits high salt tolerance, as its salt glands can secrete excess ions from the body to the outside, thereby reducing leaf ion content (Yuan et al., 2015). Its salt resistance is dependent on the level of salt gland development and salt secretion (Li et al., 2020). In recent years, the functions of numerous genes involved in salt gland development and salt secretion have been elucidated (Han et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024). 

At present, salt tolerance mechanisms in recretohalophyte are mainly focused on salt gland development and salt secretion (Li et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022). Yuan et al. (2015) suggested that the genes that regulate salt gland development are homologous to those that regulate trichome development in Arabidopsis. In Arabidopsis thaliana, the key regulators of trichome development, including TTG1 (TRANS-PARENT TESTA GLABRA1), the bHLH transcription factors and the MYB transcription factors, form the MYB-bHLH-TTG1 complex (MBW complex) (Kirik et al., 2001; Payne et al., 2000; Walker et al., 1999). Studies have shown that the mechanism by which the MBW complex regulates epidermal cell development is relatively conserved (Huang et al., 2022; Humphries et al., 2005; Imamura et al., 2020). In L. bicolor, the homologous proteins LbTTG1 and LbHLH are involved in regulating the development of salt glands (Wang et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2022). Zhang et al. (2022) suggested that ion transport and vesicle transport proteins play important roles in salt gland secretion through transcriptional and protein association analyses. However, despite these strategies, salt stress still leads to an increase in ion levels within recretohalophytes, subsequently triggering osmotic stress and oxidative stress (Li et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2024). 

Therefore, in addition to salt excretion as a crucial salt tolerance mechanism, reducing osmotic stress and oxidative stress is also vital for their salt tolerance (Yuan et al., 2018; Zhu et al., 2024). Ding et al. (2010) discovered that exogenous $\mathsf { C a } ^ { 2 + }$ enhanced the salt tolerance of L. bicolor by promoting salt gland development and decreasing malondialdehyde (MDA) content. Numerous hormones, including melatonin (Li et al., 2022), methyl jasmonate (Yuan et al., 2018), cytokinin (Liu et al., 2023) and abscisic acid (ABA) (Zhu et al., 2024), have been found to increase the salt tolerance of this species by promoting salt gland development, enhancing osmoregulatory substance accumulation and improving ROS scavenging ability. 

For the recretohalophyte L. bicolor, excreting salt from the body through salt glands is the primary mechanism of salt tolerance, additionally, reducing osmotic stress and oxidative stress is also an important mechanism, although it has received limited attention in research. With the advancement of transcriptome and metabolomics analysis, it is providing important and novel insights into the complex responses of plants to salt stress. Therefore, this study aims to uncover the osmotic regulation and antioxidant stress mechanisms involved in the salt tolerance of L. bicolor through transcriptome and metabolome correlation analysis. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report on the transcriptome and metabolome responses of L. bicolor to different concentrations of NaCl, providing novel insights for further study of the salt stress adaptation strategies of L. bicolor. These findings will uncover its salt tolerance mechanism and its potential application value under salt stress conditions. 

# Results

# Morphological, biochemical and physiological responses of L. bicolor to salt treatment

The growth parameters of L. bicolor seedlings, including root and leaf dry weight, leaf number, leaf area, shoot height and root length, exhibited a trend of increasing and then gradually decreasing as the NaCl concentration increased gradually 

(Figure 1a,b). Specifically, the S1 treatment resulted in the highest values for all these growth parameters, which were 1.25, 1.35, 1.12, 1.56, 1.18 and 1.25 times higher than those of the S0 treatment, respectively. However, when NaCl concentration exceeded $2 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ (S2), plant growth began to be inhibited. Notably, seedlings treated with 300 mM NaCl (S3) experienced significant growth inhibition, with the dry weight of roots and leaves being only about $80 \%$ of that in the S0 treatment. Overall, the growth of L. bicolor plants was promoted by low NaCl concentrations but inhibited by higher concentrations. 

The contents of chlorophyll a, chlorophyll b and total chlorophyll in L. bicolor seedlings exhibited a trend of decreasing and then increasing as the salt concentration increased (Figure 1c). Specifically, compared to the S0 treatment, the contents of these chlorophyll components were significantly decreased in the S1 and S2 treatments. To gain a deeper understanding of the impact of salt stress on photosynthesis, the net photosynthetic rate $( \mathsf { P n } )$ , transpiration rate (Tr) and stomatal conductance (Gs) were analysed, which showed an initial increase followed by a decrease. Conversely, the trend for intercellular ${ \mathsf { C O } } _ { 2 }$ concentration (Ci) was opposite to that of Pn, Tr and Gs (Figure 1d). Notably, the S1 treatment resulted in the highest values for Pn, Tr and Gs, which were $1 2 . 5 \%$ , $3 8 . 2 \%$ and $2 5 . 1 2 \%$ higher than those of the S0 treatment, respectively. However, the Pn, Tr and Gs values for the S3 and S4 treatments were significantly lower than those of the S0 treatment. Overall, the photosynthetic parameters of L. bicolor plants were positively affected by low salt concentrations but negatively impacted by higher concentrations. 

The progressively heightened NaCl concentrations administered to L. bicolor plants induced profound physiological changes. Specifically, the ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + }$ content of seedlings under the S1, S2, S3 and S4 treatment exhibited a marked increase, reaching 1.54, 2.83, 4.93 and 6 times that under the S0 treatment (Figure 2a). In stark contrast, the ${ \sf K } ^ { + }$ content exhibited a declining trend, the ${ \sf K } ^ { + }$ content of seedlings under S1, S2, S3 and S4 treatment fell to $7 5 . 9 2 \%$ , $6 1 . 2 1 \%$ , $5 7 . 4 9 \%$ and $4 6 . 8 3 \%$ of that under S0 treatment (Figure 2b). This resulted in a dramatic surge in the $\mathsf { N a } ^ { + } / \mathsf { K } ^ { + }$ ratio, indicating a disruption in the crucial ion balance that is essential for plant vitality. Additionally, the contents of soluble protein and proline increased in parallel with increasing NaCl concentrations (Figure 2c,d). Interestingly, the contents of soluble sugars, including glucose, fructose and sucrose, significantly declined under low concentration of NaCl treatment and then increased with the increasing of NaCl concentration (Figure 2e–h). In short, the plants underwent significant metabolic adjustments in response to the escalating salt stress. 

Compared to that under S0 treatment, the $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 } \cdot ^ { - }$ and MDA contents do not change when the L. bicolor seedling was under S1 treatment, and $H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ contents significantly increased, whereas the electrolyte leakage initially significantly decreased (Figure 2i–l). As the NaCl concentration progressively increased, the L. bicolor plants experienced a notable surge in reactive oxygen species (ROS), including $\mathrm { O } _ { 2 } \cdot ^ { - }$ and $H _ { 2 } O _ { 2 }$ , with more pronounced increases observed in the S3 and S4 treatments compared to the moderate rises in S1 and S2, relative to the S0 control (Figure 2i,j). Concurrently, the levels of MDA and electrolyte leakage initially decreased slightly but then escalated markedly when NaCl exceeded $2 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ (Figure $2 \mathrm { k } , \mathsfit { \tau }$ l). # Transcriptome sequencing analysis and functional annotation of differentially expressed genes (DEGs)

Physiological evaluations revealed pronounced distinctions among the S0, S1 and S3 treatment groups (Figures 1, 2). To achieve a deeper comprehension of the molecular underpinnings of salt stress responses, transcriptome sequencing followed by rigorous analysis was undertaken on L. bicolor plants subjected to these treatment conditions. The sequencing data exhibited high quality, with Q30 and GC percentages ranging from $9 1 . 8 2 \%$ to $9 3 . 8 3 \%$ and from $4 5 . 9 4 \%$ to $4 6 . 4 4 \%$ , respectively (Table S1). A total of 115 849 clean reads were obtained, and pairwise comparisons of S0 vs S1, S0 vs S3 and S1 vs S3 identified 4654 

(1902 up-regulated and 2752 down-regulated), 7093 (3472 up-regulated and 3621 down-regulated) and 5645 (3131 up-regulated and 2514 down-regulated) differentially expressed genes (DEGs), respectively (|fold change| $> 2$ and corrected P-value $< 0 . 0 5$ ) (Figure 3a,b). The heat map of all DEGs in the S0, S1 and S3 treatments indicated that salt stress could induce transcript changes in L. bicolor (Figure 3c). In addition, comparative analysis between S0 and S1 revealed 179 transcription factors (TFs), with 62 up- and 117 down-regulated, including 22 AP2/ERFs, 20 WRKYs and others. The S0–S3 comparison yielded 277 TFs, with an imbalance of 148 up-regulated and 129 down-regulated, featuring 24 AP2/ERFs, 17 bHLHs, and others. The S1–S3 comparison showed 281 TFs, with 171 up- and 110 down-regulated, encompassing 30 AP2/ERFs, 29 WRKYs and diverse families. These findings highlight the dynamic transcriptional regulation under varying conditions (Figure S1).

# Quantitative metabolome analysis and functional annotation of differential metabolites

To analyse the metabolites of L. bicolor seedlings under salt stress, a comprehensive targeted metabolome analysis was conducted using an LC-ESI-MS/MS system. A total of 817 metabolites were identified across all samples (Figure 4a, Table S2). These metabolites were clearly separated into three distinct groups based on PCA analysis (Figure 4b), indicating significant differences in metabolite profiles of L. bicolor at different levels of NaCl concentration. # Integrated transcriptome and metabolome analysis

To investigate the intricate interplay between DEGs and differentially accumulated metabolites (DAMs) in L. bicolor subjected to salt stress, comprehensive co-expression network analyses have been carried out. These analyses employed rigorous thresholds (Pearson correlation coefficient ${ > } 0 . 8$ , P-value $< 0 . 0 1$ ), to ensure statistical significance and biological relevance. Upon comparing the gene and metabolite profiles across treatment conditions (S0 vs S1, S0 vs S3 and S1 vs S3), a striking observation was that many DEGs showed robust positive correlations with DAMs (as shown in Figure 6a–c). 

In addition, the KEGG enrichment analysis performed on both transcriptome and metabolome data sets revealed the top 25 

significantly enriched metabolic pathways for each of the comparative assessments (Figure 6d–f). Notably, an intriguing overlap emerged, with 12 of these pathways consistently enriched across all treatment conditions. These pathways highlighted key metabolic activities such as ‘flavonoid biosynthesis’, ‘purine metabolism’, ‘glutathione metabolism’, ‘ABC transporter system’, ‘arginine and proline metabolism’, ‘fructose and mannose metabolism’, ‘cyanoamino acid metabolism’, ‘alpha-linolenic acid metabolism’, ‘linoleic acid metabolism’, ‘sulfur metabolism’, ‘thiamine metabolism’, ‘sulfur relay system’ and ‘glucosinolate biosynthesis’ (Figure 6d,e). The combined approach of co-expression network analysis and KEGG enrichment analysis has offered a holistic insight into the intricate interplay between gene expression patterns and metabolite accumulation profiles, elucidating the critical pathways governing the adaptive responses of L. bicolor to NaCl stress. 

Comprehensive transcriptome and metabolome analysis revealed that the key differential genes and metabolites were predominantly concentrated within the ‘phenylpropanoid biosynthesis’ and ‘flavonoid biosynthesis’ pathways, providing insightful evidence of their critical roles (Figure 7). # Discussion

Wang et al. (2012) showed that for halophyte Suaeda salsa, its fresh weight was markedly stimulated under 100 mM NaCl, 400 mM NaCl and 100 mM KCl treatment. For halophyte Salicornia europaea, the optimal growth was observed

(2014) showed that the aboveground dry weight of Tamarix chinensis (a kind of recretohalophyte) seedlings reached the maximum value at the salt content of $0 . 4 \%$ . Like other halophytes, L. bicolor grew best at a specific salt concentration (100 mM NaCl) (Figure 1a). Similarly, growth indices such as dry weight, leaf number, leaf area, shoot height and root length demonstrated the same trend (Figure 1b). The increase in biomass is attributed to the enhancement of photosynthesis (Li et al., 2022; Lv et al., 2012), and the photosynthetic parameters (Pn, Tr and Gs) in this study exhibit the same trend as growth (Figure 1d). Under salt stress, although the recretohalophyte L. bicolor can reduce the ion contents in its body through salt secretion by salt glands, the ion content in its body will still increase which may act as an osmotic regulator to balance the external water potential (Li et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2024). In this study, as the concentration of NaCl increases, the ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + }$ content in leaves gradually increases (Figure 2a). To counterbalance the osmotic stress imposed by a high salt environment, plants not only accumulate inorganic ions but also organic osmotic regulators, L. bicolor accumulated soluble sugars and proteins as well as proline (Figure 2) in order to equilibrate water potential and protect functional macromolecules (Gong et al., 2020; Munns et al., 2020). Too many ions in the tissue lead to metabolic disorders and the explosive production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) (Zhu, 2003). The accumulation of ROS can trigger membrane lipid peroxidation, which not only profoundly affects the structure and function of cell membranes but also directly leads to a significant increase in membrane permeability (Zhu et al., 2024). In this study, at the 

optimal salt concentration of 100 mM NaCl for the growth of L. bicolor, the levels of ROS and MDA, as well as electric conductivity, were low. However, at NaCl concentrations that inhibited growth, the levels of ROS and MDA, as well as electric conductivity, exhibited significant increases (Figure 3a–d), indicating that the plant was damaged. Under normal conditions, the production and removal of ROS are in a dynamic equilibrium state. However, under salt stress, as the accumulation of ROS increases, maintaining their balance requires higher antioxidant enzyme activity and an increased amount of ROS scavengers (Gong et al., 2020). In this study, at the optimal salt concentration for the growth of L. bicolor, the expression of antioxidant enzyme genes is significantly up-regulated, resulting in a marked increase in their activity, which effectively scavenges ROS. However, under high salt concentrations, the opposite result is observed (Figure 2m–t), suggesting the L. bicolor seedlings were subjected to oxidative stress. 

The carbon assimilated through photosynthesis in plant leaves is utilized to form chloroplast starch or is transported to the cytoplasm for the synthesis of sucrose, which is subsequently transported to non-photosynthetic locations within the plant. Different environmental stress factors, such as temperature, light, salt, etc., can affect the distribution of fixed carbon between sucrose and starch in plant leaves (Slewinski and Braun, 2010). Transcriptome data indicated that the KEGG pathways were rich in ‘starch and sucrose metabolites’ (Figure S2), which suggested that starch and sucrose metabolism play an important role in the 

response of L. bicolor to salt stress. In addition, the KEGG pathway of S0 vs S1 is rich in ‘MAPK signalling pathway-plant’ (Figure S2), similar results were observed in other plants (Abulfaraj, 2020; Jalmi and Sinha, 2015). The transcriptome data analysis of Hordeum spontaneum under salt stress showed that an enriched MEKK1 module, such as MEKK1-MKK2-MPK4/6, and two MEKK3 modules, such as MEKK17/18-MKK3-MPK1/2/7/14 and -MKK3-MPK8, could effectively regulate plant tolerance to salt stress (Abulfaraj, 2020). Furthermore, ROS produced by plants under stress conditions activate MAPKs, but the mechanism of ROS-specific activation of MAPK cascades needs to be further investigated (Jalmi and Sinha, 2015). 

When plants are stressed, growth is often limited, but secondary metabolites tend to increase, protecting plants from harm (Endara and Coley, 2011). The KEGG pathways of S0 vs S3 and S1 vs S3 DEG were highly enriched in the ‘glucosinolate biosynthesis’ and ‘phenylpropanoid biosynthesis’ pathways (Figure S2). Environmental factors such as salinity, drought and temperature can alter the composition of glucosinolates, but their physiological role in response to abiotic stresses is unclear (Lopez-P  erez et al., 2009a; Lopez-Berenguer  et al., 2009b; Qasim et al., 2003; Radovich et al., 2005; Velasco et al., 2007). Suppression of aliphatic glucosinolate synthesis by RNA interference (RNAi) in Arabidopsis has been reported to result in significant changes in photosynthesis, oxidative stress and hormone metabolism (Chen et al., 2012). In broccoli, low salt stress (40 mM NaCl) greatly increased the total glucosinolate content of broccoli, but high salt stress (80 mM NaCl) reduced the increase in glucosinolates (Lopez-Berenguer  et al., 2008). These results indicate that primary metabolism and growth are restricted during salt stress, but secondary metabolism and glucosinolate production are not, and the increase in glucosinolate may also be related to the synthesis of osmotic defences (Lopez-  Perez et al., 2009a; Lopez-Berenguer  et al., 2009b). The pathways involved in physiological responses are closely linked to glucosinolate metabolism, which can also be altered by abiotic stresses. Phenylpropanoid metabolism is one of the most important metabolic pathways in plants. Major metabolites include lignin, flavonoids, lignans and phenylpropanoid esters, which contribute to stress resistance (Dong and Lin, 2021). Under salt stress, up-regulation of lignin biosynthesis genes such as C4H, C3H, F5H and 4CL resulted in lignin deposition, secondary cell wall thickening and enhanced salt tolerance of plants (Chen et al., 2019a; Hu et al., 2019). In addition, flavonoids as antioxidants can reduce ROS accumulation caused by salt stress, thereby reducing oxidative damage to plants (Jiang et al., 2016; Nakabayashi and Saito, 2015). For example, overexpression of flavonoid synthesis genes such as CHS and DFR increased the levels of flavonol glycosides and anthocyanins, reduced the accumulation of ROS and improved salt tolerance in plants (Chen et al., 2019b; Cui et al., 2014; Kim et al., 2017). For L. bicolor seedlings under 300 mM NaCl stress, the accumulation of flavonoids is important to reduce the ROS content. In addition to improving stress resistance in plants, glucosinolates and phenylpropanoids also have important medicinal value. Consumption of GSL-containing vegetables (such as broccoli, cabbage, etc.) is beneficial for health, including antibacterial, anticancer, antioxidant and anti-inflammatory functions (Dinkova-Kostova and Kostov, 2012; Mazumder et al., 2016). In medicinal plants, metabolites of the phenylpropanoid metabolic pathway directly or indirectly contribute to almost all natural medicinal constituents containing the phenylpropanoid skeleton and play an irreplaceable role in the prevention and treatment of diseases such as cancer, malaria and asthma (Shang et al., 2022). In 

conclusion, under high salinity stress, L. bicolor can improve salt resistance through secondary metabolites, and its medicinal value is also enhanced. 

Transcription factors (TFs) are the key regulators of plant responses to stress. Transcription factors such as AP2/ERF, WRKY, bHLH, C2H2, bZIP and NAC are involved in response to most abiotic stresses such as salt, drought, high temperature and cold (Chen et al., 2023; Debbarma et al., 2019; Ng et al., 2018; Seo and Choi, 2015). In this study, the TF family with the largest number of members involved in the response to salt stress is the AP2/ERF family (Figure S1). In rice, overexpression of salt-responsive ERF1 (SERF1) enhances salt tolerance in rice, and SERF1 is involved in the regulation of the MAPK signalling pathway (Schmidt et al., 2013). WRKY family members play an important role in plant resistance to salt stress, and previous studies have shown that there are 63 WRKY transcription factors in L bicolor, which regulate salt tolerance in L. bicolor by affecting salt secretion and ROS clear ability (Zhu et al., 2024). The MYB TFs and the MBW ternary complex, which is composed of R2R3-MYB TFs, basic helix–loop–helix (bHLH) TFs and WD40 protein, play a crucial regulatory role in the phenylpropanoid pathway. The MBW complex and MYB TFs are controlled by NAC TFs, WRKY TFs, bZIP TFs and other self-regulatory and upstream TFs (Dong and Lin, 2021). In particular, MYB proteins are both transcriptional activators and inhibitors in the regulation of phenylpropanoid metabolism (Bhargava et al., 2010; Zhou et al., 2009). In Arabidopsis, MYB28, MYB29, MYB34, MYB51, MYB122 and other MYB transcription factors are involved in glucosinolate biosynthesis to enhance its stress resistance (Harun et al., 2020). 

The phenylpropanoid pathway generates unique phenolic compounds, such as sinapate esters, lignin and flavonoids, through a series of reactions. These substances play a crucial role in plant resistance to adversity (Huang et al., 2009; Kim et al., 2007; Zhang et al., 2023). Flavonoids are polyphenolic compounds destined for vacuoles, sharing a common C6–C3–C6 backbone (Marinova et al., 2007). Flavonoids are one of the most important secondary metabolites of plants. At present, more than 8000 types of flavonoids have been found, which can be divided into flavonoids, isoflavones, chalcones and anthocyanins, etc., which can reduce a variety of stress injuries (such as ultraviolet, drought, salt, etc.) on plants (Wang et al., 2024a, 2024b; Zhuang et al., 2023). In this study, under salt treatment, phenolic compounds, flavonoids and organic acids increased significantly (Figure 5), flavonoids content increased, the first two substances are antioxidants and can scavenge free radicals (Robak and Gryglewski, 1988). In this study, under salt stress, the key genes (CHS, F3H ) were up-regulated, and their metabolites, including pinobanksin, naringenin, naringenin chalcone and eriodictyol chalcone, exhibited significant increase (Figure 7). In soybeans, the accumulation of flavonoid glycosides and isoflavones increased under salt stress, which improved the antioxidant capacity and osmotic stress tolerance of soybeans, indicating that the accumulation of flavonoids could improve the tolerance of plants to salt stress (Yan et al., 2014). Under salt stress, the differential accumulation of flavonoid content in the leaves and roots of Solenostemma argel improved the antioxidant capacity and osmotic adjustment ability of plants under salt stress (Ahmad et al., 2021). Rathore and Chaudhary (2019) suggested that flavonoids interact with biofilms to reduce the mobility of oxidative factors and prevent the diffusion of free radicals, thereby increasing their resistance to oxidative factors. The results indicate that the ‘phenylpropanoid biosynthesis’ and ‘flavonoid biosynthesis’ pathways play crucial roles of L. bicolor in response to the oxidative stress and osmotic stress caused by salt stress. # Materials and methods

The seeds of L. bicolor collected from salt-alkaline land in the Yellow River Delta (Dongying City, Shandong Province, China) were kept at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The full, uniform seeds were sown in pots filled with moist vermiculite and cultivated under $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ conditions with $1 5 ~ \mathrm { h }$ light/9 h dark and the humidity was maintained at $6 5 \%$ . When the first true leaf appeared, the seedlings transplanted into plastic pots $1 0 \ \mathsf { c m } \times 1 0 \ \mathsf { c m } \times \ 1 3 \ \mathsf { c m } )$ filled with sand (1 plant/pot) and were irrigated with Hoagland’s nutrient solution. At the six-leaf stage, the salt treatment was initiated by exposing the seedlings to Hoagland’s solution supplemented with varying concentrations of NaCl: 0 mM (S0, control), $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ (S1), $2 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ (S2), 300 mM (S3) and $4 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ (S4). The physiological 

indices were measured after 2 weeks of treatment. To ensure statistical significance, each treatment group comprised 10 biological replicates. The results of the physiological indices showed that compared with S0, S1 significantly promoted the growth of L. bicolor, whereas S3 significantly inhibited the growth. Therefore, S0, S1 and S3 treatment groups were selected for transcriptome and metabolome analysis. 

# Measurement of the growth data

The plant height and root length of the seedlings were measured using a millimetre ruler. The number of leaves was counted and the leaf area was calculated using ImageJ software. The shoots and roots were placed in an oven at $1 0 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 10–15 min and then baked in an oven at ${ } ^ { - 8 0 } { } ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ to a constant weight, then were weighed. 

# Measurement of chlorophyll content and photosynthetic parameters

The expanded leaves at the same leaf position were used to measure the photosynthetic parameters using a Li-6000 portable photosynthesis instrument (LI-COR Inc., Lincoln, NE, USA), including net photosynthetic rate $( \mathsf { P n } )$ , transpiration rate (Tr), stomatal conductance (Gs) and intercellular carbon dioxide concentration (Ci). Thirty biological replicates were established in each group. 

Leaf fragments of $0 . 3 \ 9$ were taken from expanded leaves at the same position and added to 2 mL of dimethyl sulfoxide. Then mixture was incubated at $6 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ until the green colour of the leaves faded and the chlorophyll dissolved in the solution. After cooling and filtration, the filtrate obtained is the chlorophyll extract solution, 8 mL of acetone was added to the chlorophyll solution. Subsequently, the absorbance was measured 

at 645 nm and 663 nm, respectively. The chlorophyll content was calculated according to the method described by Zhu et al. (2024). 

# Determination the ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + }$ , ${ \sf K } ^ { + }$ , ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O } _ { 2 } , { \sf O } _ { 2 } ^ { \cdot - }$ , MDA and proline contents

The $0 . 3 \ 9$ of leaves, collected from identical leaf positions, were subjected to nitration using concentrated nitric acid. Then diluted with deionized water to a final volume of 5 mL. The concentrations of ${ \mathsf { N a } } ^ { + }$ and ${ \sf K } ^ { + }$ ions were quantitatively assessed using a flame photometer (JC-YZ-600, China). 

To extract biochemical components, 1 g of leaves was pulverized in liquid nitrogen and homogenized with 9 mL of phosphate buffer. The resulting mixture was vigorously shaken and centrifuged at 4000 rpm for 10 min at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . The clarified supernatant was collected for subsequent determination of hydrogen peroxide $( \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 } )$ , superoxide anion radical $( 0 ^ { 2 \cdots } )$ , malondialdehyde (MDA) and proline levels, following the methodologies detailed by Zhu et al. (2024), with the aid of assay kits sourced from the Nanjing Jiancheng Bioengineering Institute, Nanjing, China. 

The electrical conductivity of the leaves was measured following an adapted protocol inspired by Han et al. (2022). Leaf discs of 1 cm diameter were punched out, with each set of samples comprising five discs. Three replicate groups were established. The discs were rinsed with double-distilled water $( \mathsf { d d H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } )$ , sealed in test tubes containing $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of ${ \mathsf { d d H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ , and incubated on a rotary shaker maintained at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for $2 4 ~ \mathrm { h }$ . The conductivity of this solution was recorded as L1. Subsequently, the samples were subjected to autoclaving at $1 2 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m i n }$ , followed by equilibration at $2 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . The conductivity of the post-autoclaving solution was noted as L2. The electrical conductivity of the plant leaves was then calculated as a percentage using the formula: EC $( \% ) = ( \lfloor 1 / \lfloor 2 ) \times 1 0 0$ . 

The soluble sugar content was determined following the methods described by Zhu et al. (2024), employing anthrone colorimetry. Briefly, $0 . 3 \ 9$ of leaves from various treatment conditions was rinsed with ${ \mathsf { d o l } } { \mathsf { H } } { } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } } _ { 2 }$ , blotted dry with filter paper and hydrated with $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of ${ \mathsf { d d H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ . After boiling in a water bath for $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m i n }$ , the samples were cooled, and the volume was adjusted to $2 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ with ${ \mathsf { d o l } } { \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ . For each $0 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ aliquot of this diluent, $1 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of ${ \mathsf { d o l H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ , 0.5 mL of anthrone dissolved in ethyl acetate and 5 mL of concentrated sulfuric acid were added. The mixture was thoroughly mixed and heated for 1 min. The soluble sugar content of the samples was then interpolated from a standard curve. For the quantification of soluble protein, 0.3 g of L. bicolor leaves from different treatment groups were washed with ${ \mathsf { d o l } } { \mathsf { H } } _ { 2 } { \mathsf { O } }$ and dried with filter paper. The leaf samples were homogenized on ice in 3 mL of $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m M }$ phosphate buffer (adjusted to pH 7.0) and centrifuged at $1 5 \ 0 0 0 \ g$ for 10 min at ga low temperature to separate the soluble proteins from cellular debris. To $1 0 0 ~ \mu \iota$ of the resulting supernatant, 1 mL of Coomassie Brilliant Blue G-250 reaction reagent was added. The mixture was vigorously shaken and allowed to stand for a few minutes to facilitate protein–dye binding. The optical density (OD) at 595 nm $( \mathsf { O D } _ { 5 9 5 } )$ was then measured spectrophotometrically. Finally, the soluble protein content was calculated using a standard curve, which relates $\mathsf { O D } _ { 4 0 0 }$ to known protein concentrations. 

The quantification of carbohydrate components, specifically glucose, fructose and sucrose, was conducted in accordance with the manufacturer’s detailed guidelines. A fresh 0.1 g sample of 

leaves, was immersed in a 5mL solution of $80 \%$ ethanol. This mixture was then subjected to centrifugation at $8 0 0 0 \ { \mathfrak { g } }$ for a gduration of 10 min, resulting in the separation of the supernatant, which was subsequently stored at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ for subsequent analytical procedures. To further process the supernatant, it was incubated for a period of $4 8 ~ \mathsf { h }$ at a temperature of $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ in a solution comprising acetate buffer (concentration: $4 . 5 ~ \mathsf { m M } )$ ), water and $\textsf { \textsf { X } }$ -glucoamylase (concentration: $0 . 5 \%$ , w/v). Following this incubation, the final concentrations of glucose, fructose and sucrose were determined using spectrophotometric methods at wavelengths of 505 nm, 480 nm and $4 8 0 ~ \mathsf { n m }$ , respectively. The results for glucose contents were expressed in micromoles per gram of fresh weight $\left( \mu \mathrm { m o l } / \ g \mathsf { F W } \right)$ , whereas those for fructose and sucrose were reported in milligrams per gram of fresh weight (mg/g FW), consistent with the methodologies employed by Zhang et al. (2022) and Iqbal et al. (2023). 

The enzymatic activities of superoxide dismutase (SOD), peroxidase (POD), ascorbate peroxidase (APX) and catalase (CAT) were assessed using the standardized protocols outlined by Ali et al. (2020). For this purpose, $1 . 0 \ 9$ of leaf tissue was meticulously homogenized in $1 0 ~ \mathsf { m L }$ of chilled extraction buffer, consisting of $0 . 0 5 ~ \mathsf { m o l } \Lambda$ phosphate buffer adjusted to a pH of 7.8. The homogenate was then subjected to centrifugation at $8 0 0 0 \ { \pmb q }$ for 15 min at $4 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ , enabling the separation of the gsupernatant, which was subsequently collected for the quantification of enzyme activities as per the methodology described by Zhu et al. (2024). The enzymatic activities were expressed in terms of units per gram of protein (U/g protein), providing a quantitative measure of the antioxidant enzyme capacities present within the leaf tissue samples. 

# Sampling, extraction and transcriptome sequencing for RNA-Seq analysis

Nine samples (S0, S1 and $S 3 \times 3$ biological replications) were prepared for transcriptome sequencing. Total RNA was extracted using the Takara Kit, ensuring high quality. RNA integrity and absence of DNA contamination were initially assessed via $1 \%$ agarose gel electrophoresis, verifying intact ribosomal RNA bands. RNA purity was evaluated using a NanoPhotometer spectrophotometer by measuring 260/280 and 260/230 nm absorbance ratios. For precise RNA quantification, the Qubit 2.0 Fluorometer was employed, utilizing fluorescent dye-based assays. Finally, RNA integrity was comprehensively analysed using the Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer, providing RIN scores and electropherograms to confirm sample suitability for sequencing. 

Following the manufacturer’s guidelines, sequencing libraries were crafted with the NEB Next Ultra RNA library Prep Kit for Illumina, incorporating unique index codes for sample identification. mRNA fragmentation was induced using a fragmentation buffer, facilitating cDNA synthesis via RNase H and DNA polymerase I. Subsequently, the Genomic Sample Kit transformed these cDNA fragments into libraries, focusing on the optimal length range. Unwanted fragments were eliminated through QIAquick PCR Extraction and AMPure XP beads, culminating in PCR-amplified sequencing libraries. The multiplexed libraries, normalized to $1 0 ~ \mathsf { n M }$ , underwent quality control with pico green staining, fluorospectrophotometry, and quantification by Agilent 2100. Sequencing was executed on the Illumina HiSeq platform, generating raw reads processed by Illumina’s pipeline. These raw reads were refined by discarding low-quality bases and empty sequences, resulting in clean data. Alignments to the Arabidopsis thaliana reference genome facilitated expression analysis, 

employing FPKM (fragments per kilobase of exon per million mapped reads) and differential expression analysis with DESeq R package at an FDR threshold of 0.05. Functional annotation of DEGs was conducted via Blast2GO for GO terms and the Automatic Annotation Server for KEGG pathways. 

# Metabolomics analysis

The experimental set-up integrates an advanced Ultra-Performance Liquid Chromatography (UPLC, model SHIMADZU Nexera X2) and a Tandem Mass Spectrometry (MS/MS, model Applied Biosystems 4500 QTRAP) system, ensuring high sensitivity and resolution for compound detection and analysis. The liquid chromatography conditions are meticulously configured with an Agilent SB-C18 column $( 1 . 8 ~ { \mu \mathrm { m } } , 2 . 1 ~ \mathsf { m m } \times 1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m m } )$ for superior separation and stability. The mobile phases consist of ultrapure water with $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid (A) for pH adjustment and ionization enhancement, and acetonitrile with $0 . 1 \%$ formic acid (B) as the organic solvent. An optimized gradient elution program initiates at $5 \%$ B, linearly increasing to $9 5 \%$ B over 9 min, held for 1 min for complete elution, then rapidly returns to $5 \%$ B and equilibrates until 14 min, preparing the column for the next injection. The flow rate is set at 0.35 mL/min for optimal separation and peak shape, while the column temperature is maintained at $4 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ for stability and longevity. Each injection of $4 ~ \mu \iota$ ensures thorough column exposure and efficient separation. The differential metabolites were identified by contrasting fragmentation patterns, retention times and m/z values against both in-house and public databases (Cao et al., 2015). Utilizing the OPLS-DA model, we determined variable importance in projection (VIP) scores (Phahlane et al., 2022). Metabolites exhibiting VIP scores exceeding 1.0, a $\mathsf { l o g } _ { 2 }$ fold change greater than 1 and a $P .$ -value below 0.05 were classified as significantly altered. 

# Statistical analysis

All experimental data were visualized using Sigmaplot 12.5 and analysis of variance (two-way ANOVA) was performed using IBM SPSS Statistics 26.0. A difference of $P < 0 . 0 5$ was considered significant. Each of the above experimental results is calculated from three replicates.
