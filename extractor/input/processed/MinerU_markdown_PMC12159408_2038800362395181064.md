# Abstract

Background: Owing to the economic relevance of resistance evolution against herbicides, new chemical entities addressing unprecedented molecular targets are urgently needed to develop future sustainable weed control solutions. As part of our discovery research, the new class of aminoisothiazolamides was investigated. 

Results: Aminoisothiazolamide 3-amino-4-chloro-N-(cyclohexylmethyl)isothiazole-5-carboxamide 1a and several of its derivatives displayed potent herbicidal and fungicidal in vivo activity in initial glasshouse tests. Lysyl-tRNA synthetase 1 (KRS1) was identified as the putative target for 1a and was validated as a key contributor to the biochemical mode-of-action of aminoisothiazolamides. Thermal stability shift analysis with KRS1 from Arabidopsis thaliana (AtKRS1) revealed that 1a specifically increased the thermostability of this enzyme, proving the KRS1 enzyme as the aminoisothiazolamide target. It turned out that the inhibition of AtKRS1 and HsKRS was strongly correlated, as was the inhibition of AtKRS1 and the herbicidal activity of the aminoisothiazolamides. Hence, in vivo acute toxicity tests were initiated at a very early project stage complementing the enzyme tests. 

Conclusion: The observed toxicological effects paired with the anticipated likelihood to overcome this problem, owing to the highly conserved active sites in different species, finally resulted in the conclusion to stop the further exploration of the otherwise promising class of herbicidal aminoisothiazolamides. Thus, we opted to discard several further herbicidal lead structures before the start of in-depth investigations when sequence analyses suggested similar levels of conservation between the respective binding pockets in plants and mammalians.

Its competitive ability is not only attributed to a fast growth rate,7 but also to remarkable light interception, an outstanding water-use efficiency, and to its high aptness to evolve target-site and metabolic resistance towards established herbicides. Emerging resistance towards herbicidal active ingredients is not limited to dicotyledon weeds in warm season crops. Grass weeds such as black-grass (Alopecurus myosuroides) and ryegrass (Lolium species) can significantly reduce wheat, rye and barley yields, owing to their potent competition for nutrients and related natural resources.8 Black-grass has been successfully controlled for some time by herbicides inhibiting acetyl-CoA-carboxylase (ACCase, HRAC group 1) or ALS, whilst ryegrass has been controlled by herbicides inhibiting very long-chain fatty acid synthesis (VLCFA, HRAC group 15). Consequently, it is essential that crop protection research acts rapidly to provide farmers with new solutions that enable them to fight back against resistant weed species, for example by investigating pre-emergence herbicides or noncommercialized MoAs.3,10 New molecular targets and MoAs not only represent valuable tools in the increasingly difficult fight against resistant weeds, but often result in unique herbicidal activity profiles that build the basis for truly innovative and sustainable weed control products. Accordingly, it is not surprising that the discovery of any new chemical lead structure with a new MoA belongs to the highest priorities of weed control research. One such rare breakthrough that combines a new MoA with a new chemical scaffold is the isothiazolamide 3-amino-4-chloro-N-(cyclohexylmethyl)isothiazole-5-carboxamide 1a, which originated from exploratory fungicidal chemistry, but displayed significant phytotoxic adverse effects. As compounds that are synthesized in one indication are routinely fed into our in vivo high throughput screening cascades of other indications, it quickly became clear that 1a had a high potential to serve as a promising herbicidal lead structure. In glasshouse trials it showed remarkably broad herbicidal activity, covering both grassy and broadleaf weeds with promising hints at crop selectivity in cereals and corn. Using a Saccharomyces cerevisiae (Sc) overexpression library, lysyltRNA synthetase 1 (ScKRS1) was identified as the putative target for 1a. Inhibition of higher plant KRS by isothiazolamides was confirmed using a biochemical activity assay for AtKRS exploiting the noncanonical function of the enzyme, the synthesis of $\mathsf { A p } _ { 4 } \mathsf { A }$ . The target identification was corroborated further by X-ray examination of the co-crystal structures of isothiazolamides and AtKRS. 

Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases (aaRSs) are an ancient family of enzymes evolutionarily conserved in all life forms, that play the key role in translating nucleotide sequences of the genetic code to amino acid sequences during protein biosynthesis.11 Their canonical function is to catalyze the specific attachment of an amino acid to its cognate transfer ribonucleic acid (tRNA). Aminoacylation is a two-step process in which amino acids are first activated by ATP, forming an aminoacyl adenylate intermediate and pyrophosphate (PP ). In the second step, the amino acid is transferred to the $3 ^ { \prime }$ -end of the cognate tRNA and AMP is released. The charged tRNAs are transported to the ribosome, where the amino acid is transferred to a growing polypeptide chain. To obtain the high fidelity of tRNA charging required in 

protein synthesis, 20 different types of aa-tRNA are made by 20 different aaRSs, one for each amino acid of the genetic code. In mammalian cells, nine aaRSs (E, P, I, L, M, Q, K, R and D) reside in the cytoplasmic high molecular mass multisynthetase complex (MSC, ∼1.5 MDa), together with three accessory proteins. The MSC is in part a reservoir of regulatory factors that are based on the alternative, noncanonical functions of the aaRSs. These diverse processes are tightly regulated through post-transcriptional modifications, interactions with other proteins and targeting to the various cell compartments. For example, KRS gained additional functions in transcriptional regulation, cytokine-like signaling, transport of proteins to the cell membrane and human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) replication. Defects in either canonical or noncanonical KRS functions are associated with human diseases, including sensorineural hearing and visual loss, neuropathy, seizures and leukodystrophy.12 The function of KRS in transcriptional regulation requires a second catalytic activity of the enzyme to produce a signaling molecule, diadenosine tetraphosphate $( \mathsf { A p } _ { 4 } \mathsf { A } )$ . In vivo, this functional switch is achieved by phosphorylation of a single serine residue. Phosphorylation leads to a conformational change from a ‘closed’ form, that catalyzes amino acid acylation for protein synthesis, to an ‘open’ conformer, which catalyzes $\mathsf { A p } _ { 4 } \mathsf { A }$ formation. The formation of $\mathsf { A p } _ { 4 } \mathsf { A }$ occurs in a tRNALys-independent two-step reaction. Lysine is first activated by ATP, forming a lysine-AMP intermediate and pyrophosphate. In the second step, a reaction with a second ATP molecule results in the formation of $\mathsf { A p } _ { 4 } \mathsf { A }$ and lysine. The overall reaction is strongly stimulated by the presence of inorganic pyrophosphatase, which prevents the back-reaction by cleavage of ${ \mathsf { P P } } _ { \mathrm { i } }$ to yield two molecules of inorganic Pi per molecule of $\mathsf { A p } _ { 4 } \mathsf { A }$ formed. $\mathsf { A p } _ { 4 } \mathsf { A }$ acts as a regulator in response to stress to maintain cellular homeostasis.13 KRS has gained a lot of attention in the pharmaceutical sector as a potential target for drugs against for example malaria (Plasmodium falciparum),14–20 sleeping sickness (Trypanosoma brucei),21 cryptosporidiosis (Cryptosporidium sp.), loiasis (african eye worm Loa loa), human schistosomiasis (Schistosoma sp.), tuberculosis (Mycobacterium tuberculosis) 18,19,22 and bacterial infections (Staphylococcus aureus).23 Drug discovery efforts have shown that several classes of inhibitors selective for the respective parasite and bacteria enzymes can be obtained (Fig. 1), for example cladosporin $\pmb { 2 } , ^ { 1 4 - 1 6 }$ chromene-2-carboxamides 3a-b, 17,18 1,2-dihydro-3H-pyrrolo[3,4-c]pyridin-3-one 4a,19 6,7-dihydro-5H-pyrrolo [3,4-d]pyrimidin-5-one 4b and substituted proline amides 5a-b. 20,21 

Whilst the well-studied mammalian and bacterial KRS enzymes have been proven to serve as valuable targets for antimicrobial drug discovery, much less is known about the corresponding plant enzymes. In plants, protein synthesis occurs in all three cellular compartments: the cytosol, the mitochondria and the chloroplasts. Each compartment requires a full set of aaRSs, which are all nuclear encoded and posttranslationally targeted to their respective compartments. Because the Arabidopsis thaliana genome contains only 45 genes for aaRSs, many of them have to be targeted to at least two of these three cellular compartments. Despite the large number of aaRSs present in plants cells, reports on inhibitors of these enzymes, especially those with herbicidal activity, are unusually rare, prompting us to explore options for identifying selective herbicides.

# 2.1.1 Purification of AtKRS1 and AtKRS2

Synthetic genes encoding the protein sequences of AtKRS1 and AtKRS2 (UniProt ID Q9ZPI1 and Q9LJE2, respectively) were synthesized by GeneArt and cloned into the Novagen vector pET19. The resulting vectors were transformed into E. coli Bl21 (DE3). For the expression of the AtKRS proteins as N-terminally his-tagged fusion proteins (MGHHHHHHHHHHSSGHIDDDDKH in front of the KRS start methionine), the bacteria were grown at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ in Luria–bertani (LB) medium containing $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ carbenicillin, induced with 1 mM IPTG at optical density at 600 nm $( \mathrm { O D } _ { 6 0 0 } )$ $= 0 . 5$ and further cultivated at $1 6 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ . After $1 6 ~ \mathrm { h }$ the bacteria were harvested by centrifugation. For thermal stability shift analyses, the KRS enzymes were purified with the Ni-NTA Fast Start Kit according to the instructions of the manufacturer (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany). To obtain KRS enzymes for activity assays, bacterial pellets were resuspended in $2 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ Tris–HCl buffer $\mathsf { p H } 7 . 8$ containing $1 ~ { \mu \mathrm { L } } ~ { \mathsf { m L } } ^ { - 1 }$ benzonase/nuclease $( 2 5 \mathsf { U } \mu \mathsf { L } ^ { - 1 }$ ; Novagen, Madison, WI, USA) and $2 \mu \mathrm { L } \mathsf { m } \mathsf { L } ^ { - 1 }$ protease inhibitor cocktail III (Calbiochem, San Diego, CA, USA) at a concentration of 1 g wet weight per 5 mL buffer, and disrupted with an IMA-Disintegrator S glass-bead disintegrator. # 2.1.3 Thermal stability shift analysis

KRS melting curves were determined in 96-well microtiter plates in a Light Cycler 480 (Roche, Basel, Switzerland). The assay mixture contained $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ HEPES pH 7.5, 75 mM NaCl, $7 . 5 \times$ SYPRO Orange and $4 \mu \mu$ KRS in a total volume of $2 0 \mu \ L$ . Melting temperatures were determined from plots of the first derivative of the fluorescence intensity versus temperature. 

# 2.1.4 KRS activity assays

KRS activities were determined by measuring the noncanonical formation of diadenosine tetraphosphate $( A p _ { 4 } A )$ . Assays were performed in 384-well microtiter plates. The reaction mixture contained in a total volume of $2 0 ~ \mu \iota$ 50 mM Tris–HCl pH 7.8, 10 mM ${ \sf M g } { \sf C l } _ { 2 } ,$ 150 mM KCl, $1 5 0 \mu \mathrm { M } \ Z \mathsf { n C l } _ { 2 } ,$ 1 mM L-lysine, $7 5 ~ \mu \mu$ ATP (Sigma A3377), 0.1 U inorganic pyrophosphatase (PPase, Sigma I1643) and $2 \mu \ g$ BSA. The reaction was started by the addition of $2 . 5 ~ \mu \ g$ purified KRS enzyme. After 60 min incubation at $3 7 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ the reaction was terminated by the addition of $5 0 ~ \mu \ L$ phosphate detection reagent. After a further 30 min incubation at room temperature (RT), the $O D _ { 6 2 0 }$ was determined. The AtKRS1 activity was calculated against a blank assay without L-lysine. Phosphate detection reagent: $1 5 \mathrm { m L }$ of a $0 . 0 2 2 5 \%$ solution of malachite green in water is mixed with 5 mL of a $1 \%$ solution of ammonium molybdate $[ ( \mathsf { N H } _ { 4 } ) _ { 6 } \mathsf { M o } _ { 7 } \mathsf { O } _ { 2 4 } \cdot 4 \mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathsf { O } ]$ prepared in 4 N HCl. In line with protein purification and activity measurements in other studies the N-terminal His-tag was not cleaved off. 

# 2.1.5 Herbicidal glasshouse screening

Seeds of mono- and dicotyledonous weed plants and crop plants were sown, in plastic or organic planting pots, in sandy loam and covered with soil (replicates: $n = 1 0 .$ , i.e. 10 monocotyledonous weed seeds were grown per pot; $n = 5$ , i.e. five dicotyledonous weed seeds were grown per pot). The aminoisothiazolamides described above (1a–w and 23a–r), formulated in the form of wettable powders (WP) or as emulsifiable concentrates (EC), were applied to the surface of the covering soil as aqueous suspension or emulsion, with the addition of $0 . 5 \%$ of an additive, at an application rate of 600 L water per ha (converted). Following 

treatment, the pots were placed in a glasshouse and kept under optimum growth conditions for the test plants. The test plants were placed in the glasshouse for approx. 3 weeks under optimum growth conditions, and then the effect of the preparations was assessed visually in comparison with untreated controls (herbicidal effect: $100 \%$ effect $=$ all plants have died off, $0 \%$ effect $=$ as untreated control plants). Advanced screening was carried out with three replicate pots and a standardized number of seeds per pot depending on the plant species (five seeds for corn and 10 seeds for the wheat spectrum). # 2.2 X-ray crystallography and mModelling

# 2.2.1 X-ray crystallography

Expression and purification of AtKRS1 was carried out according to the protocols outlined above. For structural studies, residues 84–611 of AtKRS1 (UniProt ID Q9ZPI1) were cloned into the Novagen vector pET21a. The expression construct contained an N-terminal his-tag to facilitate purification and a TEV protease cleavage site to remove the tag. The protein was expressed in E. coli Bl21 (DE3) and purified by IMAC on Ni-NTA agarose. The purification tag was removed by cleavage with TEV protease overnight. Uncleaved protein and tagged protease were removed by subtractive IMAC. The sample was further purified by size exclusion chromatography and concentrated to $6 \mathrm { m g } \mathrm { m } \mathrm { L } ^ { - 1 }$ in $5 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ Tris pH 8, 200 mM NaCl, 2 mM DTT, 1 mM L-lysine. Before crystallization, 1 mM inhibitor was added from a $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ stock solution in DMSO. The protein was crystallized by sitting drop vapor diffusion at 291 K by mixing $2 ~ \mu \iota$ protein solution with $2 ~ \mu \iota$ crystallization solution and equilibration against a $5 0 0 \mathrm { - \mu L }$ reservoir. The crystallization solution contained $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ MES, pH 6.0, $1 0 0 ~ \mathsf { m m }$ calcium acetate and $1 6 - 2 1 \%$ (w/v) PEG 8000. Crystals were cryoprotected by addition of $2 5 \%$ glycerol and flash cooled in liquid nitrogen. Diffraction data were collected on a XCalibur Nova system (Oxford Diffraction, Abingdon, UK). Phases were determined by molecular replacement with MOLREP33 using HsKRS (pdb entry 3BJU)34 as a search model. The structure was refined by iterative 

cycles of manual and automatic refinement using COOT35 and REFMAC36, respectively. More details about data collection and refinement statistics are provided in the Supporting information Data S1.

# 2.3 Toxicological investigations

# 2.3.1 Human target safety assessment (TSA)

When a target of interest has been identified within Bayer's agrochemical research, an in-depth review based on literature and knowledge databases is created typically in order to evaluate the potential consequences of modulating this target in human and classical tox species; this is termed a target safety assessment (TSA).39 We adapted the TSA concept from the pharma industry to address our specific needs in the agrochemical industry by starting with the identification of an interspecies similarity based on the amino acid sequence of the respective target as the major adaption. That adaption aims at identifying the most similar proteins in rodents and humans. The document usually includes different sections covering the target biology in human (gene, protein, function, pathway, expression profile, tissue distribution), human genetic phenotypes, transgenic animal genotypes and phenotypes [knock-out (KO), mutants], pre-clinical and clinical data. Based on the data the potential safety risks and its associated mitigation plans are routinely described. We have introduced the sequence alignments of amino acids pointing into the binding site of isothiazolamide (cf. Table 3) in our manuscript. 

# 2.3.2 Acute toxicity testing

In order to meet human safety requirements an initial evaluation of acute toxicity is carried out routinely before investigate new chemical compounds in field trials. Accordingly, an acute rat oral toxicity screen (AROS) or acute mouse oral toxicity screen (AMOS) is conducted. As most safety studies are conducted in rats, AROS is the preferred model when a sufficient amount of a compound is available. # 3 RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

# 3.1 Herbicidal activity glasshouse tests

Replacing the aminothiazole moiety in fungicidal lead structure 7, affording also promising control of oomycetes, by an isothiazole group afforded aminoisothiazolamide 1a that showed only weak fungicidal effects, yet surprisingly strong herbicidal activity within our initial glasshouse tests. To validate this remarkable shift of in vivo efficacy, lead structure 1a was investigated in a dedicated advanced pre-emergence glasshouse test in comparison with two commercially relevant standards, that is diflufenican, an inhibitor of phytoene desaturase (PDS, HRAC group 12), and flufenacet acting via inhibition of very-long-chain fatty acid elongases (VLCFA, HRAC group 15). Aminoisothiazolamide 1a showed broad and strong control of the tested spectrum of cold season monocotyledon and dicotyledon weeds. Its overall efficacy surpassed both standards, albeit paired with severe crop damage in sugar beet and oilseed rape, as well as higher damage in wheat and barley at the higher dose rates. However, the results observed for wheat and barley at the two lowest dose rates of 50 and $2 5 \ g \ h a ^ { - 1 }$ provided clear hints for crop selectivity in cereals (Fig. 2). These promising initial findings prompted us to carry out a second test focusing on warm season crops and weeds. Remarkably, aminoisothiazolamide 1a exhibited good control of several relevant weeds in line with a promising crop safety in corn and soybeans (Fig. 3). # 3.2 MoA elucidation and assay development

Considering that aminoisothiazolamide 1a showed broad spectrum fungicidal activity and had been obtained by scaffoldhopping from the SDH inhibitor aminothiazolamide 7, we were intrigued by its oomyceticidal activity which is untypical of SDH inhibitors.40 Having ruled out the most prominent marketed fungicidal MoAs (e.g. multisite activity, inhibition of the respiratory chain and inhibition of sterol biosynthesis), we decided to further investigate 1a to elucidate its biochemical target. Because several analogues of aminoisothiazolamide 1a inhibited growth of baker's yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae (Sc), they were screened against a comprehensive Sc gene overexpression library.41 This resulted in the identification of several strains exhibiting reduced sensitivity to analogues of 1a, and later characterized as sharing an overexpression of ScKRS1. Because ScKRS1 overexpression alone was sufficient to increase yeast tolerance to analogues of 

1a, ScKRS1 was validated as a key contributor to the biochemical MoA and a potential target of aminoisothiazolamides in saccharomycetes. Knowing the function of KRS1 as a key step of protein biosynthesis, this finding was in accordance with previous observations that analogues of 1a inhibit phenylalanine incorporation into proteins. Aiming at confirming the inhibition of KRS as the cause for the herbicidal activity of 1a, we performed thermal shift assays with highly purified Arabidopsis thaliana KRS1 (AtKRS1). When tested at a concentration of $1 0 \ \mu \mathrm { M } ,$ , 1a shifted the melting point (deltaTm) by $9 . 3 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } ,$ , whereas any one of 17 different herbicidal compounds tested in comparison induced shifts of the melting point from $- 2 . 0 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ to $0 . 8 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C }$ (Fig. 4). A $\mathsf { K } _ { \mathsf { d } }$ -value of $6 . 4 \pm 3 . 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ for binding of 1a to AtKRS1 was determined from a plot of deltaTm versus 1a concentration [Fig. 5(a)]. With the thermal shift assay, the $\mathsf { K } _ { \mathsf { d } }$ -value for the AtKRS1 substrate L-lysine was determined as 37.0 $\pm ~ 1 0 . 1 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ (Fig. 5b), whereas the second substrate, ATP, did not induce a shift of the melting point. Interestingly, when the binding of 1a to AtKRS1 was analyzed in the presence of $3 0 ~ \mu \mu \mu$ L-lysine, a $\mathsf { K } _ { \mathsf { d } }$ -value of $1 . 6 \pm 0 . 9 \mu \mathrm { m }$ and a maximum of $2 0 . 6 \pm 0 . 9 ^ { \circ } C$ were determined [Fig. 5(c)]. The significantly decreased $\mathsf { K } _ { \mathsf { d } }$ -value and the increased deltaTm suggest that 1a binds to the ATP binding site of AtKRS1.

As eukaryotic KRS enzymes are evolutionary highly conserved, we next investigated whether aminoisothiazolamides show some specificity for plant enzymes over their mammalian counterparts by performing thermal shift assays with highly purified Homo sapiens KRS (HsKRS). The $\mathsf { K } _ { \mathsf { d } }$ -value for the substrate L-lysine was determined as $3 5 . 7 \pm 0 . 3 \mu \mathrm { M }$ [Fig. 5(d)] and 1a was found to bind to HsKRS in the presence of $3 0 \mu \mathrm { m } \iota$ -lysine with a $\mathsf { K } _ { \mathsf { d } }$ -value of $1 9 . 9 ~ \mu \mathrm { m }$ and to induce a maximum shift of the melting point of $2 6 . 5 ~ ^ { \circ } \mathsf C$ . For HsKRS phosphorylation of the Ser207 results in a functional switch of the catalytic activity from tRNA acylation to the so-called noncanonical function, the $\tan A ^ { \angle y s }$ -independent formation of diadenosine tetraphosphate $( \mathsf { A p } _ { 4 } \mathsf { A } )$ . This noncanonical function has not yet been described for any plant KRS enzyme. Because phosphorylation of Ser207 of HsKRS can be mimicked in vitro by the inclusion of $Z n ^ { 2 + }$ into the assay mixture,42–44 we tested if AtKRS1 could also catalyze the noncanonical reaction. When AtKRS1 was incubated with ATP in the presence of $Z n ^ { 2 + }$ , $\mathsf { A p } _ { 4 } \mathsf { A }$ formation was detected by HPLC in the presence, but not in the absence of L-lysine. The successful proof of the formation of $\mathsf { A p } _ { 4 } \mathsf { A }$ from AtKRS1 enabled the development of a colorimetric KRS assay, in which the $\mathsf { P P _ { i } }$ by-product of $\mathsf { A p } _ { 4 } \mathsf { A }$ formation is 

cleaved by inorganic pyrophosphatase and $\mathsf { P } _ { \mathrm { i } }$ detected by an ammonium molybdate-malachite green reagent. With this assay in hand, the $\mathsf { K } _ { \mathsf { m } }$ -values for the AtKRS1 substrates L-lysine and ATP were determined as $2 4 . 3 \pm 5 . 3 \mu \mathrm { M }$ and $7 6 . 8 \pm 5 . 4 \mu \mathrm { M } ,$ respectively. It is noteworthy that the formation of $\mathsf { A p } _ { 4 } \mathsf { A }$ was demonstrated for the first time for a higher plant KRS enzyme. This novel assay enabled us to determine the $\mathsf { p l } _ { 5 0 }$ -values of inhibition by the novel aminoisothiazolamides 1a–w and 23a–r for AtKRS1, AtKRS2 and HsKRS (Tables 1 and 2).

Echinochloa crus-galli (ECHCG), Lolium rigidum (LOLRI), Avena fatua (AVEFA) and Setaria viridis (SETVI), were chosen as model plants to assess initial pre-emergence activity using dose rates of 320 and $8 0 \ g \ h a ^ { - 1 }$ , whereas in vitro tests were carried out using human KRS (HsKRS) and plant KRS (AtKRS1 and AtKRS2 isolated from A. thaliana). As outlined in Tables 1 and 2, commercially available FAT-inhibitors cinmethylin and methiozolin (HRAC group 30) proved to be suitable reference compounds as they showed good and broad control of all grass weeds, albeit affording incomplete or weak control of commercially important grass weeds LOLRI and AVEFA at the lower dosage of $8 0 \ g \ h a ^ { - 1 }$ and insufficient control of the broadleaf weed MATIN. In line with our research goal to investigate new MoAs for the control of grass weeds in cereals, we have put particular emphasis on investigating pre-emergence efficacy within the present SAR study. This application type is still very important for cereal crops requiring continuous innovation, particularly with respect to PFAS-free lead structures ${ \mathsf { P F A S } } =$ per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances). 

First, we investigated a set of 4-chloro isothiazolamides 1a–w bearing different cyclohexylmethyl groups or structurally related substituents to assess the impact of modifications in the amide part of lead structure 1a on in vitro and in vivo efficacy (Table 1). Introducing an ortho-chlorine substituent in (Rac)-1d afforded a slightly weaker in vivo efficacy with insufficient control of commercially important grass weeds AVEFA and LOLRI at the lower application rate paired with lower target affinities at AtKRS1 and HsKRS, but slightly higher affinity at AtKRS2. It is worth noting that the stereochemical orientation of substituents in the ortho-chlorosubstituted cyclohexyl moiety had an effect on in vivo and in vitro efficacy, but not as strikingly so as in spirocyclic FAT inhibitors.30 Whilst (1S,2S)-(2-chlorocyclohex-1-yl)-methyl substituted 1c afforded moderate results with gaps against AVEFA and LOLRI, the corresponding (1R,2R)-(2-chlorocyclohex-1-yl)-methyl substituted diastereomer 1b showed strong control of all tested weed species except for LOLRI at the lowest application rate. Furthermore, compound 1b showed significantly better target affinity than parent lead structure 1a affording $\mathsf { p l } _ { 5 0 }$ values in the range of 6.0 to 7.2, albeit with a lack of selectivity between AtKRS1 and HsKRS (Table 1). Closely 

related 1-fluoro substituted (Rac)-1i showed a comparable level of efficacy including good control of ALOMY, but with a gap in the control of AVEFA, whereas 2,2-difluoro substituted analogue 1e exhibited good overall efficacy including AVEFA control at the higher application rate of $3 2 0 ~ 9 ~ \mathsf { h a } ^ { - 1 }$ . Modifying the ring size as shown in (cyclopentyl)methyl-substituted analogue 1q and (1-bicyclo[2.2.1] heptan-2-yl)methyl-substituted 1p afforded comparable levels of target affinity in line with good overall results in our in vivo glasshouse tests, albeit showing insufficient (1q) or no control (1p) of AVEFA (Table 1). Accordingly, introducing other small substituents on the cyclohexyl moiety, for example 1-methyl (1o), 2-methyl (1j, 1k), 4-methyl (1f), 4,4-dimethyl (1l), provided moderate-to-good control of ALOMY, SETVI, LOLRI and MATIN paired with insufficient AVEFA control. However, sterically more demanding 1,1- difluorospiro[2.5]octan-6-yl-substituted analogue 1g did not show any in vivo efficacy and turned out to be inactive in the three in vitro tests (Table 1). Likewise, introducing the bulky tetrahydronaphtalen-1-yl moiety in compound 1n caused a clear decline in weed control efficacy and target affinity. Thus, it can be assumed that there is very little space for structural variations of the cyclohexyl group owing to limited space in the binding pocket. Surprisingly, replacing the cyclohexyl group by substituted aromatic moieties afforded a broad range of results. Whilst (2,4-difluorophenyl) methyl and (2,4,5-trifluorophenyl)methyl substituted analogues 1r and 1s exhibited good target affinities at AtKRS1 $( \mathsf { p l } _ { 5 0 } 6 . 0 )$ ) paired with full control of ALOMY, ECHCG, SETVI, LOLRI and MATIN, the corresponding isothiazolamides 1t–v bearing chlorophenyl or pyridyl moieties turned out to be completely inactive both in vitro and in vivo. As observed for most substituted cyclohexylmethyl amides, AVEFA was not controlled by any of the substituted benzyl amides 1r, 1s and 1w.

Considering their strong control of sensitive and resistant Lolium species our new aminoisothiazolamides represent highly promising

Hence, our new aminoisothiazolamide-based KRS1-inhibitors represent a propitious class of herbicidal lead structures with the potential to control resistant grass weeds.

# 3.4 Molecular modelling

When designing our SAR study outlined in Tables 1 and 2, and in Schemes 2–5, we analyzed the binding mode of aminoisothiazolamide 1a by docking studies. The initial docking was carried out based on a modified crystal structure of E. coli ${ \mathsf { K R S } } ^ { 3 3 }$ with a bound lysil-adenylate intermediate as this ligand covers both the lysine and the ATP binding pockets. As shown in Fig. 8, the initial docking analysis suggested that lead structure 1a mimics the interaction of the adenosine moiety by forming the same hydrogen (H)-bonds to a strictly conserved arginine, the backbone of the protein, and to a conserved water molecule. The lysiladenylate intermediate has two oxygen (O) atoms located in a hydrophobic pocket for which the dehydration penalties are not compensated for by forming H-bonds. These destabilizing Os give rise to a weaker binding. Instead of these destabilizing Os, 1a has a hydrophobic moiety located in the hydrophobic pocket which increases the affinity of 1a significantly, making it more potent than the adenylate intermediate. Additionally, the docking results were identical in the presence and absence of lysine, which nicely correlates with the experimental observation that isothiazolamide 1a binds well in the presence of lysine. As a result, various lipophilic substituents or isosteric replacements were introduced to 

mimic the cyclohexyl moiety. Furthermore, we have introduced different substituents at the C-4 position of the isothiazolamide ring as the chlorine substituent at C-4 points into a cavity of considerable size (close to F371). 

# 3.5 X-ray crystallography and sequence alignments

Because there is a high probability that the binding pockets of essential enzymes such as the t-RNA synthetase show only minor differences between different species, we have characterized the interactions of aminoisothiazoles with KRS on a molecular level. Thus, we have determined ternary complex crystal structures of AtKRS1, L-lysine and compounds 1a, 1o, 23m and 23n, respectively. Furthermore, we have carried out sequence alignments to corroborate our findings. The crystal structures revealed that AtKRS1 is a typical class II synthetase. Statistics for data collection and refinement statistics are provided in Data S1. The homodimeric protein contains a N-terminal anticodon-binding domain (D86-R230) and a C-terminal catalytic domain (W243-R608) in each monomer [Fig. 9(a)]. Both domains are connected by a flexible linker that is not resolved in the electron density map. In order to identify potential differences between various species, an in-depth sequence analysis was done using KRS sequences from selected plants, fungi, mammals and pathogens. The overall sequence identity to AtKRS1 is roughly $70 \%$ to cytoplasmic KRS enzymes from other plants, whilst being $5 0 \%$ to mammalian and fungal KRS enzymes, and $30 \%$ to plant isoforms in the chloroplast and bacterial enzymes. Amongst the amino acids that form the aminoisothiazole binding site with 1a, there is only one particular amino acid that shows a difference in some species, that is Thr373. All plants, mammals and most fungi have a threonine at this position, whereas all oomycetes have a serine, and some fungi have either a smaller serine or a larger isoleucine. The variability at this position is higher in eukaryotic pathogens with some of them bearing either a smaller serine or alanine, whilst others such as S. aureus and E. coli have a larger methionine at this position. # 3.6 Toxicological analysis and comparison with biology

In order to substantiate our conclusion regarding the equal aminoisothiazolamide-sensitivity of the plant and mammalian KRS enzymes with experimental data, we investigated the inhibitory potency of the aminoisothiazolamides on the human KRS enzyme (as outlined in Tables 1 and 2). Plots of the $\mathsf { p l } _ { 5 0 }$ -values for inhibition of AtKRS2 and HsKRS versus the respective $\mathsf { p l } _ { 5 0 }$ -values for inhibition of AtKRS1 [Fig. 10(a,b)] revealed that they were highly correlated (slope: 1.0), but with an offset of −0.9 which might be attributed to assay setup parameters. Furthermore, the ${ \sf p l } _ { 5 0 }$ -values for inhibition of AtKRS1 and HsKRS were also correlated with the biological activity of the compounds [Fig. 10(c,d)]. 

The results obtained in the acute oral systemic toxicity tests with rats (AROS) and mice (AMOS) showed a strong acute effect, albeit the number of compounds investigated in these in vivo tests was strictly limited due to animal welfare and ethical considerations. Table 4 shows that five compounds over eight tested induced strong acute effects (death of animals at $< 6 0 \mathrm { \ m g \ k g ^ { - 1 } }$ or 60– $3 0 0 \mathrm { ~ m g } \mathrm { ~ k g } ^ { - 1 } ;$ ). Whilst the inhibition of AtKRS1 is similar across all tested compounds with $5 . 4 < \mathsf { p l } _ { 5 0 } A t \mathsf { K R S } 1 < 7 . 2$ they only show a ${ < } 1 0 ^ { \circ }$ -fold specificity from HsKRS1, that is $4 . 6 < \mathsf { p l } _ { 5 0 } H s \mathsf { K R S } 1 < 6 . 4$ Interestingly, the compound with the highest target affinity, that is compound 1b, showed the strongest acute profile in the rat $( < 6 0 \mathrm { \ m g \ k g ^ { - 1 } } )$ ). These data brought evidence of a potential acute toxicological issue caused by the class of aminoisothiazolamides being correlated with the herbicidal efficacy of the compounds and preventing further exploration in animal testing. The target safety assessment (TSA) on KRS1 highlighted that the inhibition of this target in mammals could induce developmental toxicity as well as several severe pathologies, including neurodegenerative disorders and seizures. Additionally, acute toxicity was observed with eight KRS1 inhibitors. Hence, the human safety assessment for the inhibition of the mammalian target is not supported and KRS1 should not be chosen as a herbicidal target if sufficient selectivity towards mammals cannot be reached.

Our initial synthetic approaches towards the aminoisothiazolamidebased lead structure 1a made use of 3,4-dichloroisothiazole-5-carboxylic acid 8 (DCIT) which is available internally as an industrial intermediate in the synthesis of Bayer's fungicidal active ingredient isotianil, that acts via systemic acquired resistance, hence priming the immune system of crops.47 The key steps of both synthetic pathways (Scheme 1) were Pd-catalyzed amination reactions. # 4 CONCLUSION

As an outcome of in vivo high-throughput screening across crop protection indications the aminoisothiazolyl carboxamides (1a– w and 23a–r) could be identified as a new promising herbicidal lead class with surprisingly low crop damage in corn. Challenges in the chemical synthesis of these compounds imposed by the unusual isothiazole ring system required considerable efforts in chemical route development and synthetic exploration. The optimization of the class resulted in several candidates with broad cross activity in pre-emergence and partly also postemergence applications, albeit with limited selectivity. Ungratifyingly, the high likelihood of adverse toxicological effects based on targetbased toxicity paired with the anticipated small chance to overcome this problem, owing to the highly conserved active sites in different species, finally resulted in the conclusion to stop the further exploration of the otherwise promising class of herbicidal KRS1 inhibitors. Likewise, the combination of results from sequence alignments, docking studies and protein crystallography together with concomittant mammalian toxicity provided scientific evidence to the corroborate that conclusion, prohibiting further research work towards a commercial application. Hence, we opted to discard several further conceivable herbicidal lead structures before the start of in-depth SAR studies, when sequence analyses suggested similar levels of conservation between the respective binding pockets in plants and mammalians.
