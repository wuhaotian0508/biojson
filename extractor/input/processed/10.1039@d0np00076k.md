# Tropane alkaloid biosynthesis: a centennial review

Jian-Ping Huang, $\textcircled{1}$ ab Yong-Jiang Wang , $\textcircled{1}$ a Tian Tian,ac Li Wang, $\textcircled{1}$ a Yijun Yana and Sheng-Xiong Huang $\textcircled { 1 0 }$ *abc

Covering: 1917 to 2020

Tropane alkaloids (TAs) are a remarkable class of plant secondary metabolites, which are characterized by an 8-azabicyclo[3.2.1]octane (nortropane) ring. Members of this class, such as hyoscyamine, scopolamine, and cocaine, are well known for their long history as poisons, hallucinogens, and anaesthetic agents. Since the structure of the tropane ring system was first elucidated in 1901, organic chemists and biochemists have been interested in how these mysterious tropane alkaloids are assembled in vitro and in vivo. However, it was only in 2020 that the complete biosynthetic route of hyoscyamine and scopolamine was clarified, and their de novo production in yeast was also achieved. The aim of this review is to present the innovative ideas and results in exploring the story of tropane alkaloid biosynthesis in plants from 1917 to 2020. This review also highlights that Robinson's classic synthesis of tropinone, which is one hundred years old, is biomimetic, and underscores the importance of total synthesis in the study of natural product biosynthesis.

# 1 Introduction

At the beginning of the 19th century, the rapid growth and development of chemistry resulted in attempts to unravel the active principles in medicinal plants by chemists and pharmacists becoming an attainable goal. From Atropa belladonna, a perennial herbaceous plant, which is among the oldest folk herbal medicines known to man, acting as a hallucinogen, mydriatic, anaesthetic, and poison, atropine (the racemate of hyoscyamine 1) was rst obtained in 1833.1,2 Thereaer the famous scopolamine 2 and cocaine 3 were isolated from Scopolia japonica3 and Erythroxylum coca, 4 respectively. The principal structures of cocaine 3 and atropine were rst elucidated by Willst¨atter based on chemical degradation5 and synthesis,6–8 which led to the recognition of a new alkaloid group, tropane alkaloids (TAs), a remarkable class of plant secondary metabolites characterized by an 8-azabicyclo[3.2.1]octane (nortropane) ring (Fig. 1). Readers interested in the details of the structural elucidation of tropane are referred to the book by R. W. Hoffmann9 and the review by Humphrey and O'Hagan.10 Various aspects of the pharmacological activity of TAs have also been studied and reviewed in detail,11 including their action on asthma, applications in ophthalmology, activities against hyperglycemia and parkinsonism, and protection against organophosphorus compounds (hyoscyamine 1); uses against nausea, drooling, and Alzheimer's disease (scopolamine 2); and

# 2 Structural diversity of tropane alkaloids

More than 300 TAs have been isolated and identied from plants belonging to the Solanaceae, Convolvulaceae, Proteaceae, Rhizophoraceae, Erythroxylaceae, Apocynaceae,

Brassicaceae, Olacaceae, Phyllanthaceae, Moraceae, and Fabaceae families. These families are scatteredly distributed within the eudicot branch, indicating convergent evolution in the biosynthesis of TAs may have occurred. The structure and occurrence of these TAs have been comprehensively covered in the fascinating reviews by Lounasmaa and Tamminen12,13 and Afewerki et al.14 Structurally, almost all the TAs are esters of various organic acids, including tropic, benzoic, cinnamic, isovaleric, and tiglic acids conjugated to hydroxylated tropane derivatives.

Based on their biosynthetic relationship and structural features, we have classied TAs into six main classes (Table 1). Type I, tropine (4, $_ { 3 \alpha }$ -tropanol) and its derivatives, are the largest group with 220 reported members. Type II includes pseudotropine (6, $3 \beta$ -tropanol) and its derivatives and contains 21 reported members. # 3 Tropane alkaloid biosynthesis

For a long time, the isolation and structure elucidation of natural products was the main focus of organic chemists, while the biogenesis and biosynthetic origins of natural products were thought to be mysterious and unrealizable in vitro. In 1917, skeletal dissection stimulated the classical total synthesis of tropinone 11 in one pot by Robinson (the Robinson–Sch¨opf reaction) using succinaldehyde 12, methylamine 13, and calcium acetonedicarboxylate 14-1 under physiological conditions22 (Fig. 3). On account of the simplicity of the reaction, Robinson immediately published an innovative theory that the plant probably employed a similar strategy in vivo for the biosynthesis of tropinone 11. This hypothesis was strongly

supported by two related pieces of evidence: (1) the same structural motifs were observed in hygrine 15 and cuscohygrine 16, both of which occur together in plants (Fig. 3) and (2) the condensation reactions between carbinol-amines and substances containing the –CHR–CO– group (Mannich reaction) could proceed to completion in aqueous solution and at the ordinary temperature.23,24 The original blueprint for tropinone biosynthesis in plants (Scheme 1) was then formed, and almost all tropinone biosynthetic studies thereaer have been based on this theory.

Over the subsequent decades, experiments in which different substances were fed to alkaloid-producing plants, have been carried out by chemists in an endeavor to garner knowledge of the essential building blocks that lead to the formation of TAs. The vital roles of arginine 20, ornithine 17 and putrescine 21, as intermediates in TA biosynthesis were suggested as a culmination of these efforts.25–28 Moving forward, from the beginning of the 1950s to the end of the 20th century, the question of biogenesis of the naturally occurring TAs was answered using new techniques, namely radio- and stableisotope labeling. By feeding isotopically labeled putative precursors and then analyzing their specic incorporation into target products, the most probable biosynthetic pathways of TAs were well established. Moreover, with the aid of the powerful analytical tools nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy and mass spectrometry (MS), an increasing number of postulated intermediates were isolated and identi-ed, constituting solid grounds for the presumed biosynthetic route of TAs. Enzymes showing the presumed activities on the established intermediates were successfully extracted in the 1980s, and target gene cloning and enzyme expression and characterization are accessible at present. We now have the complete biosynthetic pathway to hyoscyamine 1 and scopolamine 2.

3.1.1 The starting points of tropane skeleton biosynthesis: ornithine and arginine. Ornithine 17 was chosen by Robinson as the initial starting point for the construction of the tropane skeleton in plants primarily based on the report by Hess that the methylation of an amine with the aid of formaldehyde is accompanied by oxidation, with amino-alcohols yielding methylaminoketones.23 Accordingly, ornithine 17 may yield 4- methylaminobutanal 18 or succinaldehyde 12 and methylamine 13, which can then give the pyrrolidine ring of tropane nucleus by intramolecular or intermolecular condensation22 (Scheme 1). This was the rst route proposed for the biogenesis of the pyrrolidine ring moiety of TAs. More than two decades later, through non-isotopically labeled precursor feeding experiments in A. belladonna and D. stramonium, physiological chemist Cromwell proposed that arginine 20 and putrescine 21 were intermediates in the biosynthesis of hyoscyamine 1, and later found that the crude enzyme from A. belladonna is capable of catalyzing the oxidative deamination of putrescine 21 into aldehyde. Accordingly, a more detailed scheme beginning with the conversion of arginine 20 to putrescine 21 (via ornithine 17), which was then oxidized to amino-aldehyde and/or succindialdehyde 12 for the further construction of the tropinone skeleton was generated (Scheme 2).25–27

When isotopically labeled compounds were available, to conrm the true amino acid precursor, Leete and coworkers fed radiolabeled $\mathrm { \bf D L } ^ { - } \big [ 2 - \mathrm { } ^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { \bf C } \big ] .$ -ornithine 17a to D. stramonium plants and observed the specic incorporation of $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C }$ into the tropane ring fraction of hyoscyamine 1,29 thereby establishing ornithine 17 as the starting point for the biosynthesis of TA. However, further degradation analysis of the resulting radioactive hyoscyamine 1 demonstrated that the tropane bridge-head carbon (C-1) was isotopically-labeled with $^ { 1 4 } \mathrm { C } ^ { 3 0 , 3 1 }$ (Fig. 4A). 3.2.1 Ornithine decarboxylase and arginine decarboxylase (ODC and ADC). The extensive use of the radioactive tracer technique rmly established ornithine 17 and arginine 20 as the primary amino acids that make up the pyrrolidine ring unit of tropane alkaloids. Due to the non-symmetrical incorporation of ornithine 17 into the tropane skeleton observed in the Datura species, d-N-methylornithine 24 instead of symmetrical putrescine 17 was for a long time believed to be the immediate intermediate from ornithine 17 in the biosynthesis of TAs. However, by detecting enzyme activities in vitro, precursor pools, the metabolism of administered precursors, and the effect of the specic irreversible inhibitor of ornithine decarboxylase in cultured roots of H. albus, Hashimoto and coworkers speculated that a biosynthetic pathway in which the biosynthesis of the pyrrolidine ring was initiated with ornithine decarboxylase (ODC) catalyzed the decarboxylation of ornithine 17 and arginine decarboxylase (ADC) catalyzed the decarboxylation of arginine 20, which then proceeded via the symmetrical diamine putrescine 21, may be in operation.41,102 Consistent with this, further systematic degradation of the resulting radioactive hyoscyamine 1 derived from DL- ${ \big [ } 5 - { } ^ { 1 4 } \mathbf { C } { \big ] } .$ -ornithine 17b in cultured roots of H. albus uncovered equal radioactivity at the C-1 and C-5 positions of the tropine moiety.37,41 In fact, ODC and ADC are widely distributed pyridoxal phosphate (PLP)- dependent enzymes in plants. Although ODC- and ADCmediated putrescine biosynthesis plays an important role in plant cell proliferation and stress response, their relative importance for TA production, which has been studied using enzyme-specic inhibitors (diuoromethylornithine and diuoromethylarginine, respectively), seemed to be species-

dependent.102–104 In the root culture of a D. candida $\times \textit { D }$ . aurea hybrid, it was observed that the activities of ODC and ADC were present at potentially ux-limiting levels in the biosynthesis of TAs.105

In TA-producing plants, the gene encoding ODC was rst identied from the culture roots of D. stramonium. 106 However, direct evidence supporting its role in TA biosynthesis was not available until recently, where AbODC was identied from A. belladonna, knockdown or overexpression of which resulted in signicantly changed production of TAs.104 Additionally, cytosolic localization of AbODC fused with GFP was observed in tobacco protoplast. The cDNA encoding for ADC was rst characterized from a non-TA producing plant oat (Arena sativa),107 in which ADC activity was predominantly found in chloroplasts.108 Full length cDNA clones encoding ODC and ADC were also identied from a cDNA library made from young E. coca leaves, and their enzymatic activities were conrmed by heterologous expression.109 The transcription levels of both the EcODC and EcADC genes were the highest in the buds and rolled leaves, where cocaine 3 and cinnamoyl cocaine were present in highest concentrations and the highest rate of alkaloid biosynthesis took place.

As PLP-dependent decarboxylases, ODC and ADC contain a conserved lysine (Lys) residue capable of binding with PLP by forming an internal Lys-PLP aldimine. When substrates such as ornithine 17 and arginine 20 are available, PLP converts to form an external aldimine with the amino group of the substrate by transaldimination reaction. Subsequent decarboxylation drives the release of the product (putrescine 21 or agmatine 22) and regeneration of the internal Lys-PLP aldimine110 (Fig. 14).

3.2.3 N-Methyl-putrescine oxidase (MPO). N-Methylputrescine oxidase, which is responsible for catalyzing the formation of 4-methylaminobutanal 18 by oxidative deamination of the primary amino group of $N \cdot$ -methyl-putrescine 25, was rst puried from the roots of N. tabacum in 1972.119 Thereaer, the MPO activity was determined in the roots of A. belladonna and D. stramonium.112 The partially puried MPO isolated from the cultured roots of $H ,$ . niger that produced considerable amounts of TAs was studied in more detail.120 Firstly, there was no close correlation observed between TAs production and MPO activity in H. albus and H. niger. Secondly, HnMPO was a dimer with a molecular weight of around $1 3 5 \mathrm { \ k D a }$ and optimum pH between 7.5 and 8.0. 3.2.4 An unusual type III polyketide synthase (PKS). When Leete proposed the revised cocaine biosynthetic pathway to explain the stereospecic incorporation of N-methyl- $\Delta ^ { 1 }$ -pyrrolinium 26, a polyketide mechanism was rst used to rationalize the condensation reaction between $N \cdot$ -methyl- $\Delta ^ { 1 } \cdot$ -pyrrolinium 26 and malonyl CoA.54 The identication of the immediate precursor role of the condensation reaction product, 4-(1- methyl-2-pyrrolidinyl)-3-oxobutanoate 31, 64,65,68,70 to give tropinone 11 provided further evidence for the involvement of a polyketide-based mechanism in the biosynthetic pathway of TAs.

Spurred by the hypothesis that a polyketide synthasemediated reaction was involved in the formation of the C3 fragment of the tropane ring,10 a root preferentially expressed unigene encoding a type III polyketide synthase, AbPYKS, was

identied from A. belladonna transcriptome by Barry and coworkers.125 Knockdown of AbPYKS in A. belladonna by virusinduced gene silencing (VIGS) resulted in a signicant reduction in 4-(1-methyl-2-pyrrolidinyl)-3-oxobutanoate 31, tropinone 11, and all downstream TAs. Analysis of the recombinant AbPYKS-catalyzed reaction mixture, in which $N \cdot$ -methyl- $\cdot \Delta ^ { 1 } \cdot$ -pyrrolinium 26 and malonyl CoA were used as substrates, resulted in the detection of 4-(1-methyl-2-pyrrolidinyl)-3-oxobutanoate 31. Based on these two observations, AbPYKS was characterized as an atypical polyketide synthase that utilized unconjugated $N \cdot$ -methyl- $\cdot \Delta ^ { 1 } \cdot$ -pyrrolinium as the starter substrate for two rounds of malonyl CoA-mediated decarboxylative condensation to give rise to 4-(1-methyl-2-pyrrolidinyl)-3-oxobutanoate 31, the immediate precursor of tropinone 11 in the biosynthesis of TAs.

Almost simultaneously, three type III PKS genes, AaPYKS, DsPYKS, and AbPYKS, presumably involved in the biosynthesis of TAs, were also identied from root cultures of Anisodus acutangulus, D. stramonium, and A. belladonna, respectively.126 In vitro analysis of their activity toward the putative substrates $N \mathrm { \mathrm { - } }$ methyl- $\cdot \Delta ^ { 1 } \cdot$ -pyrrolinium 26 and malonyl CoA conrmed the formation of 4-(1-methyl-2-pyrrolidinyl)-3-oxobutanoate 31 by liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS) and chemical modication. 3.2.5 Cytochrome P450 monooxygenase CYP82M3. The conversion of 4-(1-methyl-2-pyrrolidinyl)-3-oxobutanoate 31 to tropinone 11 was postulated to be catalyzed by a putative cytochrome P450 via hydroxylation of the heterocyclic ring125 (Fig. 18). Subsequent dehydration, condensation and

decarboxylation can happen similar to that of Robinson's classical synthesis of tropinone 11. 22 By comparing the transcriptome data from lateral roots to that from non-root tissues, Barry and coworkers identied four root-preferential expressing unigenes that were predicted to encode cytochrome P450s in A. belladonna. 125 They then analyzed the potential tropinone synthase activity of these P450 candidates by expressing either of them in combination with AbPYKS alone or with AbPMT and AbMPO in a transient expression system established in the leaves of N. benthamiana, a non-tropane producing species. The activity of the resulting AbCYP82M3 was then veried by an in vitro enzymatic reaction involving 4-(1-methyl-2-pyrrolidinyl)-3- oxobutanoate 31 and microsomes isolated from a yeast strain expressing AbCYP82M3. Consequently, AbCYP82M3 was identied as the tropinone synthase responsible for catalyzing the cyclization of 4-(1-methyl-2-pyrrolidinyl)-3-oxobutanoate 31 to tropinone 11 in the TA biosynthesis pathway. In accordance with this conclusion, silencing AbCYP82M3 using VIGS resulted in the reduced production of tropinone 11 and all downstream TAs, and an increase in the abundance of 4-(1-methyl-2- pyrrolidinyl)-3-oxobutanoate 31.

From 1980s to 1990s, the puri- cation and characterization of enzymes exhibiting catalytic activity on tropinone 11 or tropine 4 from TA-producing plants made a massive contribution to the nal functional elucidation of TRI and TRII. Initially, it was found that an NADP(H)-specic tropine dehydrogenase from D. stramonium could catalyze the reversible and stereospecic oxidation of tropine 4 and related tropane- $_ { \cdot 3 \alpha }$ -ols to the corresponding ketones.128 Tropine 4 and/ or pseudotropine 6-forming tropinone reductase activities were then revealed in H. niger129 and D. innoxia.130 The successful separation of TRI and TRII from D. stramonium was rst achieved by Draeger and coworkers,131 and a detailed comparison of DsTRI and DsTRII was conducted. cDNA clones coding for DsTRI and DsTRII were rst identi-ed from the hairy roots of D. stramonium based on polypeptide sequencing and cDNA library screening in 1993. They were identied as short-chain dehydrogenases (SDH) based on amino acid sequence alignments. Importantly, based on the amino acid similarities between DsTRI and DsTRII, and the structural characteristics of the SDH family, the authors presumed that the different stereospecicity of these two TRs was a result of the opposite orientation of tropinone 11 at the substrate-binding site, which was presumably located at the carboxyl-terminal half of the enzyme.134 Subsequently, this presumption was supported by product stereospecicity and substrate specicity analysis on chimeric TRs constructed by exchanging segments between DsTRI and DsTRII.135 Denitive evidence for this came from the crystal structures of DsTRI and DsTRII, in which the presence of different charged residues at the substrate binding sites conferred different electrostatic environments between each TR.136 The site-directed mutagenesis of ve conserved substrate-binding residues resulted in an almost complete reversal of the DsTRI/TRII stereospecicity, conrming that the different stereospecicities were indeed determined by the binding orientation of tropinone 11.137 In addition, using specic antibodies for each TR, it was revealed that TRI and TRII mainly occurred in the lateral root of H. niger throughout its development. However, different cell-specic patterns were observed, where TRI was preferentially localized in the endodermis and the outer cortical layer, and TRII was found in the pericycle, endodermis, and inner cortical layers.138

However, in E. coca, a TRI/TRII homology-based attempt to identify the methylecgonone reductase was unsuccessful. Subsequent purication of methylecgonone reductase activity

from young leaf extracts and cloning of the corresponding gene revealed that a protein (MecgoR) of the aldo-keto reductase family catalyzed the reversible and stereospecic reduction of methylecgonone 19 to methylecgonine 32 $3 \beta$ -conguration).139 Moreover, either 6-hydroxytropinone, tropinone 11, or nortropinone could be reduced to their corresponding alcohols by MecgoR. MecgoR has its highest activity, protein level, and gene transcription levels in young, expanding leaves of E. coca, which is consistent with its putative role in cocaine (3) biosynthesis.139

3.2.7 L-Phenylalanine: 4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate aminotransferase and phenylpyruvic acid reductase. The conversion of phenylalanine 36 to phenylpyruvate 37 was supposedly mediated by an aromatic amino acid aminotransferase enzyme. Based on the principle that the genes involved in TA biosynthesis exhibit preferential or specic transcription in the root tissues, differential expression analysis of a de novo assembled transcriptome consisting of cDNA libraries derived from the callus, leaf, stem, seedling, primary root, secondary root, ower buds, owers, green fruit, ripe fruit, and seeds of A. belladonna was conducted by Barry and coworkers,140 resulting in an aromatic amino acid aminotransferase gene (Ab-ArAT4) with specic high expression in root tissues. Silencing of Ab-ArAT4 in vivo via VIGS caused signicantly decreased levels of phenyllactate 39, hyoscyamine 1 and scopolamine 2 and increased levels of tropine 4 in A. belladonna. The recombinant Ab-ArAT4 showed high enzymatic activity in using L-phenylalanine 36 as the amino donor and 4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate as the amino acceptor to generate phenylpyruvate 37 and L-tyrosine in vitro, establishing Ab-ArAT4 as the enzyme catalyzing the rst biosynthetic step in the biosynthesis of the tropic acid moiety (35).

The enzyme responsible for the catalysis of the reduction of phenylpyruvate 37 to phenyllactic acid 39 was proposed to be a homologue of hydroxyphenylpyruvate reductase (HPPR), which is responsible for catalyzing the reduction of hydroxyphenylpyruvate to its respective lactate. Thus, the conserved hydroxyl acid dehydrogenase (HAD) domain of HPPR was used to search the transcriptome data of A. belladonna by Liao and coworkers. This afforded a unigene (AbPPAR) with a secondary root-specic expression pattern.141 The participation of AbPPAR in the biosynthesis of TAs was demonstrated by a series of observations, including the specic accumulation of AbPPAR in the root pericycle and endodermis, the in vitro catalytic activity of AbPPAR in converting phenylpyruvate 37 to phenyllactic acid 39, and the dramatically reduced production of phenyllactic acid 39, hyoscyamine 1, anisodamine 5, and scopolamine 2 in AbPPAR-RNAi root cultures.

3.2.8 Cocaine synthase, phenyllactate UDPglycosyltransferase, and littorine synthase. The last step in the biosynthesis of cocaine 3 is the esterication of methylecgonine 32 with a benzoyl moiety (34-1) derived from phenylalanine 36 via cinnamic acid. Three groups of enzymes, namely Nhydroxycinnamoyltransferase, BAHD acyltransferase (benzylalcohol $O _ { \ l }$ -acetyl transferase, anthocyanin O-hydroxycinnamoyl transferase, anthranilate $N \cdot$ -hydroxycinnamoyl/benzoyl transferase, and diacetyl vindoline 4-O-acetyltransferase), and SCPL-AT (serine carboxypeptidase-like acyltransferase), were known to catalyze acylation reactions of secondary metabolites in plants when D'Auria and coworkers planned to identify the

enzyme in E. coca responsible for the acylation of methylecgonine 32. 142 The formation of cocaine 3 or its cinnamoyl analogue was observed in enzyme assays performed on crude plant E. coca extracts using methylecgonine 32 and either benzoyl-CoA (34-1) or cinnamoyl-CoA as cosubstrates. This inspired the authors to think about the involvement of a BAHD acyltransferase, which was the only type of acyltransferase known to utilize activated acyl-CoA thioesters as substrates. Therefore, they searched the transcriptome database established from E. coca and obtained eight EcBAHD candidates. Two recombinant proteins from the candidates, EcBAHD7 and EcBAHD8, gave the expected ester-forming activity in vitro. However, the propensity of EcBAHD7 to catalyze the production of cocaine 3 or cinnamoylcocaine was much more efficient than that of EcBAHD8 (1000-fold and 5000-fold, respectively). Thus, EcBAHD7 was designated as the cocaine synthase (EcCS) that participates in cocaine (3) biosynthesis.

Littorine 38, the rst esteried tropine in the hyoscyamine (1) biosynthetic pathway, is proposed to be formed by the esterication of tropine 4 with a phenyllactoyl moiety derived from phenylalanine 36 via phenylpyruvate 37. However, no phenyllactoyl-CoA: tropine acyltransferase activity could be detected.143 Therefore, phenyllactylglucose 43 instead of phenyllactoyl-CoA was presumed to be the active form of phenyllactate 39 in the acylation of tropine 4 in the biosynthesis of littorine 38. This implied that a UDP-glycosyltransferase (UGT) was responsible for the glycosylation of phenyllactate 39, and an SCPL-AT that catalyzed the esterication of tropine 4 with the resulting phenyllactylglucose 43 would be involved in the sequence.144 A search of the transcriptome database of A. belladonna using the conserved domain of UGT (PF00201) or of SCPL-AT (PF00450) resulted in 102 UGT unigene candidates and 33 SCPL-AT unigene candidates, respectively. Finally, only two candidates, UGT1 and LS, displayed the characteristic roottissue and sterile seedling specic expression pattern, and clustered with genes coding for glycosylation or esterication enzymes compatible substrates similar to phenyllactate 39. As expected, knockdown of AbUGT1 or AbLS in vivo via VIGS or RNAi resulted in the signicantly reduced production of littorine 38, hyoscyamine 1, and scopolamine 2, and a markedly increased accumulation of tropine 4 and phenyllactate 39 in LS-RNAi root cultures, or of tropine 4 in UGT1-RNAi root cultures. Accordingly, the heterologously expressed AbUGT1 was able to catalyze the formation of phenyllactylglucose 43 using phenyllactate 39 and UDP-glucose as substrates in vitro, while the transiently expressed AbLS, which was suggested to be a posttranslationally processed subunit of 36 kDa, conferred the ability of generating littorine 38 from tropine 4 and phenyllactate 39 to the tobacco cells.

To date, only a few plant SCPL-ATs have been functionally characterized at the protein level.145 When Srinivasan and Smolke decided to establish the TA biosynthetic pathway in yeast, they conducted a detailed analysis on AbLS.146 Firstly, bioinformatic analysis of AbLS revealed an N-terminal signal peptide targeting it to the vacuole, four putative N-glycosylation sites (N152, N320, N376, and N416), and an internal propeptide linker of 25- to 30-residues. Secondly, N-terminal GFP-tagged

AbLS (GFP-AbLS) was localized to vacuolar membranes in yeast. Thirdly, site-directed mutation suggested that AbLS expressed in yeast had a combination of multiple glycosylation states, part of which was similar to those expressed in tobacco. Lastly, but most importantly, the AbLS variant with wild-type propeptide swapped for the synthetic poly-arginine site, which could be recognized and cleaved by Kex2p protease, failed to produce the expected C-terminal fragment of AbLS through proteolytic cleavage in yeast, providing the crucial indication that wild-type AbLS expressed in yeast became stalled in the secretion pathway upstream of the trans-Golgi network. Consequently, the functional expression of AbLS in yeast was dependent on its well-organized vacuole localization, which could be achieved by an N-terminally fused soluble domain, such as green uorescent protein (GFP). It was also observed that the activity of AbLS expressed in yeast could be enhanced with an increase in the N-terminal domain oligomerization state. This is the rst in vivo successful characterization of plant SCPL-AT in a non-plant host, which is an impressive achievement of great importance for future functional expression of plant SCPL-ATs in synthetic biology study. Moreover, further studies on AbLS by integrating enzyme activity, substrate specicity and crystal structure are essential to elucidate the detailed functional mechanism of AbLS in vivo.

3.2.9 Littorine mutase/monooxygenase CYP80F1 and hyoscyamine dehydrogenase HDH. An enigmatic carbon skeletal rearrangement was observed on the ester moiety during the transformation of littorine 38 to hyoscyamine 1. There were two major speculations on the nature of the enzyme responsible for this skeletal isomerization. The rst was an enzyme with a mechanism and properties similar to vitamin $\mathbf { B } _ { 1 2 }$ -dependent isomerases, which was mainly based on the suggested vicinal interchange process that occurred during the biosynthesis of the tropic acid moiety from phenylalanine 36. 147–149 However, this mechanism was disproved by further stable-isotope labeling studies (vide supra),92,150 and a P450- type process was proposed instead.10,90 This speculation was supported by the observation that the P450 inhibitor clotrimazole could inhibit the conversion of littorine 38 to hyoscyamine 1 in the roots of D. stramonium. 151 Interestingly, several years later, a P450 (CYP80F1) involved in the rearrangement of littorine 38 to hyoscyamine 1 was identied in H. niger by Covello and coworkers.152 They constructed a subtracted cDNA library enriched in genes expressed specically in H. niger root cultures and chose one of the 25 putative P450 ESTs for functional analysis since its homologs were shown to be involved in plant secondary metabolism. Serendipitously, suppression of the P450 gene corresponding to the chosen EST by VIGS resulted in a notably decreased proportion of hyoscyamine 1, and an increased amount of littorine 38 in H. muticus, another Hyoscyamus species. Further conclusive evidence supporting the involvement of CYP80F1 in the rearrangement of littorine 38 to hyoscyamine 1 came from the observation that hyoscyamine 1 could be detected in CYP80F1-expressing tobacco hairy roots supplied with (R)-littorine 38. Hyoscyamine $6 \beta$ -hydroxylase (H6H), an enzyme that stereospecically converts L-hyoscyamine 1 to $6 \beta$ - hydroxyhyoscyamine 5, was rst partially puried from H. niger root cultures by Hashimoto and Yamada in 1986.154 2-Oxoglutarate, ferrous ions and molecular oxygen were established as essential factors for the reaction, and the decarboxylation of 2- oxoglutarate was accompanied by the hydroxylation of hyoscyamine 1, both of which were signs suggesting that HnH6H was a 2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenase. Moreover, H6H activity could be detected in extracts of various scopolamineproducing plant cultures, which suggested the universal involvement of H6H in the oxidative conversion of hyoscyamine 1 to scopolamine 2. In a further investigation, it was demonstrated that HnH6H had a rather low specicity for the substrate since alkaloids with only modest structural similarities to Lhyoscyamine 1, such as norhyoscyamine, phenylacetyltropine, L-homatropine, and apoatropine, and others could be hydroxylated by HnH6H at the C-6 position of the tropane moiety as well.155 Surprisingly, when 6,7-dehydrohyoscyamine 42 was chosen as the substrate, the formation of scopolamine 2 was observed under the typical reaction conditions, revealing the putative epoxidase activity present in HnH6H. Intriguingly, another 2-oxoglutarate-dependent dioxygenase from H. niger root cultures capable of catalyzing the epoxidation of $6 \beta$ - hydroxyhyoscyamine 5 to scopolamine 2 with retention of $6 \beta$ - hydroxy oxygen and loss of the $7 \beta \cdot$ -hydrogen was partially puri-ed.156 Notably, the activity of this epoxidase was observed in exactly the same fractions as the active H6H during partial purication, and showed precisely the same co-factors

# 3.3 Engineering biosynthesis of TAs in microorganisms

From the rst biosynthetic theory proposed by Robinson in 1917 to the characterization of the last missing enzyme, HDH in 2020, it took more than a century to complete the biosynthetic story of hyoscyamine 1 and scopolamine 2 in Solanaceae plants (Fig. 2). Initially, observations on in vitro chemical reactions were the only information available to imagine what may happen in plants to generate the specialized structure of TAs (1900s). Then, isotopically labeled precursor feeding studies shed light on the putative reactions and mechanisms employed by plants in synthesizing the tropane skeleton from simple molecules, although this was a confusing and puzzling journey (1930s–1990s). The characterization of enzymes catalyzing the formation of each intermediate in the TA biosynthesis pathway using the predicted substrates provided the nal conclusive evidence for elucidating the biosynthetic mechanisms of TAs (1980s–now). Particularly, modern differential transcriptome proling based on the tissue-specic biosynthesis of TAs greatly accelerated the pace in identifying key enzymes. Moreover, the wide distribution and mature genetic manipulation methods of TA-producing Solanaceae plants are two essential factors for the successful elucidation of the biosynthetic pathway leading to hyoscyamine 1 and scopolamine 2, which are also the possible reasons why the biosynthetic route of cocaine 3 is still unsolved.

The identication of enzyme blocks in TA biosynthesis providing essential genetic units for de novo building the TA biosynthesis pathway in a heterologous host. The rst attempt

However, the milestone breakthrough in this eld came when Srinivasan and Smolke successfully engineered a yeast strain to produce hyoscyamine 1 and scopolamine 2 from simple sugars and amino acids by integrating more than twenty enzymes adapted from different organisms.146 This work was a great challenge due to the ingenuities in protein engineering and understandings of metabolism in yeast since it is not only implicated in functional expression of a bunch of enzymes native to different plant cells in unicellular microorganisms but also involved in modulating multiple yeast metabolic uxes. # 4 Comparison of Robinson's synthesis of tropinone and the biosynthesis in plants

At the beginning of the 20th century, basic methods designed to isolate and determine structure of natural products had already been established. However, the knowledge and techniques

available for organic chemists and biochemists to explore the reactions and mechanisms underlying their biosynthesis were very limited. This situation was ameliorated when Robinson published his ground-breaking chemical synthesis of tropinone 11. 22 Considering the tropinone architecture elucidated by skeletal dissection, Robinson concluded that it would likely be readily accessible using simple chemical reactions and basic chemical building blocks. In an elegant and extraordinary compliment to his theoretical proposition, tropinone 11 was obtained in the laboratory in an impressive yield of $4 2 \%$ by the condensation of succindialdehyde 12 with acetonedicarboxylic acid 14 and methylamine 13 in a neutral aqueous solution at ambient temperature (Fig. 3). The astounding simplicity of this synthesis prompted Robinson to make another ambitious presumption, that is, plants likely employ similar chemical methods and use similar chemical building blocks in their biosynthesis of tropinone.23 Evidence in support of this came from nature through the fact that the co-occurring alkaloids hygrine 15 and cuscohygrine 16 could be dissected into the same units as that of tropinone 11, and was underscored by developments in the laboratory, in which formaldehyde was demonstrated to play an important role in the production of alkaloids in plants, and both the methylation and oxidation of amino-alcohols by formaldehyde yielded methylaminoketones. Considering these facts, Robinson proposed a biosynthetic pathway in which the methylation and oxidation of ornithine 17 would yield 4-methylaminobutanal 18 or succinaldehyde 12 and methylamine 13, oxidation of citric acid or a polyketide pathway would provide acetonedicarboxylic acid 14 or a similar reactive acetone derivative, and Mannich condensation of acetonedicarboxylic acid 14 with 4-methylaminobutanal 18 or with succinaldehyde 12 and methylamine 13 would yield tropinone 11, hygrine 15, and cuscohygrine 16 (Scheme 1). # 5 Conclusion and perspective

Scientists have unraveled the mysteries of hyoscyamine 1, scopolamine 2, and cocaine 3 biosynthesis in plants, and the de novo production of hyoscyamine 1 and scopolamine 2 in yeast has recently been achieved.146 However, many unresolved questions still remain. It has been demonstrated that two different types of reductase (TRI/TRII vs. MecgoR) and acyltransferase (LS vs. EcCS) participate in the reduction of tropinone 11 and methylecgonone 19, and the esterication of tropine 4 and methylecgonine 32 in scopolamine 2 and cocaine 3 biosynthesis, respectively. Does this indicate that the ability of plants to synthesize TAs has evolved completely independently in different families? Or perhaps that they employ divergent enzymes but similar mechanisms for constructing the tropane skeleton in different types of TA? Therefore, it is necessary to not only determine the genes involved in the biosynthesis of well-established TAs, such as hyoscyamine 1, scopolamine 2, and cocaine 3, but to further explore the enzymes implicated in the biosynthesis of other TAs, such as the calystegines (Table 1, type III) in certain species of Convolvulaceae, and the $4 \alpha$ -benzyltropanes (Table 1, type V) and pyranotropanes (Table 1, type VI) detected in some plants from the Proteaceae family. Moreover, the identication of new enzymes will likely nd judicious application in synthetic biology for the production of quantitatively and qualitatively improved TAs with enormous structural diversity and biological activities. The advent of genome and transcriptome sequencing technology, bioinformatic approaches and metabolomics analysis has greatly expedited the in-depth characterization of genes pertinent to the biosynthetic pathways of numerous TAs, and progress towards the elucidation of unsolved biosynthetic pathways will proceed at a much greater speed than it did in the 20th century. Furthermore, the use of X-ray crystallography to interrogate enzymes altered with site-directed mutagenesis techniques will facilitate a better understanding of key enzymes used by nature, paving the way for the further rational design of synthases, and ultimately allowing access to novel TAs with a myriad of important pharmacological properties. Finally, analyzing the origin and evolution of the TA biosynthetic pathways at the genome level is

also the focus of future research to understand the biological roles of TAs for plants.
