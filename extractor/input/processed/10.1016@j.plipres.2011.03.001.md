# a b s t r a c t

The plant galactolipids, monogalactosyldiacylglycerol (MGDG) and digalactosyldiacylglycerol (DGDG), are the most abundant lipids in chloroplast membranes, and they constitute the majority of total membrane lipids in plants. MGDG is synthesized by two types of MGDG synthase, type-A (MGD1) and type-B (MGD2, MGD3). These MGDG synthases have distinct roles in Arabidopsis. In photosynthetic organs, Type A MGD is responsible for the bulk of MGDG synthesis, whereas Type B MGD is expressed in non-photosynthetic organs such as roots and flowers and mainly contributes to DGDG accumulation under phosphate deficiency. Similar to MGDG synthesis, DGDG is synthesized by two synthases, DGD1 and DGD2; DGD1 is responsible for the majority of DGDG synthesis, whereas DGD2 makes its main contribution under phosphate deficiency. These galactolipid synthases are regulated by light, plant hormones, redox state, phosphatidic acid levels, and various stress conditions such as drought and nutrient limitation. Maintaining the appropriate ratio of these two galactolipids in chloroplasts is important for stabilizing thylakoid membranes and maximizing the efficiency of photosynthesis. Here we review progress made in the last decade towards a better understanding of the pathways regulating plant galactolipid biosynthesis.

# 1. Introduction

Two plant galactolipids, monogalactosyldiacylglycerol (MGDG) and digalactosyldiacylglycerol (DGDG), are the most abundant lipids produced in oxygen-evolving photosynthetic organisms. Indeed, in thylakoid membranes of spinach chloroplasts, MGDG and DGDG constitute $5 2 \%$ and $2 6 \%$ of total glycerolipids, respectively [1]. These galactolipids are also the major lipids in cyanobacteria [2,3]. The similarity in lipid composition of plant chloroplast and cyanobacteria is likely due to the common evolutionary origin, namely that chloroplasts are thought to have arisen from an ancient cyanobacterium via endosymbiosis [4]. MGDG and DGDG are thought to affect the efficiency of photosynthesis because these galactolipids are mainly observed in oxygen-evolving photosynthetic organisms. Indeed, analysis of the crystal structures of photosystems I and II from cyanobacteria indicated that these galactolipids are components of the photosynthetic apparatus [5–7]. Moreover, analyses of many Arabidopsis mutants of galactolipid synthesis have suggested important roles for galactolipids in photosynthetic activity [8].

The functions and biosynthetic pathways of MGDG and DGDG have long been the subject of research, but the first identification of a gene involved in galactolipid synthesis, i.e., cucumber MGDG synthase, was achieved only a decade ago [9]. Three MGDG synthases were subsequently identified in Arabidopsis [10], and these findings formed the foundation for deciphering the mechanism of MGDG synthesis by genetic approaches. Subsequent identification of a DGDG synthase prompted an intensive search for the major genes responsible for galactolipid synthesis [11–14]. Since then, many Arabidopsis galactolipid synthase mutants have been analyzed, revealing new functions for galactolipids and the regulation of their biosyntheses in plastids and suggesting that most galactolipid functions in plant plastids are indispensable and not replaceable by other plastid lipids. Moreover, it was recently elucidated that their roles are not limited to physiological functions inside plastids [15]. For example, under phosphate-limiting conditions, DGDG produced in plastids is exported to membranes outside of plastids where it compensates for decreases in phospholipid levels [16]. Here we discuss the regulation of galactolipid biosynthesis in higher plants as summarized in Table 1 and focus on recent findings pertaining to galactolipid function. We refer readers to another highly regarded review that covers the details of galactolipid structure and function [8] and a recent review of chloroplast lipid metabolism [17].

# 2. Biosynthesis of galactolipids

# 2.1. Monogalactosyldiacylglycerol

The first model for plant MGDG biosynthesis in plastids was proposed in 1958 [18], and then Douce first demonstrated that the biosynthesis of plant MGDG occurs exclusively in plastid envelope membranes [19]. The key reaction is catalyzed by MGDG synthase (UDP-galactose: 1,2-sn-diacylglycerol 3- $\cdot \beta$ -D-galactosyltransferase, MGD) which transfers galactose from UDP-galactose (UDP-Gal) to diacylglycerol (DAG) to produce MGDG as shown in Fig. 1 [2]. Because MGDG is the most abundant glycerolipid in plants, many research groups have studied the biochemistry of MGDG synthesis. Early studies mainly used envelope membranes isolated from chloroplasts. In Spinacia oleracea, MGD activity was detected in the inner envelope membrane of chloroplasts [1], whereas in Pisum sativum the activity was found in the outer envelope membrane [20]. The results confused many researchers who had sought to clarify the site of MGDG synthesis. However, these studies revealed MGD to be an enzyme of high specific activity but present in only trace amounts

in chloroplast envelope membranes, which, in addition to the typical instability of membrane-localized enzymes after solubilization from membranes, confounded the purification and identification of MGD from plants [21]. Therefore, initial biochemical studies were mainly performed using isolated envelope membranes from chloroplasts [22–26]. By taking an advantage of excellent biochemical techniques for isolating chloroplast envelope membranes, however, two groups succeeded in partially purifying MGD from spinach chloroplasts [27,28]. Both groups predicted MGD to be ${ \sim } 2 0 \mathrm { k D a }$ , but its absolute identification proved difficult owing to the limited amounts of the purified protein.

After several years, a breakthrough was made by purifying the enzyme from the microsomal fraction of cucumber cotyledons [9]. The major changes from previous partial purifications were as follows: (1) searching for a suitable starting material that had high specific activity [29], (2) beginning the purification using the microsomal fraction, which is abundant in envelope membranes, and (3) solubilizing MGD with the non-ionic detergent, lauryldimethylamine oxide, which made it possible to use various chromatographic methods during the purification [30]. Although complete purification was not achieved, MGD was predicted to be 47 kDa based on the different chromatography elution profiles [9]. Purification this time differed from previous partial purifications because a sufficient amount of protein was finally obtained for determination of internal amino acid sequences, information from which was used to clone the gene corresponding to the 47- kDa protein by screening a cucumber cDNA library. Because the recombinant protein expressed in Escherichia coli exhibited MGD activity, the 47-kDa protein was finally identified to be cucumber MGD (CsMGD). The program ChloroP [31] predicted that CsMGD contained a transit peptide for targeting to plastids, a prediction that agreed with previous biochemical data on MGD localization in plant cells. E. coli transformant expressing CsMGD accumulated MGDG, which is not a component of E. coli lipids, to comprise $\sim 2 0 \%$ of total lipid and showed a cell division defect [32]. Moreover, CsMGD had modest sequence similarity with E. coli MurG, a glycosyltransferase involved in the formation of peptidoglycan, suggesting that the MGDG biosynthetic pathway in plant plastids evolved symbiotically from the cell wall biosynthetic pathway in bacteria [9].

The identification of the first MGD from cucumber has greatly contributed to the cloning of MGD from other plants. Screening of a spinach cDNA library based on the nucleotide sequence of CsMGD identified a 45-kDa protein as spinach MGD [33]. Because the amino acid sequences of the two enzymes are highly similar, MGD was thought to be ubiquitous among seed plants. Indeed, this prediction was recently verified by construction of a phylogenetic tree of plant MGDs [34].

In Arabidopsis thaliana, Jarvis et al. first analyzed AtMGD1 knock down mutant and showed that AtMGD1 is the unique member of Type A MGD family and is necessary for chloroplast development [35]. Later, three MGD isoforms, AtMGD1, AtMGD2 and AtMGD3, have been isolated [10]. AtMGD2 and AtMGD3 have similar amino acid sequences, and both are less similar to AtMGD1. Therefore, these three isoforms were classified into two subgroups: Type A (AtMGD1) and Type B (AtMGD2 and AtMGD3). Type A has a transit peptide for targeting to plastids, whereas Type B has no predicted transit peptide. Subcellular localization was analyzed using individual isoforms fused to green fluorescent protein transiently expressed in Arabidopsis cells with subsequent western blotting of chloroplast subfractions using isoform-specific antibodies [10]. Indeed, all three MGDs localized to chloroplasts, although their specific sub-chloroplast localization sites differed between Type A (inner envelope membrane) and Type B (outer envelope membrane) [10]. Later, AtMGD1 was also shown to face the envelope inter-membrane space [36].

All recombinant AtMGDs expressed in E. coli showed MGD activity, which utilizes DAG and UDP-Gal as substrates; the molecular species of DAG varied between Type A and B, however, in that Type A utilized 18:2/18:2-DAG and 18:1/16:0-DAG equally, whereas Type B preferred 18:2/18:2-DAG over $1 8 { : } 1 / $ 16:0-DAG [10]. DAG, which is a substrate for all glycerolipid synthases in plastids, is supplied by two pathways: the eukaryotic pathway via the endoplasmic reticulum, and the prokaryotic pathway that occurs exclusively in plastids. The major form of DAG, 18:2/18:2-DAG, is generated via the endoplasmic reticulum and 18:1/16:0-DAG is produced in plastids (Fig. 2). These results suggested that Type A utilizes both prokaryotic and eukaryotic DAG substrates whereas Type B is more specialized for DAG derived from the eukaryotic pathway. A similar result was obtained for spinach MGD, which is a Type A ortholog [24,25,33]. Thus, the DAG substrate preferences of Type A and B MGDs may be common among seed plants. However, seed plants are categorized into ‘‘16:3’’ and ‘‘18:3’’ plants based on the difference of the lipid biosynthetic pathway: ‘‘16:3’’ plants have both the eukaryotic pathway and the prokaryotic pathway, whereas ‘‘18:3’’ plants only possess the eukaryotic pathway. Until now, biochemical properties for Type A MGD from spinach and cucumber, ‘‘18:3’’ plants, and that for Type A and B MGDs from Arabidopsis, ‘‘16:3’’ plants’’, have analyzed, while that for Type B MGD from ‘‘18:3’’ plants has been largely unknown. Although both genes for Type A and B MGDs are widely present and strictly conserved in angiosperms [37], more biochemical data for property of MGDs among ‘‘16:3’’ and ‘‘18:3’’ plants will be needed for supporting their functional generality in higher plants.

# 2.2. Digalactosyldiacylglycerol

The first DGDG synthase (DGD) gene to be identified was from Arabidopsis after the isolation of the DGDG mutant, dgd1 [11]. This mutant was found by lipid analysis of Arabidopsis plants mutagenized using ethyl methanesulfonate, and the gene DGD1 was identified by map-based cloning [12]. DGD1 was confirmed to be a DGD after verification of DGDG production when DGD1 and CsMGD1 were coexpressed in E. coli. The mutant dgd1 still had $\sim 1 0 \%$ of the DGDG content of wild type. Moreover, dgd1 contained more oligogalactolipids, such as trigalactosyldiacylglycerol and tetragalactosyldiacylglycerol, than wild type, suggesting that DGD1 is responsible for bulk DGDG synthesis in Arabidopsis but that another enzyme is responsible for processive galactosylation activity [12]. Later, a DGD1-like gene with weak deduced amino acid sequence similarity to DGD1 was finally isolated and confirmed to be a second DGD, DGD2, by measuring the activity of the corresponding recombinant protein expressed in E. coli [13].

Biochemical studies have clearly shown that both DGD1 and DGD2 are associated with the plastid outer envelope membrane [38]. However, DGD1 and DGD2 likely are delivered to the outer envelope membrane via distinct mechanisms because their primary structures differ substantially, especially at the N terminus, which is truncated in DGD2 compared with DGD1. The C-terminal sequence identity between DGD1 and DGD2 is high $( 5 0 . 8 \% )$ , however, and each enzyme has a C-terminal galactosyltransferase motif. Moreover, although both enzymes utilize MGDG and UDP-gal as substrates (Fig. 1) [13], they likely play distinct roles in plastids, as do the Type A and B MGDs, because their expression profiles are very different [13]. The presence of two types of DGDs also appears to be ubiquitous among seed plants, suggesting they are evolutionarily conserved for their distinct roles, as hypothesized for MGD [13,39].

# 3. Diacylglycerol supply for galactolipid synthesis

Diacylglycerol (DAG) is provided by the prokaryotic pathway or by the eukaryotic pathway in plastids [41]. In the prokaryotic pathway, DAG is produced from phosphatidic acid (PA) by plastidic PA phosphatase (PAP) within the plastids [42]. In the eukaryotic pathway, DAG is derived from PC in the endoplasmic reticulum. In Arabidopsis, lipid transport mechanism via TGD proteins has been proposed [36,40,43–47] and summarized in several review articles [40,48,49]. Recently, another transporting pathway via non-specific phospholipase C5 (NPC5) or phosphatidate phosphohydrolase 1 and 2 (PAH1 and PAH2) was suggested [50,51]. Currently, it is unknown whether these pathways are independent or not.

The pathway that eukaryotic PA is utilized for lipid metabolism in plastids was found by the characterization of the mutants, trigalactosyldiacylglycerol 1 (tgd1), tgd2 and tgd3 [36,43,44,47]. In these mutants, incorporation of the endoplasmic reticulum-derived lipid

precursors into thylakoid lipids and accumulation of triacylglycerols and trigalactolipids is observed [47]. TGD1 was identified as a permease-like protein, a possible component of a multipartite ABC transporter, and the protein was shown to be localized to the inner envelope membrane of plastids [47]. Because incorporation of PA into plastids was reduced and accumulation of PA was observed in tgd1-1 plants, TGD1 was suggested to be a part of a multi-component ABC-type PA transporter [36,40,47–49]. TGD2 was also shown to be a PA-binding protein containing a predicted mycobacterial cell entry domain [43,52]. TGD3 exhibited basal ATPase activity and was also shown to be localized inside the inner envelope membrane [44]. Homologs of TGD1, TGD2, and TGD3 are predicted to be present in Gram-negative bacteria, and the respective genes are organized in an operon, suggesting a common biochemical role for the gene products. Moreover, TGD4 and another extraplastidic protein have been reported to be essential for the lipid transport between the endoplasmic reticulum and plastids [45,46].

MGDG synthesis in the outer envelope membrane is activated under phosphate-limited growth conditions in Arabidopsis [15], suggesting that the metabolic pathway for DAG supply to the outer membrane or partially to the inner membrane may also be activated under such conditions. Based on this hypothesis, it was found that hydrolysis of PC to yield DAG was highly induced upon phosphate starvation [53]. Among two phosphate starvation-inducible phospholipase Cs (NPC4 and NPC5), NPC5 is involved in the membrane lipid remodeling [50,53]. In npc5, a NPC5 knockout mutant, almost half of the DGDG accumulation upon phosphate starvation

was decreased, suggesting that NPC5 is partially responsible for the membrane lipid remodeling and the other pathway using phospholipase D (PLD) in combination with phosphatidate phosphatase (PAP) might complement this defect [50]. Among 12 isoforms of Arabidopsis PLD, $\mathrm { P L D } \zeta 1$ and $\mathrm { P L D } \zeta 2$ are reported to be involved in the remodeling [50,54–56]. Recently, our group reported that Arabidopsis homologs of lipin, phosphatidate phosphohydrolase 1 and 2 (AtPAH1 and AtPAH2) encode a novel type of soluble and extraplastidic PAP [51]. Disruption of AtPAH1 and AtPAH2 affect the eukaryotic pathway of galactolipid biosynthesis significantly, especially under phosphate starvation [51].

# 4. Regulation of MGDG synthesis in photosynthetic organs

In Arabidopsis photosynthetic organs, the Type A MGD, AtMGD1, is the major isoform, is highly expressed, and mediates the bulk of MGDG synthesis. In vitro analysis of the recombinant enzyme expressed in E. coli also showed that AtMGD1 has the highest Vmax and DAG affinity among the three Arabidopsis MGD isoforms [10]. These results suggest that previous biochemical data from plant green tissues mainly reflect the activity of Type A MGDs.

# 4.1. Light and cytokinin

During germination of seed plants, the most important morphological change in plastids upon illumination is the formation of the thylakoid membrane, of which ${ \sim } 8 0 \%$ of the total lipid consists of galactolipids. Indeed, MGD activity increases right after illumination of dark-germinated cucumber seedlings, followed by accumulation of galactolipids [29]. Moreover, detailed biochemical analyses of CsMGD in cucumber cotyledons revealed that MGDG synthesis is regulated by illumination and phytohormones [57]. Upon illumination, MGD expression is quickly induced with a consequent increase in MGD activity and MGDG production. When cotyledons were detached and then illuminated, induction of MGD transcriptional expression and MGD activity was diminished although the accumulation of MGDG was not largely affected, suggesting that light is distinctly involved in MGDG production other than MGD transcriptional activation. On the other hand, treatment of dark-germinated seedlings with the phytohormone, cytokinin, induced MGD expression but only partially recovered MGDG production [57]. Therefore, if cytokinin signaling upon illumination only induces MGD expression, it may be interrupted by detachment of cotyledons because cytokinin is synthesized in the roots and then transported to cotyledons through the hypocotyls in plant seedlings. This suggested that light and cytokinin signaling cooperatively regulate MGDG synthesis [57]. Further analysis revealed that red light is required for MGDG production in seedlings although illumination with far-red light is sufficient to induce CsMGD expression [57]. Later, the effect of illumination and cytokinin on MGDG synthesis was also tested in Arabidopsis [58], and both parameters were found to cooperatively increase AtMGD1 expression but not that of AtMGD2 and AtMGD3. This result is consistent with the fact that AtMGD1 is an ortholog of CsMGD and mediates the bulk of MGDG synthesis in photosynthetic organs. The involvement of cytokinin in the light-mediated increase of AtMGD1 level is also supported by recent analyses of several mutants of cytokinin signaling (K. Kobayashi, S. Baba, T. Masuda and H. Ohta, unpublished data).

# 4.2. Redox regulation

Two research groups that were purifying MGD from spinach found that the reducing agent dithiothreitol (DTT) stabilized

MGD activity [27,28]. Later, isolation of the MGD gene allowed enzymatic characterization of recombinant MGD expressed in E. coli, and thus the redox regulation of MGD activity was analyzed in detail. Addition of DTT to MGD assays increased the activity of recombinant spinach MGD and CsMGD, suggesting that the reduced form of the enzyme has higher activity [33,59]. This DTTmediated activation was not observed by the addition of a thiolblocking reagent such as N-ethylmaleimide or o-phenanthroline. These results suggested that cysteine thiol groups in MGD are involved in regulating the enzyme activity [59]. Moreover, oxidization of DTT-treated CsMGD with $\mathrm { C u C l } _ { 2 }$ blocked MGD activity, suggesting that this redox regulation is reversible at least in vitro. The fact that MGD activity could also be enhanced by the addition of thioredoxin suggested that this compound may be the actual reductant in chloroplasts [59]. Further experiments are required, however, to address the issue of which type of thioredoxin is able to interact with MGD, which faces the inter-membrane space of chloroplast envelope membranes [10,35,36].

# 4.3. Phospholipids

We and others have reported activation of MGD by lipid molecules. Solubilization of MGD from chloroplast membranes caused significant loss of activity, but addition of phosphatidylglycerol to the solubilized enzyme partially recovered the activity [60]. The effect was also observed by addition of sulfoquinovosyldiacylglycerol, suggesting that negatively charged lipids such as phosphatidylglycerol and sulfoquinovosyldiacylglycerol may regulate MGD activity in vivo. However, the concentration of phosphatidylglycerol (PG) or sulfoquinovosyldiacylglycerol in chloroplasts is not likely to be sufficient for the activation. Phosphatidic acid (PA) was subsequently found to activate CsMGD activity at a lower concentration than PG [30]. Recently, Dubots et al. showed that PA and PG effects on MGD activity through different mechanisms by enzymatic analysis of point-mutated recombinant MGDs [61]. Interestingly, if PA is eliminated from isolated chloroplast envelope by activation of PA phosphatase, MGD activity is almost lost even PG is still present in the chloroplasts. However, the activity is recovered by the addition of PA. Although two amino acid residues for activation by PG were identified, the site(s) for PA have not been found. From these results, activation of MGD by the phospholipids is proposed as an important mechanism coupling phospholipid and galactolipid synthesis in plants, but the actual mechanism of MGD activation in vivo remains to be discovered.

# 4.4. Environmental stresses

MGD expression level is affected by many factors. Xu et al. isolated and characterized the dgd1 suppressor mutant, dgs1–1, and found that hydrogen peroxide induced transcription of MGD2 and MGD3 in Arabidopsis [46]. Hydrogen peroxide has been implicated in the signal transduction response to nutrient deprivation in Arabidopsis [62]. Moreover, Shin et al. reported that reactive oxygen species accumulate in response to nutrient starvation in roots [63]. However, it remains to be analyzed whether the effect of hydrogen peroxide is causally linked with phosphate-deprivation effects on galactolipid biosynthesis or if they are parallel processes.

Oryza sativa MGD, which has a markedly truncated N terminus, may represent a type of MGD distinct from Type A or B from other plants [64]. The overall amino acid sequence of O. sativa MGD is similar to Type B MGDs, and the level of its transcript is increased by submergence, drought, or salt treatment, but not by cold stress [64]. In cowpea leaves, the levels of MGD1, MGD2, DGD1 and DGD2 mRNAs increase after drought stress [65]. However, galactolipidlabeling experiments showed that the results obtained for the two cultivars tested were not amenable to straightforward inter-

pretation: in one cultivar, both MGDG and DGDG levels decreased in response to drought, whereas in another cultivar MGDG was unchanged and DGDG increased [65]. Harwood noted a salt-induced decrease in chloroplast lipids [66], and a salt-induced reduction in galactolipid synthesis has been observed in olive [67] and oilseed rape [68], and in Catharanthus roseus cultured cell suspensions [69]. Among the three Arabidopsis MGD isoforms, only AtMGD1 expression increased after wounding in shoots or drought or salt stress in roots [37]; however, the amounts of galactolipids have been not measured under these conditions in Arabidopsis. Therefore, discrepancies remain regarding the level of MGD expression and MGDG and DGDG content in plants. Furthermore, the physiological relevance of upregulated MGD expression remains unknown, and the effect of overexpression of Type A MGD on resistance to stresses must be assessed.

# 5. Regulation of MGDG synthesis in non-photosynthetic organs

Several groups have reported that non-photosynthetic organs contain large amounts of galactolipids [70,71]. Indeed, in non-photosynthetic organs of Arabidopsis, AtMGD2 is highly expressed in flowers, and AtMGD3 is highly expressed in roots [10]. AtMGD3 was found to be highly expressed in young seedlings, but the level decreased with maturation [10]. These expression patterns were also analyzed using a $\beta$ -glucuronidase (GUS) reporter gene. MGD1 promoter harboring GUS (MGD1::GUS) was constitutively expressed in all Arabidopsis green tissues [72], whereas the expression of MGD2::GUS or MGD3::GUS was restricted to pollen tubes, suggesting that MGDG synthesis by Type B MGDs may play a role in pollen tube growth. In flowers of Petunia hybrida, DGDG was identified as the major galactolipid, although the MGDG:DGDG ratio in photosynthetic organs was approximately 2:1, as expected for photosynthetic membranes [73,74]. In addition, the MGD activity increased during flower development, especially in mature pistils. These results demonstrated that substantial amounts of MGDG are produced by Type B MGDs in flowers. In Lilium longiflorum, accumulation of galactolipids was observed during pollen tube growth [75]. However, recent analyses of a double knockout mutant of Type B MGD (mgd2mgd3) in Arabidopsis did not show a defect in pollen tube growth [34], suggesting that AtMGD1 may be able to compensate for a lack of Type B MGD function in reproductive processes.

# 6. Regulation of DGDG synthesis

DGD1 is constitutively expressed and DGD1 is involved in the bulk of DGDG synthesis in plants. However, DGD1 expression is upregulated under certain stress conditions. AtDGD1 expression increases under phosphate-limiting conditions [14], nitrogen-limiting conditions [76], and drought stress [77]. Under phosphate-limiting conditions, DGD1 is partially responsible for the observed increase in DGDG in leaves [78], and the DGDG:MGDG ratio increases under nitrogen-limiting conditions or drought stress conditions [76,77]. The balance between the levels of the non-lamellar-forming lipid MGDG and the lamellar-forming lipid DGDG largely affects the stability of thylakoid membranes [79]. Moreover, a certain temperature-sensitive Arabidopsis mutant was found to be a mutant of DGD1 [80]. However, under high-growth temperature, the DGDG:MGDG ratio increased in wild-type plants, whereas DGD1 expression level remained unchanged during the shift to high temperature. As was found for DGD1, the level of DGD2 increased under phosphate-limiting conditions [14], but other factors that affect DGD2 expression remain to be defined.

The peribacteroid membranes surrounding nitrogen-fixing rhizobia in the nodules of legumes play a pivotal role for the exchange of ammonium and nutrients between the bacteria and

the host cell. DGDG of plant origin was detected in the peribacteroid membranes of soybean, Glycine max and Lotus japonicus [39]. Expression of DGD1 and DGD2 from soybean and Lotus increased during later stages of nodule development in infected cells and in cortical tissue. Because nodule development requires high amounts of phosphate in the growth medium, the DGDG accumulation in the peribacteroid membrane might be important to save phosphate for other essential processes.

# 7. Regulation of galactolipid synthesis under phosphate-starved conditions

In most organisms, phosphate is an essential nutrient for development and reproduction. Phosphate is crucial for signaling cascades and is also an essential component of nucleic acids and phospholipids [81]. In plants, phosphate in the soil or growth medium is absorbed by roots, but the amount of phosphate available in soil is often meagre because phosphate primarily exists in the form of insoluble salts. Therefore, morphology and biochemical processes can be markedly affected when plants are exposed to a phosphate-limiting growth environment. Using an Arabidopsis whole-genome Affymetrix gene chip, Misson et al. analyzed phosphate starvation–induced changes in global gene expression, and the results correlated well with a study that examined how biochemical pathways respond to phosphate starvation [82].

When plants are transferred to phosphate-limiting growth medium, the membrane lipid composition changes considerably. DGDG levels increase [78,83,84], and then DGDG is transported out of chloroplasts to the plasma membrane, mitochondrial membrane, and tonoplast membranes, where it displaces phospholipids as shown in Fig. 2 [85–87]. The substitution of phosphoglycerolipids with glycoglycerolipids is an essential process under phosphate-limited growth conditions [34,88,89]. The membrane remodeling in response to phosphate starvation is suggested to be ubiquitous to all land plants [90].

In Arabidopsis, several genes for galactolipid biosynthesis (MGD2/3, DGD1/2) are activated upon phosphate starvation [10,13,14]. Because MGDG levels increase only modestly during phosphate starvation, MGDG produced by Type B MGDs is thought to be directly used for subsequent DGDG synthesis. The importance of Type B MGDs in phosphate starvation–induced lipid remodeling was demonstrated by Kobayashi et al. via analysis of Type B MGD knockout mutants that displayed a severe phenotype under phosphate-limiting conditions [34]. Under phosphate starvation, a single mutant, mgd3-1, displayed significantly reduced accumulation of DGDG, especially in roots, suggesting that MGD3 is mainly responsible for the observed increase in DGDG under such conditions. Moreover, in mgd2mgd3 double mutants, DGDG accumulation in roots was nearly abolished, clearly showing that Type B MGDs are indispensable for remodeling of membrane lipid composition during phosphate starvation in roots.

In roots grown under phosphate starvation, lateral roots and root hairs attain a greater size than under normal conditions. This morphological change is similar to that triggered by the plant hormone, auxin. Moreover, the pattern of GUS staining of Type B MGD::GUS under phosphate starvation is similar to that observed for auxin-induced GUS activity from an auxin-responsive element harboring GUS (DR5::GUS) construct [58,91,92]. For both constructs, GUS staining was observed in the epithem cells of hydathodes, the base of trichomes, stipules, and lateral root branches. Indeed, has been suggested that changes in root architecture induced by phosphate starvation correlate with an increase in auxin sensitivity [93]. Treatment of phosphate-starved roots with the auxin inhibitors 2,3,5-triiodobenzole (an auxin transport inhibitor), $p$ -chlorophenoxyisobutyric acid (an inhibitor of auxin effects),

or N-(1-naphthyl) phthalamic acid (an auxin transport inhibitor) abated GUS staining of Type B MGD, suggesting that phosphate starvation–induced expression of Type B MGDs requires auxin [58].

Another plant hormone, cytokinin, affects root elongation and lateral root formation. Cytokinin treatment of plant roots inhibited Pi starvation–induced morphological changes and repressed the expression of Type B MGDs [58]. Moreover, simultaneous treatment with cytokinin and auxin slightly recovered Type B MGD expression, suggesting that the effects of these plant hormones are antagonistic with respect to Type B MGD expression during phosphate starvation [58]. As a result, the increase in DGDG level upon phosphate starvation was eliminated by treatment with cytokinin in roots but not in shoots. Moreover, the axr4-2aux1-7 double mutant, which is insensitive to auxin, [94,95] showed a decrease in both Type B MGD upregulation and DGDG accumulation upon phosphate starvation. These data suggested that auxin signaling plays an important role, but is not sufficient for, the activation of Type B MGD expression [58].

Phosphite, an inactive analog of the phosphate anion, suppresses various responses induced by phosphate starvation [96,97]. Phosphate-starved plants treated with phosphite had decreased expression of Type B MGDs and accumulation of DGDG. Treatment of these plants with auxin, however, did not recover expression of Type B MGD, indicating that suppression of Type B MGDs by phosphite is independent of auxin [58]. These data suggest that the expression of Type B MGDs is regulated by a phosphate-sensing signaling system rather than by phosphate availability in the cell. Moreover, Lai et al. suggested that the expression of MGD3 during phosphate starvation is mainly regulated by cell division activity, which in turn determines the demand for phosphate in phosphate-starved plants [98]. Our group recently revealed that auxin signaling plays an important part in increasing the expression of phosphate limitation–responsive genes [99].

# 8. Perspectives

Plant galactolipids localize primarily in photosynthetic tissues. However, the conditional increase in their levels upon environmental stresses such as drought or salt or their restricted accumulation in non-photosynthetic tissues clearly demonstrates their important roles in processes other than thylakoid development during chloroplast biogenesis. Further analyses are required to unravel the role of galactolipids and the functional regulation of the galactolipid synthases under stress conditions.

It is thought that the roles of Type A and B MGDs are at least distinguishable by differences in their localization patterns in plastid envelope membranes: Type A MGD is in the inner envelope membrane whereas Type B MGD is in the outer envelope membrane. Differences in the roles of these MGD types under phosphate-limiting conditions have been well described by this model, but other functions of these enzymes remain unknown. Genetic analyses of knockout mutants have shown that both MGD types are able to partially compensate for each other. An ongoing quest is the analysis of mutants whose localization is altered by genetic means. Moreover, biochemical analyses of AtMGDs will again be required because regulation of Type B MGD by redox or lipids has remained untested.

Using a phylogenetic tree, Kobayashi et al. showed clearly that Type B MGDs are conserved in seed plants as a means of surviving under phosphate-limiting growth conditions during the evolution of land plants [34]. A gene for MGD was found in the unicellular green alga Chlamydomonas reinhardtii, but not in cyanobacteria (M. Shimojima, H. Masuda, E. Nagata, and H. Ohta, unpublished

data). How plant chloroplasts acquired MGD has been a long-unanswered question since the first CsMGD was identified and no homolog has been found in cyanobacterial genomes. In cyanobacteria, MGDG is synthesized via monoglucosyldiacylglycerol (MGlcDG), which is produced by MGlcDG synthase, and the subsequent epimerization of glucose to galactose by a still unidentified epimerase [100–105]. No MGlcDG synthase homolog has been identified in plants, suggesting that at some point during chloroplast evolution MGlcDG synthase was discarded and MGD was acquired. Moreover, cyanobacterial MGlcDG synthase was recently found to be activated by heat stress [106]. Thus, MGlcDG synthase in cyanobacteria could be a crucial enzyme for surviving high temperatures by modulating membrane lipid composition, an attribute that is no longer required for plant cells as a consequence of symbiosis. Lastly, authors should note here that, after submission of this manuscript, another breakthrough of galactolipid research was made by Moellering et al. [107,108].
