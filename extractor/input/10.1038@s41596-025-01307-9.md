# INFOGEST Quant: standardized in vitro determination of digestibility and DIAAS of dietary proteins based on the INFOGEST static digestion model

Lotti Egger  1,5, Ana Blanco-Doval  1,5, Raquel Sousa1 , Cédric Brügger  1 , Eliane Binz1 , André Brodkorb  2 , Didier Dupont  3 , Isidra Recio  4 & Reto Portmann  1,5

# Abstract

This protocol describes a standardized in vitro method to determine the digestibility and digestible indispensable amino acid score (DIAAS) of dietary proteins. This ‘INFOGEST Quant’ method is an extension of our previous INFOGEST static digestion model (INFOGEST 2.0) and adds a workfow for the quantifcation of total protein digestibility, individual amino acid digestibility and DIAAS. The protocol was validated using in vivo data obtained by digesting the same food samples, and the results showed a high degree of agreement, confrming its relevance for nutritional assessments. To establish the DIAAS of a protein source, nonabsorbable peptides and proteins are precipitated after in vitro digestion and the resulting absorbable fraction is analyzed using ultrahighperformance liquid chromatography with ultraviolet detection (to assess total and individual amino acids). Two alternative quantifcation strategies are also described: protein titration using the Kjeldahl method (to assess total nitrogen) and spectrophotometric analysis with $o$ -phthalaldehyde (to assess total amino groups). Both alternative methods are valid only for the calculation of total digestibility and the proxy-digestible indispensable amino acid ratio, which gives an approximation of the DIAAS of the tested protein sources. Compared to existing approaches, this protocol is suitable for routine application in nutrition and food science laboratories. The preparatory steps take ~6 d, whereas the full workfow can be completed in triplicate in ~8 d. Analysis of the digesta takes an additional 3–5 d, depending on the method. The procedure requires only standard laboratory equipment and reagents and can be performed by anyone with basic training in biochemistry or a related discipline.

# Introduction

The nutritional quality of dietary proteins is defined by both their amino acid composition and digestibility. To meet human amino acid requirements, proteins must provide digestible indispensable (also referred to as essential) amino acids in sufficient amounts. In 2013, the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) recommended replacing the protein digestibility-corrected amino acid score (PDCAAS) with the digestible indispensable amino acid score (DIAAS). The DIAAS is based on true ileal digestibility values obtained in humans, growing pigs or growing rats (in order of preference)1 . The determination of true ileal amino acid digestibility in humans is more challenging as digesta are typically collected from the ileum via invasive procedures2 . Animal experimentation is an easier option but still requires ethical approval and adherence to the principles of Reduction, Refinement, Replacement and Responsibility for minimizing animal use whenever possible3 . In vivo trials are also not suitable for screening large sample sets, which are necessary to account for the many factors affecting protein digestibility, including the food matrix, processing conditions and protein structural properties. Thus, there is an urgent need for reliable in vitro methods to estimate food protein digestibility and DIAAS in a scalable, ethical and cost-effective manner.

# Development of the protocol

In 2019, the international INFOGEST network published a consensus protocol (INFOGEST 2.0) for the static in vitro simulation of gastrointestinal food digestion4 , building on earlier harmonization efforts in digestion modeling5 . INFOGEST 2.0 (ref. 4) is an improved version of the original INFOGEST protocol, providing clearer definitions of enzyme activities, fluid compositions and experimental conditions to minimize ambiguity and improve consistency across laboratories. It also introduced calculation sheets for enzyme activities and digestion parameters, which simplified protocol implementation and contributed to improved reproducibility. The aim of INFOGEST 2.0 was to harmonize and standardize in vitro procedures for oral, gastric and intestinal digestion across laboratories, thereby enabling comparative research and reproducibility. Since its publication in 2019, the INFOGEST 2.0 protocol has become a cornerstone in food digestion studies, earning highly cited paper status with more than 2,800 citations in Web of Science. Its validated physiological relevance6–8 has led to its widespread use in several areas of research9 . The method was intended for the study of food structure, nutrient bioavailability, probiotic survival and microbiota modulation; however, protein quality evaluation—especially with respect to amino acid digestibility and DIAAS—was not among its original applications.

Historically, a variety of methods have been used to estimate protein quality, including the amino acid score, protein efficiency ratio, nitrogen balance, net protein ratio, protein rating, net protein utilization and biological value10,11. In 1989, a joint FAO/World Health Organization expert consultation recommended PDCAAS as the standard metric11. PDCAAS corrects the amino acid composition of a food protein with total crude protein digestibility, offering a more functionally relevant metric than previous indices. However, it fails to account for the fact that individual amino acids are digested and absorbed at different rates. This simplification can lead to inaccurate estimations, particularly for indispensable amino acids12–14.

Recognizing these limitations, a subsequent FAO expert consultation in 2013 proposed the DIAAS, which corrects the amino acid content on the basis of true ileal digestibility values for each indispensable amino acid1 . Although DIAAS is now considered the gold standard, its determination typically requires invasive or ethically complex in vivo studies involving animal models or humans12,13,15–17. To address this, the FAO explicitly called for the development of a standardized and independently validated in vitro assay for protein digestibility1 .

Building on this recommendation, Sousa et al.8 recently adapted the INFOGEST digestion protocol to develop an in vitro model to determine true ileal protein digestibility, digestible indispensable amino acid ratios (DIAAR) and DIAAS values. DIAAR is the ratio of the absorbed indispensable amino acid relative to the requirements, and DIAAS is the lowest DIAAR of a protein, which corresponds to the limiting amino acid. The model was validated against in vivo

The in vitro results were directly compared to true ileal digestibility data obtained from human and pig studies, as recommended by the FAO1 . In addition, since the first direct comparisons, the method has been applied to assess digestibility of vegan versus meat burgers18, insects versus chicken meat19, and different soy products20. Although these were not direct comparisons of the exact same food sources, the method demonstrated high predictive accuracy across the tested products. Additional direct comparisons across a wider range of foods and whole meals in the future will strengthen the validity of the protocol, as well as show its limits.

This method is currently undergoing formal standardization through the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) and the International Dairy Federation (IDF). The corresponding ISO (ISO 24167:2024) and IDF (ID 261:2024) standards are presently in the Draft International Standard stage, with publication anticipated within the coming year. Upon adoption, this will constitute the first globally standardized analytical method for in vitro determination of protein digestibility.

The present protocol provides a complete, structured description of the method, including critical steps, optional modifications and validation parameters, thereby facilitating its implementation and reproducibility across research, industry and regulatory laboratories.

Following these preparatory steps, the protocol begins with the preparation of the protein test samples, which must first be processed to simulate realistic consumption conditions, such as formulation, hydration or processing (for example, heating), depending on the food type (Step 10). Protein powders that are not consumed as such must be combined with a proteinfree cookie before digestion to simulate realistic intake conditions. Solid foods are ground to a particle size of $- 2 - 3 \mathsf { m m }$ , simulating chewing. The amount of food to be digested is adjusted to 40 mg of crude protein, equivalent to 6.4 mg of nitrogen, ensuring a constant ratio between the substrate and digestive enzymes. A nitrogen-to-protein conversion factor (NCF) of 6.25 is used, as this value is currently applied in all in vivo digestibility studies and must be maintained in vitro to ensure comparability. To this end, the total nitrogen in the food sample must be determined in advance, preferably using the Kjeldahl method (Step 4)23. The sample is then diluted with Milli-Q water to a final mass of 1 g. The NCF of 6.25 has been defined by the FAO1 for the in vivo determination of DIAAS. To allow meaningful comparisons between in vitro and in vivo results, the use of this standard factor is therefore recommended. While many foods in fact have lower true NCF values, using the smaller NCF leads to an increase in the resulting DIAAR and DIAAS values. If, for any reason, the use of NCF 6.25 is not appropriate, we recommend avoiding arbitrary alternative NCFs and instead calculating DIAAR and DIAAS on the basis of the total measured (dehydrated) amino acid content24.

The actual digestion part of the procedure (Steps 15–31) follows the original INFOGEST 2.0 static protocol reported by Brodkorb et al.4 , which simulates the sequential oral, gastric and intestinal phases under standardized conditions. After digestion, the reaction mixture is treated with ice-cold methanol, which precipitates undigested proteins and peptides longer than ~10 amino acids8 . The resulting supernatant and pellet, representing the absorbable and nonabsorbable fractions, are collected by centrifugation.

Protein digestibility, DIAAR and DIAAS are determined by quantifying the individual amino acids in both fractions by ultrahigh-performance liquid chromatography with ultraviolet detection (UHPLC-UV)25. Alternatively, if only total digestibility or proxy-DIAAS (an approximation of DIAAS) is needed, the pellet and supernatant can be analyzed for either the total nitrogen (Kjeldahl method23) or total amino groups (o-phthalaldehyde (OPA) method). Importantly, the digestion pellets are not homogeneous matrices, and splitting them to perform multiple analytical procedures can lead to inaccurate digestibility measurements. This was demonstrated in a controlled experiment (Supplementary Results 1). Therefore, if total nitrogen is to be analyzed alongside total amino acid (and/or amino group) quantification, each sample must be digested twice, in triplicate. However, amino acid and amino group analyses share the same hydrolysis step and can thus be performed from the same pellet and hydrolysate, reducing the number of digestions required when combining these analyses.

# Applications

This protocol offers a robust and standardized approach for the in vitro estimation of the quality of dietary proteins by determining total and individual amino acid digestibility, and DIAAS values for different age groups—including infants, children and adults—on the basis of the physiologically relevant conditions of the INFOGEST 2.0 digestion model4 . One of its major advantages is its flexibility; it accommodates various types of protein source and offers multiple analytical options depending on available instrumentation and expertise, making it suitable for use in both academic and applied research contexts. In addition, it is suitable for routine application in nutrition research and product development owing to its moderate complexity, reliance on widely available laboratory equipment and lower resource-dependence and ethical complexity than in vivo experiments. The option of combining different quantification methods (individual amino acids by UHPLC, total nitrogen by Kjeldahl or free amino groups by OPA) allows the protocol to be tailored to specific research questions or practical constraints.

The method has been validated against in vivo data obtained from the same food matrices (collagen, whey protein isolate (WPI), pigeon peas, peanuts, black beans, All-Bran wheat bran cereal and zein), demonstrating strong predictive capacity8 . Since its publication, the procedure has been applied by us and others to a variety of food matrices, including animalbased products such as minced beef18,26 and dairy products27–29, and plant-based products such as soy, wheat, faba bean, maize and pea18,20,26,27,30. I t has also been used to evaluate complex or processed foods, such as protein bars31. In the context of alternative proteins, the method has been applied to yeast27, brewers’ spent yeast (a brewing industry byproduct32), edible insects19 and seaweed33,34. These applications demonstrate the versatility of the protocol in accommodating diverse substrates with varying structural, nutritional, and processing characteristics. As a result, the scientific validation of the method continues to grow, strengthening its relevance and credibility for protein quality assessment.

The growing body of published research using this method is also helping to build a comprehensive database of in vitro protein digestibility and DIAAR values across food categories. Such a database is essential for supporting evidence-based nutritional guidance and empowering consumers to make informed dietary choices. The method has also been integrated into sustainability frameworks (for example, in life-cycle assessments) where digestibility-adjusted protein values are increasingly considered for evaluating the true nutritional value of food relative to its environmental footprint35. Finally, the protocol provides a robust platform for further method development, allowing adaptations to model specific physiological states (for example, pediatric or elderly digestion), study the effects of matrix interactions or assess the influence of non-nutritional components and processing-related modifications on protein quality.

# Comparison with other methods

Several approaches have historically been used to estimate protein quality, including biological assays such as the protein efficiency ratio, net protein utilization and nitrogen balance, as well as chemical scores, such as the amino acid score10,11. While informative, these methods have important limitations. Biological assays are animal based, time consuming and ethically constrained, and they typically provide only an indirect estimate of protein utilization.

Chemical scoring methods, by contrast, do not consider digestibility and tend to overestimate the nutritional value of poorly digested proteins.

The PDCAAS, adopted in the 1990s, introduced the idea of correcting amino acid content for digestibility11, but it is based on true fecal nitrogen digestibility tested in rats, and assumes identical digestibility for all amino acids. This oversimplification can result in overestimation or underestimation of true amino acid availability, especially in foods with imbalanced amino acid profiles or resistant matrix structures. Furthermore, PDCAAS truncates values at $100 \%$ , preventing the recognition of proteins of superior quality that exceed the daily recommendations for amino acids per gram of protein1 .

The FAO 1 now recommends DIAAS, which corrects each indispensable amino acid on the basis of its true ileal digestibility, offering a more accurate and physiologically relevant estimation of protein quality. In vivo models—preferably involving humans, growing pigs or rats—are considered the gold standard for assessing true ileal protein and amino acid digestibility. In humans, such studies require the collection of digesta at the end of the small intestine, typically via naso-ileal intubation in healthy adults, or in individuals with terminal ileostomy. While both options are substantially invasive, the latter also raises concerns owing to potential alterations in digestive physiology related to underlying medical conditions. Moreover, such approaches are not suitable for routine analyses or studies involving vulnerable populations 2 ,36 .

To overcome these challenges, researchers have explored stable isotope techniques using intrinsically labeled proteins as a less invasive in vivo alternative. Although accurate and precise, these methods continue to be complex and resource-intensive and require specialized analytical infrastructure as well as participant recruiting36. Animal models such as pigs and rats provide another option and have been widely used in digestibility studies. Among these, the pig model is preferred owing to its closer anatomical and physiological resemblance to humans, particularly in terms of digestive and metabolic functions. Rats, by contrast, differ more substantially, especially in their elevated requirements for sulfur-containing amino acids37 –39. Despite their value, in vivo studies in animals or humans remain time-consuming, expensive and ethically constrained, especially for testing vegetarian and vegan food. Their use is increasingly limited under current regulatory frameworks advocating for Reduction, Refinement, Replacement and Responsibility.

Against this backdrop, in vitro models are gaining prominence as practical, scalable and ethically acceptable alternatives. Several in vitro methods for assessing total protein digestibility have been proposed, including pH-drop and pH-stat techniques, which estimate digestibility indirectly by measuring changes in pH caused by the release of nutrients, such as free amino acids or fatty acids, during enzymatic hydrolysis40. Although these methods— especially the pH-stat—can correlate well with in vivo data, their reliability is influenced by the buffering capacity of specific food matrices41 and the presence of lipids, which also release fatty acids that alter $\mathsf { p H } ^ { 4 2 }$ . In such cases, lipase inhibitors are required to avoid confounding effects.

Other widely used methods include spectrophotometric assays based on reagents such as ninhydrin, trinitrobenzene sulfonic acid or OPA, as well as commercial enzymatic kits43 –46. Although useful for estimating the extent of proteolysis, these approaches are limited to determining total protein digestibility and do not provide information on the digestibility of individual amino acids41. Therefore, they do not enable the calculation of DIAAS or DIAAR values.

Following the publication of the INFOGEST 2.0 protocol for static in vitro gastrointestinal digestion simulation 4 , several adaptations have been proposed to enable protein quality assessment. These include methods for separating the absorbable and nonabsorbable fractions via centrifugation, acid precipitation, ultrafiltration or combinations thereof43 ,44 ,47,48. These techniques are promising but they lack comprehensive validation against reference in vivo data. By contrast, the present protocol expands on the INFOGEST framework and provides a fully standardized, validated procedure for determining protein and amino acid digestibility, enabling the calculation of both DIAAS and DIAAR. By maintaining physiological relevance, analytical robustness and accessibility, it addresses the key limitations of both in vivo and existing in vitro methods and represents a practical alternative for researchers and food developers alike. However, to adapt it to the evaluation of protein digestibility, the method presented here includes some changes to the in vitro digestion steps from the

INFOGEST 2.0 protocol. For instance, the original protocol defines fixed enzyme activities for each phase regardless of the dietary protein concentration in the test material4 . Although this standardization simplifies interlaboratory comparisons, it may impair the accuracy of digestibility estimations when comparing foods with markedly different protein contents43. Under such conditions, excessive dilution or overload of protein relative to enzyme capacity can occur, potentially leading to underestimation or overestimation of digestibility44,47. To address this issue, the present method standardizes the amount of crude protein in the digestion assay to 40 mg of protein, thereby maintaining a constant protein-to-enzyme ratio across all experiments. In addition, in vitro and ex vivo studies suggest that high fat content negatively affects protein digestion, although the available data are still limited49,50. As the current method does not include gastric lipase, it may not be fully representative of fat-rich foods. Therefore, the applicability of the protocol has been limited to samples containing no more than $1 0 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of fat in the total digesta. It should be noted that there are also important limitations in this protocol. As with any in vitro system, the protocol cannot fully replicate the complexity of human gastrointestinal digestion, including factors such as transit time variability, microbiota interactions and transport mechanisms. Moreover, the precipitation phase (Steps 32–41) used to separate the absorbable fraction is based on solubility in methanol, which may not entirely reflect physiological absorption in the small intestine.

# Experimental design

# Enzyme assays

The key enzymes used in this protocol are pepsin (E.C. 3.4.23.1) and trypsin (E.C. 3.4.21.4), the latter being derived from pancreatin. For starchy foods, salivary α-amylase (E.C. 3.2.1.1) should also be included, as starch–protein interactions can hinder proteolysis and affect overall digestibility51. However, owing to the high cost of salivary $\alpha$ -amylase, its use is optional and can be restricted to foods containing starch.

To ensure experimental reproducibility, the enzymatic activity of pepsin, trypsin (pancreatin) and $\alpha$ -amylase, as well as the concentration of commercial bile extract, must be determined and standardized before use4,5,21. These assays must be repeated for each new batch and after extended enzyme storage to account for potential activity loss over time. Supplementary Data 1 provides a spreadsheet to facilitate the calculation of enzyme and bile stock concentrations.

Consistent with the original INFOGEST 2.0 protocol4,5 , the target enzyme activities for digestion are ${ 7 5 } \mathrm { U / m L }$ for $\alpha$ -amylase in the oral phase, $2 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { U / m L }$ for pepsin in the gastric phase and $1 0 0 \mathrm { U / m L }$ for trypsin activity in pancreatin in the intestinal phase. The final bile acid concentration is standardized at 10 mmol/L in the intestinal phase. The original publication describing the INFOGEST method outlines activity assays for each enzyme; however, updated and streamlined step-by-step protocols are provided here (Supplementary Methods 1). A minor deviation from the original protocol includes the measurement of trypsin activity by dissolving pancreatin in simulated intestinal fluid (SIF) instead of hydrochloric acid (HCl) at 1 mmol/L (pH 3), more closely mimicking the conditions of the intestinal phase. The pancreatin solution is also sonicated and centrifuged to remove insoluble material, reducing within-batch variability and improving the food-to-background signal ratio by eliminating inactive proteins.

Regarding lipid digestion, lipolysis typically begins in the stomach with gastric lipases, which account for ${ \sim } 2 0 \%$ of the total lipolytic activity by weight. These enzymes remain active in the duodenum and have a physiologically relevant role in fat digestion52. However, gastric lipase is excluded from the present protocol, as the focus is on protein digestion, and commercial preparation of gastric lipase (for example, rabbit gastric extract; Lipolytech, https://www. lipolytech.com/) is expensive and not widely available. Nonetheless, a high fat content has been shown to negatively influence protein digestion49,50. To minimize this confounding effect in the absence of gastric lipase, the fat content of the tested foods should not exceed 100 mg in the digestion assay. Excluding gastric lipase means that pancreatin is standardized solely on the basis of trypsin activity. We have chosen not to use individual enzymes (trypsin, chymotrypsin, lipase, colipase and α-amylase) as an alternative to pancreatin, as suggested by Minekus et al.5 , to reduce cost, complexity and hands-on time.

# Samples and the protein-free cookie blank

Accurate assessment of protein digestibility relies on quantifying the proportion of foodderived amino acids that become liberated and thus potentially absorbable during digestion. However, digestive enzymes also contribute amino acids through autolysis, which can confound the results. To correct this, a protein-free cookie composed primarily of carbohydrates and lipids is digested in parallel with each experimental batch. This blank accounts for non-samplederived nitrogenous compounds and serves as a baseline correction. The cookie formulation is based on Moughan et al.53, with minor modifications (Table 1). Briefly, ingredients such as acidinsoluble ash (celite), which are used as indigestible markers in in vivo studies, are excluded, because all components are collected at the end of the intestinal phase and remain available for analysis. In addition, flavoring agents, such as ground ginger, are omitted. Otherwise, the protein-free cookie is subjected to the exact same preparation, digestion and analysis steps as the food samples.

Proper preparation of test samples is critical for achieving physiologically relevant results. Numerous technological and culinary treatments, such as cooking, salting, marinating, drying or curing with nitrates and/or nitrites, have been shown to substantially affect the extent of protein hydrolysis during digestion54. Therefore, each food must be processed in a way that reflects its typical consumption. For example, legumes should be boiled before digestion, whereas yoghurt can be digested in its native state without additional treatment.

Protein powders and other ingredients that are not usually consumed on their own should be mixed with 250 mg of protein-free cookie before digestion, to simulate a realistic food matrix. Table 2 provides an example of such a mixture. Moreover, because the INFOGEST protocol does not include a mastication step during the oral phase, the particle size must be manually adjusted. Both solid food samples and cookie blanks should be ground to a particle size of $- 2 - 3 \mathsf { m m }$ to mimic the effect of chewing55. This can be achieved with a manual or electric grinder, taking care to minimize sample heating, which could alter the structural and chemical properties of the sample.

The amount of food to be digested is adjusted to contain exactly 40 mg of crude protein. This value should be calculated on the basis of the total nitrogen content, preferably determined by the Kjeldahl method23, using a conversion factor of 6.25 (refs. 1,8). Milli-Q water is then added to reach a final sample mass of 1 g.

# Preparation of digestion fluids, tubes and pH test for in vitro digestion

The SSF, simulated gastric fluid (SGF) and SIF should be prepared according to the original INFOGEST 2.0 protocol4 (Supplementary Methods 2). These solutions can be aliquoted and stored at $- 2 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until use. Before digestion, the fluids are thawed and prewarmed to $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ , and their pH must be verified and adjusted if necessary using 2 mol/L HCl or NaOH. Target pH values are pH 7.0 for SSF and SIF, and pH 3.0 for SGF.

Digestions are conducted in individual $5 0 \mathrm { m l }$ plastic centrifuge tubes. Each tube must be weighed together with its corresponding cap, and the same tube–cap combination must be used throughout the experiment. This is essential to ensure accurate mass tracking, particularly during precipitation and centrifugation steps when small differences can affect the final calculations. A spreadsheet to log and monitor all weighing steps is provided in Supplementary Data 2, which also includes automated calculations for digestibility, DIAAR and DIAAS, as well as total digestibility, proxy-DIAAR and proxy-DIAAS values in case no individual amino acid analysis is available.

All samples should be digested in triplicate. If both the amino acid/amino group and total nitrogen are to be performed, an additional triplicate set is required because the digestion pellet is not homogeneous and must be processed in its entirety for each analytical endpoint. For example, if three samples plus one cookie blank are analyzed, a total of 12 digestions $( 4 \times 3 )$ are required for the analysis of amino acids and/or amino groups, and an additional 12 digestions $( 4 \times 3 )$ for total nitrogen.

Gastric pH after meal ingestion is known to vary depending on the food composition and buffering capacity and reaches its physiological pH gradually over time5,22,56,57. However, owing to the static nature of the INFOGEST 2.0 protocol4 , the pH is kept constant during each phase and is not dynamically adjusted. To compensate for the buffering effect of different foods, a preliminary pH adjustment procedure—referred to as the ‘pH test’—must be performed for each matrix before digestion5 . During this test, the digestion process is simulated while monitoring pH, and the exact volumes of 2 mol/L HCl and NaOH required to reach pH 3.0 (gastric phase) and pH 7.0 (intestinal phase), respectively, are recorded. An example of the typical acid and base volumes used for different samples is provided in Table 2.

# Oral, gastric and intestinal digestion

The digestion procedure is carried out in three sequential phases—oral, gastric and intestinal— following the original INFOGEST 2.0 protocol4 . A practical overview of the full in vitro digestion is provided in Table 2. In the oral phase, 1 g of food (total 40 mg of crude protein and no more than 100 mg of fat) is mixed with SSF in a 1:1 (wt/wt) ratio to form a bolus. The protein-free cookie should extensively dissolve at this stage. For starchy foods, salivary α-amylase is added to reach a final activity of $7 5 \mathrm { { U / m L } }$ of digesta. The enzyme is freshly dissolved in Milli-Q water immediately before use to prevent activity loss. The samples are incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 min to initiate starch breakdown. Importantly, if α-amylase is included in any sample digestion, the protein-free cookie blank must also be treated with the same amount of α-amylase under identical conditions. In a digestion series that includes both starchy and nonstarchy foods, two strategies are acceptable: (1) all samples (including nonstarchy food) and the cookie are treated with amylase for consistency or (2) two separate cookie blanks are included, one with and one without amylase, matched accordingly to the samples they represent. The chosen strategy should be applied consistently across triplicate digestions.

In the gastric phase, the oral bolus is combined with SGF at a 1:1 (vol/vol) ratio. The pH is adjusted to 3.0 using the volume of HCl determined during the pH test experiment. Pepsin is added to achieve a final activity of $2 , 0 0 0 \mathrm { U / m L }$ . It should also be freshly dissolved in Milli-Q water immediately before use. The mixture is incubated at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 h with continuous rotation.

In the intestinal phase, the gastric chyme is mixed with SIF at a 1:1 (vol/vol) ratio. The pH is adjusted to 7.0 using the premeasured volume (pH test experiment) of NaOH. This phase includes the addition of bile to a final concentration of 10 mmol/L and pancreatin extract being adjusted to a final trypsin activity of $1 0 0 \mathrm { U / m L }$ . The pancreatin solution is prepared freshly by dissolving the calculated amount of pancreatin in SIF. The solubilization of pancreatin is enhanced using an ultrasonic bath, followed by centrifugation to remove insoluble material. The supernatant is used immediately for intestinal digestion. Conversely, bile is dissolved in SIF and kept in continuous rotation at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for at least 30 min before intestinal digestion. Incubation is carried out at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 h under continuous rotation.

# Precipitation of the indigestible fraction

Following the completion of the intestinal digestion phase, enzymatic activity is halted, and the digest is fractionated by precipitation. To achieve this, the entire intestinal phase is mixed with ice-cold methanol to reach a final concentration of $80 \%$ (vol/vol). This step simultaneously inactivates enzymes and precipitates high-molecular-weight compounds such as intact proteins and large peptides, which cannot be absorbed in the small intestine. By contrast, lowmolecular-weight digestion products—including free amino acids and oligopeptides of up to ~10 amino acids in length—remain soluble in the methanol-water phase. As the in vitro digestion model does not include brush border peptidases, these short peptides would normally be further hydrolyzed in vivo to di- and tripeptides or free amino acids before absorption58,59. Their solubility in the methanol phase therefore serves as a practical proxy for the absorbable fraction under physiological conditions.

At this point, the tubes (with their respective lids) must be weighed again to determine the postprecipitation mass. Precipitation is further promoted by incubation at $- 2 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h. The samples are then centrifuged to separate the absorbable fraction (supernatant) from the nonabsorbable fraction (pellet). The supernatant is retained for amino acid, amino group or total nitrogen analysis, while pellets are washed with $80 \%$ methanol before drying. Freeze-drying is preferred, although a centrifugal concentrator is an acceptable alternative. After drying, the final weight of the dry pellets (including the tube and lid) must be recorded precisely. These measurements are essential for the accurate calculation of protein digestibility and derived metrics such as DIAAS and DIAAR.

# Analysis and digestibility determination

Protein digestibility is assessed by quantifying the proportion of digestion products in the absorbable (supernatant) relative to the total digested protein (supernatant plus pellet). Both fractions must therefore be analyzed independently. Depending on the desired resolution,

# Protocol extension

three analytical approaches can be applied: individual amino acids (UHPLC-UV analysis after derivatisation25), total amino groups (OPA method; Supplementary Methods 2) and total nitrogen (Kjeldahl method23). The overall analytical workflow is shown in Fig. 2.

All three methods enable the calculation of total protein digestibility. However, only the analysis of individual amino acids allows for the determination of the digestibility of individual amino acids and the subsequent calculation of the DIAAR and DIAAS values. By contrast, total nitrogen and total amino group analyses can only be used to estimate proxy-DIAAR and proxy-DIAAS values. These approximations assume uniform digestibility across all amino acids, which simplifies the analysis but ignores enzymatic specificity8 .

The total nitrogen content can be measured directly in the supernatants and dried pellets. By contrast, amino group and individual amino acid analyses require a preparatory acid hydrolysis step to break down peptides and proteins into their constituent amino acids. For this, the dried pellets and $2 2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ aliquots of supernatant are hydrolyzed in 6 mol/L HCl at $1 1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ on the basis of the Association of Official Agricultural Chemists official method 2018.06 (ref. 25), with minor modifications. Specifically, the hydrolysis time is reduced from 24 h to 15 h. This adjustment mitigates the degradation of thermosensitive amino acids such as tryptophan, serine and tyrosine. However, amino acids such as histidine, valine and isoleucine benefit from extended hydrolysis time and may be underestimated after 15 h (Supplementary Results 2). Thus, the chosen duration reflects a compromise between degradation and release dynamics, and in most cases, there is no statistically significant difference in the amino acid content of pellets and supernatants after 15 or 24 h of hydrolysis. Time-course hydrolysis experiments (5, 10, 15, 20 and 24 h) were conducted in four food matrices (wheat bran cereal (All-Bran), cheese, skim milk powder and protein-free cookie) with the 15 h experiments generally providing robust results with diminished degradation of labile amino acids, particularly tryptophan (Supplementary Results 2).

Hydrolysis is carried out in the presence of 3-3′-dithiodipropionic acid (DDP), which stabilizes cysteine and cystine by converting them to S-2-carboxyethylthiocysteine25,60. Phenol, which is often used to protect tyrosine from oxidative degradation, was intentionally omitted owing to health and safety concerns related to its toxicity.

For total amino group analysis, an aliquot of the hydrolysates may be diluted with borate solution (0.1 mol/L) to adjust the concentration to the calibration range and analyzed directly. For individual amino acid analysis, samples must be derivatized before UHPLC-UV analysis. l-norvaline is included as an internal standard, and a cysteine standard is hydrolyzed in parallel for calibration25. It should also be noted that asparagine and glutamine are deamidated during acid hydrolysis, converting into aspartic acid and glutamic acid, respectively. As a result, these pairs are quantified as the sum of both forms during chromatographic analysis25.

Determination of DIAAR and proxy-DIAAR values also requires quantification of the amino acid profile of the undigested food. For this, a 24 h acid hydrolysis is performed on the nondigested substrate under standard conditions25. Labile tryptophan in the intact food sample must be analyzed separately after alkaline hydrolysis following analytical conditions described by Nielsen and Hurrell61.

# Calculations

In vitro total protein digestibility is calculated as the proportion of protein-derived total nitrogen, total amino group or total amino acid present in the supernatant relative to the total amount recovered in both the supernatant and the pellet, corrected by subtracting the corresponding enzyme blank values from the protein-free cookie. The same principle applies to the in vitro digestibility of individual amino acids, with the only difference being the use of the concentration of each specific amino acid instead of the total concentration.

In highly digestible protein sources, it is assumed that all amino acids present in the pellet originate from enzyme autolysis rather than from undigested food. Enzyme autolysis is typically incomplete, and large fragments of the digestive enzymes are precipitated with methanol and accumulate in the pellet fraction. However, owing to analytical variation, subtraction of the enzyme blank may sometimes yield negative values in the pellet fraction. In such cases, these values should be set to 0 to avoid mathematical artifacts.

# Protocol extension

The general formula for calculating digestibility is as follows:

$$
\text {I n v i t r o d i g e s t i b i l i t y} = \frac {F \mathrm {s} - C \mathrm {s}}{(F \mathrm {s} - C \mathrm {s}) + \max (0 ; F \mathrm {p} - C \mathrm {p})}
$$

Fs is the total nitrogen/amino group/amino acid content in the food supernatant, Cs is the corresponding content in the cookie supernatant (enzyme blank), Fp is the total content in the food pellet and Cp is the content in the cookie pellet (enzyme blank).

To determine the DIAAR values, all indispensable amino acids are considered. The digestibility-corrected amino acid content of the food is related to the FAO1 reference values for three population groups: infants (birth to 6 months); young children (6 months to 3 years); and older children, adolescents, and adults (3 years and above). The formula is

$$
\text {In vitro DIAAR} (\%) = \frac {\left(\mathrm {mg IAA} / \mathrm {g} \text {food protein}\right) \times \text {in vitro digestibility} _ {\mathrm {IAA}}}{\left(\mathrm {mg IAA} / \mathrm {g} \text {reference protein}\right)} \times 100
$$

IAA is indispensable amino acid.

The DIAAS corresponds to the lowest calculated DIAAR value among all indispensable amino acids and reflects the limiting amino acid of a food.

When amino acid-specific digestibility data are not available, in vitro proxy-DIAAR values can be calculated using total protein digestibility instead, with calculations similar to those of in vitro digestibility but with total nitrogen, amino group or total amino acid content. This yields an approximate score, assuming equal digestibility for all amino acids. The same FAO reference profiles are applied. The formula is

$$
\text{In vitro proxy - DIAAR} (\%) \frac {(\mathrm {mg IAA} / \mathrm {g} \text {food protein}) \times \text {total protein digestibility}}{(\mathrm {mg IAA} / \mathrm {g} \text {reference protein})} \times 100
$$

Although proxy-DIAAR values can be useful when amino acid data are not available, they do not reflect true physiological conditions, as individual amino acids exhibit distinct digestibility efficiencies12. Therefore, when feasible, direct calculation of DIAAR and DIAAS values on the basis of individual amino acid digestibility is the preferred and recommended approach for evaluating dietary protein quality.

# Procedure

# Enzyme activity determination, pH test and protein-free cookie preparation

# ● TIMING 6 d

1. Prepare SSF, SGF and SIF stock solutions (‘Reagent setup’).   
■ PAUSE POINT At this stage, fluids can be stored in aliquots at $- 2 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for up to 2 years.   
2. Determine the activity of α-amylase, pepsin and pancreatin following the protocols in Supplementary Methods 1. Measure the concentration of bile acids according to the manufacturer’s instructions.   
■ PAUSE POINT This step is performed once for each new enzyme batch or after long-term storage ( ${ > } 3$ months at $- 2 0 \mathrm { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C } )$ , and each enzyme can be tested on a separate day. Enter the final activity values into the spreadsheet provided in Supplementary Data 1.   
$\spadesuit$ TROUBLESHOOTING  
3. Prepare the protein-free cookie (enzyme blank) according to the recipe in Table 1.   
■ PAUSE POINT The cookie is prepared only once for all the digestions.   
4. Measure the total nitrogen content of the food samples, preferably using the Kjeldahl method (according to ISO 8968-3 | IDF 20-3)23, and calculate the crude protein content using a conversion factor of 6.25 for all substrates.   
■ PAUSE POINT Total nitrogen is measured on a separate day before the pH test and the digestions.   
5. Conduct the pH test experiment by simulating the digestion (Steps 6–31) and recording the volume of HCl 2 mol/L needed to reach pH 3.0 in the gastric phase of digestion (Steps 18–23), and the volume of NaOH 2 mol/L needed to reach pH 7.0 in the intestinal phase of digestion (Steps 24–31). The weighing of tubes is not required for the pH test.   
■ PAUSE POINT This step is performed on a separate day before the digestions.   
$\blacktriangle$ CRITICAL STEP Perform one pH test per food and per batch of protein-free cookie.   
◆ TROUBLESHOOTING

# Preparation of the digestion for undigested food only (not the cookie blank)

# ● TIMING 2–4 h

$\blacktriangle$ ▲ CRITICAL This section (Steps 6–14) must take place on the same day as the in vitro digestion.   
6. Prewarm the digestion fluids (SSF, SGF and SIF) to $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ using a water bath.   
■ PAUSE POINT The tubes might need up to 1 h to thaw and warm up.

# Protocol extension

7. Check and adjust the pH of the digestion fluids using 2 mol/L HCl and/or NaOH if necessary.

■ PAUSE POINT At this stage, the tubes can be kept at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until use (up to 2 h).

8. Calculate the required amounts of enzymes, bile and solvents (Milli-Q water for α-amylase and pepsin, SIF for pancreatin and bile acids) on the basis of sample number and enzyme activity. Use Supplementary Data 1 for calculations.

9. Weigh the enzymes and bile acids in tubes of appropriate size (depending on the amount needed).

■ PAUSE POINT Keep the enzymes on ice until use. The enzymes will be solubilized immediately before use.

10. Prepare food samples to simulate real consumption conditions (for example, blanching, cooking, hydration or combining with other foods or cookie).

11. Simulate mastication of solid foods by reducing particle size to $\mathbf { - } 2 \mathbf { - } 3 \mathbf { m } \mathbf { m }$ using a mincer. Mix semisolid or liquid foods gently to ensure homogeneity.

$\mid$ PAUSE POINT The samples can be stored at room temperature or at $- 2 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until use (the temperature and shelf life will depend on the food type).

12. Prepare labeled $5 0 \mathrm { m l }$ centrifuge tubes for all samples and the cookie and record the combined weight of each tube (W1) and cap in Supplementary Data 2. Digest all samples in triplicate across different days, including a cookie blank in each set.

$\blacktriangle$ CRITICAL STEP Always use the same tube–cap combination throughout to ensure accurate mass tracking, as small differences in weight can affect final calculations.

13. Weigh the appropriate sample amount into the preweighed tubes to provide $4 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of protein (based on nitrogen content $\times 6 . 2 5 )$ . Combine protein powders with more than $50 \%$ of protein with $2 5 0 \mathrm { m g }$ of cookie to simulate a proper meal. Adjust the total weight to 1.000 g using Milli-Q water. Weigh 1 g of protein-free cookie for each blank. Register all weights in Supplementary Data 2.

$\spadesuit$ TROUBLESHOOTING

14. Precool pure methanol and $80 \%$ methanol at $- 2 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathsf { C } .$ . Aliquot 32 mL of pure methanol into $5 0 \mathrm { m l }$ plastic centrifuge tubes (one per digestion) to facilitate later fraction separation (Step 32). These tubes can be reused for collecting supernatant after precipitation.

■ PAUSE POINT Methanol is stored at least 3 h before digestion.

# Digestion process

● TIMING ~6 h

15. Oral phase (15 min): dissolve α-amylase in Milli-Q water (volume calculated in Supplementary Data 1).   
16. Add ${ 8 0 0 } \mu \mathrm { L }$ SSF 1.25×, 5 µL CaCl2 0.3 mol/L and $9 5 \mu \iota$ Milli-Q water to each digestion tube.   
17. Add $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of $\alpha$ -amylase solution, mix by vortexing and incubate at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 min on a rotator. Use time intervals of 15 or 30 s between samples to guarantee the same incubation time for all samples.   
$\blacktriangle$ CRITICAL STEP If the food does not contain starch, the addition of $\alpha$ -amylase is not needed. In this case, the amount of $\alpha$ -amylase is substituted with Milli-Q water, there is no need for the 2 min incubation, and the cookie blank must not contain $\alpha$ -amylase. If starchcontaining and starch-free foods are digested simultaneously, either treat all samples with amylase or run a separate cookie blank for each condition.   
18. Gastric phase (2 h): stop the oral phase by adding 1.6 mL SGF $1 . 2 5 \times$ (consider oral phase timing intervals).   
19. Add the predetermined volume of HCl 2 mol/L from the pH test (Step 5) to reach pH 3.0.   
20. Add 1 µL $\mathbf { C a C l } _ { 2 } 0 . 3 \mathbf { m o l } / \mathrm { L }$   
21. Adjust the volume with Milli-Q water to maintain fluid balance as shown below: $\mathsf { H } _ { 2 } \mathbf { O }$ needed $\left( \mathsf { \Pi } \mu \mathbf { L } \right) = 2 9 9 \left( \mathsf { \mu L } \right) - \mathsf { H C }$ l volume added $( { \mu } { \nu } )$ , as calculated in Supplementary Data 1.   
22. Dissolve the pepsin in Milli-Q water as calculated in Supplementary Data 1.   
23. Add $1 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the pepsin solution, vortex and start the incubation on a rotator at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 h. Keep the same time intervals between processing the different samples.

# Protocol extension

24. Intestinal phase (2 h): dissolve the bile acids with SIF $1 . 2 5 \times$ as calculated in Supplementary Data 1 at least 30 min before starting the intestinal phase and agitate on a rotator at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until use.

# ◆ TROUBLESHOOTING

25. Dissolve the pancreatin with SIF $1 . 2 5 \times$ (volume calculated in Supplementary Data 1) 20 min before starting the intestinal phase, using an ultrasonic bath for 5 min (45 Hz, 130 W) at room temperature to assist the solubilization. Centrifuge the pancreatin solution at ${ 3 , 0 0 0 g }$ and $1 5 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 5 min, collect the clear supernatant with a pipette in a new tube and use it as the pancreatin extract for digestion.

CRITICAL STEP The pancreatin extract should be clear and free of slimy residue, and the ultrasonication and centrifugation conditions should be carefully followed to avoid degradation of the enzymes.

# $\spadesuit$ TROUBLESHOOTING

26. Stop the gastric phase with the addition of 1.7 mL SIF $1 . 2 5 \times$ (keep the same time intervals for processing the different samples).   
27. Add the predetermined volume of NaOH 2 mol/L from the pH test (Step 5) to reach pH 7.0.   
28. Add $\mathbf { 8 \mu L C a C l _ { 2 } O . 3 m o l / L }$ .   
29. Adjust the volume with Milli-Q water to maintain fluid balance as shown below: ${ \sf H } _ { 2 } { \sf O }$ needed $\mathrm { ( \mu L ) } = 7 9 2 \mathrm { ( \mu L ) } - \mathrm { N a O H }$ volume added (µL) as calculated in Supplementary Data 1.   
30. Add $5 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the bile solution.   
31. Add $1 { , } 0 0 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of the pancreatin solution to the tubes, vortex and incubate on a rotator at $3 7 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 2 h. Keep the same time intervals for processing the different samples.

# Precipitation

● TIMING 2–3 h and 2 d for freeze-drying

32. At the end of the intestinal phase, stop the reaction by adding 32 mL of ice-cold pure methanol. Directly pour the methanol from the prefilled tubes into the digesta and mix immediately (keep the same time intervals for processing the different samples). Record the weight (W2) of the individual digestion tubes (including the cap and contents) in the spreadsheet in Supplementary Data 2.   
33. Incubate the tubes at $- 2 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for 1 h.

■ PAUSE POINT If needed, the tubes can be stored at $- 2 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ overnight, and phases can be separated later.

34. Centrifuge at $_ { 4 , 0 0 0 g }$ and $4 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C }$ for 15 min. Precool the centrifuge in advance.   
35. Carefully transfer the supernatant to empty $5 0 \mathrm { m l }$ plastic centrifuge tubes (use of the empty methanol tubes is recommended) using a pipette, leaving ~5 mL above the pellet to avoid transferring some interphase. Store supernatants at $- 2 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ until further analysis, which should be performed within 1 month.   
36. Add 5 mL of ice-cold $80 \%$ methanol to each pellet and mix thoroughly using a vortex until the pellet is fully dispersed.   
37. Centrifuge at 4,000g and $4 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C }$ for 10 min.   
38. Discard the supernatant carefully with a pipette, avoiding disruption of the pellet or interphase (if present).   
39. Repeat Steps 36–38 for a second methanol wash.

■ PAUSE POINT At this stage, supernatants and pellets can be stored at $- 2 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for up to 1 month.

40. Freeze the pellets with liquid nitrogen, dry ice or by placing them in a $- 8 0 { } ^ { \circ } \mathbf { C }$ freezer. Proceed to dry them in a freeze-drier (a centrifugal concentrator can be used as an alternative). Depending on the freeze-drier, this step can take up to 2 d.   
41. After drying, register the weight (W3) of each digestion tube (including cap and dry pellet) in the spreadsheet in Supplementary Data 2.

# Protein digestibility determination

42. If measuring the digestibility of individual amino acids and calculating DIAAR values, follow option A and option B. If assessing total digestibility and proxy-DIAAR only, follow option A

and option C for the OPA method, or option D only for the total nitrogen method (Kjeldahl). Option B and option C can be performed with the same option A sample; however, option D cannot be combined with options A, B or C.

# (A) Hydrolysis for DIAAR with UHPLC or proxy DIAAR with OPA

# ● TIMING 5 h

(i) Acid hydrolysis for amino acid and/or amino group analysis: immediately after drying (avoid pellets absorbing water), transfer the pellets into $_ { 1 0 \mathrm { m l } }$ glass vials, crush with a spatula and record the weight of the pellet in the spreadsheet in Supplementary Data 2.

■ PAUSE POINT The vials can be stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C }$ with sealing flm for a maximum of 1 week.

(ii) Transfer $2 2 0 \mu \mathrm { L }$ of each supernatant into 1.5 mL glass vials and proceed to dry them using a freeze-drier (freeze samples beforehand) or a centrifugal concentrator (liquid sample can be used).

$\sqcap$ PAUSE POINT The vials can be stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C }$ with sealing flm for a maximum of 1 week.

(iii) Add DDP solution $1 \%$ , HCl 0.2 mol/L, HCl $3 7 \%$ and Milli-Q water to the dried supernatants and pellets following the proportions listed in Table 3.

$\blacktriangle$ CRITICAL STEP For chromatographic analysis of total amino acids, l-norvaline must be added to the sample as an internal standard, and a cysteine standard (100 pmol/µL fnal concentration) should be hydrolyzed in parallel with the samples. No internal standards are required for OPA analysis. However, if both total amino acids and total amino groups are to be analyzed from the same hydrolysates, l-norvaline must still be included. Its contribution to the amino group content is automatically subtracted in the corresponding spreadsheet (Supplementary Data 2).

(iv) Flush the headspace of the 10 mL vials (pellets) with a $\mathsf { N } _ { 2 }$ gas for 10 s to displace oxygen.

(v) Mix each vial (supernatants and pellets) thoroughly using a vortex for 10 s.

(vi) Incubate the sealed vials at $1 1 0 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { C }$ for $1 5 \mathsf { h }$ for acid hydrolysis.

■ PAUSE POINT After hydrolysis, the samples can be stored at $4 ^ { \circ } \mathbb { C }$ for up to $4 8 \mathrm { h }$ .   
$\blacktriangle$ CRITICAL STEP Samples should be analyzed within $4 8 \mathsf { h }$ , as certain amino acids (especially tryptophan) continue to degrade over time.

As noted previously, quantification of total amino acids is preferred over total nitrogen or amino group analysis when assessing protein quality, as it enables the calculation of individual amino acid digestibility, DIAAR and DIAAS. Representative chromatograms of the supernatant and pellet of a protein-free cookie and All-Bran cereal are shown in Fig. 3. Typically, pellet injections yield higher peak areas owing to the higher sample concentration, but this is corrected during calculation, assuming at least partial digestibility of the protein.

Figures 4 and 5 show that all three analytical methods yielded comparable total protein digestibility results. In fact, Sousa et al.8 reported no statistically significant differences between them. Moreover, in vitro digestibility values correlated well with in vivo data obtained from human and pig studies. The digestibility of individual amino acids is also highly correlated with in vivo data and varied depending on the amino acid type. These differences were more pronounced in lower-digestibility proteins such as All-Bran, compared to highly digestible proteins such as WPI. In addition, poorly digestible proteins tended to show greater variability among replicates. Proteins with high digestibility often reach DIAAS above $100 \%$ , because they deliver a higher amount of essential amino acids than required according to the reference protein1 . By contrast, proteins with lower digestibility usually yield lower DIAAS values, indicating an insufficient supply of at least one essential amino acid.

In vitro digestibility results are interpreted as the proportion of protein or a specific amino acid that is potentially absorbable in the small intestine. DIAAR values, by contrast, assess the capacity of a protein to deliver bioaccessible indispensable amino acids, relative to FAO reference requirements. For example, the reference leucine (LEU) value for children (6 months to 3 years) is $6 6 \mathrm { m g / g }$ protein1 . Consider the following case: food 1 contains 75 mg LEU/g protein with a LEU digestibility of 0.95, resulting in a DIAAR of $1 0 8 . 0 \%$ $7 5 \times 0 . 9 5 / 6 6 \times 1 0 0 )$ ). Food 2 contains 86 mg LEU/g protein, but with only 0.54 digestibility, with a DIAAR of $7 0 . 4 \%$ $8 6 \times 0 . 5 4 / 6 6 \times 1 0 0 )$ ). Despite the higher LEU content, food 2 supplies less digestible LEU owing to poor digestibility and would therefore not meet the nutritional requirement for LEU per gram of protein.

The DIAAS value, defined as the lowest DIAAR value across all indispensable amino acids, identifies the limiting amino acid and summarizes the overall protein quality of a food. A DIAAS value above $100 \%$ indicates that all essential amino acid requirements are met; values below $100 \%$ indicate a deficiency. DIAAS and DIAAR values are particularly useful for formulating complementary food combinations, in which the amino acid profile of one ingredient compensates for the deficiencies of another, enabling the design of balanced meals.
